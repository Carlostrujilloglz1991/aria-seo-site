RN Malpractice Insurance 2026 — Do You Really Need Your Own Policy?

Last updated: July 2026 | Reading time: 8 min

This article was created with AI assistance and reviewed for clinical accuracy.

Most nurses believe their hospital's malpractice insurance fully protects them. That belief is partially correct and critically incomplete. The hospital's policy protects the hospital. Your interests may align — or they may sharply diverge. This guide explains exactly when your employer's coverage leaves you exposed, what individual nurse malpractice insurance actually costs (hint: it's cheap), and who needs it most.

The conflict of interest you need to understand: When a patient sues both you and the hospital, your employer's insurance provides you with a defense attorney — but that attorney represents the hospital's best interests, not yours. If placing blame on your nursing judgment reduces the hospital's liability, that's what your "free" defense will argue. You are a witness in your own case, not the client.

The Licensing Board Investigation Gap

This is the most underappreciated risk for nurses. A patient complaint to your state Board of Nursing (BON) doesn't require a lawsuit — any patient (or family member) can file a complaint, triggering an investigation that could result in your license being suspended or revoked. This is a separate process from civil malpractice litigation, and your employer's insurance almost never covers BON defense costs.

BON investigations require legal representation if you want to protect your license properly. Attorney costs for BON defense: $3,000–$30,000+ depending on complexity. Individual nurse malpractice policies typically include $25,000–$50,000 in BON defense coverage. For a policy that costs $100–$200/year, this alone justifies the premium.

Who Needs Individual Nurse Malpractice Insurance Most

High Priority

  • Travel nurses — coverage gaps between contracts, working at unfamiliar facilities with higher error risk during orientation
  • Per diem / agency nurses — especially if receiving 1099 income (contractor status may mean no employer policy)
  • Nurses who volunteer — Good Samaritan acts, health fairs, school nursing, camps — none covered by employer policy
  • NPs and CRNAs — higher scope of practice, higher malpractice exposure (see CRNA malpractice guide for CRNA-specific coverage)
  • Nurses with significant personal assets — if you own property or have substantial savings, an uncovered judgment could put them at risk

Lower Priority (but still worth considering)

  • Staff nurses at large hospitals with adequate employer policies and minimal outside nursing activity
  • Nurses just starting out with few personal assets to protect

For most RNs, the annual premium is $100–$200. For that cost, you get an independent attorney representing YOUR interests (not the hospital's) and BON defense coverage that most employer policies exclude.

Occurrence vs. Claims-Made: Which Type to Buy

There are two types of malpractice policy structures:

Occurrence Policy

Covers incidents that occurred during the policy period, regardless of when the claim is filed. An incident that happened while you had the policy is covered even years later after the policy lapses. This is the type most individual nurse malpractice policies are — and it's the better type because coverage follows the incident, not the active policy status.

Claims-Made Policy

Covers claims filed while the policy is active. If you let the policy lapse, you lose coverage for incidents that happened while you were covered. Claims-made policies require "tail coverage" when you cancel — an additional purchase to extend coverage for claims filed after the policy ends. Most claims-made policies are corporate/hospital policies, not individual nurse policies. Most individual nurse policies are occurrence — verify before buying.

The math is obvious: At $150/year, individual nurse malpractice insurance costs less than a single shift's pay. For a coverage gap that your employer's policy explicitly excludes — the licensing board investigation — the policy effectively pays for itself the first time a patient (or a disgruntled family member, or a colleague with a grudge) files a BON complaint. That complaint doesn't have to be valid to cost you $5,000+ in legal fees. Buy the policy.
