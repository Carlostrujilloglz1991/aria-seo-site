CRNA Programs in West Virginia

West Virginia has two accredited nurse anesthesia programs — a long-established DNP at West Virginia University and a Doctor of Nurse Anesthesia Practice at the University of Charleston. Two in-state options give applicants more room than most small states, but both are competitive and clinically demanding.

The landscape: West Virginia has two COA-accredited nurse anesthesia programs — West Virginia University in Morgantown and the University of Charleston in Charleston, partnered with Charleston Area Medical Center. Both award doctoral degrees and run full-time over three years.

WVU's School of Nursing runs a well-established DNP Nurse Anesthetist program in Morgantown. It is offered full-time only, requires 88 credits across nine continuous semesters, and recently earned a 10-year accreditation through 2035 — the highest level the COA grants, which is a strong signal of program stability and quality. For a state program, that long accreditation term is worth weighting heavily when you compare options.

University of Charleston — Doctor of Nurse Anesthesia Practice (DNAP)

The University of Charleston offers a Doctor of Nurse Anesthesia Practice in partnership with Charleston Area Medical Center, one of the state's largest hospital systems. The CAMC partnership matters: a strong, high-volume clinical partner is exactly what a nurse anesthesia student needs to log the cases that build competence. Note the early application cycle — the Fall 2026 deadline fell in mid-December 2025, so future cohorts will likely follow a similar roughly-one-year-ahead timeline.

Admission Requirements

Both West Virginia programs follow the national CRNA template. You will need a BSN with a competitive GPA, a minimum of one year of full-time adult critical-care experience, and current BLS and ACLS certification. The ICU requirement is non-negotiable, and competitive applicants typically bring more than the minimum year, high-acuity experience, and the CCRN credential — see the CCRN guide. For the full checklist, review the general CRNA school requirements.

Two programs is an advantage — use it, but still apply regionally. Having both WVU and the University of Charleston in-state gives West Virginia applicants more than most small states get. Apply to both, then widen the list to nearby programs in Virginia, Pennsylvania, Ohio, and Kentucky to maximize your odds.

Application Strategy for West Virginia

West Virginia applicants are in a relatively strong position with two in-state doctoral programs. Apply to both WVU and the University of Charleston, watch the early-December deadline for the Charleston cohort, and build a regional backup list across Virginia, Pennsylvania, Ohio, and Kentucky. Strengthen your file with the required ICU year (ideally more), the CCRN, and a sharp personal statement. For realistic expectations, see CRNA acceptance rates.

Bottom Line

West Virginia offers two accredited CRNA paths: WVU's DNP Nurse Anesthetist in Morgantown, carrying a 10-year accreditation through 2035, and the University of Charleston's DNAP partnered with CAMC. Two in-state options give applicants real flexibility — but both are competitive, so apply to both and keep a regional backup list.
