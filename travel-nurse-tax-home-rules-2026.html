The tax-free housing and meal stipends that make travel nursing so financially attractive rest entirely on one legal concept: the tax home. If the IRS decides you don't have one — or that your tax home is wherever you're currently assigned — every stipend you received becomes taxable income. For a nurse earning $30,000 a year in stipends, that's a five-figure tax bill with penalties and interest on top.

The core rule: Tax-free travel stipends are only legal when you're temporarily away from your tax home. No tax home = no tax-free stipends. The IRS does audit travel nurses, and "I didn't know" is not a defense.

What Is a Tax Home?

The IRS defines your tax home as your regular place of business or, if you have no regular place of business, your regular place of abode. For most travel nurses, this means the city or general area where you maintain a permanent residence and have your primary professional ties.

Your tax home is not necessarily where your family lives or where you grew up. It's the place you return to between assignments, where you have ongoing financial and personal ties, and where continuing to work duplicates expenses. That duplication of expenses — paying for housing both at home and on assignment — is the economic logic behind why stipends are tax-free.

The Three-Factor IRS Test

The IRS uses a three-factor analysis to determine whether you have a tax home. Satisfying all three gives you the strongest position. Satisfying two puts you in a defensible but riskier spot. Satisfying only one is a red flag.

Most tax professionals advise that you need to satisfy at least two of the three factors, with financial ties — meaning you're actually paying for housing you're not currently living in — being the most important of all.

What "Maintaining" a Tax Home Actually Requires

This is where many travel nurses make costly mistakes. Simply claiming a parent's address or a childhood bedroom as your home address does not constitute maintaining a tax home. The IRS wants to see that staying away from that location is genuinely duplicating your costs.

Minimum documentation you should have

At a minimum, you should be paying rent on an actual apartment or home in your tax home city — even if it's a modest amount. The rent should reflect a real lease with your name on it. Alternatively, if you own a home, your mortgage payments, property taxes, and insurance all count. The key is an ongoing financial obligation that you continue to pay even while on assignment.

Beyond housing: keep your driver's license and vehicle registration in your home state, file your state income taxes as a resident of your home state, maintain bank accounts at a local institution, and if possible, pick up occasional per diem or PRN shifts at a facility in your home area. None of these alone is definitive, but together they paint a credible picture of genuine dual-expense burden.

Common mistakes that destroy tax home status: Letting your home lease expire "to save money," subletting your entire apartment for the whole duration of your assignment, moving everything you own to each new assignment city, or going back-to-back on contracts in the same city for more than a year. Any of these can cause the IRS to reclassify your tax home as wherever you're currently working.

The 12-Month / One-Year Rule

An assignment location becomes your tax home if you realistically expect to work there for more than one year. This is a critical trap for travel nurses who keep extending at the same facility. The IRS's position: if you knew or reasonably should have known that the assignment would exceed twelve months, the location is not temporary, and stipends from the beginning of that assignment may be retroactively taxable.

Practical rule: do not plan to stay at a single location for more than eleven months, and document any unexpected extensions in writing if they push you close to the limit. Each contract renewal should reflect a genuine expectation of departure.

What Happens If You Lose Your Tax Home

If the IRS determines you had no valid tax home during a period when you received stipends, those stipends — housing allowances, meals and incidentals, travel reimbursements — all become ordinary taxable income. For a nurse receiving $2,500 per month in tax-free stipends, that's $30,000 in additional gross income per year. At a 22% marginal rate, you owe $6,600 in federal income tax. Add the employee Social Security and Medicare taxes, plus state income tax, plus a 20-25% accuracy penalty if the IRS considers the underreporting substantial, plus interest — and a single year's audited liability can easily exceed $10,000.

State Tax Complication for Multi-State Travelers

Beyond the federal tax home question, travel nurses must also manage state income tax filings. Most states require you to file a non-resident return in every state where you earned income, even for short-term assignments. Your home state may also tax all your income as a full-year resident. Some states have reciprocity agreements; most don't. A CPA who specializes in travel nurse taxes can save you far more than their fee by ensuring you're not double-taxed and that you're filing correctly in every state you worked.

What good recordkeeping looks like: Keep a folder (physical or digital) for each contract year containing: your home lease or mortgage statements, utility bills for your home address, home state bank statements, paystubs from any home-area PRN work, and a log of days you were at your tax home vs. on assignment. If you're ever audited, this documentation is what wins the case.

Legitimate Ways to Maximize Stipend Income

Keeping a genuine tax home isn't just a compliance burden — it's the mechanism that lets travel nurses earn $90,000 to $130,000 annually with a much lower effective tax rate than a staff nurse at the same W-2 income would face. The math works because $40,000 in tax-free stipends is worth significantly more than $40,000 in taxable wages at a 25% marginal rate.

To optimize legally: negotiate for the maximum allowable housing and M&IE stipend that the GSA per diem rates for the assignment location support, pick assignments in high-cost cities where GSA rates are highest, and use a reputable travel nurse CPA service annually — not a general tax preparer who processes your return once and moves on.
