Nurse Practitioner vs CRNA Salary 2026

CRNAs earn roughly twice what most NPs earn — but the path is harder, longer, and requires specific ICU experience first. Here is the full comparison so you can make an informed decision about which route fits your goals.

Specialties and Market Demand

NP Specialties by Pay

NP salary varies significantly by specialty. The highest-paying NP specialties in 2026:

  • Psychiatric-Mental Health NP (PMHNP): $140,000 – $175,000 (highest demand, shortage market)
  • Acute Care NP (ACNP): $130,000 – $165,000
  • Emergency NP: $125,000 – $160,000
  • Dermatology NP: $120,000 – $155,000 (highest private practice upside)
  • Family NP (FNP): $110,000 – $140,000 (most common; most competitive)

CRNA by Setting

  • Hospital employed (academic / urban): $185,000 – $230,000
  • Anesthesia group / private practice: $220,000 – $280,000
  • Rural / critical access hospital: $200,000 – $260,000 (often higher for coverage shortage)
  • Military / federal: $180,000 – $240,000 plus benefits
  • Locum CRNA (contract): $250 – $400+/hour depending on market

Which Path Is Right for You?

Choose NP if:

  • You want to enter advanced practice in 2-3 years without ICU experience
  • You prefer outpatient or clinic-based work with more predictable hours
  • You want the flexibility to specialize across multiple populations (adult, peds, psych, women's health)
  • Telehealth or entrepreneurship appeals to you (NPs have more options here)
  • You have no interest in the procedural OR environment

Choose CRNA if:

  • You have ICU experience or are willing to build it (this is not optional)
  • The income premium matters significantly to your financial goals
  • You thrive in high-acuity, procedural, technically demanding environments
  • You want a career with strong portable value (CRNAs can practice anywhere)
  • You can handle the demand of a highly competitive graduate program

The Long-Term Math

Assuming an NP starts practice at age 28 and a CRNA starts at 32 (due to ICU time + longer program), and both work to age 65:

  • NP: 37 years x $130,000 avg = $4.8M lifetime gross
  • CRNA: 33 years x $215,000 avg = $7.1M lifetime gross

The CRNA path generates roughly $2.3M more over a career despite starting 4 years later. The 4-year delay costs about $520,000 in lost earnings, leaving a net lifetime premium of approximately $1.8M — before accounting for the larger retirement contributions a higher income enables.

Bottom line: If you have the ICU background and the drive for the program, CRNA is one of the highest-ROI graduate degrees in healthcare. If you want faster entry into advanced practice or prefer a different clinical environment, NP is a strong career with growing demand and genuine autonomy. Both are worth it — they are just different bets.
