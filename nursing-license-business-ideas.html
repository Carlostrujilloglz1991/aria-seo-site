10 Businesses You Can Start With Your Nursing License

Your RN license isn't just a credential for hospital employment. It's a business asset that permits you to deliver clinical services, provide legally-recognized healthcare advice, and supervise other healthcare workers in a way that people without your license cannot. That regulatory permission is the foundation of multiple viable business models.

Here are 10 businesses nurses have launched, with realistic startup costs and income ranges.

1. Mobile IV Therapy Business ($3,000–$8,000 startup)

You deliver IV hydration, vitamin infusions, and recovery drips directly to clients at their home, hotel, or office. In most states, this requires an RN license, a physician medical director (who can often be contracted for $200–$500/month), liability insurance ($1,500–$2,500/year for a mobile clinical practice), and supplies.

Revenue model: $150–$350 per infusion, 4–8 sessions per day on weekends = $600–$2,800/day. Experienced mobile IV businesses with 2–3 nurses report $15,000–$40,000/month in gross revenue. Startup kit including IV supplies, a reliable cooler, and marketing materials: $3,000–$5,000.

2. Private Duty / Concierge Nursing ($0–$1,000 startup)

Wealthy clients, elderly patients, and high-acuity post-surgical patients hire private duty nurses for one-on-one care that hospital staffing ratios can't provide. Rates: $45–$85/hour for individual nurses, $100–$150/hour for specialty nurses (CRNA-supervised, ICU-trained). You can start with zero overhead — your license, insurance, and a professional network are all you need.

Concierge nursing services for celebrities, executives, or medical tourists operate at the higher end of this range. A nurse who builds a referral network among plastic surgeons, orthopedic surgeons, and hospital discharge planners can fill a full schedule without advertising.

3. Legal Nurse Consulting ($500–$1,500 startup)

Attorneys in personal injury, medical malpractice, workers' compensation, and insurance defense need nurses who can read medical charts and explain clinical causation. Legal nurse consultants (LNCs) review records, write reports, and sometimes testify as expert witnesses.

Startup costs: LNC training course ($500–$1,500 — optional but helpful), professional liability insurance, and basic office setup. Income: $75–$200/hour for chart review, $200–$500/hour for expert witness testimony. A part-time LNC working 15 hours/week can earn $3,000–$8,000/month.

4. CPR / First Aid Training Company ($500–$2,000 startup)

Becoming an AHA (American Heart Association) Training Center or BLS instructor and offering CPR classes to businesses, schools, churches, and fitness studios is a low-capital business with recurring clients. Businesses that require CPR-certified staff (gyms, daycares, corporate offices, construction companies) need annual recertification.

Revenue per class: $25–$50/person × 10–20 students = $250–$1,000 per 2-hour class. Running 2 classes per week: $500–$2,000/week. Startup costs: AHA instructor certification ($300–$600), CPR mannequins ($800–$1,500 for 4 adults + 2 children), marketing materials.

Scaling path: Start solo, build recurring business clients, then add subcontractor instructors. A CPR training company with 3–4 contractor instructors can service 15–25 classes per week — $7,500–$25,000/month in revenue — with the owner managing scheduling rather than teaching every class.

5. Home Health Agency ($5,000–$20,000 startup)

Starting a home health agency — which employs or contracts CNAs, home health aides, and nurses to provide in-home care to elderly or post-surgical clients — requires state licensure (varies by state, typically $1,000–$5,000 in fees and 3–6 months to process), liability insurance, and startup working capital to cover the gap between providing care and receiving payment from Medicare/Medicaid or private pay.

This is the highest-startup-cost business on the list but also the highest-ceiling. Home health agency gross revenues of $200,000–$500,000/year are achievable within three years with the right market and operations. Your nursing license qualifies you to serve as the Agency Director of Nursing — a requirement in most state home health agency licenses.

6. Aesthetic Nursing / Med Spa ($5,000–$25,000 startup)

In states where RNs can administer Botox, dermal fillers, and laser treatments under physician supervision (most states), aesthetic nursing is a high-margin business. Botox injected at a cost of $6–$10/unit sells for $12–$18/unit. A patient who needs 40 units = $480–$720 in revenue for a 30-minute appointment.

Startup path: complete an aesthetic nursing training course ($3,000–$8,000 for a reputable in-person program), secure a physician collaborator, obtain liability insurance specific to aesthetic procedures, and start either as an independent contractor for an existing med spa or build your own client base. Nurses in urban markets building their own aesthetic practice report $8,000–$20,000/month in gross revenue after 12–18 months.

7. Nurse Coach / Wellness Coaching ($200–$1,000 startup)

Nurse coaching is a growing specialty focusing on chronic disease management, lifestyle modification, and health behavior change. The American Institute of Health Coaching and INAC offer nurse coach certifications ($500–$2,000). Sessions are delivered remotely at $75–$200/hour, with packages common ($500–$1,500 for 8-week programs).

8. Healthcare Staffing / Nurse Staffing Agency ($3,000–$10,000 startup)

Placing nurses, CNAs, and allied health professionals at local facilities as a staffing agency middleman. You need an LLC, a workers' comp policy (major cost — typically $8,000–$15,000/year), and a client relationship or two before placing your first nurse. Margin is typically 20–35% of the bill rate. At scale, a regional nurse staffing agency doing $1M in annual billings generates $200,000–$350,000 in gross margin before overhead.

9. Travel Nurse Agency or Placement Business

A lighter-capital version of the staffing agency: a travel nurse placement referral or consulting service that helps nurses navigate agency contracts, negotiate rates, and find assignments. Monetized through a finder's fee paid by the agency (typically $500–$2,000 per successful placement) or a consulting fee paid by the nurse ($200–$500 for contract review and negotiation services).

10. Clinical Education Content Business

Selling continuing education courses, clinical reference guides, and professional development content to nurses, nursing schools, and hospital education departments. Lowest startup cost on the list ($0–$500), highest leverage relative to time invested once content is created. Scales from a solo nurse selling PDFs on Etsy to an education company with multiple authors, accredited CE programs, and institutional licensing agreements.
