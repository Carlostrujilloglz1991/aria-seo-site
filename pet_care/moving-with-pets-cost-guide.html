Moving With Pets: Real Costs and What to Plan For

Moving with a dog or cat is doable — but it adds costs, logistics, and stress that are worth planning for in advance.

Home › Pet Care › Moving With Pets

Moving is consistently rated one of life's most stressful events — and adding a pet to the equation doesn't make it easier. Your dog doesn't understand why everything smells different. Your cat disappears into the walls of the new place for three days. And somewhere in the chaos, you realize you haven't found a new vet yet.

The good news: with a little advance planning, moving with pets is genuinely manageable. Here's everything you need to budget for and think through — whether you're moving across town or across the country.

The Real Costs of Moving With a Pet

Local Move (same city or state)

  • Pet-friendly housing search: $0 cost (but more time)
  • Pet deposit or non-refundable pet fee: $200–$500 typically
  • Monthly pet rent: $25–$75/month (adds up significantly)
  • New vet establishment visit: $50–$90
  • Updated ID tags and microchip address update: $5–$20
  • Boarding during moving day (if applicable): $35–$85
Total additional cost: $295–$790 upfront + $300–$900/year in pet rent

Long-Distance Move (different state)

  • Health certificate from vet (required for air travel, sometimes for interstate): $50–$150
  • Airline pet fees (in-cabin): $95–$200 each way (if flying)
  • Airline pet fees (cargo, not recommended): $200–$600 each way
  • Pet-friendly hotel nights during road trip: $10–$50/night pet fee
  • New state licensing/registration (for dogs): $10–$25
  • Pet transport service (if not bringing yourself): $300–$1,500+
  • Pet deposit and rent at new place: $200–$500 deposit + pet rent
Road trip total: $100–$400 extra | Flying: $250–$800 per pet additional

Finding Pet-Friendly Housing

This is genuinely the hardest part of moving with a pet — especially in competitive rental markets. Here's a realistic breakdown of the landscape:

Pet rent math: $50/month pet rent = $600/year = $3,000 over a 5-year tenancy. That's a real cost to factor into housing budget comparisons. A building with no pet rent but a $500 non-refundable fee is often cheaper long-term than one with $50/month pet rent.

Tips for Finding Pet-Friendly Rentals

  • Lead with your pet's strengths: Create a "pet resume" — a one-page document with your dog's photo, vaccine records, training history, and a landlord reference if you have one. It sounds over the top; it genuinely works.
  • Offer a larger deposit: Some landlords would say yes to a pet if they felt financially protected. Offering an extra $200–$300 deposit specifically for the pet can turn a "no pets" into "for a responsible tenant..."
  • Use niche search filters: Zillow, Apartments.com, and Trulia all allow filtering for "pets allowed." Facebook Marketplace often surfaces individual landlords (not management companies) who are more flexible.
  • Know your rights regarding ESAs: Under the Fair Housing Act, landlords must make reasonable accommodations for emotional support animals even in "no pets" buildings — and cannot charge pet fees for them. ESAs require documentation from a licensed mental health professional. This is a legitimate accommodation, not a workaround.
  • Look for privately owned units over large complexes: Corporate property management companies follow strict breed/size policies with no exceptions. Individual landlords have more flexibility and often care more about the quality of the tenant than the species.

Moving Day: Keeping Your Pet Safe

Option 1: Board Your Pet on Moving Day

Moving day with a dog or cat underfoot is chaotic and genuinely dangerous — open doors, furniture being moved, stressed strangers in the house, and the pet's own stress response. Boarding your pet for just moving day ($35–$85) is one of the highest-ROI expenses in the move. Book it in advance.

Option 2: Confine to One Secure Room

If boarding isn't an option, put your pet in one room that won't be part of the move traffic, with clear signage on the door ("Pet inside — keep closed"), their food, water, bed, and a familiar toy. Check on them periodically. Put a note on the front door for movers.

For Cats: The "One Room" Strategy for the New Home

Cats introduced to a new home need a controlled entry process to prevent hiding, panic, or bolting. Set up one room at the new home with everything your cat needs: litter box, food, water, familiar bedding, and their carrier (door open). Let them be confined to this room for 24–72 hours before gradually expanding their access. This dramatically reduces the stress response and prevents the "disappeared cat" situation that terrifies new arrivals.

Long-Distance Travel With Pets: Road Trip vs. Flying

Road Trip

For most pet owners moving long distance, driving is significantly less stressful for both the human and the pet. Dogs generally travel well in cars; cats less so, but they adapt.

  • Plan rest stops every 2–3 hours for dogs (bathroom, water, brief walk)
  • Never leave pets in a parked car, even briefly — temperatures rise dangerously fast
  • Use a secured crate or pet seatbelt harness — unrestrained pets are a serious safety hazard in accidents
  • Bring your pet's regular food (diet changes during a move cause GI upset) and a familiar-smelling blanket
  • Ask your vet about anti-anxiety medication for long trips if your pet is prone to travel stress — gabapentin and Trazodone are commonly prescribed for travel anxiety

Flying With Pets

Flying with pets is genuinely stressful for both pets and owners and should be avoided if a reasonable alternative exists. If you must fly:

  • In-cabin (under seat): Only for small pets (under ~20 lbs) in an airline-approved soft-sided carrier. Call the airline to confirm availability — most limit pets per flight and sell spots on a first-come basis.
  • Cargo: Highly stressful, risky (pressure/temperature variations, loading errors), and genuinely not recommended except as a last resort. Flat-nosed breeds (brachycephalic breeds like Bulldogs, Pugs, Boxers, Persian cats) should never fly cargo due to respiratory risks.
  • Health certificate required: Most airlines require a USDA-accredited vet health certificate dated within 10 days of travel. Schedule this appointment carefully — $50–$150 and needs to be timed right.

After the Move: Settling In

  • Update the microchip address immediately: Most microchip registries (AAHA Universal Pet Microchip Lookup, Found Animals) allow online updates. Don't skip this — it's the most important safety step for a moved pet.
  • New ID tag with your new address: A $5–$10 investment that matters enormously if your pet gets loose.
  • Find a new vet before you need one: Don't wait for an emergency to find your new veterinarian. Ask for a recommendation from your previous vet, check Google reviews, or ask local neighborhood Facebook groups.
  • Transfer records: Request your pet's complete medical history from your previous vet — most clinics will email records for free. Bring them to your first appointment at the new vet.
  • Keep routines as consistent as possible: Same feeding times, same walk routes (once you're settled), same bed location. Routine is enormously reassuring to anxious animals in a new environment.
