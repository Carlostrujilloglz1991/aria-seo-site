Dog Grooming Costs: What You'll Pay and How Often Your Dog Needs It

Grooming prices by breed type, what services are actually worth paying for, and how to stretch your grooming budget.

Home › Pet Care › Dog Grooming Costs

Dog grooming is one of those pet costs that sneaks up on owners — especially those who chose a Goldendoodle, Bichon Frisé, or Shih Tzu without fully accounting for the fact that these dogs need professional haircuts every 6–8 weeks for life. That's $600–$1,200/year just in grooming fees.

But grooming costs are also one of the most controllable pet expenses. Here's what you actually need to pay for, what the going rates look like, and how to spend less without letting your dog turn into a matted disaster.

Dog size also matters independently of coat type. A large Goldendoodle will cost significantly more to groom than a miniature Poodle even if both have curly coats — there's simply more dog to work with.

Reality check for Doodle owners: If you have a Goldendoodle, Labradoodle, or Bernedoodle, budget $800–$1,200/year for grooming minimum. Their curly coats mat rapidly if not maintained on a 6–8 week schedule, and badly matted dogs require "shaving down" which can cost even more and take months to grow back.

What Services Come With a Standard Grooming Appointment

A typical "full groom" at most grooming salons includes:

  • Bath with shampoo and conditioner (appropriate for coat type)
  • Blow-dry and brush-out
  • Haircut or trim (breed-appropriate or per your instructions)
  • Ear cleaning
  • Nail trim
  • Anal gland expression (at some salons — important to ask)
  • Bandana or bow (the finishing touch you didn't know you needed)

"Bath and brush" services (no haircut) are cheaper — $35–$65 for most dogs — and are useful for maintenance between full grooms.

Grooming Prices by Location

Where you live significantly affects what you pay. Urban areas and coastal cities run 30–60% higher than rural or midwest markets.

  • New York City, LA, San Francisco: Full groom for a Doodle, $120–$180+
  • Suburban mid-sized cities: Same dog, $80–$120
  • Rural areas and small towns: $55–$85

Mobile grooming services (the van that comes to your house) add a $20–$40 premium to typical salon prices — but eliminate the drop-off and pickup hassle, which many owners find worth it.

How to Reduce Grooming Costs

Brush Consistently Between Appointments

This is the single most effective thing you can do to reduce grooming costs. A dog who comes in well-brushed with no mats takes significantly less time to groom — and many groomers charge for "dematting time" as a separate fee ($10–$50+ depending on severity).

How often to brush, by coat type:

  • Short-coat breeds: Monthly or less — mostly just run a rubber brush over them
  • Medium-coat breeds: 2–3x per week minimum
  • Curly/wavy/long coats: Daily or every other day to prevent mats
  • Double coats: 3–4x per week; daily during shedding season

Learn to Do Nails at Home

Most groomers charge $10–$25 for a nail trim, or include it in the full groom price. Learning to clip your dog's nails at home takes one YouTube tutorial and a few awkward practice sessions — then it's free forever. Guillotine-style clippers or a Dremel grinder both work well. Go slowly, avoid the quick, and have styptic powder on hand.

💡 If your dog is terrified of nail trims, try the "scratchboard" method: tape sandpaper to a board at an angle, put peanut butter on one side, and let your dog scratch their own front nails while licking the treat. It sounds silly and it genuinely works.

Look for Grooming School Clinics

Grooming school students need real dogs to practice on. Grooming school clinics offer significantly reduced prices — often 40–60% below salon rates — with supervising instructors overseeing the work. Your dog might take longer, but the results are usually excellent and the price is hard to beat.

Negotiate a Regular-Client Rate

If you're bringing your dog to the same groomer every 6–8 weeks for years, it's completely normal to ask about a loyalty discount or standing appointment rate. Many small grooming businesses offer 10–15% discounts for consistent clients who book regular appointments in advance.

Learn Basic Grooming Yourself

With an initial investment of $150–$300 in a quality clipper set, grooming scissors, and a grooming table (optional), you can learn to groom your own dog. This makes the most financial sense for owners of dogs who need frequent grooming (Doodles, Poodles, Schnauzers). At $100/groom × 8 grooms/year, you break even on equipment in under 6 months. Resources: YouTube (Horsin' Around Grooming has excellent tutorials), Reddit's r/doggrooming community, and local grooming classes offered by some pet supply stores.

Signs Your Dog's Grooming Schedule Is Off

Your dog's coat tells you when grooming is overdue. Watch for:

  • Mats forming at the ears, armpits, groin, and collar area — these tangle quickly and can cause skin irritation
  • Nails clicking on hard floors — means they're too long and putting strain on paw joints
  • Excessive shedding on furniture despite regular brushing — may signal a deshedding treatment is needed
  • Dirty or smelly ears — indicate cleaning is overdue (and possibly an ear infection)
  • Scooting behavior — can indicate full anal glands needing expression

Neglected grooming isn't just aesthetic — it causes real discomfort and can lead to skin problems, ear infections, and painful matting that requires veterinary attention. The cost of prevention (regular grooming) is almost always lower than the cost of treatment.
