Cheap Dog Boarding Options and Alternatives That Actually Work

You don't have to pay $75/night at a kennel. Here are the real alternatives — and how to make them work safely.

Home › Pet Care › Dog Boarding Alternatives

Traditional dog boarding at a kennel can cost $35–$85 per night in most markets — and significantly more in cities or at premium facilities. For a week-long vacation, that's $245–$595 just to house your dog. For two weeks, you might spend more on boarding than on your actual trip.

The good news: there are several legitimate, safe alternatives that cost significantly less — or nothing at all. Here's an honest breakdown of each option, including what it's actually like to use them.

Rover vs. Wag: The Real Comparison

Both Rover and Wag are apps that connect dog owners with independent pet sitters and dog walkers. The model is similar to Airbnb — real people offering dog care services in their own homes or yours.

Rover

$25–$55/night for overnight stays

How it works: You browse sitter profiles with reviews, photos, and their home setup. You can meet the sitter in advance (highly recommended), and book directly through the app. Rover handles payment and provides a guarantee for certain situations.

Strengths: Large sitter network, detailed profiles and reviews, ability to book "meet and greets" before committing, better customer support than Wag. Many Rover sitters are experienced dog people who genuinely love animals.

Weaknesses: Rover's service fee is 5–7% on top of the sitter's rate. Quality varies — you need to read reviews carefully and do a meet and greet. Not all sitters are equally experienced, and their homes aren't inspected by Rover.

Best tip: Filter by "Only 1 dog at a time" for dogs who don't do well in chaotic environments. Read recent reviews specifically about communication and whether the sitter sent updates.

Wag

$28–$60/night for overnight stays

How it works: Similar to Rover but skews toward on-demand booking. Better for last-minute care. Also offers drop-in visits and dog walking.

Strengths: GPS tracking on walks (useful for peace of mind), generally faster to match with available sitters, operates in more urban markets.

Weaknesses: Less emphasis on the relationship-building that makes Rover successful. Sitter consistency is lower — you might get a different sitter each time. Customer service has had mixed reviews historically.

Best tip: Wag is better for drop-in visits and dog walking than overnight stays. For overnight care, Rover's more established sitter relationship model is generally the better experience.

💡 The Rover hack: Find a sitter you love on Rover, establish a relationship, do a few bookings through the app, and then — once you've built genuine trust — ask if they'd be willing to work directly in the future. Many will, at a slightly lower rate (since they skip the app fee). This is technically against Rover's terms, but it's widely practiced and both sides benefit.

Building a Friend and Family Network

The best — and cheapest — dog boarding solution is people who already love your dog. This sounds obvious, but many owners never invest in building these relationships systematically.

How to build a pet care reciprocity network:

  1. Identify your candidates: Friends, family members, and neighbors who have dogs or who love dogs. Dog parks are great for this — the people there have dogs, they're nearby, and you already have something in common.
  2. Propose reciprocity explicitly: "I'd love to watch your dog when you travel if you'd be up for watching mine when we travel." Make the arrangement clear from the start so no one feels like they're being taken advantage of.
  3. Do a trial run: Have your dog stay with them for one night on a low-stakes occasion (not before your two-week international trip). Make sure both dogs and both humans are comfortable.
  4. Offer compensation for imbalanced schedules: If one person travels significantly more than the other, offer cash or gifts to balance it. $20–$30 per night to a trusted friend is still dramatically cheaper than a kennel.
  5. Leave detailed care instructions: Every feeding, medication, quirk, and emergency contact. The more prepared they are, the more confident they'll feel, and the more likely they'll say yes next time.

Training Your Dog for Home Alone Time

For short trips of 1–3 days, a well-trained dog with a reliable dog walker may not need boarding at all. A dog who's comfortable being home alone for 8–10 hours (with a midday dog walk visit) can handle overnight absences if someone comes in the morning and evening to feed them, walk them, and give them attention.

The math: two drop-in visits per day from a dog walker at $20/visit = $40/day versus $60–$80/night at a kennel. For a 3-day trip: $120 versus $180–$240.

What makes this work:

  • A dog who doesn't have separation anxiety or destructive behavior when alone
  • A reliable dog walker who can come at specific times
  • Security cameras (optional but reassuring) — a $30 indoor camera lets you check in remotely
  • A neighbor with a key for emergencies
  • Access to a yard or reliable dog walker for multiple potty breaks

When to Just Bring Your Dog

Dog-friendly travel is more accessible than ever. If your dog travels reasonably well and you're driving (not flying), many hotels, Airbnbs, and vacation rentals now welcome dogs — often for free or a modest pet fee of $15–$35/night.

Resources for dog-friendly travel:

  • BringFido.com: Searchable database of dog-friendly hotels, restaurants, activities, and parks by destination
  • Kimpton Hotels: Zero pet fee, no size or breed restrictions — among the most genuinely dog-friendly major hotel chains
  • Airbnb and VRBO: Filter for "pets allowed" — often a one-time fee of $50–$100 rather than per-night charges

Bringing your dog does require some planning and may limit certain activities, but it eliminates boarding costs entirely and gives your dog the best possible outcome: being with you.

Red Flags to Watch For (in Any Boarding Situation)

Whether you're using a kennel, Rover, or a friend, certain red flags signal a situation that may not be safe for your dog:

  • No ability to visit before boarding (especially with kennels)
  • Overcrowded spaces without adequate per-dog separation
  • Sitter or facility that won't answer questions about their experience with dogs similar to yours
  • No emergency contact or vet protocol
  • A Rover/Wag sitter with very few reviews, no photos, or reviews that mention "minor incidents"
  • A friend who seems hesitant but is saying yes to be polite — better to find another solution than put them in an uncomfortable position
