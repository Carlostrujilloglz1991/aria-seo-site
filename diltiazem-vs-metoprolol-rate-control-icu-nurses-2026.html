Diltiazem vs Metoprolol for Rate Control 2026 — Slowing AFib with RVR

Part of the ICU Pharmacology Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response is one of the most common rhythms you'll chase in the ICU, and the first fork in the road is which drug slows the rate: the calcium channel blocker diltiazem or the beta-blocker metoprolol. Both work through the AV node, both come as IV push and drip, and the choice usually hinges on the patient's heart function and blood pressure.

The short version: Both slow AV-nodal conduction to control the ventricular rate in AFib/atrial flutter. Diltiazem (a non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker) is a reliable, titratable rate-controller but is negatively inotropic and avoided in reduced-EF heart failure. Metoprolol (a beta-1 blocker) is preferred when there's active ischemia, high sympathetic tone (thyrotoxicosis, post-op), or the need to avoid a diltiazem drip — but is cautioned in bronchospasm and decompensated heart failure. Neither belongs in a patient who is unstable (that patient needs cardioversion) or who has AFib with pre-excitation (WPW).

How each controls the rate

In AFib the atria fire chaotically; the ventricular rate depends on how many impulses the AV node lets through. Both drugs act there. Diltiazem blocks L-type calcium channels, slowing AV conduction and also relaxing vascular smooth muscle (some blood-pressure drop). Metoprolol blocks beta-1 receptors, blunting the sympathetic drive that speeds the node — which is why it shines when the fast rate is being driven by catecholamines (pain, sepsis emergence, thyroid storm, post-cardiac-surgery).

When diltiazem is the better pick

Diltiazem tends to control rate quickly and is easy to run as a continuous infusion after a weight-based bolus, making it a workhorse for sustained RVR with preserved ejection fraction and adequate blood pressure. It's also the go-to when a patient has a contraindication to beta-blockade such as significant reactive airway disease. Because it lowers blood pressure through vasodilation, a borderline pressure is the main thing to watch before and during a bolus.

When metoprolol is the better pick

Metoprolol is favored when there is active myocardial ischemia or recent MI (beta-blockade has independent benefit), when the tachycardia is clearly sympathetically driven, and often in the post-cardiac-surgery patient. Given as intermittent IV pushes, it's a reasonable first move for RVR when you want to avoid committing to a drip. It causes less direct vasodilation than diltiazem, though it can still drop pressure and, importantly, still depresses contractility.

The heart-failure caution — and where they overlap

Both are negatively inotropic — be very careful in decompensated, reduced-EF heart failure. Diltiazem (and verapamil) are generally avoided in HFrEF because their myocardial depression can tip an already-struggling ventricle into cardiogenic shock. Metoprolol is a cornerstone of chronic stable heart failure, but pushing it acutely into a patient who is currently decompensated can also worsen output. In acute decompensated HF with RVR, the team may reach for other strategies (for example, amiodarone or digoxin) precisely to avoid these agents. Know your patient's EF and volume status before you slow the rate.

Two more shared hard stops: a hemodynamically unstable patient (hypotension, ischemia, or pulmonary edema clearly caused by the rhythm) needs synchronized cardioversion, not a rate-control drug that takes minutes to work. And in AFib with pre-excitation (WPW), AV-nodal blockers can paradoxically accelerate conduction down the accessory pathway and precipitate VF — both diltiazem and metoprolol are contraindicated there.

Bedside monitoring, in short: Get the EF and current hemodynamics before dosing. Continuous ECG and frequent (or arterial-line) blood-pressure monitoring during and after a bolus or drip titration — the feared adverse effects are hypotension and bradycardia/AV block. Reassess the rate and rhythm after each change, keep atropine and fluids/pressors available, and never stack an IV calcium channel blocker on top of an IV beta-blocker without an explicit order — the combined AV-nodal suppression can cause profound bradycardia.
