Paraquat Poisoning for ICU Nurses 2026 — The Poison Where Oxygen Is the Enemy

Part of the ICU Emergencies Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Almost everything a nurse's instinct says to do for a struggling, hypoxic patient is exactly wrong in paraquat poisoning. This herbicide kills by turning oxygen into a weapon inside the lungs, so the reflex to reach for supplemental oxygen accelerates the very injury that is killing the patient. Paraquat is rare in much of the United States and more common where it is used agriculturally, but it earns a place in ICU knowledge precisely because it inverts the usual rules and because a small swallow can be lethal with heartbreaking predictability.

The short version: Paraquat is a herbicide that generates reactive oxygen species (redox cycling), destroying cells through oxidative stress. It concentrates in the lungs, causing early alveolar injury and then relentless pulmonary fibrosis, along with kidney and liver failure. The most counterintuitive nursing point: avoid supplemental oxygen unless truly necessary (target the lowest saturation that is safe, often accepting a lower SpO2), because oxygen fuels the free-radical damage. There is no proven antidote; care centers on decontamination, limiting oxygen, supportive treatment, and honest prognostication. Ingestion of even a mouthful of concentrate is frequently fatal.

Why oxygen turns lethal

Paraquat's toxicity comes from a chemical trick called redox cycling. Inside cells, paraquat repeatedly accepts and donates electrons, and in doing so it continuously generates reactive oxygen species — superoxide and other free radicals — that shred cell membranes, proteins, and DNA. The lung is uniquely vulnerable because certain cells in the alveoli actively take up and concentrate paraquat, so the toxin accumulates there to many times its blood level. The result is a two-phase lung injury: an early, ARDS-like alveolar damage in the first days, followed — in those who survive the acute phase — by a relentless, progressive pulmonary fibrosis that stiffens the lungs into failure over one to two weeks. Because more oxygen means more raw material for free-radical formation, giving a paraquat patient high-flow oxygen literally feeds the reaction. This is the mechanistic reason behind the rule that shocks every new nurse: in paraquat poisoning you withhold oxygen and tolerate lower saturations, reversing the instinct that governs almost every other critically ill patient. Kidney and liver injury follow the same oxidative logic, and renal failure is doubly dangerous because the kidneys are the main route of clearance.

Dose predicts outcome with brutal clarity

Few poisons map so directly from amount swallowed to outcome, which makes early history and any available plasma paraquat level genuinely prognostic.

Rough exposureTypical course
Small / dilute ingestionGI and local corrosive symptoms; possible survival with organ support
Moderate ingestionMulti-organ failure and progressive lung fibrosis over days to weeks; high mortality
Large ingestion (mouthful of concentrate)Fulminant multi-organ failure and death within hours to days; often unsurvivable

Early symptoms are deceptively local — a caustic burning of the mouth, throat, and esophagus, painful ulceration, nausea, and vomiting — which can make a lethal ingestion look, in the first hours, like a bad chemical burn. Over the following days the systemic picture unfolds: acute kidney injury, hepatic injury, and the beginning of the pulmonary decline. Because the lung fibrosis is delayed, a patient can appear to stabilize and then deteriorate into progressive dyspnea and refractory hypoxemia during the second week — a cruel trajectory that families need to be prepared for.

Bedside priorities: decontaminate, restrict oxygen, support, and be honest

Nursing care in paraquat poisoning is a combination of a few concrete interventions and a large amount of difficult supportive and emotional work. Early on, if the patient presents soon after ingestion, gastrointestinal decontamination with an adsorbent such as activated charcoal (or Fuller's earth, where used) may be given to bind unabsorbed poison, so the nurse expedites this while protecting the airway in a patient who may be vomiting and have a burned oropharynx. The defining ongoing nursing rule is oxygen restriction: unless the patient is dangerously hypoxic, supplemental oxygen is deliberately withheld or minimized, and the team accepts lower-than-usual saturation targets — a practice that must be clearly communicated and documented so a well-meaning colleague doesn't “fix” the sat and worsen the injury. The rest is supportive: aggressive management of the caustic GI injury and pain, careful fluid and renal support (renal replacement therapy is often needed as the kidneys fail), monitoring liver function, and treating complications as they arise. Various therapies aimed at the oxidative injury (immunosuppression, antioxidants) have been tried, but none is a reliable cure, and the nurse should not offer false reassurance. Perhaps the hardest and most important nursing role is honest, compassionate support of the patient and family through a prognosis that is frequently grim, including early involvement of palliative care for large ingestions. Because many paraquat exposures are intentional self-harm, mental health support and safety planning are an essential part of care for survivors.

Do not reflexively give oxygen to a paraquat patient. In almost every other emergency, oxygen is safe and helpful; in paraquat poisoning it accelerates the free-radical lung injury that is killing the patient. Withhold or minimize supplemental oxygen unless the patient is critically hypoxic, tolerate lower saturation targets, and make sure every team member at the bedside knows this rule is intentional.

The nursing bottom line

Paraquat poisoning is the poison that inverts the rules: a herbicide that kills by manufacturing free radicals, concentrates in the lungs, and turns supplemental oxygen from a lifesaving tool into an accelerant. Its course is defined by dose — a mouthful of concentrate is often unsurvivable — and by a delayed, relentless pulmonary fibrosis that can claim a patient who briefly seemed to stabilize. There is no dependable antidote, so care rests on early decontamination, deliberate oxygen restriction, renal and supportive management, mental health support, and honesty. The nurse who resists the instinct to crank up the oxygen, who protects a burned airway during decontamination, and who supports a patient and family through a hard truth is practicing the specific, counterintuitive discipline that paraquat demands. This article touches on self-harm; if you or someone you know is struggling, contact your local crisis line or, in the US, call or text 988.
