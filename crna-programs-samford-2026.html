Samford CRNA Program 2026

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

Samford University's Nurse Anesthesia program in Birmingham, Alabama has built a strong regional reputation in the Southeast. Here's how the doctoral program is structured, what admissions weighs, and how ICU nurses put together a competitive application.

Quick profile: Samford's Nurse Anesthesia program sits within its Moffett & Sanders School of Nursing in Birmingham, Alabama, and awards a practice doctorate. Birmingham is a major Southeastern medical hub, home to large academic and community hospital systems, which supports the program's clinical training. Samford is a private university with a faith-based mission and a strong regional presence.

Why Samford

Samford pairs a well-regarded private university with Birmingham's dense medical landscape. The city is one of the Southeast's significant healthcare centers, giving anesthesia residents access to substantial surgical volume and acuity. For nurses in Alabama and the surrounding region who want a respected doctoral program without leaving the Southeast — and who value a mission-driven institution — Samford is a strong target.

The Doctoral Structure

Samford awards a practice doctorate, consistent with the Council on Accreditation's requirement that all nurse anesthesia programs offer doctoral entry as of January 1, 2025. Expect a front-loaded curriculum — advanced physiology, pharmacology, and anesthesia principles early — transitioning into a progressive clinical residency across Birmingham-area and affiliated sites, plus a doctoral scholarly project. The program is full-time and demanding; working during it is generally not feasible.

Apply broadly: Samford is competitive, and the Southeast has several strong programs. Build a balanced list — Samford plus other regional targets — so a single cycle doesn't rest on one decision.

Building a Competitive Application

Strong Samford applicants combine solid academics with real critical-care depth: a year or more in a high-acuity ICU independently managing ventilated patients on vasoactive drips with arterial and central lines, the CCRN, and CRNA shadowing that lets them speak specifically about the role. Essays and interviews should show a clear, informed reason for pursuing anesthesia and, where relevant, alignment with the program's mission. Leadership and precepting experience help distinguish otherwise similar candidates.

Cost and Financial Planning

Samford is a private university, so tuition is on the higher end, and the full-time doctoral structure means little income during the program. Most residents finance through federal graduate loans and savings. The return comes afterward: new CRNAs commonly start around $185,000–$215,000, with locum and high-demand assignments reaching $240,000–$290,000+. Birmingham's cost of living is relatively moderate, which helps during the program — but still budget carefully. Model your total cost of attendance before committing.

Bottom line: Samford is a respected Southeastern nurse anesthesia program in a strong medical city, with a mission-driven identity. Target it with a year or more of high-acuity ICU, a CCRN, real shadowing, and a specific application — and keep a balanced regional list so your CRNA path isn't riding on one outcome.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Samford a good CRNA program? Yes — it has a strong regional reputation and trains in a major Southeastern medical hub. Verify current certification pass rates on the program's published outcomes.

Do I need the CCRN? Treat it as required. It signals critical-care mastery and is effectively expected at competitive programs.

Can I work during school? Generally no. The full-time doctoral program is demanding; plan financially for a stretch with little income.
