Travel Nurse Taxes in California 2026

Part of the Travel Nurse Tax Hub — tax homes, stipends, and every state guide in one map.

California pays some of the highest travel nurse rates in the country — and it also has one of the highest state income taxes. Understanding how California treats your wages and stipends is the difference between keeping that premium pay and handing a surprising chunk of it back in April.

Not tax advice. This is general educational information. Travel nurse taxes are fact-specific, and California is aggressive about residency. Work with a tax professional who understands multi-state travel healthcare before filing.

California Taxes the Money You Earn There

California imposes a state income tax, and it taxes income earned from work performed within the state — regardless of where you live. So when you take a California assignment, the taxable wage portion of your contract is generally subject to California income tax as nonresident-sourced income. California's rates are progressive and among the highest in the nation, so the state tax bite on a high California bill rate is meaningful. Your agency will typically withhold California tax, and you'll usually file a California nonresident return (Form 540NR) to reconcile it.

Two Buckets: Taxable Wages vs. Tax-Free Stipends

A travel contract splits your pay into a taxable hourly wage and non-taxable reimbursements (housing, meals, and incidentals — often called stipends or per diems). The stipends are only tax-free if you genuinely maintain a tax home and are working away from it on a temporary basis. If you don't have a legitimate tax home, those stipends become taxable income — a costly reclassification.

The Tax Home Rule Is Everything

Your tax home is generally your main place of business or work — but for travelers who move between assignments, it's typically the place where you maintain a permanent residence and to which you regularly return. To defend a tax home you should be duplicating living expenses — paying for a permanent home base (rent or mortgage you keep up) and paying for lodging on assignment. A common test travelers use: keep genuine ties to a home region (a residence you pay for, return visits, driver's license, voter registration, mailing address) and don't stay in one work area so long it becomes your new tax home.

The 12-month rule: The IRS generally treats work in a single location as "temporary" only if it's realistically expected to last one year or less. Stay in the same metro area beyond roughly 12 months and the IRS can deem it your new tax home — making stipends there taxable. Many travelers rotate locations and take a break before returning to the same area to stay clearly temporary.

California Residency Traps

California is unusually aggressive about claiming people as residents. If you're not a California resident, be careful not to accidentally establish residency: don't let a California assignment stretch so long, with so many California ties (a California license, registration, extended continuous presence), that the Franchise Tax Board treats you as a resident taxable on all your income — not just California-sourced wages. Conversely, if California is genuinely your home state, you'll be taxed as a resident on your worldwide income. Know which side you're on and keep the documentation to prove it.

Practical California Takeaways

California's high pay is real, but so is its high state income tax and its aggressive residency posture. Budget for California withholding when you compare a California contract to a no-income-tax state like Texas or Florida — the headline rate isn't the take-home. Protect your tax home rigorously so your stipends stay tax-free, avoid staying in one California metro past the 12-month danger zone, and file a nonresident California return unless California is genuinely your home. A travel-savvy tax preparer will save you far more than they cost.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I pay California tax on a California travel assignment? Generally yes — California taxes income earned from work performed in the state, even for nonresidents, via a nonresident return.

Are my stipends taxed in California? Stipends stay tax-free only if you maintain a valid tax home and are temporarily away from it. Without that, they become taxable income.

Can California claim me as a resident? It can if you build enough California ties or stay long enough. Keep strong home-state documentation and be deliberate about how long you stay.
