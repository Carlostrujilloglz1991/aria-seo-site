CRNA School Letters of Recommendation

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

Recommendation letters are the part of a CRNA application you control the least and worry about the most. You can't write them, you can't read most of them, and a weak one can quietly undercut an otherwise strong file. The good news: a little strategy in who you ask, and how, removes almost all of that risk.

The short version: most programs ask for two to three letters. Schools commonly want at least one from your current manager or supervisor and one that speaks to your academic ability — and many require a letter from a CRNA or other provider who has seen you in practice. Always follow each program's exact rules; the wrong recommender type can get an application rejected.

How many letters, and from whom

The typical requirement is two to three letters. Several competitive programs require three, often with specific roles attached. Common requirements include a letter from your current manager or supervisor (someone responsible for evaluating you, who can speak to your work over the last six to twelve months), a letter that attests to your academic ability and drive to learn — frequently from former nursing-school or graduate faculty — and, at many schools, a letter from a CRNA or anesthesia provider who has worked with you or whom you've shadowed.

Who makes the strongest letter

The best recommender is not the most impressive title — it's the person who can write specifically and warmly about your critical-care practice. A charge nurse who has watched you manage a crashing patient and can describe exactly what you did will write a far stronger letter than a chief nursing officer who barely knows you. Specificity is everything: committees can instantly tell a letter written from real observation from a generic template. Choose people who have seen you think.

How to ask

Ask early — at least two to three months before your deadline, and earlier during busy seasons. Ask in person or by a thoughtful message, and explicitly give the person an easy out: "Do you feel you could write me a strong letter for CRNA school?" A hesitant yes is a warning sign. Once someone agrees, make their job effortless: send your resume, your draft personal statement, the specific qualities each program wants emphasized, the submission link and exact deadline, and a short note on what you're proud of from your time working together. The more raw material you hand them, the more specific and glowing the letter.

Read each program's rules before you ask anyone. Required recommender types vary by school — some mandate a CRNA letter, some a manager letter, some an academic reference. After everything else you put into this application, you don't want it rejected over the wrong type of recommender. Confirm the requirements on each program's admissions page first.

Timing and logistics

Most programs submit letters through NursingCAS or a portal, which emails your recommender a link. Give recommenders the deadline minus a comfortable buffer, then follow up politely about a week out if you can see the letter hasn't been submitted. Line up one extra willing recommender beyond the minimum in case someone goes silent — a missing letter can hold your whole file. Always waive your right to view the letter (FERPA); committees weight confidential letters more heavily.

The move that pays off: start building these relationships a year before you apply. Volunteer for the hard assignments, precept, join a committee, and let your manager and a CRNA see you at your best. When you ask, you'll be asking people who already have a strong, specific story to tell.

A simple plan

Map each target program's letter requirements into a single sheet, identify recommenders who cover every required role, ask early and give them an easy out, hand each a complete packet, waive your viewing rights, and track submissions so nothing stalls. Letters are the one part of the application written by someone else — your job is to make it effortless for them to write you a great one.
