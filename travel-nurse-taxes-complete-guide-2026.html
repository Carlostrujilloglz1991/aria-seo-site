Every year, thousands of travel nurses get a tax bill they weren't expecting. Not because they did anything wrong. Not because they were dishonest. But because travel nurse taxes work differently than any other income category — and most people, including most CPAs, don't know the rules well enough to protect you.

The single most important fact about travel nurse taxes: your stipends are only tax-free if you maintain a legitimate tax home. Lose that, and everything changes. That $1,500/week in housing and M&IE stipends becomes fully taxable income — retroactively, going back to whenever your tax home lapsed. With a potential IRS audit adding penalties and interest on top, the financial damage can be severe.

This guide covers everything: the tax home rules, the stipend structure, the 2026 GSA rates, every deduction you're entitled to claim, and how to decide whether you need a specialist CPA. The goal is simple: you should never be surprised by your travel nurse tax bill.

Why Travel Nurse Taxes Are Different (and Why Regular CPAs Get It Wrong)

The U.S. tax code has specific provisions for workers who are "temporarily away from home" on business. Travel nurses live inside this carve-out. The rules allow you to receive tax-free reimbursements for housing and meals — not as income, but as expense reimbursements — as long as certain conditions are met.

The problem is that these rules exist in a part of the tax code (IRC Section 162 and related Treasury Regulations) that most CPAs encounter maybe once in a career. A CPA who does tax returns for small businesses, families, and local professionals has probably never seen a travel nurse return. When they encounter one, they have two common failure modes:

Failure Mode 1: They treat all stipends as taxable. This is the most conservative interpretation — and it's wrong. You end up paying taxes on money that should have been tax-free, potentially overpaying by $8,000–$15,000 per year.

Failure Mode 2: They accept the travel nurse's claim of tax-free stipends without asking the right questions. If you don't have a valid tax home — or if you've been on the road for more than 12 months in one location without moving — the stipends aren't tax-free. A CPA who doesn't know to ask is setting you up for an audit.

You need either a travel-nurse-specialist CPA, or the knowledge to evaluate your own situation and bring them specific questions. This guide gives you that knowledge.

The Core IRS Rule
IRS Publication 463 (Travel, Gift, and Car Expenses) governs travel nurse stipend tax treatment. The key standard: you must be temporarily away from your tax home for business purposes. "Temporarily" means an assignment that is realistically expected to last less than one year. Assignments that become permanent, or exceed one year in the same area, make stipends taxable.

The Tax Home Requirement: What It Is, How to Maintain It, What Happens If You Lose It

The tax home is the foundation of your entire travel nurse tax strategy. Get this right and everything else flows from it. Get it wrong and nothing else matters.

What Is a Tax Home?

Your tax home is your principal place of business — or, in the IRS's language, your "regular place of business or post of duty." For travel nurses, this is typically the area where you live permanently and would work if you weren't traveling. It doesn't have to be where you grew up, or where your driver's license is registered, or where your parents live. It's where you maintain a real, established home base.

Critically, a tax home is a financial commitment, not just a claimed address. The IRS looks at whether you're actually maintaining a home somewhere — paying rent or a mortgage, keeping utilities running, returning there between assignments. A parent's address where you occasionally crash doesn't qualify. A storage unit doesn't qualify. A friend's couch doesn't qualify.

What Happens If You Lose Your Tax Home?

If you lose your tax home — usually because you move out of your permanent residence without establishing a new one — you become what the IRS calls an itinerant worker. Itinerant workers have no tax home, which means they can never be "away from home" on business, which means their stipends are entirely taxable. All of them. Going forward and potentially retroactively if the loss of tax home happened mid-year.

This is not a gray area. The IRS has prevailed in Tax Court on this point repeatedly. If you can't point to a specific location where you maintain a real home — with documented expenses — you have no tax home.

Audit Risk: The Full-Time Traveler
Travel nurses who travel continuously for 2+ years, claim a family member's address as their tax home, but pay no rent there and return only for vacations are the most common IRS audit target in this profession. The audit process involves reviewing your bank statements, lease history, utility bills, and assignment locations over multiple years. The outcome is typically $20,000–$60,000 in back taxes, penalties, and interest.

Tax Home Maintenance Checklist: 10 Items

Maintaining your tax home is an ongoing active process. Use this checklist to confirm your tax home is defensible if the IRS ever asks. The more of these you can document, the stronger your position.

✅ Travel Nurse Tax Home Checklist (2026)

  1. You pay rent or a mortgage at your home address. Monthly payment records, lease agreements, or mortgage statements. This is the most important item — financial commitment is the IRS's primary test.
  2. Your name is on the lease or mortgage. Being a named tenant or borrower matters. Subletting with an informal arrangement is much weaker evidence.
  3. You pay utilities in your name. Electric, gas, water, or internet bills at your home address, consistently paid during travel assignments.
  4. You return to your tax home between assignments. Document this. Boarding passes, credit card charges in your home city, texts or emails placing you there. The IRS likes to see a pattern of return.
  5. Your driver's license reflects your tax home state. Not required, but helpful. If your license is in Texas and you claim a Texas tax home, it's consistent.
  6. You're registered to vote at your tax home address. Another corroborating document showing your true domicile.
  7. Your bank statements and credit cards are registered to your tax home address. Financial mail going to your tax home strengthens the claim.
  8. You have a primary care physician or dentist at your tax home. Shows ongoing ties to a community, not just a forwarding address.
  9. Your assignment location is different from your tax home area. You cannot receive tax-free stipends for working near where you live. "Near" is typically interpreted as within the metropolitan statistical area (MSA) of your tax home.
  10. Each assignment is expected to last less than 12 months. Document contract terms at signing. An assignment that was supposed to be 13 weeks but extended to 14 months in the same city becomes a permanent assignment — stipends should have been taxable after the 12-month mark.

IRS GSA Rates for 2026: Where to Find Them and How Agencies Use Them

The General Services Administration (GSA) publishes per diem rates for every U.S. city, updated each fiscal year (October 1). These rates are the IRS's benchmark for "reasonable" travel expense reimbursements. Agencies use them — in theory — to cap how much they pay in tax-free stipends. In practice, some agencies exceed them, which can create tax liability you don't know about.

Where to Find 2026 GSA Rates

Go to gsa.gov/travel/plan-book/per-diem-rates. Search by city or zip code. You'll see two numbers: the lodging rate (capped per-night hotel rate) and the M&IE rate (daily meals and incidentals).

Standard 2026 Per Diem Rates

For most U.S. locations in fiscal year 2026, the standard rates are:

  • Lodging: $110/night ($770/week)
  • M&IE: $68/day ($476/week)
  • Total standard weekly per diem: $1,246/week

The GSA Ceiling Rule
If your agency pays you a housing stipend that exceeds the GSA lodging rate for your city, the excess is taxable. Example: working in Phoenix where lodging is $133/night ($931/week), but your agency pays $1,200/week in housing stipend. The $269/week overage is taxable income — you'd owe taxes on roughly $3,500 extra per 13-week contract. Most nurses never catch this.

Deductible Expenses for Travel Nurses

Travel nurses can deduct unreimbursed work-related expenses — but only on your Schedule C if you're a 1099 contractor, or as an adjustment to income in limited situations. Since the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, W-2 employees can no longer deduct unreimbursed business expenses on Schedule A. If you're a W-2 travel nurse (which most are), your deduction opportunities are more limited than they used to be.

What's still deductible for most travel nurses:

Nursing Licenses and Compact License Fees

Fees you pay to maintain or obtain nursing licenses — including compact state licenses — are deductible if required for your work. If your agency reimburses these, they're not deductible (but also not taxable). If you pay them out of pocket on a 1099, they're a Schedule C deduction.

Continuing Education Units (CEUs)

Required CEUs to maintain licensure are deductible for self-employed (1099) nurses. If you're W-2, these are only deductible if your employer requires and you pay them out of pocket — and even then, only in limited tax situations post-2017.

Scrubs and Uniforms

Work-specific clothing that can't reasonably be worn outside of work is deductible on Schedule C for 1099 contractors. Scrubs qualify. Regular clothes that you happen to wear to work don't.

Malpractice Insurance

If you carry your own professional liability insurance separately from your agency's coverage, that premium is deductible as a business expense for 1099 nurses.

NCLEX and Certification Exams

CCRN, CEN, PCCN, and other specialty certifications required by your assignments are deductible for 1099 nurses.

Home Office (1099 Only)

If you maintain a dedicated space in your tax home that you use exclusively for business (charting, continuing education, licensing paperwork), you may qualify for a home office deduction on Schedule C. The rules are strict — the space must be used exclusively and regularly for business.

The W-2 Reality Check
Most travel nurses are W-2 employees of their agency. Post-2017, W-2 employees cannot deduct unreimbursed business expenses at the federal level. Your deduction opportunities are primarily available if you have 1099 income (even part-time) or if your state still allows employee business expense deductions on the state return. California and a handful of other states still allow the deduction on state returns.

The $600 Catch: When You Should Hire a Travel-Nurse-Specialist CPA

Here's the threshold we use: if your total tax-free stipend income exceeds $600/week, you should have a specialist review your return. That's not a rigid IRS number — it's a practical risk assessment.

At $600+/week in non-taxable income, the stakes of getting it wrong — in either direction — are high enough to justify professional help. Doing your own taxes and missing a tax-home deficiency could cost you $20,000 or more in back taxes and penalties. Doing your own taxes and over-reporting stipends as income costs you thousands in unnecessary tax payments.

Regardless of income level, you should hire a specialist CPA if any of the following apply:

  • You traveled to 3 or more states in the tax year (multi-state filing is complex)
  • You're unsure whether your tax home qualifies under IRS rules
  • You worked in California, New York, or Massachusetts (aggressive state tax enforcement)
  • Your agency structured you as 1099 at any point in the year
  • You completed or renewed an assignment that lasted longer than 12 months
  • You received a completion bonus, sign-on bonus, or relocation payment
  • You received any 1099-NEC or 1099-MISC from an agency or facility

How to Find a Travel-Nurse-Specialist CPA

Search for CPAs who specifically market to travel nurses. Names to look for in your research: The Travel Nurse Tax Man, Travel Nurse Tax (TravelNurseTax.com), and CPA firms that list "travel healthcare" as a specialty. Avoid using a CPA who processes your return without asking about your tax home. That's the single question that separates specialists from generalists.

W-2 vs. 1099 Agency Structures (And the Risk of 1099)

Most travel nursing agencies employ their nurses as W-2 employees. A smaller number use 1099 independent contractor arrangements. The difference is significant, and in some cases the 1099 structure is being used in a legally questionable way.

W-2 Travel Nurse

Your agency withholds taxes from your paycheck and pays half of your FICA taxes (Social Security and Medicare). Your W-2 at year-end shows your taxable wages. Stipends don't appear on your W-2 if properly structured. You get some liability protection as an employee.

1099 Travel Nurse

You receive the full payment with no withholding. You're responsible for both halves of FICA — self-employment tax is 15.3% on top of your regular income tax. You can deduct more business expenses on Schedule C. But the arrangement may be legally classified as employee misclassification depending on how much control the facility exerts over your schedule and practice — and if the IRS agrees, back payroll taxes fall on the agency, not you, but the disruption to your records can be messy.

1099 Red Flag
Some agencies offer 1099 arrangements with promises of "higher take-home pay" because they're not paying payroll taxes. What they don't tell you: you pay the full 15.3% self-employment tax on top of income tax, you lose workers' comp and unemployment insurance protections, and you're fully liable for your own health insurance. Quarterly Estimated Taxes: When and How Much

If you're a 1099 contractor, or if you have significant income not covered by withholding (like a large completion bonus or 1099 income alongside W-2 work), you're required to pay quarterly estimated taxes. Failing to do so results in an underpayment penalty from the IRS — even if you pay everything you owe in April.

When Are Quarterly Payments Due?

  • Q1: April 15 (income from Jan 1 – Mar 31)
  • Q2: June 15 (income from Apr 1 – May 31)
  • Q3: September 15 (income from Jun 1 – Aug 31)
  • Q4: January 15 of the following year (income from Sep 1 – Dec 31)

How Much to Pay

The safe harbor rule: pay 100% of last year's tax liability (110% if your income was over $150,000) spread across the four quarters, and you won't owe a penalty even if your actual tax this year is higher. Alternatively, pay 90% of your current year's estimated tax each quarter.

The easiest way to make payments is through IRS Direct Pay at irs.gov/payments. Keep a record of every payment — the date, amount, and confirmation number. You'll need these when you file.

Planning Your Travel Nurse Income: Making the Most of What You Earn

Travel nurses often earn significantly more than staff nurses — but without an employer automatically enrolling you in a 401(k), matching contributions, or pulling benefits deductions, the financial planning burden falls entirely on you. The nurses who come out of travel nursing years ahead are the ones who treat the income differential as an investment opportunity rather than a lifestyle upgrade.

Emergency Fund First

Travel nursing income has gaps. Contracts don't renew. Assignments get cancelled. Healthcare facilities have hiring freezes. Before you do anything with the extra income, you need a 6-month emergency fund in a high-yield savings account. The standard advice of 3 months is too thin for a travel nurse — you need runway for a full assignment search plus a contract to start.

Open a Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA

If you have any 1099 income, you qualify to open a Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA. For 2026, you can contribute up to $23,500 to a Solo 401(k) as an employee (plus $7,500 catch-up if you're 50+) and up to 25% of net self-employment income as the employer contribution. That's a massive tax shelter unavailable to most W-2 workers. Even if most of your income is W-2, a SEP-IRA on your 1099 income can move money to a tax-advantaged account efficiently.

Roth IRA: Max It Every Year

In 2026, the Roth IRA contribution limit is $7,000 ($8,000 if you're 50+), with income phase-outs beginning at $150,000 for single filers. Travel nurses earning near or above that threshold should look at the backdoor Roth IRA strategy — converting non-deductible traditional IRA contributions to Roth. This is worth a conversation with your CPA.

Student Loan Strategy

Many travel nurses carry nursing school debt. The higher income available through travel creates a real opportunity for accelerated payoff — but only if you resist lifestyle inflation during travel years. We recommend modeling the math: calculate how many additional contract weeks it takes to eliminate your student loan balance, then compare to the cost of keeping that debt through income-driven repayment during lower-income years.
