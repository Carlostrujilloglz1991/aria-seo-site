Figs gets all the hype. But which scrubs actually hold up to 12-hour ICU shifts, industrial washing, and a career's worth of wear? An ICU nurse's honest breakdown.

Quick picks: Best overall → Figs Zamora / Catarina. Best value → Cherokee Infinity. Best for ICU/ED pockets → Grey's Anatomy by Barco. Best budget → Dickies EDS.

What Actually Matters in Scrubs

Most scrub reviews focus on how they look in a dressing room. Working nurses care about different things: how they hold up after 100 industrial washes, how many pockets they have and where, whether the fabric breathes during a code at hour 11, and whether they still look professional when you're presenting to an attending after a difficult night.

I've worked ICU for years and I've gone through a lot of scrubs. Here's my honest breakdown of every brand worth considering.

The Top Brands Ranked

BEST OVERALL ~$65–$80/set

Figs — Zamora Top + Yola / Figspro Bottoms

Figs earned their reputation. The fabric (their proprietary TECKNIK stretch) is genuinely better than anything at this price point — four-way stretch, moisture-wicking, soft against skin during a 12-hour shift, and it doesn't pill after repeated washing. The Zamora top has a good pocket layout for ICU: two front patch pockets, a chest pocket, and an inner pocket for your phone.

The criticism Figs gets is mostly about price and the fact that they market heavily to people who care more about Instagram aesthetics than function. That's fair, but the product itself is excellent. If you can get them on sale or use a referral discount, they're worth it.

✅ Pros

  • Best fabric durability in regular washing
  • Four-way stretch — full range of motion
  • Holds color wash after wash
  • Sizing is consistent and runs true
  • Good pocket count on most styles

❌ Cons

  • $65–80/set is expensive for new nurses
  • Some styles are more fashion than function
  • Pocket depth varies — check before ordering
  • Returns can be slow
🎯 Best for: Nurses who want to invest in quality and plan to wear these for 3–5 years. Buy 3 sets — enough to rotate through a week of shifts without running out before laundry day.
BEST VALUE ~$35–$45/set

Cherokee Infinity — Core Stretch Top + Pants

Cherokee is the workhorse of hospital scrubs for a reason. The Infinity line specifically is where they got the fabric right — four-way stretch, tagless, and durable enough for the institutional washing machines most nurses rely on. Not as premium a feel as Figs, but at half the price and with comparable pocket count, the Cherokee Infinity is the smart budget choice for nurses building out their scrub rotation.

Hospital gift shops almost universally carry Cherokee. When yours are in the wash and you need something today, you can actually find these locally.

✅ Pros

  • Half the price of Figs
  • Widely available — can replace pieces locally
  • Good pocket count and layout
  • Holds up well to repeated washing
  • Wide size range including petite and tall

❌ Cons

  • Fabric feels less premium than Figs
  • Some colors fade faster than Figs after heavy washing
  • Waistband can lose elasticity over time
🎯 Best for: New nurses building their scrub wardrobe on a budget, or anyone who needs reliable everyday scrubs without the Figs price tag.
BEST FOR POCKETS (ICU / ED) ~$50–$65/set

Grey's Anatomy by Barco — Classic V-Neck Set

Grey's Anatomy scrubs are quietly excellent for nurses who need a lot of pocket real estate. The classic top has six pockets, including a large chest pocket that fits a full-size notebook and a deep side pocket that holds a tablet or larger items. ICU and ED nurses especially love the pocket layout because you're carrying a lot: brain sheet, phone, pens, trauma shears, alcohol wipes, and whatever else your unit requires.

The fabric is a soft four-way stretch that's not as structured as Figs but more comfortable for long shifts. They wash well and the colors stay true longer than most mid-range brands.

✅ Pros

  • Best pocket layout of any brand reviewed
  • Soft, comfortable fabric for long shifts
  • Good color retention through washing
  • Wide size range

❌ Cons

  • Less stretch than Figs for high-mobility situations
  • Name recognition works against them — often overlooked
  • Sizing can run slightly large — size down if between sizes
🎯 Best for: ICU and ED nurses who carry a lot and need organized pocket access. The 6-pocket top genuinely changes how your shift feels.
BEST BUDGET ~$25–$35/set

Dickies EDS Signature V-Neck Scrub Set

The Dickies EDS line is what you buy when you're a new nurse who can't afford Figs yet or when you need scrubs that are going to get absolutely destroyed during orientation and you don't care. They're basic, functional, and cheap enough to replace without grief. The fabric is stiffer than premium options and doesn't breathe as well, but they hold up to washing and look professional.

Good as a starter set or for days when you know you're going to go home looking like something happened (trauma bays, pediatrics codes, messy procedures).

✅ Pros

  • Cheapest option that still looks professional
  • Durable, holds shape
  • Easy to find at scrub shops and online
  • Good for orientation when you don't know what you'll need yet

❌ Cons

  • Stiffer fabric — less comfortable for 12+ hour shifts
  • Minimal stretch
  • Basic pocket layout
  • Not what you want long-term
🎯 Best for: New nurses, orientation, or high-risk shift situations where you expect to ruin what you're wearing.

Built for durability above all else. Great for OR and procedural settings. Stiffer fabric, utilitarian look, excellent pockets. ~$45/set.

Budget-friendly with better fabric than Dickies. Good starter option. Colors fade faster than Cherokee or Figs. ~$30/set.

Hospital Color Policies

Check your unit's color policy before buying. Many hospitals assign scrub colors by role — RNs in navy or ceil blue, CNAs in a different color, lab in white, etc. Some hospitals enforce this strictly; others are relaxed about it as long as you look professional. ICU policies especially vary. Find out before you order 5 sets of the wrong color.

How Many Sets Do You Actually Need

For 3 shifts per week (standard RN): 4 sets minimum. You need one for each shift day plus one spare for when something goes wrong with laundry timing or a shift gets extended. If you're doing 4-day weeks or working extra, get 5–6.

New nurses often underestimate this and spend the first month re-wearing scrubs that should have been washed. Buy enough to rotate properly from day one.

Scrub Care to Make Them Last

Wash inside out in cold water. Tumble dry low or hang dry if possible. Don't use bleach on colored scrubs — it destroys the fabric faster than anything. Figs specifically recommends their laundry instructions — follow them and a $65 set can last 4–5 years.
