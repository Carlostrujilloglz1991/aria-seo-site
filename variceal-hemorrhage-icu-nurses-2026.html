Variceal Hemorrhage for ICU Nurses 2026 — The Bleed That Empties the Tank in Minutes

Part of the ICU Emergencies Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

A bleeding esophageal varix is one of the most dramatic emergencies in critical care: a patient with liver disease vomiting large volumes of bright red blood, hemodynamics collapsing, and an airway drowning in blood all at once. Varices are engorged, thin-walled veins that form because a scarred liver dams up the portal circulation, and when one ruptures the bleeding is torrential. Mortality is high, but it has fallen substantially with a well-drilled bundle of care — and much of that bundle is nursing execution under pressure. For the ICU nurse, variceal hemorrhage is airway, access, volume, and drugs, all needed in the first few minutes.

The short version: Portal hypertension from cirrhosis swells veins in the esophagus and stomach until they rupture. The resuscitation bundle: protect the airway (high aspiration risk), large-bore access and restrained transfusion (target hemoglobin ~7 g/dL, don't over-transfuse), start a splanchnic vasoconstrictor (octreotide) and prophylactic antibiotics right away, and get to urgent endoscopy for band ligation. Balloon tamponade is a temporary bridge when bleeding won't stop.

Why the veins rupture: portal hypertension

A cirrhotic liver is stiff and scarred, so blood backing up from the gut can't flow through it easily. Pressure rises in the portal vein, and the blood finds detours through smaller collateral veins — including thin-walled vessels just under the lining of the esophagus and stomach. As portal pressure climbs, these varices enlarge and their walls thin until they burst. Because the whole splanchnic bed is under high pressure and the patient is often coagulopathic from liver disease, a ruptured varix bleeds fast and doesn't clot well. Understanding this explains the treatment logic: every drug and maneuver is aimed at either dropping the portal pressure or physically closing the vein.

The first minutes: airway and access

The most immediate threat in massive upper GI bleeding is not always the blood loss — it's the airway. A patient vomiting large volumes of blood while confused from hepatic encephalopathy is at extreme risk of aspiration, and many of these patients are intubated early specifically to protect the airway before endoscopy. At the same time, establish two large-bore IVs (or intraosseous access if you can't), send a full set of labs including type and crossmatch, and begin volume resuscitation. This is a moment to activate help early: the endoscopy team, the intensivist, and the blood bank all need to be moving at once.

Resuscitate — but don't over-transfuse

It feels natural to pour blood into a hemorrhaging patient, but variceal bleeding is the classic case where restrained transfusion saves lives. A restrictive strategy — generally targeting a hemoglobin around 7 g/dL — produces better outcomes than aggressive transfusion, because flooding the circulation raises portal pressure and can worsen or re-provoke the bleed. So resuscitate to perfusion, not to a "normal" hemoglobin. For genuinely massive, uncontrolled hemorrhage a massive transfusion protocol with balanced products is used, and coagulopathy and thrombocytopenia are corrected per the team's plan — but the default mindset is measured, not maximal.

The two drugs that are almost always ordered

Two medications should be on your radar the moment a variceal bleed is suspected, because both improve survival and both are frequently a nurse's to give. Octreotide (a somatostatin analog) constricts the splanchnic circulation and lowers portal pressure, reducing bleeding; it's given as a bolus then a continuous infusion and is often started before the patient even reaches endoscopy. Prophylactic antibiotics — typically a third-generation cephalosporin such as ceftriaxone — are a genuinely life-saving, evidence-based part of the bundle: cirrhotic patients with GI bleeding have high rates of infection, and antibiotics reduce both infection and rebleeding. Giving these two promptly, without waiting to see how the endoscopy goes, is a hallmark of good variceal care.

Endoscopy, and the balloon of last resort

The definitive treatment is endoscopic band ligation, in which the bleeding varix is snared with a rubber band, and it's performed urgently once the patient is stable enough and the airway is protected. Your role is preparation and recovery: suction that works, the patient positioned and monitored, blood available, and continuous assessment for rebleeding afterward. When endoscopy can't control the bleeding, or while waiting for it, a balloon tamponade device (such as a Sengstaken-Blakemore or Minnesota tube) can be inserted to physically compress the varices — a temporary, high-risk bridge to a more definitive procedure like a TIPS (a shunt that decompresses the portal system). Balloon tubes carry serious risks of esophageal rupture and airway obstruction, so they demand an intubated patient, meticulous documentation of balloon pressures and volumes, and scissors taped at the bedside to deflate emergently.

Rebleeding is expected — keep watching. Controlling the first bleed is not the end. The highest risk of rebleeding is in the first days, and a second hemorrhage is more lethal than the first. After the scope, keep the patient on continuous monitoring and reassess constantly: fresh hematemesis, melena, a rising heart rate, a falling blood pressure, or a dropping hemoglobin all signal a rebleed. The octreotide infusion and antibiotics continue for days for exactly this reason. Never treat a "controlled" variceal bleed as over.

The nursing bottom line

Variceal hemorrhage is portal hypertension rupturing thin-walled esophageal veins into a torrential, poorly-clotting bleed. Win the first minutes on airway and access, resuscitate to perfusion with a restrictive hemoglobin target near 7 rather than over-transfusing, and get octreotide and prophylactic antibiotics on board early — both save lives — while moving toward urgent endoscopic band ligation. Keep balloon tamponade in mind as a temporary bridge with real dangers, watch relentlessly for rebleeding in the days that follow, and remember that these patients are often encephalopathic and coagulopathic at the same time, which makes the airway and the bleeding two simultaneous emergencies the nurse has to manage together.
