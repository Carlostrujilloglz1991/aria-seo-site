Nurse Entrepreneur: 5 Businesses Nurses Start With Their Medical Knowledge

Nursing licenses open business doors that other entrepreneurs don't have. The clinical credibility, patient communication skills, and specialized knowledge that come with years of bedside nursing are exactly what certain high-margin businesses are built on. Here are five businesses nurses actually start — with realistic income figures and what it takes to get each one going.

Important framing: These are not passive income ideas that generate money while you sleep without any work. They are real businesses that require time, effort, and some upfront investment. But for nurses willing to apply the same discipline they bring to patient care to building something of their own, these businesses can create income streams that outlast any shift differential or travel stipend.

1. BLS/ACLS/PALS Training Business

This is the most accessible nurse business to start and one of the most reliably profitable. After completing AHA instructor certification (a few hundred dollars and one day of training), you can teach life support courses to businesses, schools, dental offices, fitness studios, daycares, and other organizations that need certified employees.

How it works: You affiliate with an AHA Training Center (or eventually become your own training center), market to businesses in your area, and run group certification classes on weekends. BLS courses for groups of 8 to 12 take 4 hours and can be priced at $75 to $120 per student. ACLS recertification courses for nurses and paramedics take 6 to 8 hours and can be priced at $150 to $225 per student.

Realistic revenue: Running two group BLS classes per weekend (10 students each at $80/student) = $1,600/weekend = $6,400/month if consistent. Add ACLS courses for clinical markets and revenue grows further. Startup costs: $500 to $1,500 for instructor certification, manikins (2 to 4 for a class), AED trainer, and basic supplies. Most nurses recoup startup costs in the first 2 months.

Resources: The AHA instructor resource materials provide the curriculum structure; the business side is yours to build.

2. Legal Nurse Consulting

Already covered in depth in our legal nurse consulting guide, but worth including here as a business model overview. LNC work is high-margin, solo-practitioner friendly, and leverages your clinical expertise at $100 to $200/hour without a physical location or inventory.

Business model: You review medical records for law firms handling medical malpractice, personal injury, and workers' compensation cases. You deliver written reports and, optionally, expert witness testimony. Clients are attorneys; you find them through cold outreach, referrals, and the AALNC directory.

Realistic revenue: 10 billable hours per month at $125/hour = $1,250/month part-time. A nurse with an established client base billing 20 to 40 hours per month earns $2,500 to $5,000 — primarily from review work on weekends and evenings.

3. IV Hydration and Wellness Clinic

IV hydration clinics — businesses that administer intravenous fluid, vitamins, and electrolytes to clients for wellness, hangover recovery, athletic performance, or anti-aging purposes — have proliferated in most metros over the past decade. Nurses are the clinical backbone of these businesses; many are nurse-owned.

How it works: In most states, nurses can administer IV therapy under a physician's standing orders without a physician physically present. The business model involves a supervising physician (often contracted remotely), a nurse or nurses who perform the infusions, and a retail/spa-style environment. Mobile IV services (coming to the client's location) have lower overhead than a fixed clinic.

Realistic revenue: A typical IV drip package (1 liter of saline + add-ons) is priced at $100 to $250. A busy mobile IV nurse doing 4 to 6 sessions per day can generate $500 to $1,200 in daily revenue. Clinic overhead is higher; mobile models have better margins for solo operators.

Legal considerations vary by state. Some states require physician ownership or closer physician oversight of IV therapy businesses. Before investing in this model, consult with a healthcare attorney in your state who understands the specific scope of practice rules. The regulatory landscape has also tightened in some states following early iterations of the industry.

4. Nurse Health Coaching

Health coaching is the practice of supporting clients in behavior change — nutrition, exercise, stress management, chronic disease management — through structured coaching relationships rather than clinical diagnosis or treatment. RNs make credible coaches because the clinical background provides real depth, even when the coaching work is non-clinical.

The distinction matters legally: nurse health coaching is not medical advice, and you should not diagnose, prescribe, or treat. The coaching relationship is about behavior change and accountability, not clinical management. This distinction lets you serve clients without triggering scope-of-practice concerns.

Business models: 1-on-1 coaching packages ($150 to $500/month per client), group coaching programs ($50 to $150/month per participant), corporate wellness contracts with employers, and online courses. Certification through the National Board of Health and Wellness Coaching (NBHWC) is well-regarded and increasingly expected by serious coaches. The NBHWC exam prep resources are available on Amazon.

Realistic revenue: A nurse with 10 coaching clients at $250/month generates $2,500/month in recurring revenue with no physical location. Scale comes through group programs and online course products, not just hourly coaching.

5. Wound Care Consultation and Home Health Contracting

Wound care is a specialized nursing skill that's in significant demand outside hospital walls. Nurses with wound care experience (and ideally WOC — Wound, Ostomy, Continence — certification) can contract with home health agencies, long-term care facilities, assisted living communities, and physician practices to provide wound assessment, treatment, and care planning.

How it works: As an independent contractor, you visit facilities or patients' homes to assess and treat complex wounds — pressure ulcers, diabetic foot wounds, post-surgical wounds, venous ulcers. You document, develop care plans, and train facility staff. You bill per visit or on a contract retainer.

Realistic revenue: Independent wound care contractors charge $75 to $150 per visit for facility consulting. A nurse doing 8 to 10 visits per week on days off earns $600 to $1,500 weekly — $30,000 to $75,000 annually part-time. WOC certification through the WOCNCB is the gold standard credential.

The nurse entrepreneur advantage: Every one of these businesses is built on a clinical foundation that took years to develop. The competitors who don't have your license, your bedside experience, and your clinical credibility can't just decide to enter your space overnight. That protection is real, and it's one reason nurse businesses command the rates they do. The barrier isn't capital — it's the credential and experience you already have.
