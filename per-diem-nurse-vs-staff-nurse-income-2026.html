Per Diem Nurse vs Staff Nurse Income Comparison 2026

Per diem nursing offers higher hourly rates than staff positions, but the comparison is more complicated than the paycheck suggests. Once you account for benefits, schedule volatility, retirement contributions, and taxes, the net financial advantage of per diem is real — but smaller than the gross hourly gap implies. Here's the complete picture.

The headline gap: Per diem ICU nurses typically earn $55 to $80 per hour in major markets vs. $40 to $55 per hour for staff nurses in the same unit. But staff nurses get employer-subsidized health insurance, retirement matching, and paid time off that add $8,000 to $20,000 of real value annually.

The Benefits Gap — What Per Diem Nurses Don't Get

This is where per diem math gets complicated. Staff nurses typically receive an employer-subsidized benefits package worth $15,000 to $25,000 per year in real dollar value:

Health insurance

Employer-sponsored health insurance for a family covers premiums that would cost $18,000 to $24,000 per year on the individual market (2026 ACA rates). Even with employee contributions, staff nurses often receive $10,000 to $15,000 in premium subsidy. A per diem nurse must purchase their own insurance — often through ACA marketplace plans or a spouse's employer plan.

Retirement matching

Most hospital employers match 3% to 6% of salary in 403(b) contributions. On an $80,000 salary, a 4% match = $3,200 per year in free money. Per diem nurses get no match — they can open a Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA as self-employed (if working 1099) or a regular Roth IRA if W-2, but there's no employer contribution.

Paid time off

Staff nurses accumulate PTO — typically 3 to 4 weeks per year worth $5,800 to $7,800 on an $80,000 salary. Per diem nurses receive no PTO; unworked days are simply unpaid.

Other benefits

Disability insurance, life insurance, tuition reimbursement (significant for nurses pursuing MSN, DNP, or CRNA school), and EAP programs are typically staff-only. Tuition reimbursement alone can be worth $5,000 to $12,000 per year at hospitals with generous education benefits.

The per diem nurse advantages improve significantly if: (1) they work at multiple facilities to fill schedule gaps, (2) they have access to coverage through a spouse's employer plan, or (3) they work in a high-rate market like California where per diem rates exceed $80/hour.

When Per Diem Is Financially Better

Per diem wins when: you have health insurance through another source (spouse, VA benefits, part-time W-2 job), you're maximizing a Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA independently, you work in a high-demand market where per diem rates are $70+/hour, and you can maintain consistent hours because the facility relies on you. It also makes sense as a supplement to a primary staff job — many nurses work staff at their main hospital and pick up per diem shifts at a second facility, getting both benefits security and the premium per diem rate.

Best of both worlds: Keep your staff position for the benefits (health insurance, retirement match, PTO, tuition reimbursement) and add 1 to 2 per diem shifts per month at a second facility. This adds $1,500 to $3,000 per month in premium-rate income without giving up your benefits package. It's the most financially efficient structure for most experienced ICU nurses.
Schedule volatility risk: Per diem nurses are the first to be cancelled when census drops. A hospital may guarantee you zero hours — meaning a slow flu season or a hospital-wide census drop can result in weeks of unpaid forced downtime. Always have 4 to 6 months of emergency reserves before going fully per diem.
