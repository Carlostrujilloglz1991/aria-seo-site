Nitroprusside (Nipride): The ICU Nurse's Guide

Part of the ICU Pharmacology Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Sodium nitroprusside is the fastest, most powerful blood-pressure knob in the unit — and the one with the most dangerous hidden cost. It drops pressure within seconds and disappears just as fast, which makes it superb for a true hypertensive emergency. But every molecule you infuse releases cyanide, and that single fact governs how you run it.

The short version: Nitroprusside is a direct arterial and venous vasodilator (nitric oxide donor) for hypertensive emergencies and afterload reduction. Range is roughly 0.3–3 mcg/kg/min (higher risky). It's light-sensitive, works in seconds, and its signature danger is cyanide/thiocyanate toxicity — worse with high doses, prolonged use, and renal/hepatic impairment.

What nitroprusside is and how it works

Nitroprusside breaks down to release nitric oxide, relaxing both arterial and venous smooth muscle. That dual action reduces both afterload (arterial) and preload (venous), dropping blood pressure fast. Onset is essentially immediate and the effect vanishes within a minute or two of stopping — the most titratable antihypertensive there is. That precision is why it's used in aortic dissection, severe hypertensive emergencies, and acute afterload reduction in heart failure.

Every nitroprusside molecule contains cyanide groups. At normal doses the body detoxifies them, but high rates, prolonged infusions, and impaired liver or kidney function let cyanide and its metabolite thiocyanate accumulate.

Cyanide toxicity — know the signs. Suspect it with an unexplained metabolic acidosis, rising lactate, altered mental status, or tachyphylaxis (needing more drug for the same effect). It can be lethal. Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest time, watch acid-base status, and escalate early. Thiocyanate accumulates especially in renal failure.

The side effects that matter at the bedside

Precipitous hypotension. The pressure can crash. Titrate against an arterial line, in small steps, and never walk away during titration.

Cyanide and thiocyanate toxicity. The defining hazard, covered above.

Reflex tachycardia. The rapid vasodilation can drive heart rate up.

Coronary steal. It can divert blood away from ischemic myocardium, a concern in active coronary ischemia.

Raised intracranial pressure. Cerebral vasodilation can be a problem in some neuro patients.

Nitroprusside vs. nicardipine and nitroglycerin

Because of the cyanide risk, many units now favor titratable agents like nicardipine for hypertensive emergencies, reserving nitroprusside for situations needing instant, second-by-second control (classically aortic dissection, alongside a beta-blocker like esmolol to blunt reflex tachycardia). Nitroglycerin is more venous-predominant and preferred for cardiac ischemia. Knowing which vasodilator fits which emergency is the mark of a seasoned critical-care nurse.

Bottom line

Nitroprusside is the ultimate titratable vasodilator — instant, powerful, and gone in a minute — which makes it perfect for the hypertensive emergency that demands second-by-second control. But it releases cyanide, so you run it at the lowest effective dose, protect it from light, watch it on an arterial line, and stay alert for an unexplained acidosis. Handle this drug with that respect at the ICU bedside and you'll be ready for the tightly controlled pressures anesthesia demands.
