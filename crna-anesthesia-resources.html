Top Resources to Pass CRNA School: Books, Apps, and AI Tools

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

This article was created with AI assistance and reviewed for clinical accuracy.
Bottom line: CRNA school is a two to three year full-time academic and clinical commitment. The resources that matter are the ones that match how you actually learn, not the most expensive or most commonly marketed. This guide covers the full stack — from foundational textbooks to AI study partners — prioritized by what CRNA students in 2026 actually report using to pass.

CRNA programs graduate approximately 3,000 new CRNAs per year in the United States. The NBCRNA boards pass rate hovers around 80% on first attempt — which means roughly 20% of graduating CRNA students fail their boards on the first try. The students who pass consistently are not necessarily the most naturally intelligent; they're the ones who built effective study systems early and maintained them through 2-3 years of simultaneous academic and clinical demands.

This guide covers the full resource stack, organized by category, with honest assessments of what works and what's marketing.

Core Textbooks: What You Actually Need

Nagelhout & Elisha — Nurse Anesthesia

The most widely used primary textbook in CRNA programs in the U.S. Written specifically for nurse anesthesia students (as opposed to physician anesthesiologists), the content is pitched at exactly the right level for program coursework. Every chapter covers pathophysiology, pharmacology, and anesthetic considerations in a readable, organized format. If your program hasn't specified a textbook, this is the default starting point.

Morgan & Mikhail — Clinical Anesthesiology

The companion text most CRNA students keep alongside Nagelhout. Morgan & Mikhail is organized more clinically — by patient population and procedure type — making it particularly useful during clinical rotations when you're preparing for specific case types. The pharmacology sections are especially strong and readable. Many CRNA students prefer Morgan & Mikhail for exam prep because the question-focused organization maps well to how boards questions are structured.

Miller's Anesthesia

The authoritative reference text of anesthesia. Not a reading-through book — a look-it-up book. When you need the deepest, most complete answer on any anesthesia topic, Miller's is the source. Every CRNA program requires it in some edition. The most recent edition is substantial in cost; older editions (2-3 editions back) cover the vast majority of boards content at significantly lower cost.

Barash — Clinical Anesthesia

The third major textbook in the rotation. Barash is particularly strong on physiology and pharmacology — areas that are heavily tested on CRNA boards. Some students find it more readable than Miller's for self-directed study. It's not required at every program, but CRNA students preparing for boards who find gaps in their Nagelhout coverage often turn to Barash.

Question Banks: The Boards Prep Non-Negotiables

Valley Anesthesia

The most widely used CRNA question bank. High content validity — the questions mirror the NBCRNA exam format closely. Most programs either integrate Valley officially or recommend it strongly. Start using question banks in the first semester of didactics, not the semester before boards. Six months of question practice is cramming. Two years of question practice is preparation.

Apex Anesthesia

The primary competitor to Valley and preferred by a meaningful subset of CRNA students for its explanation depth. Apex answer explanations walk through clinical reasoning rather than just stating the correct answer, which builds conceptual understanding alongside fact recall. Strong for pharmacology and physiology questions specifically.

NBCRNA Practice Examination

The official practice exam from the certifying body is the single most important resource in the last four weeks before boards. It's the best available predictor of actual exam difficulty, format, and content weighting. Do at minimum two full timed sessions under exam conditions, separated by at least two weeks.

AI Tools for CRNA School

ChatGPT Plus: Concept Clarification and Card Generation

Two high-value uses: First, instant concept explanation. When a lecture or textbook explanation isn't landing, asking ChatGPT to explain the concept differently — using a different analogy, at a simpler level, or focusing specifically on the clinical implication — often makes it click. Second, Anki card generation. ChatGPT generates accurate, well-formatted CRNA Anki cards faster than you can write them manually.

Cross-reference specific drug doses and clinical values against validated sources. AI explanation is reliable for conceptual understanding; exact values need verification.

Claude for Document Analysis

Anthropic's Claude handles very long documents well. CRNA students have begun uploading entire study guides, lecture notes, or textbook chapters and asking Claude to: identify the five most important concepts, generate 20 practice questions, explain sections they highlighted as confusing, or create a summary for rapid review the morning before an exam. This workflow — upload your own content, query it conversationally — is one of the most efficient review methods available.

Anki: The Foundation

Free spaced-repetition flashcard software. Non-negotiable. The question isn't whether to use Anki; it's how to build good cards. Start in semester one. Review daily — even 10-15 minutes on clinical days. By boards time, you'll have 18+ months of spaced repetition on every concept you've added. The CRNA community has shared decks available through the r/CRNA subreddit and various student networks — evaluate quality carefully before adopting a shared deck, as accuracy varies.

Audio and Video Resources

Some CRNA content doesn't land well in text. Pharmacology mechanisms, ventilator waveforms, and regional anatomy are often better absorbed through visual and audio media.

Thieme's Regional Nerve Block Atlas is the reference for regional anesthesia anatomy. For visual learners preparing for regional rotations, it's worth the investment. OpenAnesthesia.org provides free video content from academic anesthesia programs — particularly strong for procedural techniques. The Crashing Patient podcast covers critical care scenarios from an anesthesia and intensivist perspective, ideal for ICU nurses in the didactic year.

Clinical Year Resources

The clinical year shifts the resource mix. Textbooks give way to case-specific prep. The most valuable clinical year habit is pre-case preparation: the evening before, research the procedure, patient population, and anesthetic considerations using Morgan & Mikhail's case-based format and your question bank. The nurses who learn most during clinical year are the ones who show up having done the reading, not the ones who rely on their CRNA preceptor to deliver the education in real time.

Clinical year tip: For every case type you haven't done before, ask ChatGPT: "What are the most important anesthetic considerations for [case type]? Cover: preoperative assessment priorities, induction approach, intraoperative monitoring, emergence considerations, and potential complications." Review the output, cross-reference with Morgan & Mikhail, then adjust based on your specific patient's comorbidities. This pre-case prep habit will build your case library faster than any other approach.
