CRNA Programs in Kentucky 2026

Kentucky offers an unusually rich set of CRNA programs for a state its size — four accredited options spread from Louisville to Murray to the Cincinnati border. For an ICU nurse staying in the region, that is real flexibility. Here is the 2026 landscape, what it costs, and how to get in.

The short answer: Kentucky has 4 accredited CRNA programs — Murray State University, Northern Kentucky University, the University of Louisville, and Bellarmine University — all at the doctoral level, with the University of Louisville being the newest entrant.

Accredited CRNA programs

Every program below is accredited by the Council on Accreditation of Nurse Anesthesia Educational Programs (COA). Confirm current accreditation status on the COA list before applying, since review dates change.

Murray State University runs a long-standing COA-accredited DNP in nurse anesthesia in western Kentucky — a 36-month full-time program and, as a public university, often the most cost-effective route for Kentucky residents.

Northern Kentucky University sits in the Greater Cincinnati region and offers a DNP with an anesthesia specialization, with both BSN-to-DNP and post-master's entry tracks. Its border location opens clinical exposure across the Cincinnati metro.

The University of Louisville launched a new nurse anesthesia program built around a 112-credit-hour curriculum and 2,880 required clinical hours, aligned to the COA's 2021 accreditation standards. As a major academic medical center, U of L brings high-acuity clinical sites in-house.

Bellarmine University, also in Louisville, delivers the COA-accredited Juneja Nurse Anesthesia Program at the doctoral level, giving the city two distinct options and the state four routes in total.

Resident status is the cost lever. Kentucky's public programs (Murray State, NKU, U of L) typically offer lower in-state tuition than the private route. If you are a Kentucky resident, the public option can meaningfully cut the debt you carry out of a three-year program you generally cannot work through.

Newer programs need extra diligence. The University of Louisville's program is recent, so ask for its early board-pass rates and clinical-site roster. Across all four, apply broadly — in-state status is an edge, not a guarantee — and consider strong neighbors in Tennessee, Ohio, and Indiana to widen your odds.

How to strengthen your application

Get into the highest-acuity ICU you can and stay long enough to lead — a busy CVICU, SICU, or trauma unit carries more weight than years on a quieter floor. Earn your CCRN early; it is close to non-negotiable. If your GPA is below ~3.4, add or retake upper-level science courses and earn As to show momentum. Document CRNA shadowing before applying, and treat the interview as the deciding factor, because once you clear the screen, it usually is.

Build against a checklist, not a feeling. Benchmark your GPA, ICU type, hours, CCRN, and shadowing against the typical admitted profile, then close your widest gap first. See the CRNA school requirements guide, the acceptance-rate breakdown, and the ICU-to-CRNA timeline.

Bottom line

For its size, Kentucky offers an exceptional four accredited CRNA programs — Murray State, Northern Kentucky University, the University of Louisville, and Bellarmine. Public options can cut your cost, and Louisville alone gives you two routes. Land a high-acuity ICU year, a CCRN, and a ~3.5 GPA, apply to several, and a Kentucky CRNA seat is well within reach.
