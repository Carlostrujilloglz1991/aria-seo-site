Sepsis in the SNF: Recognition, First Actions, and the Transfer Decision

Updated July 2026  |  More clinical guides →

Clinical education note: This article is educational and reflects general principles. Follow your facility's protocols, your state's scope of practice, and provider orders. Sepsis thresholds and bundle elements are set by your medical director and current guidelines — when they conflict with anything here, your protocol wins.

Sepsis in a skilled nursing facility is a different clinical problem than sepsis in the ED or the ICU — not because the pathophysiology changes, but because everything around it does. The patient is older and presents atypically. There is no stat lactate, often no stat anything. The "team" may be one nurse covering dozens of residents with a provider available by phone. And the single highest-leverage intervention isn't a drug — it's recognizing the change early and moving the patient to definitive care with a handoff that survives the transfer. This guide covers the SNF-specific version of sepsis care: recognition without labs, first actions within your scope, the transfer decision, and the goals-of-care layer that setting adds. It completes our sepsis-by-setting series alongside ED sepsis and ICU sepsis.

Why septic elders don't look septic

The textbook triad — fever, tachycardia, elevated white count — is unreliable in the very population SNFs serve:

  • Blunted fever response. A meaningful fraction of older septic adults mount little or no fever; some present hypothermic, which is a worse sign. In a frail elder, a "low-grade temp" above their own baseline may be the whole fever you get. Know each resident's baseline temperature, not just the textbook 38°C line.
  • Medication camouflage. Beta-blockers cap the tachycardia. Baseline cognitive impairment hides new confusion unless you know the resident well enough to spot "more confused than her usual."
  • Nonspecific heralds. The earliest SNF sepsis signs are often functional: new falls, stopping eating, new incontinence, "just not himself today," increased lethargy. In long-term care, a change from baseline IS the vital sign.

Screening when you have no lactate

SNF screening leans on structured observation plus bedside vitals, because that's what exists at 2 a.m.:

  • Early-warning tools. Programs like INTERACT's "Stop and Watch" formalize what CNAs notice first — eating less, new agitation, weaker than usual. Take these reports seriously; the aide who knows the resident is your most sensitive monitor.
  • Bedside red flags that need no lab: new confusion or altered mentation; respiratory rate climbing above the resident's baseline (one of the earliest and most-ignored signs); SBP notably below baseline; mottling or cool extremities; oliguria across a shift; hypothermia.
  • Screening scores, used honestly. qSOFA-style checks (altered mentation, fast respirations, low BP) are quick and equipment-free, but no score replaces the "change from baseline plus suspected infection" judgment in this population — frail elders can look "score-negative" while dying. Use scores to communicate concern, not to overrule it.
  • Hunt the source while you work: urinary symptoms or a chronically instrumented bladder, cough or new hypoxia, wounds and pressure injuries under the dressing, recent aspiration events, indwelling lines.

First actions inside SNF scope

While escalation is in motion, the SNF nurse can meaningfully shorten the sepsis timeline:

  1. Full set of vitals including pulse ox and a real respiratory rate (counted, not estimated), plus a fingerstick glucose — hypoglycemia and sepsis travel together in elders who've stopped eating.
  2. Call the provider with a structured SBAR: baseline vs now, suspected source, your stated concern — use the word "sepsis." Vague reports get vague orders.
  3. Execute what your facility can: oxygen per protocol, IV access if you have the capability and orders, first-dose antibiotics if on site and ordered, fluids per order with sensible caution in the HFrEF/ESRD resident. Timing matters: every hour of delay to definitive sepsis care worsens outcomes, which is exactly why the transfer decision below shouldn't wait on a slow workup you can't perform anyway.
  4. Document times — symptom recognition, provider contact, EMS call. The receiving team will reconstruct the sepsis clock from your notes.

The transfer decision — and the goals-of-care layer

The default for suspected sepsis with instability is transfer to an ED that can do what a SNF cannot: lactate, cultures, broad-spectrum antibiotics within the hour, imaging, and pressors if needed (what happens next is exactly the ED sepsis pathway). But long-term care adds a layer the hospital rarely faces first: the POLST/advance directive conversation. Check the orders before the crisis conversation with family. A resident with clearly documented comfort-focused goals may be appropriately treated in place with provider guidance and family alignment — that is not a failure of recognition; it's honoring the person. What you must not do is let an ambiguous goals-of-care status slow a transfer the resident would have wanted. Ambiguity defaults to treatment while someone clarifies.

A handoff that survives the transfer

ED teams consistently report that SNF transfer packets tell them everything except what they need. Make your verbal and written handoff carry the five things only you know:

Handoff elementWhy the ED needs it from you specifically
Baseline vs now"Alert and feeding herself yesterday; obtunded today" reframes triage instantly — the ED can't know baseline
The timelineWhen the change started, when vitals turned — this sets the sepsis clock and frames lactate interpretation downstream
Suspected sourceWound photos/description, catheter history, aspiration event — findings buried under a dressing get missed for hours
What was givenAntibiotic doses, fluids, antipyretics — masked vitals mislead the next assessor
Goals of careSend the actual POLST copy and the family contact who speaks for the resident

After the transfer: close the loop

The best SNF sepsis programs treat every transfer as data: Was the resident admitted with sepsis? What was the source? What sign appeared first in retrospect? Feeding that answer back to the floor — especially to the CNA who flagged the change — sharpens the whole building's recognition and is exactly the kind of QI story that elevates a resume and a unit.

Bottom line: In the SNF, sepsis recognition is baseline-deviation detection: trust the CNA's "something's off," count the respiratory rate, say the word "sepsis" in a structured SBAR, execute what your scope allows without delaying transfer, check the POLST before the crisis, and hand off the baseline, timeline, source, treatments, and goals — the five things only you know.
