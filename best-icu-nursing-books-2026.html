Detailed Reviews

1. The ICU Book — Paul L. Marino, MD
Price: approximately $65–$80  ·  Format: Hardcover  ·  4th Edition

This is the ICU bible. If your unit had one book and one book only, this should be it. Marino writes the way great attendings teach — clear reasoning over memorization. You'll understand why a hyponatremia correction is done slowly, not just that it is. The chapters on hemodynamics, acid-base disorders, and respiratory mechanics are worth the price of admission alone.

It's not a pocket reference — it's a textbook you read cover to cover (or chapter by chapter as clinical situations arise). New ICU nurses often feel overwhelmed by it at first, but returning to specific chapters as you encounter those patients in real life makes each section click.

✓ Pros
  • Unmatched depth on pathophysiology
  • Evidence-based with clear clinical reasoning
  • Excellent acid-base and hemodynamic sections
  • Trusted by residents and attendings
✗ Cons
  • Too heavy for beside quick-lookup
  • Dense reading; not beginner-friendly
  • Some sections feel dated vs. newer editions
Who should buy it: Any ICU nurse with 6+ months of experience who wants to understand what's actually happening physiologically. This is a career book, not just a study book.

2. Pocket ICU — Matthew M. Dettmer, MD
Price: approximately $45–$60  ·  Format: Pocket Paperback

This is the book that actually lives in your scrubs. Pocket ICU is organized exactly the way you need it at the bedside — quick-access protocols, drug dosing, ventilator settings, and ICU scoring systems organized logically, not alphabetically. The sepsis bundle checklist alone has saved many nurses' sanity during a fast admission.

It's not meant to teach you the "why" — that's what Marino is for. This is the "I need the PEEP/FiO2 table right now and I have 45 seconds" book. The small format means it actually travels with you. The spiral binding keeps it flat when you need both hands free.

✓ Pros
  • Pocket-sized — actually goes to work with you
  • Tabbed for fast lookup by system
  • Updated dosing and protocol tables
  • Best ICU pocket reference available
✗ Cons
  • No deep explanations — reference only
  • Small print (carry a pen light)
  • Wears out if you use it daily for 2+ years
Who should buy it: Every ICU nurse, period. New grad or 20-year veteran. This lives in your pocket alongside your badge.

3. Critical Care Nursing Made Incredibly Easy
Price: approximately $50–$65  ·  Format: Paperback  ·  Lippincott Williams & Wilkins

The "Incredibly Easy" series gets mocked by nurses who've been around long enough — but before you dismiss it, know that it's genuinely well-designed for someone who has just landed their first ICU position and feels like they don't know what they don't know. The humor and illustrations aren't condescending — they make intimidating concepts approachable before you've seen them clinically.

Chapters cover ventilator management, hemodynamic monitoring, cardiac rhythms, shock, and end-of-life care. The mnemonics are corny but they stick. If you're a new ICU grad and someone offers you a copy of this vs. Marino's ICU Book on your first week, start here — then graduate to Marino within 6 months.

✓ Pros
  • Genuinely beginner-accessible
  • Colorful visuals and memory aids
  • Good breadth across all ICU systems
  • Less intimidating than pure textbooks
✗ Cons
  • Not enough depth for experienced nurses
  • Won't replace a real reference for complex cases
  • Some clinical content is simplified to the point of being incomplete
Who should buy it: New ICU nurses in their first 6 months. Step-down nurses looking to cross-train into ICU.

4. AACN Essentials of Critical Care Nursing
Price: approximately $70–$90  ·  Format: Hardcover  ·  McGraw-Hill

This is the AACN's official text — which means it's as close to "official" as critical care nursing gets. What sets it apart from Marino or the Lippincott books is its emphasis on evidence-based nursing practice rather than physician-centric physiologic reasoning. Chapters on patient safety, family-centered care, and ICU-specific nursing interventions reflect the actual scope of what nurses do and own in the ICU.

It's heavy but comprehensive. The chapters on multimodal sedation-analgesia management, prevention of hospital-acquired infections, and end-organ failure management are particularly strong. If your unit follows AACN guidelines (most do), this is the reference that explains why those protocols are written the way they are.

✓ Pros
  • Official AACN content — authoritative
  • Strong nursing-specific framing
  • Excellent on sedation, infection prevention
  • Good for CNS/CCNS exam prep
✗ Cons
  • Expensive for a reference that goes out of date
  • Not a quick lookup book
  • Some overlap with Marino without the depth
Who should buy it: ICU charge nurses, preceptors, and anyone pursuing CNS or CCNS certification. Less essential if you already own Marino and a pocket reference.

5. Lippincott's Critical Care Nursing
Price: approximately $55–$70  ·  Format: Paperback  ·  Lippincott Williams & Wilkins

Think of this as the procedural companion to the ICU. Where Marino explains the hemodynamics of a pulmonary artery catheter, Lippincott's tells you step by step how to set it up, maintain it, and troubleshoot it. The book is organized by body system and includes detailed nursing care plans, which makes it especially useful for documentation-heavy environments.

It's a solid reference but not a standout. If you already own Marino, Pocket ICU, and Pass CCRN, this doesn't add much. Where it earns its place: units that rely heavily on nursing care plans and standardized procedures, or nurses who work in ICUs where the procedural "how to" is as important as the physiologic "why."

✓ Pros
  • Strong on procedures and nursing care plans
  • System-by-system organization
  • Good drug reference sections
✗ Cons
  • Less conceptual depth than Marino
  • Redundant if you own AACN Essentials
  • Format feels dated
Who should buy it: Nurses who want a solid all-in-one procedural reference, especially in surgical or trauma ICUs where procedures are frequent.

6. Pass CCRN — Linda D. Urden et al.
Price: approximately $55–$70  ·  Format: Paperback + Online Questions  ·  Elsevier

If you are preparing for the CCRN exam, this is the book to build your prep around. Pass CCRN is organized around the CCRN test blueprint, which means your study time is efficient — you're reviewing in the same order the exam will test you. The online practice questions are updated to match the current exam format and the rationale explanations are detailed enough to actually teach you, not just tell you the right answer.

It's not a bedside reference — it's a test prep tool. But because the CCRN blueprint tracks closely with real ICU competencies, studying from it makes you a better nurse at the same time. The cardiovascular and pulmonary chapters are particularly rigorous and worth reading even if you're not testing soon.

✓ Pros
  • Organized by the official CCRN test blueprint
  • Includes current online question bank
  • Strong rationale explanations
  • Most efficient path to CCRN certification
✗ Cons
  • Not useful as a bedside reference
  • Online access may expire

Who should buy it: Anyone sitting for the CCRN in the next 6 months. Pair it with Marino for the conceptual foundation.

7. CCRN Certification Made Easy
Price: approximately $40–$55  ·  Format: Paperback

A leaner, more affordable CCRN prep option. CCRN Certification Made Easy strips down the content to the essentials — useful if you've already been studying and want a condensed review rather than a comprehensive course. The style is accessible and the format is less dense than Pass CCRN, which some nurses prefer when fatigue sets in close to exam day.

Honest assessment: it's a good supplement, not a primary resource. Use Pass CCRN as your main study guide and reach for this when you need a quick review of a specific system or want a different explanation of a concept that isn't clicking.

Our Recommended Stacks

You don't need all seven. Here's what we'd actually buy depending on where you are:

  • New ICU grad: Critical Care Nursing Made Incredibly Easy + Pocket ICU. Start here. Marino can wait 6 months.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which ICU nursing book is best for new grads?

Start with Critical Care Nursing Made Incredibly Easy to build foundational confidence, paired with Pocket ICU for bedside reference. Graduate to Marino's The ICU Book once you've seen the clinical scenarios the concepts describe.

Do I need to buy multiple ICU books?

Not all seven. Two books — one comprehensive reference and one pocket guide — covers most nurses' needs. For CCRN prep, add a test-specific book. Buying more than three is usually procrastination, not preparation.

Is The ICU Book worth the price?

Yes, if you intend to build genuine clinical mastery. It's the single most cited critical care reference by physicians and advanced practice nurses, and the concepts translate directly to better patient assessment. Think of it as a long-term investment rather than a study guide.

What's the best CCRN prep book?

Pass CCRN is the most structured and exam-aligned. It's organized around the official test blueprint, which makes your study time efficient. Pair it with online question banks for the best pass rate odds.
