1. The 10 Rights of Medication Administration

  1. Right Patient — 2 patient identifiers before every medication (name + DOB or MRN; NOT room number)
  2. Right Drug — verify against MAR; read label 3 times (when taking from storage, when preparing, when administering)
  3. Right Dose — calculate independently; use weight-based dosing when applicable; independent double-check for high-alert meds
  4. Right Route — oral vs IV vs subQ vs IM vs topical; never assume route
  5. Right Time — administer within 30 min of scheduled time (1 hr for non-time-critical); note onset/peak for monitoring
  6. Right Documentation — document immediately after giving (never before); sign out controlled substances immediately
  7. Right Reason — understand WHY the medication is prescribed; question orders that don't match diagnosis
  8. Right Response — assess and document therapeutic effect; monitor for adverse effects
  9. Right to Refuse — patient has the right to refuse; document refusal and notify provider
  10. Right Education — patient understands what medication is for, how to take it, side effects to report

2. ISMP High-Alert Medications

High-alert medications are drugs that bear heightened risk of causing significant harm when used in error. They require independent double-checks, special labeling, and additional safeguards.

4. Insulin Safety — Critical Points

  • Never abbreviate "units" as "U" — can be misread as "0" causing tenfold overdose. Always write out "units."
  • U-500 insulin is 5× the concentration of U-100 — only use U-500 syringes; never draw in U-100 syringe
  • Independent double-check required for all insulin drips and any high-dose insulin
  • Insulin drip protocol: check blood glucose q1h; if <70 stop drip and treat hypoglycemia per protocol
  • When transitioning from IV to subQ insulin: overlap subQ injection with IV drip by 1–2 hours before stopping drip
  • NPH is the ONLY insulin that is cloudy — all others should be clear; roll NPH (do NOT shake)
  • Lantus (glargine) and Levemir (detemir) are long-acting — should NEVER be mixed with other insulins
  • Rapid-acting insulins (lispro, aspart, glulisine): give with or just before meals; if patient not eating, hold

6. Medication Error Reporting

Types of medication errors:
  • Prescribing error: wrong drug, dose, route, frequency ordered
  • Dispensing error: pharmacy dispenses wrong drug or dose
  • Administration error: nurse gives wrong drug, dose, route, patient, or time
  • Monitoring error: failure to monitor for expected effects or toxicity
When an error occurs:
  1. Assess patient safety FIRST — treat any adverse effects immediately
  2. Notify the provider immediately
  3. Document objectively in the medical record (what was given, when, patient response)
  4. Complete an incident report (variance report) — this is NOT punitive; it's for system improvement
  5. Do NOT document the incident report in the patient's chart
  6. Pharmacy notification for any medication-related near-miss or error
NCLEX High-Yield: Medication Safety
  • Two patient identifiers before every medication — never use room number
  • High-alert meds require independent double-check: insulin drips, IV heparin, chemotherapy, concentrated electrolytes
  • KCl concentrate: NEVER give IV push — cardiac arrest. Must be diluted.
  • NMBA (neuromuscular blockers): patient may be AWAKE — always ensure sedation/analgesia is running
  • Vincristine: NEVER intrathecal — fatal
  • "U" for units: NEVER acceptable abbreviation — write "units"
  • HIT: platelets drop >50% on days 5–14 with heparin → STOP all heparin products
  • Hydromorphone (Dilaudid) is ~5–7× more potent than morphine — common cause of opioid overdose errors
  • Incident report: complete it; do NOT document it in the patient's medical chart
  • Insulin NPH: only cloudy insulin; roll, do not shake; can mix with regular insulin
  • Antidote: dabigatran = idarucizumab; rivaroxaban/apixaban = andexanet alfa; warfarin = Kcentra (urgent) or vitamin K; heparin = protamine sulfate
