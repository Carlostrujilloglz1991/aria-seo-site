Travel Nurse License Compacts: Which States Accept Multi-State License

Licensing is one of the biggest logistical headaches in travel nursing — or it was, before the Nurse Licensure Compact expanded dramatically. If your home state is a compact member, you can practice in dozens of other states without applying for a separate license. Here's exactly how it works and what you need to know.

The core concept: The Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC) allows RNs and LPNs who are licensed in a participating state to practice in other compact states using their single multi-state license. As of 2025, 41 states plus the U.S. Virgin Islands are NLC members. If you live in a compact state, your license is automatically multi-state — no extra application needed.

How the NLC Works

The NLC is administered by NCSBN (the National Council of State Boards of Nursing). When you obtain your RN license in a compact state, you receive a multi-state privilege by default — it's not a separate license, just an expanded scope of your existing one. You can work in any other compact state without applying for that state's license or paying that state's licensing fee.

The key rule: your primary state of residence (your tax home state — where you live and declare legal domicile) determines which state's license you hold. If you move permanently to a new state, you must transfer your license to that state within 90 days. This is why the tax home issue intersects with licensing — your legal domicile determines your compact license's home state.

Notable non-compact states as of 2025: California, New York, New Jersey, Illinois, Massachusetts, Oregon, and Washington state are NOT NLC members. These states require a separate license regardless of your compact status — and California in particular has its own NCLEX and licensing requirements. High-demand assignments in California require a California RN license applied for separately, which can take 6 to 12 weeks.

How to Get a Compact Multi-State License

If you already live in a compact state and passed NCLEX there, you already have a multi-state license — check your Nursys record at nursys.com to confirm. Your license will show "multi-state" in the license type field.

If you live in a non-compact state and want to practice in compact states, you must obtain a license in a compact state where you establish legal residency. This means actually moving your legal domicile — not just getting a P.O. box. The most practical approach for travel nurses: if you're relocating your tax home (as many travel nurses do intentionally), choose a compact state with no income tax (Texas, Nevada, Florida) to maximize both the compact license access and the tax benefit simultaneously.

License Endorsement for Non-Compact States

For assignments in California, New York, or other non-compact states, you must apply for endorsement — essentially applying for a license in that state based on your existing license, rather than retaking NCLEX. Each state has its own application, fees ($50 to $200), and processing times. California averages 4 to 8 weeks for endorsement; New York can take 6 to 12 weeks. Start the endorsement process before you accept a contract in a non-compact state — your agency cannot place you without a license in hand.

Some agencies will reimburse licensing fees; negotiate this in your contract. If you frequently work in California, it's worth maintaining your California license as a standing authorization rather than scrambling to endorse for each contract.

APRN Compact: Different Rules

The APRN Compact (for Nurse Practitioners, CRNAs, and CNMs) is separate from the RN NLC and has far fewer member states. As of 2025, only a small number of states have enacted the APRN Compact. If you're planning to become a CRNA, do not assume your RN compact license extends to CRNA practice — it does not. You'll need to verify CRNA licensing requirements separately in each state where you intend to practice.

Practical Compact License Tips for Travel Nurses

Keep your license information current in Nursys — this is where hospitals and agencies verify your status. Never let your home state license lapse; the multi-state privilege depends on your primary license being active and unencumbered. If you get a traffic ticket or any legal issue in any compact state, it can affect your privileges in all compact states — not just the state where it occurred. Report any disciplinary actions proactively.

Strategy for maximum flexibility: Establish your tax home in a compact, no-income-tax state (Texas or Florida are the most popular choices among experienced travel nurses). This gives you multi-state access to 40+ states, eliminates state income tax on your taxable wages, and positions you optimally for both licensing flexibility and financial efficiency. It requires genuine legal domicile — not just a forwarding address — but for nurses willing to commit, it's the single best structural setup for a long travel nursing career.
