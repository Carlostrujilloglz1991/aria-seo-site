Vet Visit Checklist: How to Get More Out of Every Appointment

Most vet appointments are 20-30 minutes. Without preparation, that time gets consumed by paperwork, basic history-taking, and explaining symptoms vaguely. Coming prepared lets the vet spend that time on examination and clinical decision-making, not filling in gaps you could have anticipated.

The most useful thing you can bring: A written list of concerns in order of priority, plus a short video of any abnormal behavior you've observed at home. Cats especially rarely reproduce their symptoms in a clinical setting — the video is often the only way the vet sees what you're describing.

Documents to bring to every visit

Vaccination records if you're seeing a new vet or haven't been in over a year. Your previous vet's records if you've recently moved or changed practices — call ahead so they can request them electronically if possible. Any prescription label from medications your pet takes regularly. Pet insurance card and policy number if you have coverage.

New puppy or kitten visit: breeder or rescue paperwork, any vaccines administered before you acquired them, and any deworming history if provided.

Symptom information to prepare in advance

Vets diagnose based on history first, physical exam second, diagnostics third. The better your history, the more efficiently they can reach a diagnosis. Prepare these before you arrive:

When did it start? "A few days ago" is much less useful than "exactly 4 days ago." If symptoms started suddenly vs. gradually, note that specifically.

Frequency and pattern: Is vomiting once a day or ten times? Does limping get worse after rest or after activity? Is the cough happening at night or only when excited?

What's changed recently? New food brand, new treat, change in water source, new household member, travel, construction nearby, pest control application, access to a garbage can.

What you've already tried: Over-the-counter remedies, dietary changes, rest. What helped and what didn't.

Questions to ask at the appointment

Write them down beforehand. Common important questions that get forgotten in the moment: What are the possible diagnoses and how do we rule them out? If you're recommending a test, what does a positive result mean for treatment? What are signs I should watch for at home that would warrant an emergency visit? Is this condition likely to recur? Is this medication safe with any others my pet takes?

What to bring for a urine or stool sample

For urinalysis: collect a clean midstream urine sample in a clean container within 2-4 hours of the appointment. Keep refrigerated and label with your pet's name. Ask your vet in advance whether they want you to bring it or whether they'll collect at the office.

For fecal testing: a fresh sample (less than 24 hours old, collected that morning is ideal) in a sealed bag or sample cup. A pea-sized amount is sufficient. Most practices can provide a sample cup in advance if you call.

The weight log habit: Weigh your pet monthly at home (hold them while stepping on a bathroom scale, then subtract your own weight) or use the scale at a pet store. Unexpected weight loss is often the first sign of serious illness in both cats and dogs, and it happens gradually enough that owners don't notice without a record. A 2-pound loss in a 12-pound cat is a 17% body weight loss — clinically significant and easily missed without numbers.

After the visit: tracking the follow-up

Before leaving, confirm: when should I follow up if symptoms don't improve? Are there signs of worsening that warrant coming back sooner? When is the next scheduled wellness visit? Write the answers down or add them to your phone. Follow-up instructions forgotten within 24 hours are the most common reason pets return to the vet for the same problem.
