The ICU Pharmacology Hub — Drips, Sedation, Antidotes & the Correction Rules

ICU nursing is, hour to hour, a pharmacology job. The pumps at the head of the bed are doing more minute-to-minute physiology than anything else in the room, and every one of those channels has a guide on this site: what the drug actually does at the receptor, how it is titrated, what the monitor shows when it is working, and the specific errors that hurt patients. This hub organizes the whole medication library by drug family so you can find the right guide in seconds.

How to use this page: Each section is a drug family, and inside each family the head-to-head comparisons sit next to the single-drug deep dives — if you are deciding between two agents, read the versus guide first, then the deep dive on the one hanging on your pump. New medication guides get added here as they are written, so this page is worth a bookmark.

Vasopressors and inotropes

The squeeze-versus-pump distinction is the whole game: pressors buy blood pressure, inotropes buy cardiac output, and choosing the wrong one treats the number instead of the patient. Norepinephrine is the default for a reason, vasopressin is the catecholamine-sparing second, and the rest are situational tools.

Sedation, analgesia and the RASS conversation

Modern ICU sedation is analgesia-first, lightest-effective-dose, and re-assessed constantly — the drips are easy to start and hard to stop well. RSI and neuromuscular blockade

Paralysis without sedation is the error this family of guides exists to prevent. The induction pair — etomidate or ketamine — and the paralytic pair — rocuronium or succinylcholine — cover nearly every intubation, and the long game belongs to cisatracurium.

Cardiac drips: rate, rhythm and pressure

Electrolytes, insulin and the correction rules

Electrolyte replacement looks like scut work and is actually high-stakes pharmacology — the correction speed limits exist because correcting the number too fast injures the patient a second time.
