Do Travel Nurses Need a CPA? The Honest Decision Line

Part of the Travel Nursing Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Every travel nurse Facebook group has the same two people: one who insists a $500 traveler-tax CPA is throwing money away because "software does the same thing," and one who insists filing your own multi-state return is financial suicide. Both are wrong at the edges and right in the middle. The real answer depends on which return you're filing — and this year's version of you may need a different answer than last year's.

The one-line version: one agency, two states, clean tax home — good software handles it. Three-plus states, a tax-home question mark, an agency switch, 1099 income, or an IRS letter — pay the $300–$800 for a preparer who works with travelers all day. The mistake isn't picking either lane; it's picking one permanently.

What "travel nurse taxes" actually demands

A traveler's return has four layers a staff nurse's doesn't: a tax home determination that decides whether five figures of stipends stay tax-free; a resident state return plus nonresident returns for every state worked, with credits so you're not double-taxed; W-2s from every agency you touched (each with its own stipend structure); and, increasingly, 1099 side income from per-diem apps with quarterly estimated payments. None of this is exotic — but each layer is a place where generic preparation quietly gets it wrong, usually in the state-credit math or by treating stipends as deductible-expense territory that hasn't existed for W-2 employees since 2018.

CPA, EA, or "tax preparer" — what the letters mean for you

You don't specifically need a CPA. A CPA is a state-licensed accountant (many specialize in things other than individual tax); an Enrolled Agent (EA) is federally licensed specifically in taxation and can represent you before the IRS just like a CPA; an unlicensed preparer may be excellent or may be a seasonal storefront. For a traveler, the credential that matters most is none of these — it's volume of traveler returns. An EA who prepares hundreds of travel-clinician returns a year will beat a general-practice CPA who sees two. Several national firms specialize exclusively in traveling healthcare professionals and work remotely with clients in all states, which fits the traveler lifestyle better than a hometown office anyway. Expect $300–$800 for a multi-state traveler return depending on state count and 1099 complexity — and treat quotes far below that with the same suspicion as a too-good pay package.

Six vetting questions (and the answers you want)

Ask these before handing over documents. "How many travel nurse or travel clinician returns did you file last season?" — you want dozens-plus, not "a few." "Walk me through how you'd evaluate my tax home." — you want questions about your residence, expenses, and time at home, not a shrug. "How do you handle multi-state credits when my resident state taxes everything?" — they should explain resident-state credits fluently. "Will you represent me if this return is audited, and what does that cost?" — CPAs and EAs can; know the fee now. "What's your position on stipends above GSA per-diem rates?" — you want caution, not "never a problem." "Who actually prepares my return?" — some shops sell the expert and delegate to juniors.

Red flags that should end the conversation: promises of a specific refund before seeing documents; advice to claim tax-free stipends without a qualifying tax home ("everyone does it"); fees calculated as a percentage of your refund; refusal to sign the return as paid preparer; and any suggestion to deduct unreimbursed W-2 job expenses on your federal return — that deduction died for employees in 2018, and a preparer who doesn't know that will miss much more.

Make whoever you choose cheap to work with

The preparer's fee scales with the mess you hand them. Show up with the one-folder system from our audit-readiness guide: every W-2 and final pay stub, contracts with the taxable/stipend split, tax-home payment proof, and a one-page list of every state worked with dates. That turns a $700 engagement into a $450 one, and — more importantly — it means the return is built on facts rather than reconstruction. The same folder is exactly what defends the return if it's ever questioned, which closes the loop: good records make both the cheap year (DIY) and the expensive year (specialist) work.

Bottom line: match the tool to the year. Simple year, good software, careful reading — keep your money. Multi-state, shaky tax home, agency switches, 1099s, or any letter from a tax authority — hire a traveler-volume EA or CPA, learn from the return they produce, and re-decide next January. The only unforgivable choice is the storefront preparer who's never heard of a tax home.
