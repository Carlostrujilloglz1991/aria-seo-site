Nurse Licensure Compact: Which States Are In, How It Affects Your Pay

Part of the Nurse Money Hub — licensing, travel nursing, and nurse income strategy in one map.

The Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC) allows RNs and LPNs to hold one multistate license that is valid in all member states. As of mid-2026, 41 states plus the U.S. Virgin Islands are NLC members. For travel nurses, the compact is the difference between taking a new contract in 2–3 weeks versus waiting 4–12 weeks for a single-state license to process.

The compact only works if your primary state of residence is a member: You hold a multistate license from your home state. If you live in California or New York (non-compact), you cannot access multistate privileges even if you take a contract in Texas. This is the single most misunderstood thing about the NLC.

Non-compact states (where you need a separate license)

The following states are NOT NLC members as of 2026: California, New York, Massachusetts, Connecticut, Illinois, Minnesota, Nevada, Oregon, Washington, Hawaii, Vermont, Rhode Island, and Alaska. These include some of the highest-paying states for nurses. If you want to work in California — median RN pay $133,000 — you must apply for a California RN license separately, a process that takes 6–16 weeks and costs $100–$350 in fees plus fingerprint processing.

California NCLEX exam location matters: Nurses who took their NCLEX in California automatically have a California single-state license. If you later move to a compact state and want multistate privileges, you must apply to your new state's board to "re-endorse" your license under the compact framework. Don't assume your existing license automatically converts.

How the compact affects travel nurse income

Compact membership directly affects the speed and cost of accessing higher-paying contracts. The practical impact:

Speed to revenue: A Texas nurse (compact) can accept a contract in Florida (compact) and start within 2–3 weeks with no additional licensing steps. The same nurse accepting a California assignment must wait 8–16 weeks for CA licensure — forgoing potentially $8,000–$16,000 in income during that window.

Contract flexibility: Compact nurses can simultaneously hold "privileges" in multiple compact states, making it easy to pick up a last-minute contract in any member state. Agencies give compact nurses higher priority for short-turnaround contracts because credentialing is simpler.

Multiple state licenses: High-earning nurses often maintain their compact home-state license plus a California and New York single-state license for maximum market access. This covers roughly 70% of all available travel nursing positions and all the high-paying west coast markets.

Background check and discipline: the compact's enforcement teeth

The NLC's Coordinated Licensure Information System (CLIS) means that a disciplinary action in one compact state is immediately visible in all others. There is no hiding a license issue by working in a different compact state. This is good for patient safety and should matter to nurses who take their practice seriously — but it's worth knowing that a reprimand in Texas is not a "Texas problem only."

If you're planning to travel nurse: The first financial decision is where to establish primary residence. Living in a compact state with no income tax (Texas, Florida, Nevada, Wyoming) while holding a multistate license is the combination that maximizes travel nursing income — no state income tax on your taxable base pay, and access to all compact states plus your separately held California/NY licenses.
