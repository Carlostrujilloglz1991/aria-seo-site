Nurse Curriculum & Content Writing 2026

Part of the Nurse Side Hustle Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Nursing schools, NCLEX-prep companies, CE providers, and health-education startups all pay clinicians to write. If you can explain a concept clearly, this is one of the most flexible remote side incomes in nursing.

The pitch: Companies that sell nursing education need people who both understand the clinical content and can write it clearly. That combination is rare, so clinicians who can write are in demand — and the work is fully remote and deadline-based, not shift-based.

What you would actually write

TypeWho buys itWhat it involves

Course & curriculum contentNursing programs, ed-tech startupsLesson objectives, lectures, case studies, rubrics

Clinical blog / SEO articlesHealth brands, agenciesEvidence-based articles for a lay or professional audience

Why nurses have an unfair advantage here

Anyone can write; few writers can accurately explain sepsis management, NGN item logic, or a med-pass safety check. Editors constantly fact-check generalist writers on clinical content. A nurse who writes clean copy removes that burden, which is why clinical-writer rates hold up. Bedside experience also lets you write realistic case studies and clinically sound test items — the hardest part to fake.

What it pays

Rates range widely by format and client. Test-bank questions are often paid per accepted item (with rationale and reference). CE modules and curriculum work are commonly project- or hourly-based. Freelance clinical articles are typically paid per piece. As with all freelancing, your rate climbs fast once you have a portfolio and repeat clients — the first few gigs establish credibility, then you raise prices.

How to start with no writing portfolio

Write two or three strong samples on your own: a polished patient-education handout, one NGN-style question with a full rationale, and a short evidence-based article in your specialty. These are your portfolio. Then pitch:

Contact nursing-education publishers and NCLEX-prep companies about item-writing (they run continuous recruitment). Approach CE providers about authoring modules in your specialty. List services on freelance platforms targeting healthcare content. Tell your own nursing-school faculty — programs frequently need adjunct content help. An optimized LinkedIn profile that signals "ICU RN + writer" attracts inbound work.

The compounding benefit: Unlike shift-based side gigs, writing builds an asset — a portfolio and a reputation that raise your rate over time and can grow into a consistent second income or even a full remote career in nursing education or health-content strategy.
