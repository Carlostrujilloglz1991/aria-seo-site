CRNA Programs in Alaska

Alaska has no in-state accredited CRNA program. Like other large, sparsely populated states, it relies on CRNAs for rural anesthesia — but training happens in the Lower 48, most often the Pacific Northwest. With a clear plan, an Alaska base is workable.

The landscape: There is no COA-accredited nurse anesthesia program located in Alaska. CRNAs are essential to anesthesia care in Alaska's rural and remote hospitals, but nurses pursuing the credential must attend a mainland program, most commonly in the Pacific Northwest or Mountain West.

Why Alaska Has No Program (and Why That's Manageable)

Alaska's small, dispersed population and limited number of high-volume surgical centers make it impractical to sustain a nurse anesthesia program with the case variety accreditation demands. The flip side is that CRNAs are highly valued across Alaska's rural health system, so an applicant who plans to train in the Lower 48 and return to practice in Alaska brings a compelling, mission-aligned story.

Admission Requirements

Wherever you apply, the national CRNA template holds. You will need a BSN (or equivalent qualifying nursing degree) with a competitive GPA, a minimum of one year of full-time adult critical-care experience, an unencumbered RN license, and current BLS and ACLS certification. The ICU requirement is non-negotiable, and competitive applicants typically bring more than the minimum — the CCRN credential strengthens a file; see the CCRN guide. For the complete checklist, review the general CRNA school requirements.

Make your rural-practice intent part of the story. Pacific Northwest programs value applicants committed to rural and remote anesthesia. If you plan to return to Alaska to practice, say so — it aligns with the clinical mission of many regional programs and strengthens your file.

Application Strategy for Alaska

Plan to relocate and apply regionally from day one. Anchor your list with the closest options in Washington (including Gonzaga's DNAP) and Oregon, then widen to California and the Mountain West. Strengthen your file with the required ICU year (ideally more), the CCRN, and a sharp personal statement, and budget for a mainland move. For realistic expectations, see CRNA acceptance rates.

Bottom Line

Alaska has no in-state CRNA program, so the route runs through the Pacific Northwest — Washington and Oregon first, then California and the Mountain West. CRNAs are essential to Alaska's rural hospitals, so a clear plan to train in the Lower 48 and return home is a real strength.
