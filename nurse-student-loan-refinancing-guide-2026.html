Best Student Loan Refinancing for Nurses in 2026

The average nursing school grad leaves with $47,000 in debt. If you went the BSN-to-MSN route or got an APRN, you might be sitting on $80,000–$120,000. This guide is for nurses who are tired of watching interest compound and want to know — plainly — whether refinancing actually makes sense, or whether they should be chasing Public Service Loan Forgiveness instead.

Spoiler: for most hospital nurses, PSLF is the better deal. But "most" isn't "all," and refinancing can be the right move for nurses in private practice, travel nursing, or anyone who just wants to pay the debt off fast and be done with it. Let's walk through the real math so you can decide for yourself.

The Nursing Student Loan Burden: By the Numbers

Nursing isn't a cheap education anymore. According to the American Association of Colleges of Nursing, BSN programs at four-year universities cost between $40,000 and $95,000 in total tuition alone. Factor in living expenses, textbooks, NCLEX prep courses, and licensure fees, and the median new grad nurse carries roughly $47,000 in federal student loan debt — higher than the national average for all bachelor's degree holders.

Advanced practice nurses carry significantly more. Nurse practitioners and CRNAs often graduate with $90,000 to $170,000 in debt, depending on the program. And because nursing salaries, while solid, aren't physician-level, the debt-to-income ratio can feel crushing — especially in the first few years post-graduation.

There are essentially three paths forward:

  1. Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) + PSLF — pay 10 years on an IDR plan while working for a nonprofit hospital, get the balance forgiven tax-free.
  2. Refinancing — replace federal loans with a private loan at a lower interest rate, pay it down aggressively.
  3. Hybrid approach — refinance only private loans, keep federal loans for PSLF eligibility.

Which path wins depends entirely on where you work, how much you owe, and how long you plan to stay in nursing. Let's look at the comparison head-on.

When Refinancing Actually Makes Sense for Nurses

Refinancing isn't the wrong choice — it's just the wrong choice for some nurses. Here's when it legitimately wins:

  • You work for a for-profit employer. Travel nursing agencies, private outpatient clinics, staffing agencies, and most for-profit hospital systems (HCA, Tenet, Community Health Systems) don't qualify for PSLF. If forgiveness isn't on the table, a lower interest rate is the next best thing.
  • Your balance is under $30,000. At low balances, the math on PSLF is less compelling. You might pay off a $25,000 loan in 4–5 years through refinancing and be completely debt-free years before PSLF would forgive a smaller remaining balance.
  • You want certainty. PSLF has had implementation problems — wrong paperwork, employer certification errors, program changes. Some nurses prefer the clean, predictable path of a private loan they control.
  • You have high-interest private loans. Private loans from undergrad or nursing school at 9–12% are always refinancing candidates. You can't do PSLF on private loans anyway.
  • Your income is high and growing. NPs, CRNAs, and CNSs earning $120,000+ may find IDR payments nearly as high as a standard repayment anyway — reducing the PSLF "forgiveness windfall" significantly.

Step-by-Step: How to Apply for Student Loan Refinancing

The actual application process is simpler than most nurses expect. Here's what to do, in order:

  1. Pull your current loan details. Log into StudentAid.gov and download your loan summary. You'll need: each loan's servicer, balance, current interest rate, and loan type (Direct vs. FFEL vs. Perkins). This takes 15 minutes and is worth doing before you do anything else.
  2. Confirm your employer's PSLF eligibility. Before you refinance a single federal dollar, check whether your employer qualifies at StudentAid.gov/PSLF. If your hospital is eligible and you plan to stay 10 years, stop here — do not refinance federal loans.
  3. Check your credit. Free credit check at AnnualCreditReport.com. SoFi looks at FICO score, debt-to-income ratio, and employment history. A score above 700 gets you competitive rates. Below 680, consider adding a co-signer or improving your score first.
  4. Get rate quotes from 2–3 lenders. SoFi is a strong starting point, but also check Earnest and Laurel Road (which has a nurse-specific program). All three offer soft pulls. Compare APR (not just rate), term options, and forbearance protections.
  5. Calculate your break-even. Use the Student Loan Planner calculator or a simple spreadsheet: How much do you pay under your current plan vs. the new plan over the same number of years? If refinancing saves you $10,000 over 10 years but you'd get $40,000 forgiven under PSLF, you haven't won.
  6. Submit the full application. You'll need: government-issued ID, your last 2 pay stubs (or offer letter if new job), proof of degree, and your current loan servicer information. Most lenders give a decision within 1–3 business days.
  7. Continue making payments until the transition is complete. Refinancing usually takes 2–4 weeks to fully process. Keep paying your original servicer until you receive confirmation the loan has been paid off and transferred.

A Note on PSLF for ICU, ER, and Hospital Nurses

If you're working at a nonprofit hospital — and most large hospital systems are 501(c)(3) entities — you are almost certainly PSLF-eligible. This is one of the most underutilized financial tools in nursing, and it's genuinely worth understanding before making any decision.

The math is straightforward: after 120 qualifying payments on an income-driven repayment plan (SAVE, IBR, or PAYE), your remaining federal loan balance is forgiven completely, tax-free. On a $75,000 balance at a $60,000 starting salary, an IDR payment might be around $350/month — and after 10 years, whatever remains is gone.

The key is submitting annual employer certification forms through your servicer (MOHELA handles most PSLF accounts). Don't wait 10 years and assume it worked — certify annually so problems get caught early.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can travel nurses qualify for PSLF?

It depends on the agency. If you're a W-2 employee of a staffing agency that itself is a nonprofit, technically yes — but most travel nursing agencies (Aya, AMN, Host Healthcare, etc.) are for-profit companies. Your qualifying employer is the agency, not the hospital you're placed at. Most travel nurses do not qualify for PSLF, which is one reason refinancing is more commonly the right call for travelers.

Will checking my rate at SoFi hurt my credit?

No. The initial rate check is a soft pull — it has zero effect on your credit score. Only if you accept an offer and submit a full application will SoFi do a hard pull, which typically drops a score by 2–5 points temporarily.

I have both federal and private student loans. Can I refinance just the private ones?

Yes, and this is often the smartest move. You can refinance only your private loans through SoFi or another lender, leaving your federal loans intact and eligible for PSLF or IDR plans. Private loans don't qualify for federal forgiveness anyway, so there's no benefit to keeping them federal — the main goal is getting a lower rate.

What's the minimum credit score to refinance student loans?

Most lenders want 680+, with the best rates going to borrowers above 720. If you're below 680, consider 6–12 months of credit-building first (keep utilization under 20%, pay everything on time), or add a creditworthy co-signer.

Is there a nurse-specific student loan forgiveness program separate from PSLF?

The NURSE Corps Loan Repayment Program is the main alternative. It pays 60% of qualifying nursing student debt over 2 years in exchange for working at a Critical Shortage Facility (typically underserved, rural, or safety-net hospitals). You can apply for a 3rd year for an additional 25%. Competition is high — acceptance rates are roughly 30%. It's worth applying, but don't count on it as your primary plan. See our NURSE Corps guide for application details.

What if I refinance and then get laid off or become unable to work?

This is a real risk with private refinancing. Federal loans have robust forbearance options; private loans vary. SoFi offers an unemployment protection program that pauses payments if you lose your job involuntarily, in 3-month increments up to 12 months total. Read the fine print before committing. If you have any concern about job stability, federal loan forbearance protections are worth preserving.

The Bottom Line

Here's the version for nurses who are short on time: if you work at a nonprofit hospital and owe more than $40,000 in federal loans, do PSLF. Full stop. Read everything you can, certify your employer today, and don't refinance federal loans.

If you're at a for-profit employer, in private practice, in travel nursing, or your federal balance is low — refinancing is worth exploring seriously. SoFi is a strong option with no fees, a genuine 2-minute rate check, and real protections for nurses who might hit a rough patch. Compare at least two lenders before committing.

And if you're not sure which path applies to you? Start with the PSLF eligibility check at StudentAid.gov. Five minutes of research there can save you tens of thousands of dollars.
