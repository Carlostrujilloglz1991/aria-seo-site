Per Diem vs Travel Nursing 2026

Both per diem and travel nursing pay significantly more than staff positions. But they are different tools. Which generates more income depends on your market, your tax home situation, and how many shifts you want to work. Here is the honest comparison.

How Each Model Works

Per Diem Nursing

Per diem (PRN) nurses work shift-by-shift with no guaranteed hours. They are usually employees of the hospital or a local staffing agency, picking up shifts based on census needs. Pay is a flat hourly rate — often 25-50% above the staff rate for the same unit — with no benefits, no guaranteed hours, and no multi-week commitment.

Travel Nursing

Travel nurses work 13-week contracts (though 4, 8, and 26-week contracts exist) at hospitals away from their tax home. Compensation is split between a lower base W-2 hourly rate and tax-free housing and meal stipends. The stipends, when the tax home qualification is met, dramatically increase effective take-home pay without increasing taxable income.

Gross pay comparisons favor travel in most markets. But the real comparison is after-tax take-home, which is where travel nursing's structural advantage shows up most clearly.

The Tax Advantage: Where Travel Nursing Wins

A travel nurse earning $3,000/week gross may have a package structured as:

  • $24/hour base rate (W-2 taxable) x 36 hours = $864 taxable
  • $1,200/week tax-free housing stipend
  • $936/week tax-free meals and incidentals stipend

Result: $864/week hits their W-2. The rest is tax-free. At a 22% federal bracket plus state taxes, that's roughly $500-700/week in avoided taxes compared to earning the same $3,000 as fully taxable wages. Over a full year, the tax advantage alone can be worth $25,000-$35,000.

Per diem nurses pay taxes on 100% of their hourly rate. There are no stipends. A per diem nurse earning $65/hour ($2,340/week at 36 hours) keeps significantly less than a travel nurse earning a similar gross because the entire amount is taxable.

When Per Diem Actually Wins

Per diem is the better choice in specific situations:

  • You don't have or can't maintain a valid tax home — if your stipends would be taxable anyway, travel's pay structure loses its advantage
  • You need maximum geographic flexibility — per diem lets you pick up shifts at multiple hospitals without committing to a 13-week contract anywhere
  • You have family or custody obligations that prevent relocation — per diem keeps you local with more shift control
  • You're approaching a 12-month limit at one hospital — per diem at your local facility may let you stay without triggering the CRRT tax home conversion
  • Your local per diem market is unusually strong — California hospitals sometimes pay per diem rates that approach travel packages because of staff shortages and state ratios

When Travel Nursing Wins

  • You have a valid, documentable tax home you maintain and pay for
  • You can commit to 13 weeks at a location
  • You want to build savings aggressively (the stipend advantage is real and compounding)
  • You want to build experience at different hospital systems (travel RNs see more variety in 2 years than many staff nurses see in 10)
  • You're targeting geographic markets with large pay differentials (California, New York, Pacific Northwest)

The Hybrid Strategy Most Experienced Nurses Use

The highest-earning nurses in 2026 often blend both models. A common pattern: work a 13-week travel contract, return home for 4-6 weeks during which you pick up local per diem shifts, then start another travel contract. This maintains your tax home (you're paying housing and physically returning), keeps income flowing during gaps, and lets you control geographic exposure.

The bottom line: For pure income maximization, travel nursing with a valid tax home beats per diem in most markets. But per diem offers something travel cannot: the ability to stay home, control your schedule shift-by-shift, and work across multiple hospitals simultaneously. The best strategy depends on where you are in your life and what constraints you're managing.
