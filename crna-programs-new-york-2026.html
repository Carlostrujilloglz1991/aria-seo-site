New York has a small but strong set of accredited nurse anesthesia programs, ranging from an Ivy League school in Manhattan to a public SUNY program upstate. If you're an ICU nurse planning to stay in New York for CRNA school, here are the accredited options, what they cost, and what it takes to get in for 2026.

The short answer: New York has roughly 4 accredited CRNA schools offering nurse anesthesia programs, all now at the doctoral (DNP) level. Tuition spans a wide range — roughly $88,000 to $178,000 — depending on whether you choose a public or private institution. All require ICU experience and a competitive GPA.

Accredited CRNA programs in New York

Every program below is accredited by the Council on Accreditation of Nurse Anesthesia Educational Programs (COA), the national body that approves all U.S. nurse anesthesia training. Always confirm a program's current accreditation status on the COA list before applying.

Columbia University runs a 36-month, full-time DNP in nurse anesthesia for experienced critical-care RNs and is among the most competitive programs in the Northeast. Its clinical placements across major Manhattan hospitals are a major draw — and a reason application volume is high.

University at Buffalo (SUNY) is the public standout, offering a terminal nurse anesthesia degree at a lower in-state cost than the private programs. For New York residents who want to control debt, a SUNY program is often the most capital-efficient route.

St. John Fisher University in Rochester provides a COA-accredited DNP nurse anesthesia program with strong regional clinical ties in western New York. Hunter-Bellevue School of Nursing (CUNY) offers a distinctive public-institution program in the NYC metro that prepares graduates for dual certification as both a CRNA and an adult-gerontology acute care nurse practitioner.

Public vs. private is the big cost lever. With New York tuition ranging from roughly $88,000 to $178,000, choosing a SUNY or CUNY program over a private school can cut your borrowing nearly in half. Because you generally can't work during a full-time anesthesia program, that gap compounds into years of repayment difference.

Admission requirements

New York programs follow the same competitive benchmarks you'll see nationwide. The published minimums get you eligible; the real bar to be admitted is higher.

RequirementTypical expectation
DegreeBSN (or RN-to-BSN completed before entry)
LicenseActive, unencumbered NY RN license
ICU experienceMinimum 1 year; competitive applicants have 2–3 in high-acuity units
GPAMinimum ~3.0; admitted students cluster around 3.4–3.7
CCRNRequired or strongly preferred
OtherShadowing hours, strong references, and an interview
Apply broadly within and beyond the state. With only about four programs in New York and tiny cohorts at each, a New York–only application strategy is risky. Many strong applicants also apply to programs in neighboring Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and Connecticut to improve their odds of an offer.

How to strengthen your application

The same levers that work nationally apply in New York. Get into the highest-acuity ICU you can — a busy CVICU, SICU, or trauma unit in a New York City or major upstate hospital carries real weight. Earn your CCRN early. If your GPA is below ~3.4, retake or add upper-level science courses and earn As to show an upward trend. Document CRNA shadowing before you apply, and prepare for the interview as if it decides everything, because once you clear the GPA-and-experience screen, it usually does.

Build against a checklist, not a feeling. Benchmark your GPA, ICU type, hours, CCRN, and shadowing against the typical admitted profile, then close your widest gap first. See the CRNA school requirements guide, the acceptance-rate breakdown, and the ICU-to-CRNA timeline.

Bottom line

New York offers a compact but high-quality set of CRNA programs — an Ivy League option at Columbia, an affordable public route through SUNY Buffalo, and solid programs at St. John Fisher and Hunter-Bellevue. The biggest decisions are cost (public vs. private can swing your debt by tens of thousands) and odds (apply broadly, since seats are scarce). Nail a high-acuity ICU, a CCRN, and a ~3.5 GPA, and a New York CRNA seat is within reach.
