CRNA School Requirements 2026: The Complete Checklist

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Getting into nurse anesthesia school is one of the most competitive processes in healthcare. Programs receive hundreds of applications for a handful of seats, and the difference between acceptance and rejection often comes down to meeting—or exceeding—baseline requirements before you even write your personal statement. Here's the complete checklist for 2026.

The Non-Negotiables

Every accredited CRNA program in the country requires these before your application is considered:

Active RN license. You need an unencumbered, current RN license in the United States. Some programs require licensure in the state where the program is located before enrollment, though many accept any U.S. license at the time of application.

BSN or equivalent. A Bachelor of Science in Nursing is the minimum degree requirement. If you have a non-nursing bachelor's degree and an ADN, some programs will accept that combination, but it's increasingly rare. A few programs consider applicants with a BSN in progress if it will be complete before enrollment.

Minimum GPA. Most programs publish a floor of 3.0 overall and 3.0 in sciences, but the real story is in what accepted students actually look like. Competitive programs routinely enroll classes with average GPAs of 3.4–3.6. If you're at 3.0, you're eligible; if you're at 3.5+, you're competitive.

Critical care experience. This is the big one. The minimum is typically one year of full-time ICU experience, but the accepted applicants at top programs average two to three years. More on the specifics below.

ICU Experience Requirements

Not all ICU experience counts equally. Programs want experience in an adult medical-surgical ICU, a cardiac ICU, a cardiovascular ICU, a neuro ICU, or a level I trauma ICU. Pediatric and neonatal ICUs may count but aren't preferred at programs that lean toward adult anesthesia training.

The type of experience matters too. You need to be managing ventilators, titrating vasopressors, interpreting hemodynamic monitoring, and caring for the highest-acuity patients in the unit—not floating to step-down or covering lighter assignments. Programs will ask about this in interviews, and your references will be expected to speak to it.

Hours required: Programs typically ask for 1–2 years (roughly 2,000–4,000 hours). Some programs specify hours; most specify years of full-time experience. Part-time ICU work generally doesn't satisfy the requirement unless you can document equivalent full-time hours.

Science Prerequisite Courses

Most programs require specific undergraduate science courses completed within a set window—usually 5–7 years. Standard prerequisites include:

  • Anatomy and Physiology (often two semesters)
  • Chemistry (general and/or organic)
  • Microbiology
  • Statistics
  • Pharmacology (some programs)
  • Pathophysiology (some programs, though this may be satisfied by graduate coursework)

If your undergrad sciences are old, retaking them shows both eligibility and initiative. Online versions are generally accepted for sciences that don't require a lab component, but lab courses (A&P, Chemistry) typically need to have an in-person or proctored lab.

Graduate-Level Requirements

CRNA programs have been doctoral-level (DNP or DNAP) since January 2022 by AANA mandate. That means you're applying to a doctoral program, and many schools want evidence you can handle graduate coursework. Some require:

  • Graduate-level statistics before enrollment
  • Graduate-level pathophysiology or advanced pharmacology
  • A graduate GPA if you've completed any post-bachelor's coursework

If you don't have any graduate coursework, consider completing a graduate-level course—statistics is the most practical—before applying to signal academic readiness.

Certifications That Help

CCRN is the most impactful. It's not required anywhere, but it signals that you've voluntarily tested yourself beyond the minimum and that you understand your ICU patients at a theoretical level. In a competitive applicant pool, CCRN is a differentiator.

BLS and ACLS are universally required. Most programs also want PALS. Some want NIH Stroke Scale or other specialty certifications depending on their clinical focus.

The GRE Question

The GRE requirement has changed dramatically. Many programs dropped it during COVID and never brought it back. Check each program's current requirements—some have it as optional, some have eliminated it entirely, and a handful still require it. If a target program still requires the GRE, aim for 150+ verbal, 155+ quantitative.

Letters of Recommendation

Most programs want three letters. The standard configuration:

  • One from an ICU nurse manager or supervisor who can speak to your clinical competence
  • One from an MD, CRNA, or other anesthesia provider who has observed you clinically (or can speak to your potential)
  • One from an academic reference if you have one; otherwise, a second clinical reference

Letters that describe specific cases or moments—not generic praise—carry the most weight.

Shadowing

Most programs expect documented anesthesia shadowing, typically 20–50+ hours in an OR or procedure suite, observing a CRNA or anesthesiologist. This requirement has two purposes: confirming you actually know what the job involves, and giving you material to discuss in your personal statement and interview.

Arrange shadowing through your hospital's anesthesia department, through personal contacts, or by reaching out to CRNAs directly. Some will say yes to an email from an ICU nurse.

What Actually Gets You In

Meeting requirements gets your application reviewed. What gets you accepted:

  • ICU experience in a high-acuity, evidence of independent decision-making
  • CCRN certification
  • GPA above 3.4 in sciences
  • A compelling personal statement that explains why anesthesia specifically (more on that in our CRNA personal statement guide)
  • Strong performance in the interview—behavioral questions, clinical scenarios, your career trajectory

Your 2026 Action Checklist

  • [ ] Verify ICU hours and document the type of unit and acuity level
  • [ ] Pull your transcripts and check GPA in sciences
  • [ ] Check prerequisite age windows for target programs
  • [ ] Schedule CCRN exam if not yet certified
  • [ ] Arrange anesthesia shadowing (target 40+ hours)
  • [ ] Identify three strong LOR sources now, before you need them
  • [ ] Research GRE requirement for each program individually
  • [ ] Set a GPA improvement plan if below 3.3

Start this process 12–18 months before your target application cycle. Most CRNA programs have rolling or annual admission timelines, and the preparation takes longer than applicants expect.
