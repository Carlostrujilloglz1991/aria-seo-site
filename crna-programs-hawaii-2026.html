CRNA Programs in Hawaii

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Hawaii has no in-state accredited CRNA program. Demand for nurse anesthetists across the islands is strong, but training means relocating to the mainland — most often the West Coast — with a clear plan to return and practice in Hawaii.

The landscape: There is no COA-accredited nurse anesthesia program located in Hawaii. The state has steady demand for CRNAs, but no school currently offers the doctoral training in-state. Hawaii nurses pursuing this career should plan to attend a mainland program, most commonly on the West Coast.

Why Hawaii Has No Program (and How to Plan Around It)

Hawaii's geography and the concentration of its surgical volume make it difficult to sustain a full nurse anesthesia program with the case variety accreditation requires. The practical reality is that you will relocate for school. The good news is that demand for anesthesia providers in Hawaii is real, so a graduate with ties to the islands and a clear intent to return is well positioned in the job market.

Admission Requirements

Wherever you apply, the national CRNA template holds. You will need a BSN (or equivalent qualifying nursing degree) with a competitive GPA, a minimum of one year of full-time adult critical-care experience, an unencumbered RN license, and current BLS and ACLS certification. The ICU requirement is non-negotiable, and competitive applicants typically bring more than the minimum — the CCRN credential strengthens a file; see the CCRN guide. For the complete checklist, review the general CRNA school requirements.

Ask about Hawaii clinical rotations. A handful of mainland programs have arranged rotations at Hawaii facilities. If completing part of your training near home matters to you, raise it with each program early — it can shape which schools you prioritize.
Confirm the current details. Program availability, residency rules, clinical-site arrangements, and admission requirements change. Treat this page as a planning baseline and verify each program's current information on its official site and the COA accredited-programs list before applying.

Application Strategy for Hawaii

Plan to relocate and apply regionally from day one. Anchor your list with California, Oregon, and Washington programs, and budget realistically for a mainland move. Strengthen your file with the required ICU year (ideally more), the CCRN, and a sharp personal statement. For realistic expectations, see CRNA acceptance rates.

Bottom Line

Hawaii has no in-state CRNA program, so training means a mainland move — most often to California, Oregon, or Washington. With strong island demand for nurse anesthetists, a Hawaii nurse who trains on the West Coast and returns home has a clear and valuable path.
