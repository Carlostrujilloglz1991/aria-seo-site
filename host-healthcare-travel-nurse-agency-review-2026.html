Host Healthcare Review 2026

Part of the Travel Nursing Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Host Healthcare is the agency travel nurses recommend when the question is "who will actually take care of me?" It's a mid-size, service-obsessed shop that competes on recruiter quality and support rather than raw job volume. Here's what that trade looks like in practice.

The one-line version: Host is the high-touch counterweight to the marketplace apps. You get a genuinely responsive recruiter, strong benefits, and real housing help — in exchange for a smaller job board than the mega-agencies and pay packages that are competitive but rarely the absolute top of the market.

The recruiter model, done the way it's supposed to work

Every traditional agency claims great recruiters; Host's reputation is that its recruiters actually answer the phone at 10 p.m. when a facility cancels your shift or your housing falls through. The company is consistently at or near the top of traveler-satisfaction rankings and "best agency" lists year after year, and nearly all of that standing traces to the recruiter relationship: low turnover, low pressure, and follow-through during a contract — not just before you sign.

This matters more than new travelers think. An agency's real product isn't the job listing; it's what happens when things go sideways mid-contract — floating disputes, guaranteed-hours arguments, cancelled contracts. A recruiter who advocates for you in those moments is worth real money.

Benefits: strong where it counts

Host runs a full W-2 package: medical coverage that can begin day one of a contract, dental and vision, a 401(k) with matching after eligibility requirements, licensure and certification reimbursement on qualifying assignments, and travel reimbursement. Day-one health coverage removes the classic gap-anxiety of switching agencies mid-year, and it's a real differentiator against agencies that impose waiting periods.

Check the between-contracts rules anyway. As with every travel agency, maintaining coverage during a gap between assignments has conditions — typically requiring your next contract to start within a set window. If you plan extended time off, price COBRA or a marketplace plan into your budget rather than assuming continuity.

Housing: actual support, not just a stipend number

Most agencies today hand you a stipend and wish you luck. Host still maintains a housing team that will help you find and set up housing if you'd rather not hunt on your own — useful in tight rental markets and for first-time travelers. If you take the stipend and self-arrange (usually the more profitable route), our housing stipend guide covers how to keep more of it.

Pay: competitive, not chart-topping

Host's packages land in the competitive middle-to-upper band for most specialties. High-touch service costs margin, and that margin comes from somewhere. On any given contract, an aggressive marketplace listing or a rate-crisis agency may post a higher gross. The honest framing: Host rarely wins a pure pay-rate spreadsheet battle, but pay-per-unit-of-headache is where it shines. Always comparison-quote the same facility and unit through a second agency before signing — that's true with every agency, Host included.

What to ask before your first Host contract

Service reputation doesn't exempt you from due diligence. Before signing, get clear answers on four items. First, guaranteed hours: how many hours per week are guaranteed, and what exactly counts as a facility call-off versus your missed shift? Second, cancellation: if the facility cancels the whole contract at week three, what does Host pay, and what happens to your housing lease? Third, the extension package: extensions are where agencies quietly trim stipends, so ask whether your package carries over unchanged. Fourth, float expectations: get the float policy — which units, how often — written into the contract, because "you might float occasionally" verbal assurances evaporate the first short-staffed night shift. A strong recruiter, which Host usually provides, will answer all four in writing without being chased. Treat any hedging as data.

Who it fits — and who it doesn't

Host fits first-time travelers who want a guide through tax homes, stipends, and facility vetting; nurses who've been burned by ghost-recruiters at bigger shops; and anyone who values a fast, human answer mid-crisis over an extra $50 a week. It fits less well for volume shoppers who want the largest possible job board, nurses chasing absolute peak crisis rates, and self-directed travelers who'd rather never talk to a recruiter at all — that last group should read our Trusted Health review and Nomad Health review instead.

Bottom line: Host Healthcare is arguably the best "first agency" in travel nursing and a strong permanent home for nurses who value service. Keep a second profile active at a higher-volume agency so a thinner job board never stalls your next contract, and comparison-quote pay on every assignment. If the packages come back close, the recruiter quality is the tiebreaker — and Host usually wins tiebreakers.
