Travel Nurse Salary 2026

Travel nursing pay peaked in 2021–2022 and has normalized since. But ICU travel nurses in 2026 are still pulling $120,000–$175,000/year — significantly above staff equivalents. Here's what the real packages look like.

The number that matters most: Total gross weekly pay — taxable base plus non-taxable stipends combined. Headlines about "$65/hr travel nurse pay" are misleading because that rate is usually the taxable portion only. The full package including housing and meal stipends is what you live on. Build your comparison on weekly gross, not hourly rate.

How the Package Breaks Down

A $2,800/week ICU travel package typically looks like this: $30–$36/hr taxable base (36hrs × $33 = $1,188 taxable) + $1,000–$1,200/week housing stipend + $250–$350/week meals and incidentals stipend. Total: ~$2,700–$2,750/week gross.

The taxable portion (~$1,188) is subject to federal and state income tax. The stipends (~$1,550) are tax-free if you maintain a valid tax home. The effective tax rate on the package is much lower than on a comparable staff salary because such a large portion is non-taxable. A $2,800/week travel package often results in more take-home than a $110,000 staff salary after taxes.

The $150k/Year Breakdown

What does $150,000/year travel nursing actually look like? Approximately 48 working weeks at $3,125/week average package. That's 4 contracts of 13 weeks each with minimal gaps. Achievable for a CVICU or high-demand ICU nurse working California or Pacific Northwest markets, negotiating packages rather than accepting first offers, and maintaining a valid tax home to preserve stipend non-taxable status.

The 4-week gap buffer is important. Most travel nurses don't work 52 weeks — contracts end, orientation for the next assignment takes time, and intentional time off between contracts is common. Budgeting on 48 working weeks rather than 52 is realistic. On 46 weeks: $143,750. On 44 weeks: $137,500. Know your actual working week target before committing to a financial plan based on travel income.

The CRNA-track calculation: A nurse who travels for 2 years before CRNA school earning $140k/year saves aggressively: $30k/year into Roth IRA and emergency fund, $50k/year into CRNA tuition savings, $60k/year living expenses. After 2 years: $60k in savings/retirement, $100k toward tuition, zero additional debt entering school. That's the version of travel nursing that changes the trajectory — not just the income, but what you do with it.
