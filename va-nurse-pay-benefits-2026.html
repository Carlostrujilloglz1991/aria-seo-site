The short answer: VA nurses trade some headline salary for a benefits stack no private hospital matches: a defined-benefit FERS pension on top of a TSP match, automatic PSLF-qualifying employment, generous leave that accrues from day one, health insurance that can follow you into retirement, and education debt repayment programs that can exceed six figures. In high-cost markets the salary gap versus private systems can be real; in many mid-cost markets, VA locality pay is competitive outright. The package rewards nurses who stay — the pension and retiree health insurance are worth the most to a 15–30 year career, least to a 2-year stopover.

VA registered nurses are not paid on the familiar GS schedule. They're hired under Title 38, on the Nurse Locality Pay System — each VA medical center sets its own RN pay ranges based on surveys of what local facilities pay. That's why VA RN pay in one city can look very different from another, and why the only number that matters is the range at the specific facility you'd work at (it's published in the job posting on USAJOBS).

How VA Nurse Grades Work

VA nurses are boarded into grades — Nurse I, II, and III, each with steps — by a Nurse Professional Standards Board that evaluates education and practice against the qualification standards. In practical terms: education level heavily gates the grades (a BSN matters for advancing past Nurse I, and a master's strengthens Nurse III candidacy), and documented practice accomplishments — the same portfolio-building covered in our clinical ladder guide — drive promotions. Boards review nurses periodically, meaning promotion doesn't require changing jobs.

The Benefits Stack — Where VA Actually Wins

FERS pension: a defined-benefit annuity — roughly 1% (1.1% if retiring at 62+ with 20+ years) of your high-3 average salary per year of service, for life. A nurse retiring after 25 years with a high-3 of $110,000 is looking at a pension in the neighborhood of $27,500/year before survivor elections — on top of TSP and Social Security. Almost nothing in private-sector nursing still offers this.

TSP with match: the federal Thrift Savings Plan matches up to 5% of salary and carries some of the lowest fund expenses in existence — the fee traps we flag in the nurse retirement guide mostly don't exist in TSP.

PSLF, automatically: the federal government is a qualifying employer for Public Service Loan Forgiveness, full stop. Every payment made while at the VA counts toward the 120. If you're carrying nursing school debt, run the numbers in our PSLF guide — for some nurses this alone outweighs a private-sector pay bump.

EDRP: the VA's Education Debt Reduction Program can repay a substantial amount of qualifying education debt (historically up to $200,000 over five years) for nurses in designated hard-to-fill positions. It's position-specific — ask HR whether the posting carries EDRP before you accept, not after.

Leave: VA RNs accrue 8 hours of annual leave per pay period from day one — 26 days a year — plus 13 sick days and 11 federal holidays. Private hospitals typically make new nurses wait 15+ years to reach that accrual, if it's offered at all.

FEHB into retirement: carry federal health insurance into retirement (with the government still paying its share) after retiring with 5 continuous years of coverage — one of the most underrated pieces of the package, and a real bridge for anyone eyeing early retirement math like our nurse FIRE guide readers.

The stay-length math (illustrative): 2-yr VA stint: locality salary + match + PSLF credit (pension accrual trivially small) 15-yr career: pension ~15% of high-3 for life + retiree FEHB eligibility path + 26 days/yr leave the whole time The package back-loads its value. Decide based on how long you realistically expect to stay.

The Honest Trade-Offs

In the highest-paying private markets — California union hospitals especially — VA locality pay can trail staff rates meaningfully, and no VA package matches travel-contract income (see weekend option vs per diem vs travel). Hiring is slower than private systems: federal onboarding, boarding, and background checks commonly take months, so never resign a job on a VA tentative offer timeline. Bureaucracy is real, floating and staffing challenges exist like anywhere, and pay flexibility (counteroffers, sign-on negotiation) is limited compared to private facilities.

ICU-to-CRNA note: VA ICUs count as critical care experience for CRNA applications, and several VA facilities have strong ICU acuity. A VA ICU job with PSLF credit accumulating can be a deliberate CRNA-runway move — the requirements guide covers what programs look for in ICU experience.
