Credit Card Points for Beginners: How to Earn $1,000+ in Travel Rewards Your First Year

In 2025, experienced travel hackers routinely extracted $3,000–$8,000 in travel value from credit card sign-up bonuses. You don't need to go that far to benefit. A beginner who opens two well-chosen cards and pays the balance in full every month can realistically get $800–$1,500 in flights or hotel stays in the first 12 months — from spending they'd do anyway.

The only rule that matters first: This strategy works only if you pay your full statement balance every month, on time, every time. Carrying a balance at 20%+ APR eliminates every cent of rewards value and then some. If you have any tendency to carry a balance, stop reading here and pay off the existing debt first.

How sign-up bonuses actually work

Most travel credit cards offer a sign-up bonus (also called a welcome offer) in the form of points or miles after you spend a certain amount in the first 3 months. A typical offer looks like: "Earn 60,000 Chase Ultimate Rewards points after spending $4,000 in the first 3 months." Those 60,000 points are worth approximately $600 in cash travel redemptions or $900+ when transferred to airline partners for business class flights.

The spending requirement ($4,000 in 90 days) sounds high but works out to $1,333/month. If you redirect rent, groceries, utilities, and insurance payments to the card while maintaining your normal cash flow, this is achievable for most households without spending a dollar more than usual. The key is discipline: the money must already be in your checking account before you charge it.

The two-card beginner strategy

For year one, open two cards spaced 3–4 months apart. Start with a mid-tier card like the Chase Sapphire Preferred (the most beginner-friendly premium travel card). Spend 3 months hitting the sign-up bonus using normal expenses. Month 4, evaluate whether your credit utilization has returned to normal and your score has recovered (it will dip 5–10 points temporarily from the hard inquiry, then typically rise as the new credit limit improves your utilization ratio).

Month 4–5: open a no-fee everyday card like the Chase Freedom Unlimited. Its sign-up bonus is small but its ongoing 1.5x earning rate makes it the best "everything else" card to use after the Sapphire covers dining and travel categories. In the Chase ecosystem, Freedom Unlimited points transfer to Sapphire Preferred and become worth 25% more when redeemed for travel.

Chase's 5/24 rule: Chase will not approve most of its cards if you've opened 5 or more credit cards (from any bank) in the past 24 months. Start with Chase cards before opening cards from other issuers, or you'll lock yourself out of their most valuable products.

How to hit the spending minimum without overspending

Put every possible recurring expense on the card: groceries, gas, utilities (check if they allow credit card payments), phone bill, streaming subscriptions, insurance, Amazon purchases, and any medical copays. If you have a large planned purchase coming (appliance, furniture, dental work), time your card application 1–2 months before it. Pay the full balance immediately from checking.

What not to do: invent spending categories, take out cash advances (they don't count toward spend and accrue interest immediately), buy gift cards you don't need, or loan the card to family members to hit the minimum.

What $1,000 in points actually buys

60,000 Chase Ultimate Rewards points can be redeemed for: $600 in cash back, $750 in travel booked through Chase's portal, or 1–2 business class domestic flights if transferred to United MileagePlus (where 30,000 miles can book a saver award on shorter routes). The transfer partner strategy consistently gets 1.5–2.5 cents per point, compared to 1 cent for cash back. Understanding which redemption fits your actual travel plans determines whether the value is $600 or $1,200+ from the same points.

Annual fee calculus: A $95 annual fee card only makes sense if you're getting more than $95 in value from sign-up bonus, ongoing rewards, and card benefits. In year one, the sign-up bonus alone typically covers 6–8 years of annual fees. In year two and beyond, evaluate whether ongoing spending and benefits still justify it. Many people downgrade to a no-fee version of the card rather than cancel (canceling reduces your available credit, which can hurt your score).
