✅ The 10-Point Travel Nurse Money Checklist

1

Verify Your Tax Home Is Established Correctly

Your tax home is the permanent residence you maintain while working away — and it determines whether your stipends are tax-free. Most travel nurses get this wrong and either lose the stipend benefit or create IRS exposure.

  • You must have a permanent home you return to and contribute to financially (mortgage/rent, utilities, etc.)
  • Your tax home must be in a different metro area from your assignment — same city doesn't count
  • Keep documentation: lease or mortgage, utility bills, and evidence of visits home during or between contracts
  • If you "gave up" your permanent home, your stipends become taxable income — no exceptions
⚠ IRS exposure risk: A traveler without a valid tax home who claims tax-free stipends is accepting audit risk on every contract. Talk to a travel nurse tax specialist (not a general CPA) before contract 1.
2

Maximize Stipends: Housing + Meals + Incidentals

The IRS sets per diem maximums by city and county. Your stipend is tax-free up to those maximums — agencies often pay under the cap, but you can negotiate up to it.

  • Housing: GSA rate for your assignment city — check at gsa.gov/perdiem
  • Meals & Incidentals (M&IE): $59–$79/day depending on location (2024 rates); partial days at 75%
  • First and last day of any work period = 75% of M&IE rate
  • If agency offers a "blended rate" (base + stipend combined), break it apart and compare to IRS maximums — many blended rates are below what you could legally receive
💡 Quick check: Look up your city's combined GSA rate. If your agency's housing + M&IE stipend is below it, ask them to bring it to the GSA maximum. This is 100% legal and adds $0 in taxes.
3

Calculate Your True Hourly Rate

Agencies advertise weekly package totals, not hourly rates — which makes comparison almost impossible. Always convert every offer to a true hourly rate before comparing.

True Hourly = (Weekly Base Pay + Weekly Stipends) / 36 hours Example: Weekly base: $900 Weekly housing stipend: $700 Weekly M&IE stipend: $252 Total weekly package: $1,852 True hourly = $1,852 / 36 = $51.44/hr Compare Agency B: Weekly package: $2,100 / 36 = $58.33/hr The $248/week difference = $3,224 over a 13-week contract.

Use 36 hours as the denominator (standard 3x12-hour shifts) even if your agency quotes 48 — it normalizes across agencies for comparison.

4

Compare 3 Agencies Before Signing

The same hospital posting can vary $500–$1,200/week across agencies due to margin differences. Never sign with the first agency that reaches out.

Specific comparison criteria — ask each agency for:

  • True weekly gross (base + all stipends, not net)
  • Benefits cost during contract (health insurance premium deducted from pay?)
  • Benefits cost during time OFF between contracts
  • Overtime rate and whether overtime is allowed
  • Housing: agency-provided or stipend only? (Agency housing = less flexibility)
  • Cancellation clause: what happens if the hospital cancels?
  • Extension rate vs. new contract rate (they often differ)
💡 Recommended agencies to get quotes from: Aya Healthcare, Travel Nurse Across America (TNAA), and AMN Healthcare are consistently top-rated for pay transparency. Cross-reference offers on NurseRecruiter.com.
5

Negotiate the Right Items (What's Moveable, What Isn't)

Not everything is negotiable — but more is than most travelers know to ask about.

Almost always negotiable:

  • Stipend amounts (up to GSA maximums)
  • Travel reimbursement (ask for a flat amount, not mileage)
  • Start date (1–2 weeks flexibility common)
  • Completion bonus / extension rate
  • Licensing/certification reimbursement

Rarely negotiable:

  • The hospital's base bill rate (agencies have a set margin)
  • Shift type / unit assignment (hospital controls this)
  • Overtime policies set by the hospital
💡 Script that works: "I have an offer from another agency at $[X] true hourly. Can you match or beat it?" Agencies have more room than they initially show. If they say no and it's the posting you want, ask specifically about stipend adjustments.
6

Understand Your Health Insurance Gap Between Contracts

This is the blind spot that catches most travelers. Most agency health plans only cover you during active contracts. The gap between contracts — even a few days — is uninsured time.

  • Option 1 — COBRA: Continue agency coverage for up to 18 months; expensive ($400–$700/mo for a single person) but keeps existing care network
  • Option 2 — Healthcare.gov short-term plan: Available during gaps; may not cover pre-existing conditions
  • Option 3 — ACA plan: Loss of employment-based coverage = qualifying life event; enroll within 60 days
  • Option 4 — Transition overlap: End one contract on a Friday, start the next Monday — eliminates the gap entirely
⚠ Action required before contract ends: Ask your agency exactly when coverage ends (last day of contract? End of month? 30 days after?). Do not assume — the answer varies by agency and matters a lot.
7

Set Up a Travel-Friendly Checking Account

Standard bank accounts bleed money from travelers — ATM fees, foreign transaction fees, and out-of-network charges add up fast when you're moving every 13 weeks.

What to look for:

  • No foreign transaction fees (essential if you take international assignments)
  • ATM fee reimbursement (Schwab Bank and Ally both reimburse all ATM fees globally)
  • No minimum balance requirements
  • Strong mobile app with early direct deposit
  • No monthly fees
💡 Top picks for travelers: Charles Schwab High Yield Investor Checking (unlimited ATM rebates globally, zero foreign transaction fees) and Ally Bank (no fees, competitive savings rate linked). Keep one account for daily spending and one HYSA for savings — never let your emergency fund live in your spending account.
8

Save 25% of Every Paycheck for Taxes

Travel nurses often have income in 3–4 states in a single year. Combined with self-employment taxes if you're an independent contractor, and the end-of-year bill can be brutal if you didn't plan for it.

  • 25% of your taxable income (not total package) is a safe baseline; bump to 30% if you're in a high-income year
  • Set up a dedicated "tax" savings account — never commingle with emergency fund
  • Pay quarterly estimated taxes (April 15, June 15, Sept 15, Jan 15) to avoid underpayment penalties
  • Multi-state income: you file a return in every state where you earned income; some states have reciprocity agreements, most don't
⚠ W-2 vs. 1099: Agency W-2 workers withhold taxes automatically. Independent contractors (1099) must save and remit quarterly. Know which you are — it changes everything about tax planning.
9

Build a 6-Month Emergency Fund Before Your First Contract

This isn't optional for travelers — it's more important than for staff nurses. You can be cancelled, experience contract end gaps, or need to walk away from a toxic assignment. Without a buffer, you're trapped.

  • 6 months of fixed expenses: rent/mortgage at home, car payment, insurance, student loans — not travel expenses
  • Keep it in a high-yield savings account (see Move 1 in our Starter Pack)
  • Do not touch it unless: medical emergency, involuntary job loss, or contract cancellation
  • If you're starting travel nursing without this, spend your first contract building it before anything else — even before extra debt payments
💡 Target number: Add up your fixed monthly obligations at home (not travel costs). Multiply by 6. That's your number. Most travelers target $8,000–$15,000 depending on their debt load.
10

Track Every Deductible Expense From Day 1

Travel nurses have significant legitimate deductions that most never claim — because they didn't track. You can't deduct what you can't prove.

Track and save receipts for:

  • Licensing fees and renewals (every new state license)
  • Continuing education, certifications (CCRN, CEN, etc.)
  • Mileage for assignment travel (2024 rate: 67 cents/mile)
  • Professional association dues (ANA, AACN, etc.)
  • Scrubs and clinical footwear
  • Stethoscope, clinical tools, medical apps
  • Job-search expenses for new contracts (travel to interviews, phone bills)
💡 Tool to use: Everlance or Mileage IQ for automatic mileage tracking. Google Drive folder with dated photos of every receipt. Takes 10 seconds per receipt and can save you $1,000–$3,000 in deductions at tax time.
⚠ What you CANNOT deduct: Commuting miles (home to assignment), housing at your assignment location (that's the stipend's job), meals covered by your M&IE stipend. Don't double-dip.
