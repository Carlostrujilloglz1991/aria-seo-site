Nurse LLC vs S-Corp Guide 2026

For nurse entrepreneurs earning $50,000+ from self-employment, the difference between an LLC and an S-Corp election can be $5,000–$20,000 in annual tax savings.

The Core Difference: Self-Employment Tax

When you earn self-employment income (1099, side business, KDP royalties, digital products), you owe both the employer AND employee sides of Social Security and Medicare taxes — totaling 15.3% on net earnings up to the SS wage base ($168,600 in 2026), then 2.9% on earnings above that.

An LLC (sole proprietorship tax treatment) pays SE tax on ALL net profit. An S-Corp pays SE tax only on "reasonable salary" — the rest of profit passes through as a distribution, which is NOT subject to SE tax. This is the entire S-Corp advantage.

When NOT to Elect S-Corp Status

S-Corp is not cost-effective when net self-employment income is below $50,000. Below that threshold, the payroll admin costs eat most of the tax savings. Stay as a simple single-member LLC (Schedule C filing) until revenue justifies the complexity.

Wyoming LLC: Why Nurse Entrepreneurs Love It

Wyoming has no state income tax, low annual LLC fees ($60/year filing fee), strong asset protection laws, and allows anonymous members (privacy). You can form a Wyoming LLC even if you don't live there — it's a popular choice for digital product businesses where your work happens online, not in a state-specific physical location. Check with a CPA about your specific state's nexus rules.

Setting Up Your Nurse Business Entity

Step 1: Form your LLC in your state of residence or Wyoming (use Northwest Registered Agent or Registered Agents Inc. for $49–$99). Step 2: Get an EIN (free, instant at IRS.gov). Step 3: Open a business bank account (Mercury or Relay are popular free business banking options for LLCs). Step 4: When revenue hits $50k–$60k+, talk to a CPA about S-Corp election (Form 2553).
