GRE vs MCAT for CRNA School: Which Do Programs Require in 2025?

If you're preparing a CRNA school application, figuring out the standardized test situation is step one — because getting this wrong means wasting months studying for the wrong exam. The short answer is that the GRE dominates, many programs have gone test-optional, and almost no CRNA programs require the MCAT. Here's the full picture.

Bottom line up front: The vast majority of CRNA programs that require a standardized test require the GRE. The MCAT is rarely if ever required for nursing anesthesia programs — it is primarily a medical school admissions test. A significant number of programs have gone test-optional post-2020 and never brought back the requirement. Check each program individually — requirements can change year to year.

The GRE: What It Tests and Why Programs Use It

The Graduate Record Examination (GRE) is designed to predict graduate school academic success. It tests three areas: Verbal Reasoning, Quantitative Reasoning, and Analytical Writing. For CRNA programs, the Verbal and Quantitative sections matter most. The Analytical Writing score is rarely used as a standalone filter, though a score below 3.5 can raise eyebrows.

Programs use the GRE as an academic signal, especially for applicants with a borderline GPA or a GPA from several years ago. A strong GRE score (305+ combined V+Q) can compensate for a 3.0 cumulative GPA in some cases. A poor GRE score confirms the admissions committee's concerns about academic readiness for graduate-level coursework.

Score Expectations for CRNA Programs

GRE Score (V+Q)How Programs View It
Below 290Below most programs' stated minimums; likely filtered out automatically
290–299Minimum threshold; acceptable but not competitive
300–309Solid; competitive at mid-tier programs
310–319Strong; competitive at most programs
320+Exceptional; genuinely differentiating

These are general guidelines — program-specific cutoffs vary. Some programs publicly post their median GRE scores for accepted students; this is the most useful benchmark for that specific program.

The MCAT: Not What You Think

The Medical College Admission Test (MCAT) is a 7.5-hour, 230-question exam covering biology, biochemistry, physics, psychology, and critical analysis. It is designed for pre-medical students applying to MD and DO programs. The content and structure are significantly different from the GRE — and the preparation burden is enormous. Most pre-med students spend 3 to 6 months of intensive full-time study preparing for the MCAT.

As of 2025–2026, the MCAT is not a standard requirement for CRNA school admissions. A small number of programs have historically accepted MCAT scores in lieu of GRE scores, but this is the exception, not the rule. If you already have a recent MCAT score (within 5 years) from a previous attempt at medical school admission, check with your target programs — some will accept it. But taking the MCAT specifically for CRNA school would be a significant misallocation of time and energy for almost any applicant.

Test-Optional Programs: The Growing Trend

Post-pandemic, a meaningful number of CRNA programs waived their GRE requirement and have not reinstated it. For these programs, your GPA, ICU experience, CCRN status, and interview performance carry the full evaluative weight. If your GPA is strong (3.5+) and your clinical profile is excellent, applying to test-optional programs can be a strategic choice that lets you focus preparation time on other application components.

Don't assume test-optional = no tests matter. For some programs, test-optional means optional for all applicants. For others, it means GRE is optional if your GPA is above a certain threshold, but required for applicants below that GPA. Read the fine print, or email admissions directly to confirm.

How to Prep Efficiently as a Working ICU Nurse

You don't have the luxury of studying 8 hours a day. Working nurses who crack 305+ on the GRE typically use 3 to 4 months of structured study at 1 to 2 hours per day. The most efficient approach:

Quantitative: This is where most working nurses have the biggest gap — it's been years since you've done algebra or geometry. The Manhattan Prep 5 lb. Book of GRE Practice Problems is the gold standard for building quant skills through repetition. Don't skip the explanations — understanding why an answer is right builds transferable skills.

Verbal: The GRE Verbal section is heavily vocabulary-dependent. Use Magoosh's free vocabulary flashcard app (1,000 words) and commit to 15 minutes of review every shift, before or after work. Most nurses with strong reading comprehension score well on Verbal with modest prep.

Practice tests: Take the two free official ETS PowerPrep practice tests. These are the most accurate predictors of your actual score. Take one at the start of your prep to baseline, one near the end to gauge progress.

Timeline: Register for your test date at the 4-month mark. Give yourself a 6-week buffer before the application deadline to allow one retake if needed. GRE scores are valid for 5 years.

What If Your Score Is Low?

You can retake the GRE up to 5 times per year (with a 21-day gap between attempts). Most programs accept the highest score if you send multiple score reports, though verify this with each school. If your score remains below competitive thresholds after two attempts, it's worth evaluating whether applying to test-optional programs is a better strategy than a third retake — time has real opportunity cost.

Summary: Take the GRE if your programs require it — don't take the MCAT unless you already have a recent score. Check each program's current requirements (they change). Prep efficiently with 3 to 4 months of structured study, target 305+ combined, and apply to test-optional programs if your GPA and clinical profile are strong enough to let the application stand without it.
