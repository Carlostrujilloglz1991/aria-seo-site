Do You Need the GRE for CRNA School in 2026?

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

The short answer: it depends on your program list, and the trend is clearly away from the GRE. But "many programs dropped it" is not the same as "no program needs it." Here's how to decide whether to sit the exam.

The honest answer: A large and growing share of CRNA programs no longer require the GRE — many phased it out after 2020–2022. But some programs still require or recommend it, and a handful use it as a tiebreaker or as a way to offset a lower undergraduate GPA. Whether you should take it depends entirely on the specific programs you plan to apply to and how strong the rest of your application is.

Why So Many Programs Dropped the GRE

Two forces pushed CRNA programs away from the GRE. First, the move to doctoral-entry (DNP/DNAP became the standard, with master's-level entry phased out January 1, 2025) prompted programs to rethink admissions criteria from scratch. Second, a wave of research across graduate health professions questioned how well GRE scores predict clinical and academic success — many programs concluded that ICU experience, CCRN, GPA trend, and the interview were stronger predictors than a standardized test. The pandemic-era disruption to testing centers accelerated the shift.

The practical rule: build your program list first, then check each program's current admissions requirements. If even one program you genuinely want requires the GRE, you take it — and once you've taken it, you can submit the score to programs that accept (but don't require) it, where a good score only helps.

What Score Is Competitive?

For programs that consider the GRE, competitive applicants generally aim for around the 50th–70th percentile or higher on each section. In rough scaled-score terms that often means Verbal and Quantitative scores in the 150s (the scale runs 130–170 per section) and an Analytical Writing score of 4.0 or above. Programs that require the GRE sometimes publish a minimum (commonly a combined score around 300, or section minimums near 150). Treat published minimums as floors, not targets — a score comfortably above the minimum reads better, especially if it is offsetting a weaker GPA.

Verify before you assume: CRNA program requirements change year to year, and a program that dropped the GRE can reinstate it (or vice versa). Do not rely on a forum post or an old article — confirm the current requirement on each program's official admissions page during the cycle you are applying. This single check can save you months of unnecessary studying or a disqualified application.

Studying Efficiently as a Working ICU Nurse

If you do need the GRE, you are studying around 12-hour shifts and nights — efficiency matters more than total hours. A focused 6–10 week plan usually beats a sprawling six-month effort that loses momentum.

Quantitative

The GRE quant is high-school-level math (arithmetic, algebra, geometry, data interpretation) dressed up in tricky wording. The challenge for most nurses is not the content but the test format and pacing. Drill official practice problems, learn the question types, and use the on-screen calculator strategically. Most score gains here come from format familiarity, not relearning math.

Verbal

Verbal rewards vocabulary and reading-comprehension speed. Build a focused vocabulary list of high-frequency GRE words, and practice reading dense passages quickly for structure and main idea — a skill that transfers directly from reading clinical literature.

Analytical Writing

The essays are formulaic. Learn the two task types (analyze an issue, analyze an argument), memorize a clean structure, and write three or four timed practice essays. A 4.0–4.5 is very achievable with template practice and is sufficient for nearly all programs.

The Opportunity Cost Question

Every hour spent on the GRE is an hour not spent on the things that move CRNA applications more reliably: earning your CCRN, accumulating high-acuity ICU hours, arranging CRNA shadowing, and drafting a sharp personal statement. If none of your target programs require the GRE, that time is almost always better spent elsewhere. The GRE is a gate to clear when a program puts it in front of you — not a credential worth chasing for its own sake.

Bottom line: Build your program list, check each program's current GRE policy, and take the exam only if a target program requires it or if a strong score would meaningfully offset a weaker GPA. For most applicants with solid GPAs and strong ICU credentials, the GRE is increasingly optional. Put your energy into the CCRN, your ICU hours, and your personal statement first.
