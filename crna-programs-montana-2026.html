CRNA Programs in Montana

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Montana does not have an in-state nurse anesthesia program. If you are a Montana RN who wants to become a CRNA, the path runs through programs in neighboring states — which means your plan should be built around relocation and a smart regional application list from the start.

The landscape: There is currently no COA-accredited CRNA program located in Montana. Montana nurses apply to programs in surrounding states, most commonly across the Mountain West and Pacific Northwest. That is normal — nurse anesthesia is a small, doctoral-level field, and several states have no in-state program at all.

Why Montana Has No Program

Nurse anesthesia education is concentrated and resource-intensive. A program needs a sustained pipeline of high-acuity ICU nurses, faculty who are practicing CRNAs, and a network of clinical sites with enough surgical volume to give every student the cases the national certification exam requires. In a state with Montana's population and hospital footprint, those conditions are hard to assemble — which is why aspiring CRNAs here, like those in several other low-population states, train elsewhere. The good news: rural clinical experience is genuinely valued, and Montana's ICU background can be a strength in a regional application.

Admission Requirements

Wherever you apply, the CRNA template is national. You will need a BSN (or an equivalent qualifying nursing degree) with a competitive GPA, a minimum of one year of full-time adult critical-care experience, an unencumbered RN license, and current BLS and ACLS certification. The ICU requirement is non-negotiable, and competitive applicants typically bring more than the minimum — high-acuity experience and the CCRN credential matter; see the CCRN guide. For the full checklist, review the general CRNA school requirements.

Plan for relocation early. Because every option requires leaving the state, treat relocation as part of the application — not an afterthought. Programs are full-time and clinically intensive, so you generally cannot keep a Montana job while enrolled. Map the cost of living near each target program, confirm whether any offer hybrid or regional clinical placements, and apply to several to widen your odds.
Verify program status before you apply. Accreditation, deadlines, and even whether a new in-state option has appeared can change year to year. Always confirm current details on each university's official site and the COA accredited-programs list before committing time or money to an application.

Application Strategy for Montana

Build a regional list from day one. There is no home-state program to anchor to, so apply broadly across Washington, Idaho, Utah, Oregon, and Colorado. Strengthen your file with the required ICU year (ideally more), the CCRN, and a sharp personal statement that turns your rural and high-acuity experience into a clear story. For realistic expectations, see CRNA acceptance rates.

Bottom Line

Montana has no in-state CRNA program, so the route to the credential runs through neighboring states. That is a logistics challenge, not a dead end: Montana nurses successfully enter programs across the Mountain West and Pacific Northwest every year. Plan for relocation, apply regionally, and lean on your rural ICU experience. For the bigger picture, see how to become a CRNA and the CRNA job outlook.
