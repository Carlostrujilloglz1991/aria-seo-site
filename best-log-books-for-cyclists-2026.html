What Makes a Cycling Log Book Worth Using?

Most general fitness journals fail cyclists because they're built for gym-goers. They have spaces for sets, reps, and weights — not power zones, cadence targets, or TSS accumulation. A good cycling training log is designed around how cyclists actually train: by zone, by power, by aerobic capacity development.

Essential sections for a cycling log: ride date and route, distance and elevation, average power and normalized power, heart rate (average and zones), TSS (Training Stress Score), CTL/ATL trends, weather and road conditions, nutrition consumed during ride, and notes on how legs felt at different intensities.

Annual vs. Daily Logs: Which Is Better?

Annual logs work well for high-volume riders who want to see season-level patterns. Daily logs work better for periodized training with specific microcycle goals. If you're following a structured training plan (FasCat, TrainerRoad, Zwift Academy), a daily log that captures adherence to the plan and RPE relative to prescribed intensity is most useful.

Paper vs. App: The Honest Answer

Use both. Apps capture data you can't manually record (GPS, power curves, elevation profiles). Paper captures what apps can't: perceived exertion nuances, tactical notes ("the QOM section is easier in a higher gear than I expected"), and emotional state.
