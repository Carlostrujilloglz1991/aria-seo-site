Northeastern University CRNA Program: 2026 Overview

Northeastern University in Boston runs a well-regarded nurse anesthesia program housed in its School of Nursing, and it sits in one of the densest academic-medical markets in the country. For ICU nurses in New England — or anyone willing to relocate to the Northeast for training — Northeastern comes up regularly when mapping a path to CRNA. Boston's concentration of major teaching hospitals gives programs like this a deep and varied pool of clinical sites, which is a real advantage during the residency phase. Here's a practical, applicant-focused overview for 2026.

Verify the details before you apply. Program length, tuition, prerequisites, and admissions criteria change year to year and by cohort. Treat everything below as an orientation, not a source of truth — confirm current specifics directly with Northeastern University's nurse anesthesia program office and the official program materials before making decisions.

Program format and degree

Like every US nurse anesthesia program today, Northeastern's culminates in a doctoral credential (a DNP-level nurse anesthesia degree), in line with the national requirement that all new CRNAs graduate at the doctoral level. Programs of this length generally run about 36 months of full-time study — front-loaded didactic coursework in anatomy, physiology, pharmacology, and anesthesia principles, followed by an intensive clinical residency rotating through affiliated hospital and surgical sites. Northeastern is also known institutionally for its experiential-learning model, and its Boston location means clinical placements draw on a rich network of academic medical centers.

Typical prerequisites

Nurse anesthesia admissions requirements are fairly standardized across programs. For Northeastern, expect the core elements common to competitive CRNA schools:

  • An unencumbered RN license and a BSN (or equivalent) from an accredited program.
  • A minimum of one year — realistically more — of recent, full-time critical care experience in an adult ICU where you independently manage vasoactive drips, ventilators, and invasive monitoring.
  • A competitive GPA, with science and any graduate-level coursework weighted heavily; many admitted applicants exceed a 3.5.
  • GRE requirements that vary by cycle — confirm whether the current year requires, recommends, or waives it.
  • CCRN certification, strong clinical references, a personal statement, and a formal interview.
  • CRNA shadowing hours, and sometimes prerequisite graduate chemistry or pathophysiology depending on the year.

Admissions competitiveness

CRNA admission is competitive everywhere, and a Boston program in a prestigious research university is no exception. The levers that move applications are consistent nationally: the acuity of your ICU experience (a high-acuity unit — CVICU, medical/surgical ICU, or trauma — reads stronger than a step-down or PCU), CCRN certification, an upward GPA trend, demonstrated understanding of the CRNA role through shadowing, and a sharp interview that tests clinical reasoning under pressure. New England's applicant pool is deep, so treat every element of the application as a differentiator rather than a box to check.

Cost and financing

Northeastern is a private university, and private nurse anesthesia tuition typically reaches the six figures across the full program once fees, Boston's high cost of living, and the near-impossibility of working during the clinical phase are all accounted for. Most students finance this with a mix of federal graduate loans and savings. The offset is the payoff — CRNAs are among the highest-earning advanced practice nurses, so the return on investment tends to be strong despite the sticker price. Model the numbers honestly with our CRNA cost breakdown before committing, and factor Boston housing into your budget specifically.

The admissions formula is the standard CRNA one — high-acuity adult ICU experience, CCRN, a strong GPA, shadowing, and a strong interview — and the private-school tuition plus Boston cost of living is a real but usually worthwhile investment. Confirm every current detail with the program directly before applying.
