Best CRNA Programs Ranked by Cost 2026

Financial Disclaimer: Tuition figures are approximate and based on publicly available program data. Costs change annually; verify directly with each program's financial aid office before applying.

With CRNA program tuition ranging from $35,000 to $200,000 total, choosing a lower-cost program without sacrificing clinical quality can mean graduating with $80,000 to $120,000 less debt. Given that all NBCRNA-accredited programs produce the same credential and take the same licensure exam, cost should weigh heavily in your decision — especially since CRNA salary is largely determined by geography and practice model, not school prestige.

The prestige-vs-cost reality: No published data shows that CRNA graduates from expensive private programs out-earn graduates of affordable public programs. The NCE pass rate matters; the school's price tag does not appear on your license. Choose the program where you'll thrive academically and clinically, at the lowest cost.

Most Expensive Programs (for Comparison)

Private programs and some university programs charge significantly more — often $120,000 to $180,000+ in total tuition. Examples of higher-cost programs include Georgetown University, Rush University, and various private medical center-affiliated programs. These programs are not necessarily better clinically or in terms of NCE pass rates. They do offer certain clinical site networks and research opportunities that may matter for specific career goals, but for most CRNA students, the ROI comparison does not favor the premium.

Military Pathway — Zero Cost with Service Obligation

The Uniformed Services University and military-sponsored CRNA programs pay all tuition, fees, and a salary during school in exchange for a military service commitment (typically 3 to 5 years as a military CRNA). For nurses willing to serve, this is the most financially favorable CRNA pathway available. Military CRNAs gain exceptional experience rapidly (high-volume trauma settings, austere environments) and graduate with zero educational debt from the CRNA program itself.

How to Evaluate Total Cost — Not Just Tuition

Tuition is only part of the cost equation. A complete comparison should include:

Cost of living during the program

A program in a high-cost-of-living city (New York, San Francisco, Chicago) requires significantly more in living expenses than a program in a mid-size Southern or Midwestern city. A $20,000 tuition difference can be entirely wiped out by 3 years of higher rent. Map the full cost of attendance — tuition plus estimated living expenses — for every program you're seriously considering.

Program length

A 28-month program versus a 36-month program means 8 months less of lost RN income and lower borrowing for living expenses. A 28-month program at a slightly higher tuition rate may still be cheaper overall when lost income is included in the calculation.

NCE first-time pass rate

This is the quality metric that matters most. Program NCE pass rates are publicly available through the NBCRNA. Programs with pass rates below 85% are worth avoiding regardless of cost. Most accredited programs post rates above 90%. A program with a 95% pass rate and low tuition is the value sweet spot.

Decision framework: (1) List all programs you qualify for academically and clinically. (2) For each, calculate total cost = tuition + fees + living expenses × program length. (3) Cross-reference with NCE pass rates (aim for 90%+). (4) Consider geographic location relative to where you want to practice post-graduation (proximity to your intended practice market matters for networking). (5) Choose the program with the best score on this rubric, weighted heavily toward cost.
Online CRNA programs are not accredited for full clinical training: All CRNA programs require in-person clinical rotations. Programs that market themselves as "online" are referring to the didactic (classroom) portion only. Clinical hours must be completed in person at hospital sites. Be skeptical of any program suggesting the full curriculum is remote.
