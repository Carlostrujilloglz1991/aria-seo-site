2. Safe Chemotherapy Handling

Chemotherapy is a hazardous drug — exposure risk to nurses during preparation and administration.
  • PPE: Chemotherapy-rated gloves (double-glove), impermeable chemo gown, eye protection/face shield if splash risk; some agents require respiratory protection
  • Preparation: Only in certified biosafety cabinet (pharmacy); never on the floor or at bedside
  • Administration: Closed system transfer devices (CSTD); check for IV patency BEFORE connecting; verify blood return on peripheral IVs for vesicants
  • Waste: Yellow chemotherapy waste bags and sharps containers; all contaminated materials in chemo waste
  • Spills: Chemo spill kit; wear full PPE; contain spill; notify pharmacy/safety officer
  • Body fluids: Patient's urine, stool, vomit contain active chemotherapy for 48–72+ hours after treatment; wear gloves when handling
  • Pregnancy: Pregnant nurses should not handle or administer chemotherapy

4. Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS)

TLS = massive lysis of tumor cells → release of intracellular contents into bloodstream
Most common with: large tumor burden, hematologic malignancies (Burkitt lymphoma, ALL, AML), highly chemosensitive tumors

Laboratory TLS (Cairo-Bishop criteria) — 3 of 4 within 3 days before to 7 days after chemo:
  • Uric acid ≥8 mg/dL (or 25% increase)
  • Potassium ≥6.0 mEq/L (or 25% increase)
  • Phosphorus ≥4.5 mg/dL (or 25% increase)
  • Calcium ≤7 mg/dL (or 25% decrease) — phosphate binds calcium → hypocalcemia
Clinical TLS: Lab TLS + creatinine ≥1.5× ULN, cardiac arrhythmia, seizures

Management:
  • Aggressive IV hydration: 2–3 L/m²/day NS to promote renal excretion
  • Allopurinol: prophylaxis; reduces uric acid production (xanthine oxidase inhibitor)
  • Rasburicase: enzymatic uric acid breakdown; faster than allopurinol; used for high-risk or established TLS; contraindicated in G6PD deficiency (hemolysis)
  • Treat hyperkalemia: EKG monitoring; kayexalate; insulin/dextrose; dialysis if refractory
  • Treat hyperphosphatemia: phosphate binders (calcium carbonate, sevelamer)
  • Avoid supplemental calcium unless symptomatic hypocalcemia (giving calcium when phosphate high → calcium phosphate precipitation in tissues)

5. Neutropenic Fever (Febrile Neutropenia)

Definition: Single oral temperature ≥38.3°C (101°F) OR sustained temperature ≥38°C (100.4°F) over 1 hour PLUS ANC <500 cells/mm³ (or <1000 and expected to drop to <500)

This is a medical emergency — mortality can be >10% if not treated promptly.

Immediate actions:
  1. Blood cultures × 2 sets (from two separate sites or one peripheral + one central line lumen) BEFORE antibiotics
  2. Start broad-spectrum antibiotics within 60 minutes of presentation (door-to-antibiotic time goal)
  3. First-line: anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam (piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, or carbapenem)
  4. Add vancomycin if: hemodynamic instability, suspected line infection, mucositis, skin/soft tissue infection, MRSA colonization
  5. Complete history and physical; CXR; UA/urine culture; wound cultures if applicable
Neutropenic precautions: Private room; strict hand hygiene; no fresh fruits/vegetables or flowers; limit procedures; HEPA filtration (for prolonged neutropenia); mask for patient when transported

G-CSF (filgrastim/neulasta): Stimulates neutrophil production; given 24–72h after chemo completion; do NOT give within 24h of chemo; shortens duration of neutropenia

NCLEX High-Yield: Oncology
  • Anthracyclines (doxorubicin): cardiotoxicity — monitor ECHO; red/orange urine is normal (not hematuria)
  • Vincristine: severe neurotoxicity; NEVER intrathecal; prophylactic laxatives for constipation
  • Cisplatin: nephrotoxicity — aggressive IV hydration before and after; ototoxicity
  • Paclitaxel: severe hypersensitivity — premedicate with steroids + diphenhydramine + H2-blocker
  • Vesicant extravasation: STOP infusion; LEAVE needle in place; aspirate; notify provider
  • TLS: hyperK, hyperP, hypoCa, hyperuricemia; aggressive hydration; rasburicase
  • Febrile neutropenia: ANC <500 + temp ≥38.3°C; blood cultures THEN antibiotics within 60 min
  • SVC syndrome: upper extremity swelling + facial plethora; NO upper extremity IVs
  • Spinal cord compression: back pain → emergent MRI; dexamethasone immediately; time-sensitive
  • Rasburicase: contraindicated in G6PD deficiency (causes hemolysis)
