Sunoh.ai Scribe: An Honest Review for Nurses and Clinicians (2026)

Updated July 2026  |  More clinical AI reviews →

This article was created with AI assistance and reviewed for clinical accuracy.

Every ambient AI scribe has to solve the same last-mile problem: getting the finished note into the EHR without a clunky copy-paste. Sunoh.ai's whole identity is built around that problem. It comes out of the eClinicalWorks ecosystem, and its biggest selling point is how tightly it plugs into that EHR. If your practice runs on eClinicalWorks, Sunoh is a different value proposition than a standalone tool. Here is an honest look.

What Sunoh does

Sunoh.ai is an ambient AI scribe. It listens to the clinical conversation, separates clinician and patient speech, and generates a structured draft note. The clinician reviews and finalizes it. That is the standard ambient loop. What sets Sunoh apart is distribution and integration: it was built to work natively inside eClinicalWorks and is offered through that ecosystem, so the note flows into the chart where an eCW practice already works rather than living in a separate app.

The value proposition is the category standard — reclaim charting time — with the added promise that the note lands in the right place with minimal friction because the tool and the EHR come from the same world.

Who it's built for

Sunoh's natural buyer is a practice already using eClinicalWorks, from solo offices to larger ambulatory groups. That native fit is the entire pitch: if you are on eCW, adopting Sunoh is far less of an integration project than bolting a third-party scribe onto your workflow. Practices on other EHRs get less of the advantage, since the integration story is the differentiator.

The clinicians who benefit are those doing encounter-based, conversation-driven visits — physicians, NPs, and PAs in ambulatory care. For bedside ICU and floor nurses, the familiar caveat holds: inpatient nursing documentation is flowsheet-heavy — vitals, intake and output, assessments, medication administration — not the narrative visit note ambient scribes generate best. Sunoh fits the outpatient exam room, not the ICU flowsheet.

Pricing and privacy

Sunoh has been notable for a low, usage-based price point — historically marketed on a per-visit basis rather than only a flat monthly seat, which can make it attractive for lower-volume clinicians. Because pricing and plans change, confirm the current numbers through eClinicalWorks or Sunoh directly before budgeting. It advertises HIPAA compliance and describes its handling of captured audio and generated notes. As always, the compliance question that matters most is your organization's policy: patients should be told the encounter is being captured, two-party consent laws still apply in some states, and the business associate agreement governs where data lives. Confirm before you record, and disclose recording to patients.

The through-line in this category is that integration usually decides the winner. Abridge wins large systems partly on its Epic integration; Sunoh wins eClinicalWorks practices on the same logic within a different EHR. If you already live in eCW, that native fit plus per-visit pricing is a strong combination; if you do not, the advantage largely evaporates and a standalone tool like Freed or Heidi may serve you better.

Check policy before you record. Even an EHR-native scribe is still recording the encounter. Using any ambient tool on employer patients without approval can violate hospital policy, state two-party consent laws, and business associate agreement requirements. Is it worth it?

For an ambulatory practice already on eClinicalWorks, Sunoh is one of the lowest-friction ways to add ambient documentation, and the per-visit pricing model can suit clinicians whose volume does not justify a flat monthly seat. For practices on other EHRs, the core advantage — native integration — mostly disappears, so the comparison flattens to any other standalone scribe. As with every AI-generated note, the clinician remains fully responsible for accuracy: ambient tools occasionally mishear or hallucinate, and an unreviewed note is a liability, not a shortcut.

Bottom line: Sunoh.ai is an ambient AI scribe whose defining strength is native integration with the eClinicalWorks EHR and a usage-based, per-visit price. It is an easy add for ambulatory practices already on eCW, less compelling for anyone on a different system. It fits bedside nursing poorly, requires policy approval and patient disclosure to use, and — like every AI scribe — needs every note reviewed before it is signed.
