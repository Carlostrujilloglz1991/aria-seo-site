Duke vs Georgetown CRNA: Which Program Fits Your Application?

Scope note: Educational comparison for ICU nurses planning applications — not admissions advice from either school. Prerequisite lists, tuition, cohort sizes, and test policies change every cycle. Always confirm current specifics directly with Duke's School of Nursing and Georgetown's School of Nursing before you apply.

Duke and Georgetown are two of the most recognizable names an ICU nurse can put on a CRNA application list, and applicants routinely weigh them against each other: both are elite private universities, both award a Doctor of Nursing Practice (DNP), both sit on the East Coast, and both run selective admissions where a strong GPA plus real high-acuity ICU time is the entry ticket. But the two programs train anesthesia residents in structurally different clinical worlds, and that difference — more than prestige — should drive your choice.

The one-sentence difference

Duke concentrates your training inside one quaternary academic fortress; Georgetown distributes it across a dense metro network. Duke's program is anchored at Duke University Hospital in Durham, North Carolina — a Level I trauma, transplant, cardiac, and neuro center where the sickest patients in the region funnel to one campus. Georgetown's strength is the Washington, D.C. metro itself: a clinical web spanning major academic centers, federal and military facilities, and large community systems across the mid-Atlantic. One model gives you depth in a single high-acuity ecosystem; the other gives you breadth across many.

Clinical training: fortress vs network

If you learn best by going deep, Duke's model is hard to beat. Residents live inside a quaternary center with transplant, complex cardiac, neurosurgery, and Level I trauma under one roof, so the case log fills with the hardest anesthetics available anywhere. The tradeoff is homogeneity: one institution's culture, one set of attendings, one way of doing things.

Georgetown's D.C. network flips that tradeoff. You rotate through different hospital systems — academic, federal, military-adjacent, community — and see how anesthesia practice varies across them. That breadth builds adaptability, which matters if you plan to work independent-practice or community settings after graduation. The tradeoff is logistics: multiple sites can mean commuting across one of the country's worst traffic metros, and your experience depends more on which rotations you draw.

Cost: the D.C. tax is real

Both are private universities, so expect tuition in the premium tier either way — neither school competes on price, and you should pull current per-credit numbers from each program directly. The bigger controllable variable is living cost across a full-time, three-year doctoral program in which you cannot realistically work: Durham rent and daily costs run meaningfully below Washington, D.C., where housing alone can add tens of thousands of dollars across the program. Build a full cost-of-attendance budget for both cities before you decide — our CRNA school cost guide and financing guide walk through the math.

Admissions: same bar, different flavor

Neither program is the "easier" admit. Both want an accredited BSN, an unencumbered RN license, a strong overall and science GPA, at least a year (realistically two or more) of adult high-acuity ICU experience, CCRN certification, CRNA shadowing, and a convincing interview. Where they differ is emphasis: Duke's research-forward culture rewards applicants who can speak to evidence-based practice and scholarly work, while Georgetown's Jesuit, whole-person mission rewards applicants who articulate service, ethics, and patient-centered motivation. Read each program's language and mirror it honestly in your essay and interview — our personal statement guide and interview guide cover how.

Quick fit test: Want maximum acuity concentrated in one academic powerhouse, with a research-flavored DNP and a lower cost of living? Lean Duke. Want breadth across many hospital systems in a major metro, with a mission-driven program culture — and you can absorb D.C. living costs? Lean Georgetown. Applying to both is common and reasonable; they draw from the same applicant pool.

What actually decides it for most applicants

In practice, three things settle this choice more often than rankings do. First, geography and support system — three years of doctoral training without income is far more survivable near family or a working spouse. Second, total cost of attendance including rent, not just tuition. Third, interview-day gut check: cohort culture varies year to year, and the program where current residents seem sane and supported is worth more than a brand halo. Both schools will train you into a competent, employable CRNA — certification pass rates and employment are strong at both — so optimize for the three years of your life, not the logo.
