Best Travel Nursing Agencies 2026

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

The agency you sign with affects your pay package, your contract options, and whether your recruiter actually picks up the phone at 11pm before your first shift. Here's what the data and the nurse community say.

The most important thing about choosing an agency: No single agency has the best contracts at every hospital. The nurses who earn the most travel nursing income typically work with 2–3 agencies simultaneously and take contracts with whichever one has the best package for a given position. Exclusivity to one agency is a marketing preference of the agency, not a financial strategy for the nurse.

How to Evaluate a Pay Package

Travel nursing pay is quoted as a blended package: a taxable hourly rate plus non-taxable stipends (housing, meals, incidentals). The stipend portion is only non-taxable if you maintain a legitimate tax home — read the tax home guide before your first contract.

When comparing packages across agencies, use the gross weekly pay as the comparison point: (taxable hourly × hours worked) + weekly stipends. Do not compare hourly rates alone — an agency offering $30/hr taxable + $1,400/week stipends is paying more than one offering $40/hr taxable + $400/week stipends, but the hourly number looks worse.

Ask every agency for a pay package breakdown: taxable base, housing stipend, meals and incidentals stipend, any overtime rate, and any sign-on or completion bonus. Agencies that refuse to break it down are a yellow flag.

What to Ask Your Recruiter Before Signing

Before your first contract with any agency, ask: What is the bill rate for this position (what the hospital pays the agency per hour)? What percentage of the bill rate am I receiving? Is the housing stipend based on GSA rates for that city? Will you help me find housing or just give me a stipend? What happens if the contract is canceled — do I get a cancellation fee? What's your process if I have a problem at the hospital at 2am?

Agencies that answer these questions directly, with numbers, are worth signing with. Agencies that deflect or give vague answers on the bill rate and breakdown are structured to obscure how much margin they're keeping from your package.

The Multi-Agency Strategy

Submit profiles to 3–4 agencies simultaneously. Complete your profile fully with each (credentials, skills checklist, references) so they can submit you quickly when a hot contract opens. Tell each recruiter you're working with multiple agencies — honesty prevents problems when you accept a contract. When you find a contract you want, the agency that submitted you first to that hospital typically gets your contract — coordinate submission timing carefully.

The multi-agency approach gives you leverage: agencies know they're competing for your placements and are more likely to negotiate pay packages and advocate for extensions when they don't have exclusivity.

ICU nurses specifically: MICU, CVICU, and SICU nurses are among the highest-demand travelers in 2026. You have leverage that med-surg and step-down nurses don't. Use it. Push for the top of the pay range, ask for sign-on completion bonuses, and don't accept the first number offered. The agency margin on a critical care contract can be $15–$30/hr over your package — there is room to negotiate.
