How Travel Nurse Pay Actually Works

Travel nurse pay is not a salary. It's a compensation package made up of two fundamentally different buckets — and the distinction between them determines how much you actually keep.

Bucket 1: Taxable Hourly Base Pay

This is your "official" wage — the number that gets reported on your W-2, used to calculate Social Security and Medicare contributions, and typically the only number that counts for things like disability insurance, retirement contributions, and future employment verification. For most travel packages in 2026, this sits between $18 and $28 per hour, depending on specialty and agency. ICU and ED nurses usually see the high end. Med-surg and long-term care sit lower.

This number gets taxed at your full marginal rate. Federal income tax, state income tax (if applicable), Social Security (6.2%), and Medicare (1.45%). By the time it clears withholding, expect to keep roughly 65–75 cents of every dollar here.

Bucket 2: Tax-Free Stipends

This is where travel nursing gets genuinely interesting — and where agencies bury the real value. Stipends are non-taxable reimbursements for housing, meals, and incidentals. They aren't wages. They don't show up on your W-2 as income. They aren't subject to Social Security or Medicare. You keep nearly every dollar.

But — and this is a hard but — they're only tax-free if you qualify. That requires maintaining a legitimate tax home. We cover that in detail in our travel nurse tax guide.

Quick Definition
A compensation package = taxable base hourly rate + tax-free housing stipend + tax-free meals & incidentals stipend (+ sometimes overtime rates, completion bonuses, or travel reimbursements). Most agencies advertise the gross weekly total. They don't subtract taxes for you.

Why the "Package" Number Is Misleading

Here's how agencies market their jobs: "$2,400/week package for ICU RN in Phoenix, AZ." They aren't lying. That $2,400 is real. But the number obscures something critical: not all of it is equal in your pocket.

If your package is $2,400/week and $700 of that is taxable base pay (before withholding), you're not taking home $2,400. You're taking home $2,400 minus the taxes on $700. That difference could be $130–$200 per week — easily $5,000+ over a 13-week contract.

There's a second problem: agencies can legally shift money between the taxable and non-taxable buckets. An agency that wants to look competitive on job boards can inflate the stipend portion (which you see in the headline) while lowering the base pay (which doesn't hurt the posted number). The result looks identical until you look at your retirement savings, disability insurance, or malpractice coverage — all of which tie to your base pay.

The Low-Base Trap
Some agencies post packages with a base rate as low as $14/hour to maximize the tax-free stipend percentage. While this inflates your weekly take-home slightly, it destroys your W-2 income — which affects loan eligibility, Social Security credits, and future income verification. Always get the base hourly rate, not just the weekly package total.

Tax-Free Stipends: Housing, M&IE, and Incidentals

Three types of stipends can be tax-free for travel nurses. All three are governed by IRS rules and the General Services Administration (GSA) per diem rates, which are updated annually. For 2026, you can find the current rates at gsa.gov/travel/plan-book/per-diem-rates.

1. Housing Stipend

This is the largest component of most packages. Agencies typically offer between $700 and $1,400/week for housing, depending on the cost of living in the assignment city. The amount doesn't need to match a specific IRS cap — it just needs to be "reasonable" relative to the actual cost of housing in the area. In San Francisco or New York, $1,400/week is defensible. In rural Kentucky, $1,000/week raises flags.

You can either use agency-provided housing (most agencies offer this) or take the stipend and arrange your own. Taking the stipend and staying with a friend or family member while traveling is technically allowed — but document everything if you do.

2. Meals & Incidental Expenses (M&IE)

The GSA sets M&IE rates by city. In 2026, the standard M&IE rate is $68/day for most U.S. locations, with higher rates in expensive cities (up to $92/day in places like New York City). At the standard rate, that's $476/week — though many agencies pay a flat M&IE stipend regardless of city, which can work in your favor in lower-cost areas.

3. Incidentals

Incidentals are a separate per diem for things like tips, parking fees, and minor expenses. The GSA rate is typically $5/day. Some agencies roll this into the M&IE figure; others list it separately. It's a small number but worth confirming is included in your package.

The IRS Rule on Stipends
Stipends are only tax-free if you are temporarily away from your tax home for business purposes. A travel nurse assignment qualifies — but only if you have a legitimate tax home you're maintaining. No tax home = all stipends become taxable income. This is the #1 audit risk for travel nurses.

States With No Income Tax (And Why It Matters)

Your federal tax burden is the same regardless of where you work. But state income tax varies dramatically — and for travel nurses who choose their assignments, this is a lever you can actually pull.

As of 2026, nine states have no state income tax on wages:

  • Alaska — no state income tax
  • Florida — no state income tax
  • Nevada — no state income tax
  • New Hampshire — no tax on wages (interest/dividends only)
  • South Dakota — no state income tax
  • Tennessee — no state income tax on wages
  • Texas — no state income tax
  • Washington — no state income tax on wages
  • Wyoming — no state income tax

On a package with $792/week in taxable base pay, working in Texas versus California (which tops out at 13.3% state income tax) saves you roughly $80–$120/week — up to $5,600 over a 13-week contract. Over a full year of travel nursing, working strategically in no-income-tax states can be worth $12,000–$18,000 in pure savings without changing your package at all.

State Tax Residency Warning
You pay state income tax where you earn the income, not necessarily where you live. If you work a 13-week contract in California, California will want its share of your wages even if you're a Texas resident. This is one reason many travel nurses choose assignments in no-tax states when salary is equal.

How to Compare Packages: The 3 Numbers That Actually Matter

When you're comparing offers from two different agencies for the same or similar assignments, the gross weekly package number is nearly useless. Here are the three numbers that actually let you compare apples to apples.

Number 1: Taxable Hourly Rate

Ask every recruiter: "What is my taxable base hourly rate?" This is the number your W-2 is built on. It determines your retirement contributions, your Social Security credits, and your eligibility for credit and loans. A good travel nursing package in 2026 has a base rate of at least $18/hour for med-surg and $22–$26/hour for critical care. Below $15/hour is a flag — you're being shifted into a stipend-heavy structure that inflates headline pay but weakens your financial foundation.

Number 2: Total Non-Taxable Stipend Per Week

Add up all the stipend components: housing + M&IE + incidentals. This is money you keep nearly dollar-for-dollar (as long as your tax home is intact). Compare this to the GSA per diem for the city you're going to. If an agency is offering you more in stipends than the GSA rate for that city supports, be cautious — the IRS requires stipends to be "reasonable." Inflated stipends can turn into taxable income if audited.

Number 3: Total Weekly Take-Home (Your Estimate)

Run the math yourself: (taxable base × hours × 0.72) + stipends. The 0.72 factor accounts for approximately 28% going to federal income tax, FICA, and average state income tax. This isn't exact — your actual rate depends on your bracket and assignment state — but it's a reliable comparison tool when you're evaluating multiple offers quickly.

Don't Compare Packages. Compare Take-Home.
Agency A offers $2,450/week with a $14/hr base. Agency B offers $2,300/week with a $24/hr base. Agency B's package is lower on paper but may pay more in take-home because the stipend is structured at a legal GSA-compliant level and your base pay is actually creditable income. Calculate, don't guess.

Red Flags in Travel Nurse Contracts

Most travel nursing agencies are legitimate. But the industry has enough complexity that bad actors can hide a lot in the fine print. These are the flags that should make you slow down or walk away.

  • Taxable base pay below $14/hour. This is usually a sign the agency is inflating your stipend to make the package look competitive while minimizing what they owe on payroll taxes. It hurts you on every W-2-dependent benefit.
  • No written contract before you accept. Verbal packages change. Get everything in writing — base rate, stipends, hours, overtime rate, housing terms, completion bonus conditions — before you give notice at your current job.
  • Completion bonus with impossible clawback terms. A $2,000 completion bonus sounds great until you read that you must complete 100% of scheduled shifts (including days they cancel) or repay the entire bonus. Read the forfeiture language carefully.
  • Housing deposit or lease in your name. If the agency books housing through a third party and puts you on a lease, you may be liable if the agency cancels your contract.
  • "Guaranteed hours" that aren't actually guaranteed. Some contracts include language that lets facilities cancel shifts at the agency's discretion. "Guaranteed hours" clauses must specify that you're paid for canceled shifts to be worth anything.
  • Non-compete clauses restricting your ability to work at the facility directly. These are common and vary in enforceability — but know they exist before you sign.
  • Recruiters who can't explain how the package is structured. A good recruiter should be able to break out your base pay, stipend amounts, and overtime rate without hesitation. If they deflect or "don't have that information," that's a problem.

The Staff Equivalent Salary Calculation

Here's a question worth asking before your first travel assignment: What staff salary would I need to match what I make as a traveler? This is called the staff equivalent salary, and it's the only honest way to compare travel nursing to a permanent position.

The calculation works like this: start with your estimated annual take-home as a traveler. Add back benefits you'd get as staff — health insurance (typically $300–$600/month for a single employee), employer 401(k) match (3–5% of salary), paid time off (typically 10–15 days valued at your hourly rate), and other perks. That total is what a staff job needs to pay to match your travel package.

For the Phoenix ICU example above:

Lost 401(k) employer match (3% on ~$42k W-2)–$1,260
No paid sick days (10 days at $22/hr × 8hrs)–$1,760
True travel value (annualized, take-home)~$93,988

To match $93,988 in take-home as a staff nurse earning a salary with standard benefits, you'd typically need a staff gross salary of roughly $105,000–$115,000, depending on your state and tax situation. In most U.S. markets, that's significantly above median staff nurse pay. Travel nursing's financial edge is real — the point is knowing exactly how big it is, not taking a recruiter's word for it.
