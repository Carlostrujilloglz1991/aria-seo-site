“Opt-out” is one of the most misunderstood terms in nurse anesthesia. It does not mean “CRNAs are fully independent here.” It refers to a specific Medicare billing rule. Understanding the difference matters for where you train, where you work, and what you can earn.

The short version: In 2001, CMS created a rule letting a state's governor opt out of the federal requirement that a physician supervise a CRNA when anesthesia is billed to Medicare. As of 2026, roughly 25 states plus Guam have formally opted out. Opting out changes the Medicare condition of payment — it does not automatically rewrite what a CRNA may legally do under that state's nurse practice act.

What “Opt-Out” Actually Means

Federal Medicare rules historically required physician supervision of CRNAs as a condition of payment. The 2001 CMS rule let governors exempt their state from that single federal requirement, with the stated goal of expanding anesthesia access in rural and underserved areas. That's the entire scope of “opt-out” — it touches the Medicare condition of payment and nothing else.

Two separate legal layers actually govern your day-to-day autonomy:

LayerWhat it controls
Federal Medicare opt-outWhether physician supervision is required to bill Medicare in that state.
State nurse practice actWhat a CRNA may legally do — supervision, collaboration, prescriptive authority.
Facility bylawsWhat a specific hospital or surgery center permits, regardless of state law.

A state can be an opt-out state while individual hospitals still require a supervising or collaborating physician through their own bylaws. Conversely, some non-opt-out states grant broad scope under their practice act. Always read all three layers, not just the opt-out headline.

States That Have Opted Out

The list has grown steadily. States and territories commonly counted as having opted out of the federal supervision requirement include Iowa, Nebraska, Idaho, Minnesota, New Hampshire, New Mexico, Kansas, North Dakota, Washington, Alaska, Oregon, Montana, South Dakota, Wisconsin, California, Colorado, Kentucky, Guam, and others — with more recent additions including Delaware and Wyoming (2023) and Massachusetts (June 2024). The total sits around 25 states plus Guam as of 2026.

Independent / full-practice momentum: A broader set of states — including Alabama, Arizona, Arkansas, Connecticut, Hawaii, Illinois, Maine, Oklahoma, Vermont, West Virginia, and Washington, D.C. — allow varying degrees of independent CRNA practice under their own state law, separate from the Medicare opt-out. Recent 2025 actions included Kansas prescriptive authority (SB 67), West Virginia's cooperative-practice model (SB 810), and a California reaffirmation (AB 876). Because these change frequently, verify the current status with the AANA and your state board before relying on it.

Why It Matters for Your Career

Practice environment affects three things you'll care about:

FactorHow opt-out / scope affects it
AutonomyMore independent settings let CRNAs run their own cases without an anesthesiologist present.
CompensationStates and facilities relying on CRNA-led models often pay strong wages, especially rural and locum roles.
Job availabilityRural and critical-access hospitals in opt-out states frequently depend on CRNAs as sole providers.

If autonomy is a priority, factor practice environment into where you apply and ultimately settle — not just program prestige. A CRNA who wants to run independent practice will have a very different short list than one who prefers a large academic care-team model.

Don't confuse opt-out with “no oversight”: Opt-out is a Medicare billing exemption, not a license to ignore facility bylaws or state collaboration rules. New CRNAs sometimes assume an opt-out state means total independence everywhere in it — the reality is set hospital by hospital. Confirm the specific facility's model before you sign.

Bottom Line

Roughly 25 states plus Guam have opted out of the federal Medicare supervision requirement as of 2026, and an even broader set grant independent practice under state law. But “opt-out” is narrow: it's a billing rule, layered under state practice acts and facility bylaws that ultimately decide your autonomy. Use it as one input when choosing where to train and work — alongside salary, cost of living, and program access — and verify current status directly, because the map keeps changing.
