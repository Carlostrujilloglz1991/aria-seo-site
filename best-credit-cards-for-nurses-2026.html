Best Credit Cards for Nurses 2026

Maximize every dollar you spend on scrubs, meals, CME conferences, and travel nursing flights

The average nurse spends $2,000–$5,000 per year on work-related expenses: scrubs, stethoscopes, CME credits, licensing fees, and travel between facilities. If you're not earning rewards on those purchases, you're leaving money on the table. The right credit card can net you $500–$1,500 in value per year — essentially a free weekend trip or an extra loan payment.

Here are the best credit cards for nurses in 2026, matched to how you actually spend money.

Best for Travel Nurses: Chase Sapphire Preferred

Why it wins for travel nurses

You're already spending on flights, hotels, and restaurant meals between contracts. The Chase Sapphire Preferred gives you 3x points on dining and 2x on all travel — and those points transfer to United, Southwest, Hyatt, and 11 other partners at 1:1. A single sign-up bonus covers a round-trip flight home.

The math for a travel nurse spending pattern

Average monthly travel nurse expenses eligible for rewards:

  • Flights/transportation: $400/month × 2x = 800 points
  • Dining out: $600/month × 3x = 1,800 points
  • Hotel/extended stays: $500/month × 2x = 1,000 points
  • Everything else: $500/month × 1x = 500 points

Monthly total: ~4,100 points = ~$51 in value

Annual value: ~$612 + $750 sign-up bonus = $1,362 year one

Annual fee: $95. Net gain year one: $1,267

Key benefits beyond points: $50 annual hotel credit, primary rental car insurance (huge for travel nurses renting cars at assignments), trip delay coverage, and no foreign transaction fees.

Best No-Fee Card: Chase Freedom Unlimited

If you're paying off debt and not ready for an annual fee card, the Chase Freedom Unlimited is the best no-fee option for nurses. You earn 3% on dining and drugstores (where you buy most of your scrubs and supplies), 5% on travel booked through Chase, and 1.5% on everything else. The $200 sign-up bonus triggers after just $500 in spending — easy to hit in the first month.

Better yet: if you later get the Chase Sapphire Preferred, your Freedom Unlimited points combine with it, and you can transfer them to airline/hotel partners. The two cards work together as a system.

Best for High-Earning Nurses (ICU, CRNA, NP): Capital One Venture X

If you gross more than $100,000 and travel regularly, the Venture X pays for itself. The $395 fee sounds high until you realize the card gives you a $300 annual travel credit and 10,000 anniversary bonus miles (worth ~$100). Net effective fee: ~zero. Everything earns 2x, and you get Priority Pass airport lounge access — relevant when you're doing long-haul flights to assignments or CME conferences.

Best Cash Back (Zero Complexity): Citi Double Cash

For nurses who hate thinking about categories

The Citi Double Cash gives you 2% on every purchase — 1% when you buy, 1% when you pay. No categories, no portals, no thinking. If you spend $3,000/month on this card, you earn $720/year in cash back with zero annual fee.

It's not the highest ceiling, but it has no floor either. For nurses who want passive rewards without optimization, this is the move.

Best for Nurses Who Spend on Food: Amex Gold

Night shift nurses eat out constantly, and the Amex Gold card gives 4x points on dining and 4x on U.S. supermarkets (up to $25,000/year). With a $120 dining credit and $120 Uber Cash included annually, the effective fee on the $250 card is $10/year for nurses who actually use those credits.

The catch: Amex points don't transfer to Chase's partners, so if you're already in the Chase ecosystem, the Gold card competes rather than complements.

What to Avoid

Do not open a store-branded nursing/medical supply card. They offer 10% back on the first purchase then drop to 1–2% with high APRs. If you carry any balance, the interest eliminates all rewards. Use a general rewards card instead.

Never carry a balance on a rewards card. Credit card APRs average 24–27% in 2026. Earning 2–3% while paying 25% interest is a net loss of 22%. These strategies only work if you pay in full every month.

How to Stack Cards (The 2-Card System)

The optimal setup for most nurses uses two cards:

  1. Chase Sapphire Preferred — for dining, travel, and hotel (2–3x)
  2. Chase Freedom Unlimited — for everything else (1.5% base)

Points from both cards pool together in your Chase account. You spend 3x on dinner and 1.5x on scrubs — all points combine and transfer to airline partners. Annual value for a nurse spending $3,000/month: $900–$1,400 depending on redemption method. Total annual fees: $95.

Credit Score Requirements

Most premium rewards cards require a credit score of 700+. The Chase Sapphire Preferred and Capital One Venture X typically want 720+. If you're working on your credit score first, see our nurse credit score guide — the fastest path to 700+ takes 6–12 months with the right strategy.

The Bottom Line

The best credit card for you depends on one thing: how you spend. Travel nurses who eat out and fly frequently should start with the Chase Sapphire Preferred. Staff nurses who want simplicity should grab the Citi Double Cash. No nurse should use a 1% cash back card when 2–4% alternatives exist with no annual fee.

Every dollar you earn rewards on is a dollar compounding toward your financial independence timeline. For a nurse spending $36,000/year on a rewards card at 2%: that's $720 — essentially a month of investment returns doing nothing extra.
