CRNA case log requirements are among the most strictly enforced graduation requirements in any healthcare professional program. The NBCRNA (National Board of Certification and Recertification for Nurse Anesthetists) mandates minimum case numbers across specific categories — cases by patient class, by anesthesia type, by body region, by patient age, by ASA physical status. Fall short in any required category and you cannot sit for boards, regardless of your academic performance.

Getting to graduation with complete, accurate case logs requires a tracking system from day one of clinical rotations. Students who wait to log cases — or who trust that their program's institutional system is capturing everything automatically — sometimes discover gaps that are difficult or impossible to remediate in the final weeks of the program. Don't be that student.

NBCRNA Case Log Requirements (2026)

The NBCRNA requires CRNA students to complete a minimum of 650 cases during their clinical training. Within that total, specific sub-requirements must be met across multiple categories. Key minimums include cases with general anesthesia, regional anesthesia cases, OB anesthesia, pediatric anesthesia, and cases across the range of ASA physical status classifications. The full, authoritative breakdown is in the NBCRNA Standards of Accreditation and should be reviewed directly from the NBCRNA website — requirements are periodically updated.

Your program's clinical coordinator tracks your progress against these requirements. The risk is that you rely entirely on the institutional tracking system without maintaining your own record. If there's a data entry error, a system migration, or a case logged to the wrong student, you want a parallel personal record to reconcile against.

MedHub: The Institutional Standard

MedHub is the most widely used graduate medical and nursing education management system in the U.S. Most CRNA programs have adopted it as the official case logging platform. You'll enter case data — patient demographics, ASA classification, procedure type, anesthesia type, specific techniques used, complications — directly into MedHub after each clinical day.

MedHub's case log features are comprehensive but the interface is not intuitive. Most CRNA students spend the first few clinical weeks figuring out the logging workflow before it becomes routine. The platform generates reports that map your case totals against NBCRNA minimums, so you can see at a glance where you're ahead and where you need to prioritize specific case types.

MedHub access is provided by your program — there's no individual purchase. If your program uses MedHub, you'll receive access at the start of clinical rotations.

New Innovations (E*Value): The Alternative Platform

Some programs use New Innovations (now part of E*Value) rather than MedHub. Functionally similar — web and mobile case logging, report generation, case mapping to accreditation requirements. If your program uses E*Value, the workflow is the same as MedHub; the interface differs. Again, program-provided access at no cost to students.

Personal Case Logs: Why You Need One Anyway

Even if your program uses MedHub or E*Value and you're logging diligently, maintaining a personal parallel case record is valuable for three reasons: backup if the institutional system has errors, portability (you own your own data and can use it for your CV and credentialing after graduation), and learning reflection (notes on cases you found challenging or instructive are useful for boards prep and early career development).

Google Sheets Personal Case Log

The simplest and most accessible parallel tracking system. A well-designed Google Sheet with columns for date, site, procedure, patient ASA class, anesthesia type, techniques used (regional, airway, invasive monitoring), and personal notes provides everything you need for a personal backup record. Google Sheets is free, accessible from any device, and automatically backs up. Several CRNA student communities share template sheets — search r/CRNA for current community resources.

Notion or Airtable for Richer Case Tracking

For CRNA students who want more from their personal case log — searchable notes, tagging by case complexity, linked study resources for case types you found challenging — Notion or Airtable (both free for basic use) offer more structure than a flat spreadsheet. You can build a case database that links each procedure type to your Anki study decks, reference resources, or notes from your preceptor debrief. This type of reflective case log is also excellent material for CRNA program portfolio requirements.

Using ChatGPT to design your personal case log template is worth 20 minutes of your time in week one. Prompt: "Design a comprehensive CRNA student case log template in spreadsheet format. Include columns for: date, clinical site, procedure, patient ASA class, age group, anesthesia type, specific techniques used (intubation type, regional block type, invasive monitoring placed), case complexity (1-5 scale), learning points, and resources to review. Include a summary tab that auto-calculates case counts by category against NBCRNA minimums."

Third-Party CRNA Case Log Apps

Several third-party apps have entered the CRNA case log space, promising easier mobile logging, smarter categorization, and automatic NBCRNA compliance checking. Evaluate these carefully before adopting them for your primary case record:

Verify that the app's case categories and reporting align with current NBCRNA requirements — apps may not update immediately when requirements change. Confirm your program accepts the app's output if you ever need to present documentation from it. Check data privacy and storage — your case log contains de-identified but sensitive clinical training data. And confirm the app will exist throughout your program — startup apps in niche professional markets have a higher-than-average attrition rate.

Important: Your program's institutional system (MedHub, E*Value) is the authoritative record for graduation and certification purposes. Third-party apps and personal spreadsheets are supplementary. Never skip logging in the institutional system because you logged in an app.

Case Log Best Practices

Log every case the day it happens — not at the end of the week, not when you remember. Memory for case details fades quickly, and an uncorrectable logging gap discovered at semester end is a stressful situation that is entirely preventable.

Review your cumulative case totals against NBCRNA minimums monthly. Identify any categories where you're running behind and communicate with your clinical coordinator about scheduling to address gaps. Some case types (pediatric, OB, certain regional techniques) require specific placement and may have limited availability — the earlier you identify a shortage, the more options you have to remediate it.

Tracking tip for regional anesthesia cases: Regional anesthesia cases are among the most strictly defined in NBCRNA requirements. Each specific block type (spinal, epidural, combined spinal-epidural, peripheral nerve block by location) may have its own minimum count. Log each block type separately with the specific technique and anatomic location. A "regional anesthesia" log entry without specificity may not count correctly against the right requirement.
