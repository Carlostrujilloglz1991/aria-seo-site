Nursing Documentation Guide 2026: Legal Charting Standards

"If it wasn't documented, it wasn't done." In legal proceedings, the medical record is the only evidence of care provided. Complete, accurate, timely documentation protects patients and nurses alike.

Core Documentation Principles

The 7 C's of Good Nursing Documentation

  • Clear: Legible, unambiguous; use standard abbreviations only; spell out anything potentially confusing
  • Concise: Relevant, factual; no unnecessary repetition; document findings, not assumptions
  • Complete: All relevant information included; nothing left out that could affect care decisions
  • Correct: Factually accurate; document what you observed and did, not what someone else told you happened (unless documented as such)
  • Contemporaneous: Document as close to the time of the event as possible; document AFTER the care is given (never before)
  • Chronological: Events recorded in time order; late entries clearly labeled
  • Confidential: Protected under HIPAA; never discussed in public areas; proper system access only

What to Document

  • Patient assessment findings (objective and subjective)
  • Nursing interventions performed and patient response
  • Medications administered: drug, dose, route, time, site (injections), patient response
  • Patient/family teaching and their demonstrated understanding
  • Physician/provider notifications: time called, who you spoke with, what you reported, what orders were given
  • Patient refusals and their stated reasons
  • Changes in condition (and who was notified)
  • Informed consent discussions
  • Patient response to treatments (pain reassessment after analgesics, vitals after blood pressure medication)

Charting Formats

SOAP Notes

ComponentWhat It IncludesExample
S — SubjectiveWhat the patient says in their own words; use quotation marks"Patient states, 'My chest feels tight and I can't catch my breath.'"
O — ObjectiveMeasurable, observable findings; vital signs, lab values, physical exam findings"RR 28, SpO2 89% on RA, bilateral crackles, accessory muscle use noted."
A — AssessmentNurse's interpretation of S + O data (nursing diagnosis or clinical impression)"Impaired gas exchange related to fluid accumulation. Worsening respiratory status since 0600."
P — PlanPlanned interventions; what was done and what will be done"Notified Dr. Smith at 0845. New orders received: O2 via NRB at 15 L/min, STAT portable CXR, furosemide 40 mg IV. Repositioned HOB 45°. Patient reassessment in 30 min."

DAR Notes (Focus Charting)

ComponentWhat It Includes
D — DataSubjective and objective information about the focus (problem, concern, or nursing diagnosis)
A — ActionNursing interventions taken in response to the data
R — ResponsePatient's response to the interventions; outcome

Narrative Notes

  • Chronological, story-format documentation
  • Common in older systems or for incident documentation
  • Easier to capture complex, evolving situations
  • Less structured; more prone to missing key elements if nurse is not thorough

PIE Notes

  • P = Problem identified
  • I = Interventions taken
  • E = Evaluation of response
  • Similar to DAR; used in some acute care settings

EHR Best Practices

  • Never share your login credentials — every entry in the EHR is time-stamped and attributed to the logged-in user
  • Log out or lock your session when stepping away from a workstation — even for 30 seconds
  • Copy-paste carefully: "copy-forward" of previous notes without updating is a major source of errors and potential fraud allegations
  • Auto-populate fields with caution: verify the data is accurate for THIS patient on THIS day before signing
  • Use structured fields (checkboxes, dropdown menus) when available — they are more searchable and standardized
  • Free-text in narrative fields only when structured fields don't capture the nuance needed
  • Always complete your documentation before leaving your shift — handoff of undocumented care creates liability

Late Entries and Corrections

Late Entries

Late entries are acceptable and expected — they are NOT an admission of wrongdoing. Failing to document care because you ran out of time is far worse than a properly labeled late entry.
  • Label clearly: "Late entry for [date and time of care], documented [current date and time]"
  • Enter in chronological order of when care occurred, not when you're documenting
  • Document only what you personally recall; do NOT reconstruct from memory beyond what you're confident of
  • Do NOT make late entries that might appear to "fill in" documentation for legal purposes after an adverse event without full factual accuracy

Correcting Errors in Paper Records

  • Draw a single line through the error (so it remains readable)
  • Write "error" above it, initial, and date
  • NEVER use correction fluid (Wite-Out), scribble out, or use multiple lines to obliterate the entry — this looks like tampering
  • Write the correct information next to or after the correction

Correcting Errors in EHR

  • Use the amendment or addendum function — never delete an entry
  • Both the original incorrect entry AND the correction remain visible in the audit trail
  • EHR systems track all modifications with user ID and timestamp — alterations are permanent and discoverable

Documentation Errors to Avoid

ErrorWhy It's a ProblemCorrect Approach
Pre-documenting (signing before giving)If patient refuses or an event prevents administration, the record is falsifiedAlways document AFTER care is provided
Leaving blank spacesBlanks can be filled in later, creating legal risk; looks incompleteDraw a line through blank spaces on paper forms
Using unapproved abbreviationsMisinterpretation; "qd" mistaken for "qid"; JCAHO's "Do Not Use" list appliesUse only facility-approved abbreviations; write out "daily," "right," "left"
Documenting for another nurseFraudulent — documentation implies personal observation and careEach nurse documents their own care; document who gave you the report if necessary
Subjective opinions without data"Patient was uncooperative" — not objective; opens nurse to complaint"Patient refused medication, stating 'I don't want it.' Provider notified."
Not documenting provider notificationWithout documentation, there's no proof the provider was called — nurse has no protectionDocument: time called, who answered, what was reported (SBAR), orders received
Copy-forward without verificationPerpetuates errors from prior notes; daily assessment says "no complaints" when patient is in distressAlways review and individualize copied content before signing

Specific Documentation Requirements

Pain Documentation

  • Assess: location, quality, severity (0–10 scale), onset, radiation, alleviating/aggravating factors
  • Document pain scale used (numeric, FLACC, CPOT for non-verbal)
  • Re-assess AND document after intervention: typically 30–60 minutes after oral meds, 15–30 min after IV meds

IV/Line Documentation

  • Date and time of insertion, site, gauge, number of attempts, patient tolerance
  • Flush after use, daily site assessment (PIV q8h or per policy; central line q shift)
  • Dressing changes: date, appearance of site, patient tolerance

Fall Documentation

  • Circumstances of fall, witnesses, injuries assessed, provider notification, interventions implemented
  • Incident report filed (separate from medical record — do NOT reference incident report in chart)

NCLEX High-Yield Points

  • Document AFTER providing care — pre-documentation is unsafe and potentially fraudulent
  • Late entries are acceptable: label clearly with original time of care + time of documentation
  • Paper error: single line through, write "error," initial and date — NEVER use Wite-Out
  • EHR amendments are permanent and auditable — never try to delete or overwrite
  • Never document care you did not provide; never chart for another nurse
  • Always document provider notification: time, name, what was reported, orders received
  • Do NOT reference the incident report in the medical record — incident reports are for quality improvement, not legal proceedings
  • HIPAA: patient records are confidential; violation = civil and criminal penalties; never discuss in public areas
