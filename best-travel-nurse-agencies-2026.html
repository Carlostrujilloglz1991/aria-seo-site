Best Travel Nurse Agencies 2026: Pay, Benefits, and Reputation

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

The agency you sign with affects your pay package, your job placement options, your contract cancellation protection, and how supported you feel when things go wrong at 2 AM in an unfamiliar hospital. Here's how to evaluate the major agencies and which ones consistently perform.

What Makes an Agency Worth Working With

Before naming agencies, the criteria that separate good from mediocre:

Pay transparency. Will the recruiter show you the full pay breakdown—taxable hourly rate, housing stipend, M&IE stipend, and any bonuses—in writing before you sign? Agencies that obscure the split or won't let you negotiate are red flags.

Guaranteed hours or cancellation pay. If the facility cancels your shift or your contract, do you get paid? The best agencies guarantee a minimum number of hours per contract period and pay you if the facility cuts hours.

Health insurance options. Does coverage start on day one of your first assignment? Is it quality coverage with reasonable premiums? Some agencies offer insurance only after 30+ days.

Recruiter responsiveness. A recruiter who disappears between contracts or doesn't answer after hours when you have a housing crisis is a liability. Ask how recruiter performance is measured and what your escalation path is.

Joint Commission certification. Indicates the agency meets healthcare staffing quality standards. Most large agencies carry this; lack of it is a warning sign.

Contract volume in your specialty. An agency that places 5,000 ICU nurses per year has more facilities and leverage than one with 200. Volume means more placement options in your target markets.
