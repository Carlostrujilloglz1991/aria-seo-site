Best Travel Nurse Agencies 2026: Pay Package Breakdown and Red Flags to Avoid

What nobody tells you before your first contract: The highest bill rate is not always the highest take-home. The difference between a $3,200/week package from Agency A and a $2,900/week package from Agency B can easily flip when you factor in housing stipend structure, compliance requirements, overtime policies, and whether the agency actually fills your hours when census drops. Choose the agency before you chase the number.

How Travel Nurse Pay Packages Actually Work

Travel nursing pay has a structure that is deliberately opaque to people who haven't seen it before. Understanding it lets you compare apples to apples across agencies.

Every travel nurse contract has a "bill rate" — the dollar amount per hour the hospital pays the agency. The agency keeps a margin and passes the rest to you. Your take-home breaks down into two buckets:

  • Taxable wages: The hourly rate you're paid as W-2 income. This is the part that appears on your pay stub, counts toward Social Security, and gets taxed. It should be at or above the applicable minimum wage and ideally above your permanent staff equivalent rate to avoid IRS scrutiny.
  • Non-taxable stipends: Housing, meals, and incidentals. These are not taxed because they are treated as expense reimbursements for maintaining a tax home elsewhere. For a travel nurse who qualifies (maintains a permanent residence in another location), stipends are the most financially powerful part of the package — tax-free income that doesn't increase your marginal rate.

Tax home requirement: The non-taxable stipend benefit only applies if you have a legitimate tax home you maintain and return to between assignments. If you sell your house, move into your parents' garage for free, or have no regular place of business or residence in your home area, you may not qualify — and incorrectly claiming non-taxable stipends is a risk the IRS has been increasing audits around. Consult a tax professional who specializes in travel nursing before your first contract.

What Separates a Good Agency from a Bad One

The staffing agency market for travel nursing is large and fragmented. Large national agencies (AMN Healthcare, Aya Healthcare, Cross Country Nurses, Supplemental Health Care, CHG Healthcare) have more hospital relationships and more open contracts, which matters most when you want to be choosy about location. Smaller boutique agencies sometimes offer better recruiter attention and faster communication but may have limited hospital contracts in your target city.

Negotiating your package: Everything in a travel nursing contract is negotiable before you sign. Recruiters have a spread they work within. Common wins include: higher taxable base (better for retirement contributions and loan payments), higher housing stipend in high-cost-of-living cities, and extension bonuses if you commit to staying past 13 weeks. The leverage point is knowing your market value — run your contract offer through multiple agencies and use the best offer as a baseline when negotiating.

Critical Care Travel Nursing: What the Market Looks Like in 2026

The pandemic-era travel nursing surge peaked in 2022 and rates have since normalized, but ICU travel nursing remains one of the highest-compensated specialties in the travel market. Experienced MICU, SICU, and CVICU nurses with 2+ years of critical care experience and CCRN certification regularly see packages ranging from $2,200 to $3,000 per week in most markets, with California and high-cost markets like New York City, Seattle, and Boston pushing toward $3,400 to $3,800 for experienced travelers.

The most reliable way to benchmark your offer is to compare the taxable hourly rate against the local permanent staff equivalent at the hospital — agencies are required to keep this at least at market rate, and many travelers have begun demanding this information as IRS scrutiny of inflated stipend ratios has increased. If the taxable base wage is suspiciously low (say, $18/hour for an ICU nurse in California) with a very large stipend, that structure may not survive an audit.
