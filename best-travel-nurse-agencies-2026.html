Best Travel Nurse Agencies 2026: Pay, Benefits, and Reputation

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

The agency you sign with affects your pay package, your job placement options, your contract cancellation protection, and how supported you feel when things go wrong at 2 AM in an unfamiliar hospital. Here's how to evaluate the major agencies and which ones consistently perform.

What Makes an Agency Worth Working With

Before naming agencies, the criteria that separate good from mediocre:

Pay transparency. Will the recruiter show you the full pay breakdown—taxable hourly rate, housing stipend, M&IE stipend, and any bonuses—in writing before you sign? Agencies that obscure the split or won't let you negotiate are red flags.

Guaranteed hours or cancellation pay. If the facility cancels your shift or your contract, do you get paid? The best agencies guarantee a minimum number of hours per contract period and pay you if the facility cuts hours.

Health insurance options. Does coverage start on day one of your first assignment? Is it quality coverage with reasonable premiums? Some agencies offer insurance only after 30+ days.

Recruiter responsiveness. A recruiter who disappears between contracts or doesn't answer after hours when you have a housing crisis is a liability. Ask how recruiter performance is measured and what your escalation path is.

Joint Commission certification. Indicates the agency meets healthcare staffing quality standards. Most large agencies carry this; lack of it is a warning sign.

Contract volume in your specialty. An agency that places 5,000 ICU nurses per year has more facilities and leverage than one with 200. Volume means more placement options in your target markets.

Top Agencies for 2026 (By Reputation and Performance)

Aya Healthcare One of the largest travel nursing agencies in the U.S. with a strong technology platform, wide facility network, and generally competitive pay. Known for transparent pay packages and a large inventory of contracts across specialties. Recruiter experience varies—as with all large agencies, you might get an exceptional recruiter or an average one.

AMN Healthcare (Medical Staffing Network) The largest healthcare staffing company in the U.S. Extensive facility network, strong credentialing team, solid benefits package. Pay tends to be slightly below peak market rates, offset by strong contract availability and brand recognition that opens doors at major hospitals.

Trusted Nurse Staffing Rapidly grown into one of the higher-rated agencies among nurses, known for pay transparency and high-touch recruiter relationships. Trusted's nurse-first reputation is unusual in the industry. Strong presence on travel nurse forums and communities.

FlexCare Medical Staffing Mid-sized agency with consistently strong nurse reviews. Known for dedicated single-recruiter relationships rather than a pool system, which means better continuity. Competitive pay packages.

Fusion Medical Staffing Frequently mentioned in travel nurse communities for recruiter quality and pay competitiveness. Strong in allied health as well as nursing. Good option for nurses early in their travel career.

CrossCountry Nursing Large network, strong presence at major academic medical centers and children's hospitals. Benefits package is often highlighted as above-average. Competitive for specialty positions.

NurseFly / Vivian Health Not a staffing agency itself, but a job board that aggregates contracts from multiple agencies and makes pay packages comparable side-by-side. Use this to benchmark pay before accepting any offer.

Agency Comparison Strategy

Don't sign with one agency. Most experienced travel nurses work with two to three agencies simultaneously—one primary recruiter relationship and one or two backups. Here's why:

Different agencies have exclusive contracts with different facilities. An agency that has an exclusive with UCLA Medical Center may be the only way to get that assignment. Working with multiple agencies maximizes your access to contracts.

You can also compare packages for the same facility across agencies (when that's possible) to optimize pay.

How to manage multiple agencies: - Be transparent that you're working with other agencies - Don't let multiple agencies submit your application to the same facility—this creates a conflict and can disqualify you - Tell each recruiter which facilities you're targeting and let them know if another agency is already submitting you there

Red Flags

  • Recruiter won't provide a written pay breakdown before you commit
  • Contract cancellation clause with no guaranteed pay if facility reduces hours
  • Health insurance doesn't start until 30+ days on assignment
  • No overtime pay on hours worked over 36 or 40 in a week
  • Housing provided by the agency with no option for a stipend
  • No 24/7 clinical support line for nurses in the field

Negotiating Your Package

Everything is negotiable, but not everything bends equally:

  • Stipend amounts often have some flex; they're constrained by GSA rates and the facility's bill rate
  • Taxable hourly rate can sometimes be negotiated up with competing offers in hand
  • Start date is usually flexible within reason
  • Completion bonuses are sometimes available, especially for crisis contracts
  • Extension rates should be negotiated before you extend, not after—you lose leverage once you've told the facility you want to stay

Come to recruiter conversations with: your license information ready, a clean resume, your specialty and years of experience summarized, and specific location preferences. Prepared nurses get better placements and faster responses.
