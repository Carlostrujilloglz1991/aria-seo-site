Bacterial Meningitis: The ICU Nurse's Guide

Acute bacterial meningitis is one of the few infections where the clock is measured in minutes and the intervention is stunningly simple: give the antibiotics. Every hour of delay raises the odds of death and permanent brain injury, and the most common reason antibiotics are late is waiting for a test. The ICU nurse's job is to make sure that wait never happens.

The short version: Bacterial meningitis is a medical emergency — antibiotics (and often dexamethasone) go in FAST, before the lumbar puncture if the LP will be delayed. Empiric coverage is broad; dexamethasone is given with or just before the first antibiotic dose to blunt inflammation. The nurse watches for rising intracranial pressure, seizures, and septic shock, and keeps the patient on droplet precautions until an organism like meningococcus is excluded.

The one rule: do not delay antibiotics for the LP

The classic diagnostic test is a lumbar puncture (LP) to sample cerebrospinal fluid. But if the LP will be delayed for any reason — the patient needs a CT first, the procedure is not immediately available, the patient is unstable — the antibiotics do not wait. Blood cultures are drawn, and empiric antibiotics plus steroids are given immediately. A dose of antibiotics given an hour before the LP does not meaningfully change the CSF cell counts and chemistries in that window, and cultures from the CSF may still grow or be supplemented by blood cultures and molecular testing. Nothing about the workup is worth a delay that costs neurons.

Antibiotics before LP, not after, whenever the LP is not immediate. The single biggest modifiable driver of bad outcomes in bacterial meningitis is time-to-antibiotics. If the order is "CT then LP," push to get the drug hanging now.

When does CT come before the LP?

An LP in a patient with dangerously raised intracranial pressure risks precipitating herniation. So certain features prompt a CT head before the LP to look for a mass effect: new focal neurologic deficits, seizures, a significantly depressed or declining level of consciousness, immunocompromise, or papilledema. Crucially, ordering the CT is a reason to give antibiotics first — the imaging is what would otherwise create the delay the rule is designed to prevent.

The dexamethasone timing rule

In many adults with suspected bacterial meningitis, dexamethasone is given to reduce the inflammatory response that drives much of the brain injury and hearing loss (especially with pneumococcus). The timing is specific and easy to get wrong:

Dexamethasone is given with — or ideally just before — the first dose of antibiotics. Its benefit comes from being on board as the antibiotics kill bacteria and release inflammatory debris. If antibiotics have already been running for a while, the window for starting steroids has largely passed. This is why the steroid and the first antibiotic dose are treated as a paired, simultaneous event.

What the CSF tells you

Once the LP is done, the CSF pattern usually distinguishes bacterial from viral meningitis. Nurses do not interpret this in isolation, but recognizing the picture speeds care:

CSF findingBacterialViral (for contrast)
Opening pressureHighNormal / mildly high
White cellsVery high, neutrophil-predominantModestly high, lymphocyte-predominant
GlucoseLow (bacteria consume it)Normal
ProteinHighNormal / mildly high
AppearanceOften cloudy/turbidUsually clear

The most memorable bacterial signature is low CSF glucose with high protein and a flood of neutrophils. A Gram stain and cultures then name the organism and let the team narrow the antibiotics.

The empiric coverage, in plain terms

Because the organism is unknown at first, empiric therapy is broad and age/risk-adjusted — typically a third-generation cephalosporin (like ceftriaxone) plus vancomycin, with ampicillin added at the extremes of age or immunocompromise to cover Listeria, and antivirals if HSV encephalitis is on the table. Your job is fast, correct administration and drawing levels (e.g., vancomycin) as ordered; the narrowing happens once cultures return.

What the nurse is watching for

Meningitis kills through several mechanisms, and each is a nursing surveillance target:

  • Rising intracranial pressure. Inflamed meninges and brain swelling raise ICP — watch for worsening headache, vomiting, declining level of consciousness, pupil changes, or Cushing's triad. Head-of-bed and the same ICP principles from brain-injury care apply.
  • Seizures. Cortical irritation makes seizures common; have rescue benzodiazepines and a plan ready.
  • Septic shock. Especially with meningococcus, the patient can crash into distributive shock — watch the pressure, lactate, and urine output, and look for the petechial/purpuric rash that signals meningococcemia and DIC.
  • SIADH and sodium shifts. Meningitis can drive inappropriate ADH and hyponatremia — track the sodium and fluid balance.

Protect yourself and the unit: droplet precautions

Until Neisseria meningitidis (meningococcus) is excluded, the patient goes on droplet precautions — a mask within close range, ideally for the first 24 hours of effective antibiotics. Meningococcus spreads person-to-person, and close contacts (including staff with unprotected exposure to secretions during intubation or suctioning) may need post-exposure prophylaxis. Flag your exposure early rather than after the fact.

A spreading petechial or purpuric rash plus meningitis signs is meningococcal until proven otherwise. Mask up, expect rapid deterioration into shock and DIC, and make sure exposed staff are identified for prophylaxis.

Bottom line

Bacterial meningitis rewards speed and punishes hesitation. Draw the cultures, but do not let the lumbar puncture — or a CT that must precede it — delay antibiotics. Give dexamethasone with or just before the first antibiotic dose, then shift into critical-care surveillance for rising ICP, seizures, septic shock, and the meningococcal rash, all while keeping the patient (and yourself) behind droplet precautions. The nurse who gets the drug hanging fast changes the outcome more than almost anything downstream.
