Virginia Commonwealth University's Department of Nurse Anesthesia is one of the oldest and most established programs in the country. If you want an academically deep program at a Level I trauma center, here's how VCU's DNAP works and how to get in.

Quick profile: VCU's Department of Nurse Anesthesia sits in Richmond, Virginia and awards a Doctor of Nurse Anesthesia Practice (DNAP). Training is anchored at VCU Medical Center, a Level I trauma and academic hospital in downtown Richmond that gives residents exceptional case volume and acuity. VCU has a long history and a strong national reputation, and it's widely known to recruiters across the mid-Atlantic.

Why VCU

VCU is a flagship. Its nurse anesthesia department is among the most established in the nation, and its home base — VCU Medical Center — is a high-acuity academic center with trauma, cardiac, neuro, and complex surgical services. That translates into deep, varied clinical exposure during residency. For an ICU nurse who wants a program with academic weight, a recognized name, and outstanding clinical training, VCU sits at the top of Virginia's options.

Because prerequisite lists and test policies change, confirm current requirements directly with VCU. As with other flagship programs, the real bar is strong academics plus genuine high-acuity ICU depth — not just the minimum year.

The DNAP Structure

VCU awards a DNAP, consistent with the Council on Accreditation's requirement that all nurse anesthesia programs offer doctoral entry as of January 1, 2025. The program is front-loaded: intensive didactics in physiology, pharmacology, and anesthesia principles early, followed by a progressive clinical residency at VCU Medical Center and affiliated sites, plus a doctoral scholarly project. It's full-time and rigorous — working during the program is generally not realistic.

Apply broadly: VCU is competitive. With only two nurse anesthesia programs in Virginia (VCU and Old Dominion), most strong in-state applicants also apply to programs in North Carolina, Tennessee, Maryland, and Pennsylvania so their cycle doesn't hinge on one or two decisions.

Building a Competitive VCU Application

Successful VCU applicants typically bring one to two-plus years in a genuinely high-acuity ICU — CVICU, trauma/surgical ICU, or a large academic MICU — where they independently manage ventilated patients on multiple vasoactive drips with arterial and central lines. They hold the CCRN, have shadowed CRNAs enough to speak specifically about the work, and can articulate a clear, informed reason for pursuing anesthesia in their essays and interview. Precepting, charge experience, and committee involvement help distinguish otherwise similar candidates.

Cost and Financial Planning

VCU is a public university, so in-state residents can benefit from lower tuition than a private program — a real advantage for Virginia nurses. Estimated total program cost varies with residency status and cohort year, so confirm current figures with the school. Either way, the full-time doctoral structure means little income during the program; most residents finance through federal graduate loans and savings. On the other side, new CRNAs commonly start around $185,000–$215,000, with locum and rural assignments reaching $240,000–$290,000+ — a strong return, provided you plan for a lean stretch in school.

Bottom line: VCU is a historic, academically strong nurse anesthesia program at a Level I trauma center, with public-university tuition that's especially attractive for Virginia residents. Target it with a year or more of high-acuity ICU, a CCRN, real shadowing, and a specific application — and apply to out-of-state programs too, since Virginia has only two options.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is VCU a good CRNA school? Yes — it's one of the oldest and most established nurse anesthesia programs in the country, based at a Level I trauma center. Verify current pass rates on VCU's published outcomes.

DNAP vs DNP — does it matter? Both are practice doctorates that qualify you to sit for the national certification exam. The clinical training and eligibility are equivalent; the credential name differs by program.

How many programs are in Virginia? Two accredited nurse anesthesia programs — VCU in Richmond and Old Dominion in the Hampton Roads area — so most applicants also apply out of state.
