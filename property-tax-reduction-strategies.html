How to Lower Your Property Taxes (Most Homeowners Miss This)

Updated July 2026  |  10-minute read  |  Homeownership Finances

This article was created with AI assistance and reviewed for clinical accuracy.

Property taxes are one of the largest ongoing costs of homeownership — and one of the few that can actually be reduced if you know how the system works. Most homeowners pay whatever assessment they receive, assume it's final, and never question it. That assumption costs them hundreds or thousands of dollars every year.

This guide covers every legitimate strategy for reducing your property tax bill: assessment appeals, exemptions most homeowners miss, and the process for challenging your valuation when comparable homes say yours is overassessed.

How Property Taxes Are Calculated

Your property tax bill has two components: the assessed value of your home (set by your local assessor's office) and the tax rate (mill rate) set by local government entities like school districts, municipalities, and counties. You can't easily change the tax rate. But you can challenge the assessed value — and that's where the opportunity lies.

The formula: Assessed Value × (Assessment Ratio × Mill Rate) = Annual Tax Bill

Example: A home assessed at $420,000 in a county with an 85% assessment ratio and 22 mill rate: $420,000 × 0.85 × 0.022 = $7,854/year in property taxes. Reducing that assessed value by $40,000 saves approximately $748/year — every single year going forward until the next reassessment.

Step 1: Check Your Assessment for Errors

The starting point: Obtain your property record card from the assessor's office. This is a public record and available in most jurisdictions through the assessor's website or by request. It lists the specifics your home was assessed on: square footage, bedroom/bathroom count, lot size, basement type, garage status, and any special features like pools or outbuildings.

Errors are more common than most homeowners expect. Assessors often work from permit records and data going back years, and mistakes persist through multiple reassessment cycles. Common errors that lead to overassessment:

  • Wrong square footage: If your home shows 2,400 sq ft in the record but actual measured square footage is 2,100, you are paying taxes on 300 phantom square feet. Correction alone could reduce the assessed value by $30,000–$60,000 depending on your market, saving $500–$1,200/year.
  • Extra bedroom or bathroom listed: An unfinished bonus room counted as a finished bedroom, a half bath listed as a full bath — these inflate the assessment and are factual errors that should be corrected.
  • Incorrect lot size: Especially common in areas with multiple plat maps or properties that have been subdivided or merged over time.
  • Condition rating errors: Assessors assign condition ratings (excellent, good, average, fair). A home with significant deferred maintenance, an outdated kitchen, or an older HVAC system may warrant a lower condition rating than the record shows.
How to check: Compare the record card against your actual home. Measure rooms if square footage seems off. Check bedroom and bathroom counts. Pull your original purchase documents for lot size. Any discrepancy is grounds for a correction request — often without a formal appeal.

Step 2: Run a Comparable Sales Analysis

Even if the factual details are correct, your assessment may be higher than what comparable homes in your neighborhood sold for. This is the most common — and most winnable — basis for an appeal.

Pull 3–6 comparable recent sales (ideally within 6–12 months, within your neighborhood, with similar size and features) from your county assessor's website or a public tool like Zillow or Redfin. Calculate the implied assessment for each: if comparable homes sold for $380,000 and your home is assessed at $430,000, there's a case to make.

The appeal argument: "The market evidence shows comparable properties at X% lower than my assessment. My property should be assessed at $[amount] based on these sales." Courts and review boards favor specific, documented market data over general arguments that the assessment "feels too high."

Step 3: File a Formal Appeal

Every jurisdiction has a property tax appeal process. The timeline and procedures vary, but the general structure is:

  1. Receive your assessment notice (typically sent annually or after a reassessment cycle)
  2. You have a window to file an appeal — often 30–90 days from the notice date. Missing this window means waiting until the next cycle. Mark this date immediately on your calendar.
  3. File the appeal form with your local Board of Review, Assessment Appeals Board, or equivalent body (varies by state). Most allow online filing.
  4. Submit supporting documentation: comparable sales, property record card with noted errors, photos of condition issues, independent appraisal if available
  5. Attend an informal hearing (in most jurisdictions, the first step is an informal meeting with the assessor's office where the majority of cases resolve without further escalation)
  6. If not resolved informally, proceed to a formal board hearing

Success rates on well-documented appeals are substantial: studies suggest 40–60% of formally appealed assessments result in some reduction. Even a modest reduction of $15,000–$25,000 in assessed value can save $300–$700/year in a mid-rate jurisdiction — and that savings repeats every year until the next reassessment.

Appeal volume is low: In most jurisdictions, fewer than 5% of homeowners ever appeal their assessment. Of those who do, a majority receive some reduction. The barrier is not merit — it's that most homeowners don't know the process exists or assume it's more complicated than it is.

The Homestead Exemption — Apply If You Haven't

The homestead exemption is available to primary residence owners in nearly every state and is one of the most commonly unclaimed exemptions. In Florida, it provides up to $50,000 in assessed value reduction plus the Save Our Homes cap that limits assessment increases to 3% annually. In Texas, it provides a $100,000 reduction off the assessed value for school district taxes. In California, it protects $300,000 of home value from forced sale. Benefits vary enormously by state — but the filing requirement is usually a one-time form with your county assessor's office. If you haven't filed, do it now.

Senior Freeze Programs

Many states have programs that lock the assessed value or cap the tax payment for seniors meeting age and income requirements. Illinois's Senior Citizens Assessment Freeze, for example, freezes the equalized assessed value for qualifying seniors — meaning their tax bill doesn't rise as property values increase. Some programs require annual recertification. Check your state's assessor website or senior services agency for current requirements.

Step 5: Don't Add Assessed Value Unnecessarily

This is the prevention strategy: before pulling a permit for any major improvement, understand how it will affect your assessment. Adding a pool adds $30,000–$80,000 in assessed value in most markets, increasing annual taxes by $600–$1,600. A permitted addition increases the assessed value proportionally.

Some improvements — finishing a basement, adding a garage, completing an attic conversion — trigger a reassessment in many jurisdictions when the permit is pulled and inspection completed. This isn't a reason to avoid improvements, but it's a cost to factor into the project's total budget.

Unpermitted work caveat: Suggesting you skip permits to avoid assessment changes is not advice worth taking — unpermitted improvements create insurance voids, inspection problems at resale, and liability exposure. The assessment increase is a known, manageable cost. Unpermitted improvements are unknown, unmanageable risks.

Hiring a Property Tax Consultant

For complex appeals or high-value properties, professional property tax consultants and some real estate attorneys specialize in assessment appeals. Most work on contingency — they receive 25–50% of the first year's tax savings if successful, with no fee if the appeal fails.

This model makes professional help accessible with no upfront cost. On a $12,000 annual tax bill with a potential $2,000/year reduction, a consultant might charge $500–$1,000 for the first year's savings. Years two through the next reassessment, you keep the full reduction. For large commercial properties or high-value residential homes, this can be worth thousands annually.
