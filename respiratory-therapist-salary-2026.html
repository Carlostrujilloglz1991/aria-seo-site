Respiratory Therapist Salary 2026

RRT pay by state, specialty differentials, the CRNA path for RTs, and passive income strategies for shift workers

Respiratory therapists (RTs) are among the most financially underestimated professionals in critical care. The RRT credential combined with ICU experience puts respiratory therapists in a unique position: they are the only non-nursing clinicians who can apply directly to CRNA programs at many institutions, bypassing the requirement for a nursing degree.

Travel Respiratory Therapy: The Earnings Multiplier

Travel RT pay mirrors travel nursing structure: taxable base + non-taxable housing and meal stipends. A travel RT in 2026 working a California ICU assignment can clear $2,200–$3,000/week total package — $95,000–$145,000 annually. The rules that apply to travel nurses (tax home, duplicate expenses, GSA compliance) apply identically to travel RTs.

For RTs in low-cost-of-living states, even one California contract per year significantly changes annual income. See our travel stipend guide for the mechanics — the same rules apply.

The RRT-to-CRNA Path

This is the most powerful career leverage available to respiratory therapists and is widely underutilized.

Most people assume CRNA school requires being an RN first. This is wrong in many programs. Approximately 20–25 CRNA programs in the United States accept applicants with an RRT plus critical care experience, bypassing the nursing school requirement entirely. These include programs specifically designed for respiratory therapy to anesthesia transitions.

RRT → RN → CRNA (traditional path)

Time: 8–10 additional years
Cost: $60k–$120k
Required: RN school + 2 years ICU as RN
Most common path, but not the only one

RRT → CRNA (direct path, select programs)

Time: 4–5 additional years
Cost: CRNA tuition only
Required: 2+ years critical care RT experience
Available at ~20–25 programs nationwide

The salary difference between an ICU RT career ($70k–$90k) and a CRNA career ($195k–$230k) is $100,000–$140,000 per year. Over a 20-year career post-CRNA, that's $2,000,000–$2,800,000 in additional earnings. The 3-year CRNA program cost of $80,000–$180,000 breaks even in 12–18 months at CRNA income.

Programs that have historically accepted RRTs include some affiliated with Baylor College of Medicine, Georgetown, and a number of state university programs. Research current requirements carefully — program policies change.

RT Financial Planning: The Same Principles Apply

The financial planning principles from the nurse guides on this site apply equally to respiratory therapists — the income level is different but the strategy is identical:

  • Capture your 403b or 401k employer match before any other investing
  • Build a 4–6 month emergency fund as a travel RT (income stops when your contract ends)
  • Target 20%+ savings rate during high-income years
  • The tax home rules for travel RT are identical to travel nursing
  • The CRNA path, if you pursue it, changes your income trajectory more than any other single decision

Bottom Line

The median respiratory therapist earns $67,000 nationally, with top-end earners in critical care reaching $88,000–$95,000. Travel RT packages can reach $140,000+ with stipends. But the most financially significant option available to experienced critical care RTs is the CRNA school path — it represents the highest-leverage income decision in the profession, with a payoff that exceeds every other career move by an order of magnitude.
