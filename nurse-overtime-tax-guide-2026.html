Nurse Overtime Tax Guide 2026 — Is Extra Shifts Worth It After Taxes?

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

Last updated: July 2026 | Reading time: 7 min

"I worked overtime all month and barely saw the difference in my paycheck." This is one of the most common complaints from nurses — and it's based on a misunderstanding of how tax withholding works on overtime paychecks. The good news: overtime is taxed at exactly the same rate as regular income. The confusing part: your paycheck withholding is calculated differently in a higher-earning period, making it look like overtime is more heavily taxed than it is.

The Key Myth: "Overtime Is Taxed at a Higher Rate"

Overtime income is taxed at your marginal federal rate — the rate that applies to additional income above your current income level. This is the same way all income is taxed. There is no special "overtime tax bracket." The federal tax code does not distinguish between regular hours and overtime hours.

What's true: overtime pay, because it pushes your income higher in a specific pay period, may cause your employer to withhold more federal income tax from that check — because the payroll software annualizes your pay period income and projects a higher annual salary. But at the end of the year when you file, you receive a credit for any over-withholding as a refund. The actual tax you owe on overtime earnings is identical to what you'd owe on the same dollars earned through regular hours.

How Overtime Pay Is Actually Taxed

Example: Staff nurse, $65,000 base salary, picks up 8 hours of overtime in a biweekly pay period at 1.5x her regular rate ($35/hr x 1.5 = $52.50/hr overtime).

The overtime shift paid $420 gross, but $302 net hit the bank account — a 72% take-home rate. That looks like a big haircut, but consider: if the nurse had worked those hours at any rate (regular or overtime), the effective tax rate would be the same. The "lost" $118 isn't gone — part of it is federal income tax she'd pay on any equivalent income, part is FICA, and any over-withholding comes back as a tax refund in April.

Is Overtime Worth It After Taxes?

Yes — with appropriate context. The real question isn't whether overtime is "worth it" after taxes (it almost always is, mathematically), but whether it's worth it for your time and energy.

When Overtime Pushes You Into a Higher Bracket

A nurse's marginal rate can genuinely change if overtime income pushes their annual income into the next federal bracket. The 2026 federal brackets (single filer):

Taxable IncomeFederal Rate
$0 – $11,60010%
$11,601 – $47,15012%
$47,151 – $100,52522%
$100,526 – $191,95024%
$191,951 – $243,72532%

A nurse earning $95,000 base (in the 22% bracket) who earns an additional $10,000 in overtime may push $5,525 of that overtime into the 24% bracket. The effective tax difference on that portion: 2% more = $111 extra in federal taxes on $5,525. Not a significant amount — but this is the mechanism by which overtime "costs more" in taxes at the bracket boundary.

The Tax Deferral Strategy for High Overtime Earners

If you consistently earn significant overtime — $20,000–$40,000/year above your base — consider increasing your 403(b) or 401(k) contribution. Pre-tax retirement contributions reduce your taxable income dollar-for-dollar, potentially keeping you in a lower bracket and reducing the tax impact of high-overtime years. A nurse earning $100,000+ who maxes their 403(b) ($23,000 in 2026) reduces taxable income by $23,000 — keeping more of the overtime in lower-bracket territory.

The real cost of overtime: The tax cost of overtime is marginal. The real cost is physiological — nursing already requires significant physical and mental energy at 36 hours/week. Working 48–60 hour weeks consistently has well-documented impacts on burnout rate, medical error risk, and long-term health. Before picking up many overtime shifts for financial goals, consider whether travel nursing, per diem, or side hustle income achieves the same financial outcome with a better work-life structure.
