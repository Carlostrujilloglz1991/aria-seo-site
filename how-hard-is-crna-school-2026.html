How Hard Is CRNA School?

Short answer: it's one of the more demanding graduate programs in all of healthcare — but the difficulty is front-loaded into getting in and surviving the schedule, not in a hidden wall that washes most people out. The data tells a more reassuring story than the reputation suggests. Here's what the numbers actually say, and where the real pressure lives.

The headline data: Across accredited programs, the average attrition rate sits around 4% (individual programs range from 0% to roughly 28%, and more than a third report zero). First-time pass rates on the National Certification Exam (NCE) average about 85% year to year, with an 80% minimum first-time pass standard every program must maintain. In other words, the people who get admitted overwhelmingly finish and pass.

What "Hard" Actually Means Here

CRNA school is hard in three specific, measurable ways — and easier than its reputation in one important way.

DimensionReality
Getting inThe hardest part. Small cohorts, competitive GPAs, years of ICU experience, and low acceptance rates.
The scheduleBrutal. ~36 months of full-time didactics plus clinical call, with little ability to work for income.
The materialDense but learnable. Pharmacology, physiology, and chemistry at a level most nurses haven't seen.

The Admissions Wall Is the Real Difficulty

The single hardest thing about becoming a CRNA is getting accepted. Programs admit small cohorts, acceptance rates run low, and applicants typically arrive with two to five years of high-acuity ICU experience, a strong GPA, CCRN certification, and shadowing hours. The bottleneck is structural: the supply of seats is capped by accreditation and faculty, while demand from qualified nurses is high. The Schedule Is the Second Wall

Once admitted, the difficulty shifts from competition to endurance. As of January 1, 2025, entry-level nurse anesthesia education is doctoral, which means roughly 36 months of largely full-time study. The clinical years bring early mornings, overnight call, and constant evaluation in the operating room. Most programs strongly discourage or outright prohibit working during school, so the challenge is as much financial and logistical as academic. The Material: Dense, But Not Mysterious

The coursework — advanced pharmacology, physiology, chemistry, pathophysiology, and anesthesia principles — is genuinely harder than a typical BSN curriculum. But it's structured, sequenced, and taught by people who want you to pass. Nurses who struggle usually struggle with the volume and pace rather than the concepts. Strong time management, a study system, and the discipline to keep up day by day matter more than raw intelligence.

The reassuring number: A ~4% average attrition rate means roughly 24 of every 25 admitted students finish. Programs invest heavily in remediation and support because their accreditation depends on graduation and pass rates. Getting admitted is the gatekeeper; the program is designed to carry you across the finish line, not to fail you.

The Certification Exam

The path ends with the National Certification Exam administered by the NBCRNA. First-time pass rates average around 85% nationally, and every accredited program must keep its first-time pass rate at or above 80% to stay in good standing. Programs build their entire clinical and didactic sequence around preparing you for this exam, which is part of why the failure rate is comparatively low for a credential this rigorous.

So Is It Worth the Difficulty?

For most who finish, yes. CRNAs earn a mean wage north of $200,000 with strong projected job growth, which is what makes the front-loaded pain rational. The hard part is a defined, finite gauntlet: get in, survive 36 months, pass one exam. For the demand side of the equation, see the CRNA job outlook.

Don't underestimate the income gap: The academic difficulty is survivable for almost everyone admitted — the more common derailment is financial. Three years of reduced income plus tuition is the part people fail to plan for. Build the cash-flow plan before you accept a seat, not after.

Bottom Line

CRNA school is hard, but in predictable ways: the admissions competition is fierce, the 36-month doctoral schedule is exhausting, and the material is dense. What it is not is a meat grinder — average attrition near 4% and NCE first-time pass rates around 85% mean that admitted students overwhelmingly finish and certify. If you can get in and absorb the financial gap, the difficulty is a finite climb with a six-figure credential at the top.
