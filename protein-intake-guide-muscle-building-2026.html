Protein Intake for Muscle Building: What the Research Actually Says

Protein recommendations for muscle building have been pushed higher by supplement marketing than the research actually supports. The science is more nuanced than "eat 1 gram per pound of bodyweight" and more permissive than you've probably been told. Here's what the evidence actually says.

The evidence-based range: A 2022 meta-analysis of 74 studies in the British Journal of Sports Medicine found that protein intakes above 0.73 grams per pound (1.6 g/kg) of bodyweight produced no additional muscle gain in most adults. The sweet spot for most people is 0.7-1.0 grams per pound of body weight daily.

Calculating your actual target

Use lean body mass if you know it, or just your total bodyweight if you don't. For a 170-pound person aiming to build muscle:

  • Minimum effective: 0.7 × 170 = 119 grams/day
  • Optimal target: 0.85 × 170 = 145 grams/day
  • Upper range (diminishing returns above this): 1.0 × 170 = 170 grams/day

The difference between 119g and 170g in muscle building outcomes over a year is smaller than most people think. The difference between 119g and 60g (what many people actually eat) is large. Hitting the minimum consistently beats hitting the maximum inconsistently.

Does protein timing matter?

The "anabolic window" myth (you must eat protein within 30 minutes post-workout) has been largely debunked. A 2013 meta-analysis found that protein timing had a minor effect on strength and hypertrophy when total daily protein was controlled. Total daily intake matters far more than when you eat it.

That said, spreading protein across 3-4 meals maximizes muscle protein synthesis better than eating it all in one or two meals. Each protein feeding stimulates MPS for roughly 3 hours. Four 30-35g servings throughout the day is modestly better than two 60-70g servings.

Plant-based protein: what's different

Most plant proteins are "incomplete" (lacking one or more essential amino acids) and have lower leucine content than animal proteins. Leucine is the amino acid most directly responsible for triggering muscle protein synthesis. Compensate by eating slightly more total protein (1.0-1.1g per pound instead of 0.7-0.85g) and combining protein sources across the day. Soy, quinoa, and hemp seed are the most complete plant proteins and are closest to animal protein in amino acid profile.

Protein supplements: useful but not magic. Whey, casein, and plant-based protein powders are food — they're not superior to whole food protein, just convenient when hitting targets from whole foods alone is difficult. If you can hit your protein target from chicken, eggs, yogurt, and legumes, you don't need a supplement. If you can't, powder is a practical bridge.

The protein-fat loss connection

High protein intake helps with fat loss as well as muscle building. Protein has the highest thermic effect of any macronutrient (your body burns roughly 20-30% of protein calories digesting protein itself). It also produces the strongest satiety signal. People on higher-protein diets spontaneously eat less without calorie counting. Maintaining muscle during a caloric deficit requires protein intakes at the higher end of the range (0.9-1.2g per pound) because muscle is being used as energy if protein and resistance training aren't high.
