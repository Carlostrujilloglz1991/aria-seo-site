In-Depth Reviews

1. Marino's The ICU Book, 4th Edition Top Pick

by Paul L. Marino, MD, PhD | Lippincott Williams & Wilkins | ~750 pages

This is the closest thing the ICU world has to a Bible. Marino explains the "why" behind every intervention — hemodynamic monitoring, fluid management, ventilator management, sepsis, acid-base — with the kind of pathophysiological rigor that builds lasting clinical judgment. Unlike nursing-specific texts, Marino writes from a physician's perspective but is accessible to advanced practice nurses and experienced RNs who want to understand rather than just follow protocols.

Who should buy it: ICU nurses with 6+ months of experience who want to understand what's actually happening with their patients, not just what to do. Also essential for CCRN candidates who want depth beyond bullet-point review books.

✅ Pros

  • Unmatched pathophysiology depth
  • Evidence-based, frequently updated
  • Explains the "why" behind protocols
  • Covers hemodynamics, vents, fluids, meds

❌ Cons

  • Heavy and not bedside-friendly
  • Written for physicians — density can be high
  • Not structured for certification exam prep

Price range: $70–$90 (check for used editions — 3rd edition is still excellent)

2. Critical Care Nursing: Diagnosis and Management, 9th Edition Nursing-Specific

by Linda D. Urden, Kathleen M. Stacy | Elsevier | ~1,100 pages

Urden and Stacy is the nursing-perspective counterpart to Marino. Where Marino explains the medicine, Urden/Stacy explains nursing assessment, nursing diagnoses, collaborative care, and patient/family education — the full scope of what the RN actually manages. The 9th edition integrates updated sepsis guidelines (Surviving Sepsis 2021), COVID-era respiratory protocols, and expanded content on ECMO and CRRT nursing care.

Who should buy it: New ICU nurses (0–2 years) who need a comprehensive reference that speaks their language. Also a strong choice for CCRN candidates who want nursing-framework alignment.

✅ Pros

  • Nursing-diagnosis framework throughout
  • Updated for current guidelines
  • Excellent ECMO/CRRT nursing content
  • Good patient education resources

❌ Cons

  • Over 1,100 pages — not a quick reference
  • Expensive ($80–$110 new)
  • Some sections feel textbook-dense

Price range: $80–$110 new; $40–$60 used. Older editions (7th/8th) still very usable.

3. AACN Core Curriculum for Progressive and Critical Care Nursing, 8th Edition CCRN Essential

by AACN | Elsevier | ~900 pages

If you're preparing for the CCRN or PCCN certification exam, this book is the official content outline in textbook form. The AACN wrote it, which means it maps perfectly to what's actually tested. It's organized by body system, covers cardiac, pulmonary, neuro, renal, GI, endocrine, hematology, and multisystem — every domain on the blueprint. That said, it reads like a study guide, not a narrative text. It's better used alongside Marino or Urden/Stacy for deeper understanding.

Who should buy it: Any nurse sitting for CCRN within the next 12 months. Essential. Not a substitute for a full ICU reference for everyday clinical use.

✅ Pros

  • Official AACN content — aligns to exam blueprint
  • Comprehensive body-system coverage
  • Authoritative and evidence-based

❌ Cons

  • Dense reading — not narrative-friendly
  • No practice questions (get a separate Q-bank)
  • Pricey for a reference that becomes less useful post-exam

Price range: $85–$110 new

4. Pocket ICU, 2nd Edition Bedside Favorite

by Daniel R. Gorman, MD | Jones & Bartlett | ~350 pages

Pocket ICU is what you grab when your patient's vasopressor is running and you need to double-check the norepinephrine titration range right now, not in 90 seconds after navigating a textbook. It's a true pocket reference — fits in a scrub pocket, covers drug dosing, ventilator settings, hemodynamic parameters, common lab values, and rapid overview of ICU syndromes in a format that's actually usable mid-shift. No other book on this list accomplishes this.

Who should buy it: Every ICU nurse, regardless of experience level. Especially valuable in the first two years when you haven't yet internalized every vasoactive drug range.

✅ Pros

  • Fast to navigate under pressure
  • Affordable ($35–$50)

5. The Little ICU Book, 2nd Edition

by Paul L. Marino, MD | Lippincott Williams & Wilkins | ~550 pages

Marino condensed his flagship ICU Book into a more accessible format for students, new nurses, and anyone who finds the full ICU Book intimidating. The pathophysiology explanations are still excellent, but the language is more approachable. Think of it as Marino Light — same rigor, lower barrier to entry. A great starting point before graduating to the full ICU Book.

Who should buy it: Nursing students in ICU rotations, new grads transitioning to critical care, or experienced floor nurses considering an ICU move.

✅ Pros

  • More accessible than the full ICU Book
  • Same Marino pathophysiology rigor
  • Good stepping stone for new ICU nurses

❌ Cons

  • Less comprehensive than the full edition
  • Not ideal for experienced nurses

Price range: $50–$65
