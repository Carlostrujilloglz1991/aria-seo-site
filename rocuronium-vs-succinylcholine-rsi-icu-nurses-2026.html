Rocuronium vs Succinylcholine for RSI 2026 — Choosing the Paralytic

In rapid sequence intubation the paralytic buys the team a still, relaxed airway for the few seconds that matter most. Two drugs dominate that slot: succinylcholine, the fast, short-acting classic, and rocuronium, the safer-in-many-ICU-patients alternative. Knowing why the team picks one over the other — and what each commits you to afterward — is core ICU knowledge.

The short version: Both produce excellent intubating conditions in about a minute. Succinylcholine (a depolarizing agent) wears off in minutes but carries hard contraindications — hyperkalemia and conditions that make it dangerous, malignant hyperthermia, and more. Rocuronium (a non-depolarizing agent) is clean of those risks but paralyzes for roughly 45 minutes to over an hour, which means the patient is committed to the airway — a critical safety consideration if intubation fails. The availability of sugammadex to rapidly reverse rocuronium has shifted many units toward it.

How they differ mechanically

Succinylcholine is a depolarizing blocker: it mimics acetylcholine, briefly activating the muscle (you see fasciculations) and then holding the junction open so the muscle can't re-fire. It is broken down by plasma pseudocholinesterase, which is why its effect is so short. Rocuronium is a non-depolarizing blocker: it competitively blocks the acetylcholine receptor without activating it — no fasciculations — and its effect ends as the drug redistributes and is cleared, mostly hepatically.

Onset and duration — the practical trade-off

Both give intubating conditions in roughly 45–60 seconds when rocuronium is dosed at the higher RSI dose. The decisive difference is the back end. Succinylcholine's paralysis fades in about 5–10 minutes, so a patient can begin breathing again relatively quickly if the airway can't be secured. Rocuronium at RSI dosing keeps the patient paralyzed for 45–60+ minutes. That long tail is the single most important thing to understand: with rocuronium and no reversal agent immediately available, a failed intubation means an apneic, fully paralyzed patient for a long time — which is exactly why sugammadex being on hand changes the calculus.

Why succinylcholine gets avoided: the contraindication list

Succinylcholine can trigger lethal hyperkalemia in the wrong patient. Its depolarizing action releases potassium from muscle. In patients with upregulated receptors — burns (after ~24–48h), crush and denervation injuries, prolonged immobility/ICU-acquired weakness, spinal cord injury, some neuromuscular diseases — that release can be massive and cause cardiac arrest. It is also contraindicated in known or suspected hyperkalemia, in personal or family history of malignant hyperthermia, and in pseudocholinesterase deficiency (which causes prolonged paralysis).

Other cautions: succinylcholine transiently raises potassium even in normal patients, can cause bradycardia (especially in children or with repeat dosing), raises intraocular and intracranial pressure modestly, and the fasciculations produce post-procedure myalgias. This long hazard list is precisely why many ICUs now default to rocuronium for the crashing, often hyperkalemia-prone critically ill patient.

Rocuronium's trade-offs

Rocuronium sidesteps the hyperkalemia and malignant-hyperthermia problems entirely, which is its main appeal in the ICU. Its costs are the long duration already discussed and the fact that it provides no sedation or analgesia — a point that applies to succinylcholine too. A paralyzed patient who is under-sedated is fully aware and unable to move or signal. Ensuring adequate sedation and analgesia alongside any paralytic is a nursing safety imperative, not an afterthought.

Reversal

Succinylcholine needs no reversal — you wait it out. Rocuronium (and vecuronium) can be reversed with sugammadex, which encapsulates the drug and can reverse even a deep block within minutes; neostigmine reverses only a shallow block and works more slowly. The ready availability of sugammadex is a major reason rocuronium has become a comfortable RSI choice: the "committed airway" concern is mitigated when a rescue reversal is minutes away.

Bedside role, in short: Confirm sedation/analgesia are ordered and given with any paralytic — paralysis without sedation is awareness without escape. Before succinylcholine, know the potassium and the contraindication list; before rocuronium, know the airway is committed and where the sugammadex is. Have suction, rescue airway, and a pressor ready, and monitor continuous SpO2, capnography, and hemodynamics through the peri-intubation window.
