Cat Health Monitoring: Catching Problems Before They Become Emergencies

Cats are obligate predators who evolved to hide illness and weakness. In the wild, showing vulnerability invites predation. In your home, this means a sick cat will often act normally until a disease is significantly advanced. Active monitoring — noticing changes before they become obvious — is the most effective tool cat owners have.

The baseline rule: You can only notice a change if you know the normal. Spend two weeks documenting your healthy cat's baseline: how much they eat, how much they drink, how often they use the litter box, their typical energy level, coat appearance, and weight. Changes from that baseline are what you're watching for.

Daily observations (takes 2 minutes)

Is your cat eating? Complete food refusal for more than 24-36 hours in a cat is always a reason to call the vet — cats who don't eat develop hepatic lipidosis (fatty liver disease) faster than any other domestic animal.

Is your cat drinking? Most cats drink less than you'd expect (they evolved to get moisture from prey). Increased drinking (polydipsia) is one of the earliest signs of kidney disease, diabetes, and hyperthyroidism — the three most common serious feline diseases. If you notice your cat visiting the water bowl more, note it with a date.

Did they use the litter box? Monitoring output is most easily done with a single litter box (rather than multiple). A cat that hasn't urinated in 24+ hours may have a urinary blockage — a life-threatening emergency in male cats especially.

Weekly checks

Weight: A bathroom scale with you holding the cat works fine. Track the number monthly. A 0.5 lb loss in a 10 lb cat is a 5% body weight loss — medically significant. Cats lose weight slowly from chronic disease, and owners often attribute it to "aging" when it's something treatable.

Coat quality: A healthy cat's coat is smooth, consistent, and sheds moderately. A dull, clumped, or unkempt coat often indicates pain (the cat can't groom normally), dental disease, or arthritis. Cats that stop grooming their back half are often experiencing spinal or hip pain.

Eyes and ears: Eyes should be clear without discharge or squinting. Pawing at one ear, head shaking, or dark discharge in the ear canal suggests an ear infection or mites.

Changes that always warrant a vet call

Sudden hiding or behavioral change. Straining in the litter box without producing urine. Breathing with mouth open (cats almost never pant). Vomiting more than twice in 24 hours or blood in vomit. Limping that doesn't resolve in 24-48 hours. Rapid breathing at rest. Third eyelid (white membrane) visible at the corner of the eye.

Senior cat standard: Cats over age 10 should see a vet every 6 months rather than annually. Kidney disease, hyperthyroidism, and dental disease progress quickly in older cats, and twice-yearly bloodwork can catch them a year before symptoms appear. The cost of twice-yearly senior exams is almost always less than the cost of emergency treatment for a disease caught late.

Building a health record at home

Keep a simple log: monthly weight, quarterly coat/mobility observations, any vet visits with dates and findings. When something changes, you have a documented baseline to compare against rather than guessing whether your cat has "always been this way" or whether something shifted. This record also makes vet appointments more efficient — "she lost 0.4 lbs since my last note four weeks ago" is more clinically useful than "she seems thinner lately."
