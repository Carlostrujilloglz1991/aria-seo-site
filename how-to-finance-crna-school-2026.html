How to Finance CRNA School 2026

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Financial Disclaimer: This article is general educational information, not personalized financial or legal advice. Consult a financial planner familiar with healthcare professional debt before borrowing for graduate school.

CRNA school requires full-time commitment, which means most students cannot work as an RN during the program. This creates a two-part financial problem: tuition must be paid, and living expenses must be covered, typically for 28 to 36 months. The financing strategies below cover both problems, from the most favorable to least favorable terms.

Best-case financing scenario: Max out federal Direct Unsubsidized loans first (lowest rates, income-driven repayment eligible, PSLF eligible). Apply for every scholarship. Negotiate a hospital employment agreement post-graduation. Keep savings for living expenses. This combination can reduce borrowing to $80,000 to $120,000 for a public program — manageable on a CRNA salary.

1. Federal Direct Unsubsidized Loans

For graduate and professional students, the federal Direct Unsubsidized Loan program is always the first choice. In 2026, graduate students can borrow up to $20,500 per academic year in Direct Unsubsidized Loans. The interest rate for graduate loans is typically 7 to 8% (set annually each July 1 — verify current rates at studentaid.gov). These loans are eligible for income-driven repayment plans (SAVE, IBR, PAYE) and Public Service Loan Forgiveness — a critical advantage over private loans. If you end up practicing at a nonprofit hospital, PSLF can forgive remaining balances after 10 years of qualifying payments.

Most CRNA programs cost more annually than the $20,500 Direct Unsubsidized cap. The excess — the gap between the annual loan limit and actual school costs — must come from other sources.

2. Federal Grad PLUS Loans

Grad PLUS loans cover the gap up to the full cost of attendance (as calculated by the school). The interest rate is higher than Direct Unsubsidized loans — typically 1% above the Direct Unsubsidized rate, or approximately 8 to 9% in 2026. Grad PLUS requires a credit check (no significant adverse credit history) but no income or debt-to-income qualification. Like Direct Unsubsidized loans, Grad PLUS are eligible for IDR plans and PSLF. Borrow Grad PLUS last, after maximizing Direct Unsubsidized, but prefer it over private loans because of the federal protections.

4. Employer Tuition Reimbursement

Many hospital systems reimburse graduate nursing education for employed nurses — typically $3,000 to $12,000 per year. Check your current employer's policy before leaving for CRNA school. Some hospitals will continue reimbursing your tuition even once you've transitioned to full-time student status, as long as you maintain some employment connection (per diem or PRN). This is worth negotiating explicitly before you leave.

5. Hospital Work Agreements (Post-Graduation)

Many hospitals and health systems that have trouble recruiting CRNAs offer signing bonuses ($20,000 to $60,000) or loan repayment assistance ($10,000 to $50,000 paid out over 2 to 5 years) in exchange for a commitment to work at that facility for a specified period after graduation. These agreements must be negotiated with eyes open — the penalty for leaving early (typically full repayment of the bonus) can be significant. In rural critical access hospitals in high-need areas, the terms are often more favorable. Negotiate any work agreement with an attorney review before signing.

6. Private Student Loans

Private loans should be used as a last resort. They carry higher interest rates than federal loans (often 8-13%), lack income-driven repayment options, and are not eligible for PSLF or other forgiveness programs. They also have no deferment protections and no forbearance rights comparable to federal loans. If you must borrow privately: compare rates across multiple lenders, choose fixed rates (variable rates can explode if benchmark rates rise), and borrow only the absolute minimum needed.

7. Living on Loans — Managing the Budget

CRNA school students who cannot work during the program typically fund living expenses with excess loan disbursements (Cost of Attendance minus tuition). This is legal and expected — the school's COA calculation includes living expenses for this reason. However, borrowed living expenses accrue interest immediately on Unsubsidized and PLUS loans. To minimize interest accumulation: live with a partner or in shared housing, drive an older car already paid off, eliminate all discretionary expenses during school, and make interest-only payments on your loans during school if at all possible (payments are not required during enrollment, but voluntary interest payments prevent capitalization of accrued interest at graduation).

Pre-school financial preparation: In the 12 to 24 months before starting CRNA school, aggressively build your cash savings to cover living expenses, ideally 6 to 12 months worth. This reduces the amount you need to borrow for living costs during school. An extra $15,000 to $30,000 saved as an RN can reduce loan interest by $5,000 to $10,000 over the repayment period.
