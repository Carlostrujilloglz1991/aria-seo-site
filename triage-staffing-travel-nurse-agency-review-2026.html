Triage Staffing Review 2026

Part of the Travel Nursing Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Triage Staffing markets itself with a bluntness that's rare in this industry — the pitch is essentially "we'll tell you the truth even when it's not what you want to hear." That's a low bar that most agencies still manage to trip over, so it's worth examining whether Triage clears it, and what else you're signing up for when you do.

The one-line version: Triage is a mid-size traditional agency whose differentiator is communication culture: one recruiter as your single point of contact, direct answers about pay and problems, and less sugarcoating than the industry norm. Pay is competitive-but-negotiable, and the job board is mid-size — so benchmark anyway.

What "no-nonsense" means in practice

Every agency claims transparency; Triage builds its brand on it. In practice, travelers report that Triage recruiters are more willing than most to say the unglamorous things out loud: that a facility has a rough reputation, that a package is at the top of its range and won't move, that a contract cancellation is coming down the pipeline. None of this is guaranteed by policy — it's culture, enforced by hiring and by the fact that the company has staked its brand on it. Culture-based promises are weaker than structural ones (a marketplace that prints pay on the listing can't hide the number even on a bad day), but they are real, and they compound over a multi-contract relationship.

Structurally, Triage runs the classic model: one recruiter owns your file end to end — job search, submission, extension, problems at 2 a.m. That single-point-of-contact design cuts both ways, exactly as it does at Fusion (its Omaha neighbor): a strong recruiter is a career asset, a weak one is your whole experience. Vet the human, not just the logo.

Pay: solid, negotiable, never assume

Triage packages are generally competitive for the mid-size tier — frequently a notch above the most service-heavy agencies, usually below the highest number a hard negotiator can find in a hot market. Because pay is quoted per conversation rather than posted, the information asymmetry is still on the agency's side. The fix is the same as always: pull the identical or comparable contract on a transparent marketplace like Trusted Health, get a second quote from one other traditional agency, and negotiate with numbers rather than feelings. A recruiter culture that prides itself on straight talk tends to respond well to a traveler who arrives with the market rate in hand.

Watch the package structure, not just the gross. Two $2,200/week quotes can differ enormously in taxable base, stipend split, and travel reimbursement — with real consequences for overtime rates, disability and loan paperwork, and your tax-home exposure. Benefits and the practical details

The W-2 benefits package is industry-standard: medical, dental, and vision, a 401(k) with eligibility rules worth reading, license and certification reimbursement on qualifying contracts, and travel reimbursement. Triage also staffs allied disciplines (lab, radiology, rehab therapy, cardiopulmonary) at meaningful volume, which — as with Fusion — diversifies the company's revenue and makes it a workable single-agency answer for travel couples in different disciplines. Day-one insurance and gap coverage between back-to-back contracts have specific rules; confirm them in writing before you plan a contract break, because the friendly phone answer is not the policy document.

Where it shows its limits

Three honest caveats. First, mid-size means mid-size: in niche specialties and smaller markets, the mega-agencies' exclusive facility contracts will surface jobs Triage simply doesn't have. Second, the candor brand sets a high bar — when a traveler does hit a communication failure (it happens at every agency), it stings more because the marketing promised otherwise; escalate early and ask for reassignment rather than quietly riding out a bad match. Third, candor is not advocacy: a recruiter telling you the truth about a package is not the same as fighting to enlarge it. Bring your own leverage.

Who it fits — and who it doesn't

Triage fits experienced travelers who are tired of being handled — people who would rather hear "that facility churns travelers, here's why" than discover it in week two. It fits travel couples spanning nursing and allied roles, and it pairs naturally with a marketplace account used purely for rate benchmarking. It fits less well for brand-new travelers who need heavy coaching on tax homes and licensing (possible at Triage, but service-first shops like Host Healthcare are built for it), and for rate-maximizers chasing the absolute top of the market.

Bottom line: Triage delivers a rarer commodity than high pay: fewer surprises. If your last agency's sins were vagueness and vanishing recruiters, it's a strong candidate for your shortlist. Keep the benchmarking discipline anyway — candid quotes are still quotes, and the market rate is still your job to know.
