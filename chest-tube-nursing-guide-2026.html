Water-Seal Drainage System (Pleur-evac/Atrium)

Three-Chamber System

ChamberFunctionNormal Finding
Collection chamber (right)Collects fluid and blood draining from pleural spaceMark output hourly; normal: serous to serosanguineous; alert physician if >100 mL/hr or bright red blood suddenly increasing
Water seal chamber (middle)One-way valve: allows air out of pleura, prevents air back in; filled with 2 cm of sterile waterTidaling (fluctuation with respiration); intermittent bubbling if air leak present; no bubbling if lungs re-expanded
Suction control chamber (left)Regulates suction; gentling bubbling = suction working; amount of water (not suction dial) controls suction levelGentle continuous bubbling (not vigorous) = suction at correct level (-20 cm H2O typical); vigorous bubbling is unnecessary and wasteful

Key Observations

  • Tidaling: Water in the water-seal chamber rises with inspiration and falls with expiration (opposite in mechanically ventilated patients). Normal — shows the tube is patent and in the pleural space
  • Bubbling in water seal (intermittent): Air leak from the pleural space — expected in pneumothorax; should decrease as lung re-expands
  • Bubbling in water seal (continuous): May indicate large air leak or air entering system from a loose connection — assess connections first
  • Bubbling in suction control chamber: Normal — confirms suction is active; should be gentle, not vigorous
  • No tidaling + no drainage: May indicate tube obstruction (clot, kink, patient position) or lung fully re-expanded

Nursing Assessment

Every Shift / Every 1–2 Hours

  • Respiratory assessment: breath sounds bilateral, SpO2, RR, work of breathing
  • Insertion site: dressings intact, no crepitus (subcutaneous emphysema = air tracking into subcutaneous tissue), no sign of infection
  • Tubing: no dependent loops (fluid pools in loops → reduces drainage); tubing not kinked, clamped, or compressed under patient
  • Collection chamber: mark fluid level with time; note color (serous, serosanguineous, bloody); report sudden increase in bright red output
  • Water seal chamber: presence/absence of tidaling; presence/absence of bubbling; document
  • Suction control chamber: gentle bubbling (if on suction)
  • Connections: all connections taped or secured; no visible separation
  • Keep drainage system BELOW the level of the patient's chest at all times — gravity-dependent drainage

Subcutaneous Emphysema

  • Crackling sensation under the skin around the insertion site or spreading up the neck and face
  • Caused by air tracking into subcutaneous tissue, often from a poorly sealed insertion site or tube dislodgement
  • If rapidly spreading: notify physician immediately — can compromise airway if it reaches the neck

Chest Tube Clamping Rules

Never clamp a chest tube for routine transport or ambulation unless specifically ordered by the physician. Clamping a pneumothorax tube can cause a tension pneumothorax, which is rapidly fatal.
SituationClamp?Rationale
Routine transport/patient movementNO (unless ordered)Tension pneumothorax risk if clamped with ongoing air leak
System disconnected/broken (emergency)YES — briefly, while replacing systemPrevents air from entering pleural space; replace system immediately
Identifying air leak sourceYES — at the insertion site to test (momentarily)If bubbling stops, leak is in the system; if continues, leak is from the lung
Chest tube removal (per order)YES — per physician order to test readinessClamp x 24h, then CXR; if no pneumothorax, remove tube

Chest Tube Removal Care

Pre-Removal

  • Confirm physician order and indication (lung re-expanded, drainage <150–200 mL/day, no air leak for 24h, no bubbling)
  • Gather: suture removal kit, petroleum gauze (Xeroform), 4x4 gauze, tape, gloves
  • Administer analgesic or anxiolytic 30–45 minutes before removal (procedure is painful)

During Removal

  • Position patient in semi-Fowler's or lateral (away from tube side)
  • Physician removes suture and withdraws tube quickly at end of exhalation (or during Valsalva) — patient instructed: "take a deep breath in, hold it / bear down"
  • Nurse simultaneously applies petroleum gauze and dry dressing quickly over site to create an airtight seal
  • Secure with tape on all sides

Post-Removal

  • CXR within 1–2 hours to confirm no pneumothorax
  • Respiratory assessment: breath sounds equal bilaterally, SpO2, RR, dyspnea?
  • Assess dressing: airtight, no air hissing through site
  • Document removal time, patient tolerance, dressing applied

NCLEX High-Yield Points

  • Tidaling = normal; shows tube patent and in pleural space; rises with inspiration (spontaneous breathing)
  • Intermittent bubbling in water seal = air leak from lung (expected in pneumothorax; should decrease)
  • Continuous bubbling in water seal = may indicate loose connection in system — check connections first
  • Suction control chamber: GENTLE bubbling = correct; vigorous = turn suction down
  • NEVER clamp a chest tube for routine transport (tension pneumothorax risk)
  • Tube falls out: cover with petroleum gauze taped on 3 SIDES (flutter valve, not airtight)
  • Tube removal: at end of exhalation or Valsalva; apply occlusive dressing immediately; CXR after
  • Keep drainage system BELOW patient's chest at all times — never lift it above chest level (reverse siphon)
