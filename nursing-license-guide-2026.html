Nursing License Guide 2026: Initial RN Licensure, Endorsement, Renewal, and the Nurse Licensure Compact

Your nursing license is your most important professional asset. It is also the easiest professional asset to lose through administrative neglect — missed renewal deadlines, unreported name changes, and lapsed continuing education requirements result in license expiration that can interrupt employment, require reinstatement fees, and in some cases trigger investigations. This guide covers the complete lifecycle of an RN license: initial application, endorsement to a new state, renewal, continuing education requirements, and the Nurse Licensure Compact.

Initial RN Licensure: License by Examination

Initial RN licensure (license by examination) follows this process for most new graduates:

1. Apply to the State Board of Nursing. Submit the initial licensure application to the State Board of Nursing in the state where you plan to work first. Applications are submitted online through the state board's portal. Required documents typically include: completed nursing program verification (your school submits this directly to the board), official transcripts, application fee ($75–$250 depending on state), photo ID, and any required background check authorization.

2. Receive Authorization to Test (ATT). The State Board of Nursing processes your application and, when approved, sends your ATT. The ATT is your authorization to schedule the NCLEX — without it, you cannot register for the exam. ATT processing times vary from 2–8 weeks depending on state and application completeness. Incomplete applications (missing school verification, outstanding fees) delay the ATT.

3. Register for NCLEX with Pearson VUE. Once you have the ATT, register at pearsonvue.com/nclex to schedule your exam appointment. The NCLEX is administered at Pearson VUE testing centers nationwide. Registration fee: $200 (paid at registration, regardless of whether you pass or fail).

4. Pass the NCLEX-RN. The NCLEX uses Computerized Adaptive Testing (CAT) — the exam adjusts question difficulty based on your performance. The exam ends when the computer has determined with 95% confidence whether you are above or below the passing standard. Minimum 85 questions; maximum 145 questions. Results are typically available 48 business hours after testing via the Quick Results service (fee applies) or 6 weeks by mail.

5. License Issued. After passing, your license is issued by the State Board of Nursing and appears in the public license verification database. Processing after passing: typically 1–4 weeks. Most hospitals allow you to start orientation and complete non-clinical training while awaiting the license number, but you cannot practice independently until the license appears in the state database.

License by Endorsement: Moving to a New State

If you hold an active RN license in one state and want to practice in another state, you apply for licensure by endorsement — you don't retake the NCLEX. The endorsing state verifies your original license and NCLEX passage through NURSYS (the national nurse licensure database) and issues a new state license.

Endorsement requirements typically include: Active RN license in your current state (cannot endorse from an expired license); application and fee to the new state board; NURSYS verification or primary source verification of your original license; criminal background check (most states); any state-specific additional requirements (jurisprudence exam in some states — a short exam on that state's Nurse Practice Act).

Timeline: Endorsement processing ranges from 2–12 weeks depending on state. Some states offer temporary permits that allow you to work while the endorsement is processing; others require you to wait for the full license. If you are relocating for a new job, apply for endorsement immediately after accepting the offer — do not wait until after your move.

The Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC)

The Nurse Licensure Compact is an agreement among 41 states (as of 2026) that allows nurses who live in a compact state to hold one multistate license that is valid to practice in all compact member states — without applying for separate licenses in each state.

How the NLC works: You apply for your license in your state of legal residence (your "home state"). If your home state is a compact member, you receive a multistate license that allows practice in all other compact member states. To practice in a compact state where you do not live, you use your multistate license — no separate application, no additional fees, no waiting period. If you move your legal residence to a new compact state, you must apply for a new license in that state (it becomes your new home state) within 60–90 days.

NLC is based on residence, not employment. If you live in Texas (compact) and take a travel nursing assignment in Colorado (compact), you use your Texas multistate license for the Colorado assignment — no endorsement needed. If you live in California (non-compact) and take a travel assignment in a compact state, you must obtain a single-state license by endorsement in that state. This is why California nurses pay significantly more in licensing fees for travel nursing than nurses who live in compact states.

States NOT in the NLC as of 2026 include: California, New York, Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Michigan, Illinois, Nevada, and others. Check the NCSBN website (ncsbn.org) for the current compact member state list — membership changes as new states legislate to join.

License Renewal

RN licenses must be renewed periodically — renewal cycles are 2 years in most states, though some states use 1-year or 3-year cycles. Renewal requires: the renewal application (submitted online through your state board portal), the renewal fee ($30–$150 depending on state), and completion of required continuing education hours.

What Happens If Your License Expires

A lapsed or expired license means you are not legally permitted to practice nursing. Working with an expired license is practicing without a license — a violation of the Nurse Practice Act that can result in civil and criminal liability, and will almost certainly result in board investigation and disciplinary action if discovered.

Most states have a grace period (30–90 days after the expiration date) during which you can still renew with a late fee without requiring reinstatement. After the grace period, you enter reinstatement territory — which may require additional documentation, board review, possible re-examination requirements (in some states, a lapse of more than 5 years triggers NCLEX retake requirements), and significantly higher fees.

Set calendar reminders 90 days before your renewal date — and again at 60 days and 30 days. Your employer is NOT responsible for tracking your license expiration. Many nurses have been surprised to find their license expired because they assumed their employer's credentialing system would notify them. It may or it may not — your license is your responsibility.

Background Checks and License Discipline

Initial licensure and most renewal processes include criminal background checks. Arrests (even without conviction), prior license discipline in any state, and certain civil judgments must be disclosed. Non-disclosure of a reportable event is considered falsification of the application — a more serious offense than the underlying incident in many boards' view.

If you have a criminal history or prior board action, consult a nursing license defense attorney before applying — the process for disclosure and the likelihood of approval vary significantly by state, offense type, and time elapsed. Many nurses with past issues successfully obtain and maintain licenses through proper disclosure and demonstrated rehabilitation.
