New Hampshire has no accredited nurse anesthesia program. That is not a dead end — it just means your plan starts as an out-of-state plan, and the good news is that some of the country's strongest CRNA programs sit within a short drive across the border.

The landscape: As of 2026, there is no COA-accredited nurse anesthesia program based in New Hampshire. Schools like Saint Anselm College and Franklin Pierce University offer nursing degrees, but neither runs a CRNA program. New Hampshire nurses pursue anesthesia training in neighboring states — most often Massachusetts, Connecticut, or New York.

Why New Hampshire Has No Program

Nurse anesthesia programs are clinically intensive and depend on high-volume surgical sites to give every student enough cases. Small states without a large academic medical hub often don't host their own program — New Hampshire, Maine, and Vermont are all in this position. For applicants, the practical takeaway is simple: don't wait for an in-state option to appear. Build your plan around accredited programs in the surrounding region from the start.

For most New Hampshire residents, the Boston-area programs in Massachusetts are the natural first target — they are the closest cluster and several are nationally regarded. From there, widen the list into Connecticut and the deep bench of programs in New York. Casting a regional net is not a compromise here; it is the correct strategy for any applicant from a state without its own program.

Admission Requirements Don't Change

Applying out of state does not change what programs expect. You will need a BSN with a competitive GPA, a minimum of one year of full-time adult critical-care experience, and current BLS and ACLS certification. The ICU requirement is non-negotiable, and competitive applicants typically bring more than the minimum year, high-acuity experience, and the CCRN credential — see the CCRN guide. For the full checklist, review the general CRNA school requirements.

Plan for relocation early. Most programs are full-time and clinically demanding, so commuting long distances is rarely realistic for all three years. Budget for a move, line up your finances, and factor housing into your decision — treating relocation as part of the plan from the beginning removes a major source of stress later.
Confirm program availability before you build a list. New programs do open over time. Before finalizing your targets, check the COA accredited-programs list and each school's official site to confirm current status, deadlines, and requirements.

Application Strategy for New Hampshire

Because there is no in-state program, your plan is regional by necessity — which is actually the same advice strong applicants everywhere follow. Anchor your list on the Boston-area Massachusetts programs, then add Connecticut and several New York options. Strengthen your file with the required ICU year (ideally more), the CCRN, and a sharp personal statement. For realistic expectations, see CRNA acceptance rates.

Bottom Line

New Hampshire has no accredited CRNA program in 2026, but it sits beside one of the richest clusters of programs in the country. Anchor your application on the Boston-area Massachusetts schools, widen into Connecticut and New York, plan for relocation, and apply broadly. For the bigger picture, see how to become a CRNA and the CRNA job outlook.
