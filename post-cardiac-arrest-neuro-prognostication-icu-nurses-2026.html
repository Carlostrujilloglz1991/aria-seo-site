Neuroprognostication After Cardiac Arrest: The ICU Nurse's Guide to the 72-Hour Wait

Part of the ICU Emergencies Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

ICU clinical · Updated 2026 · Educational overview — follow your unit protocol and neurology team

The resuscitation is the loud part. The hard part is the quiet 72 hours that follow, when the family asks the question every ICU nurse dreads at 3 a.m.: "Is she going to wake up?" Post-cardiac-arrest neuroprognostication — predicting neurologic recovery after hypoxic-ischemic brain injury — is one of the highest-stakes judgments in critical care, and one where the nurse's role is larger than most nurses realize. This is an educational overview of why the process is built the way it is; the actual determination belongs to your intensivist and neurology team, and specifics follow your unit's protocol and current resuscitation guidelines.

Scope note: nurses do not perform neuroprognostication. But nurses control the confounders that make it valid, run the tests that inform it, and carry the family through it. Getting the nursing part wrong can literally produce a falsely pessimistic prediction. That is why this matters at the bedside.

Why we wait: the self-fulfilling prophecy problem

The central danger in this field is the self-fulfilling prophecy: a team predicts a poor outcome too early, withdraws life-sustaining therapy based on that prediction, the patient dies, and the prediction is recorded as "correct" — when the patient might have recovered. Modern guidelines are built specifically to prevent this. That is the reason for the two pillars every ICU nurse should internalize: wait long enough (generally at least 72 hours after return of circulation, and longer if the patient is still being warmed or sedated), and never prognosticate on a single test — use multiple concordant findings.

The confounders you must clear first (this is the nursing job)

A poor-prognosis exam is meaningless if something reversible is suppressing the brain. Before any test counts, the team must exclude confounders — and nurses are the ones tracking most of them:

  • Sedatives and analgesics: residual propofol, midazolam, opioids, and their metabolites (worse in renal/hepatic dysfunction, and after temperature management, which slows drug clearance). Accurate off-times and infusion records are gold here — see the propofol and sedation comparison guides.
  • Neuromuscular blockade: a paralyzed patient cannot demonstrate a motor response. Train-of-four monitoring is the nurse's check. Compare agents in the cisatracurium guide.
  • Hypothermia and rewarming: temperature itself suppresses the exam. The determination window is anchored to normothermia off sedation.
  • Metabolic and physiologic derangements: hypoglycemia, severe electrolyte disturbance, hypotension, hypercapnia, hepatic/renal failure — each can mimic a devastating exam.

No single one of these is used alone to withdraw care. Guidelines emphasize concordance — multiple robust findings pointing the same direction after the clock and confounders are cleared.

The nurse at the family's side

Families hear "we have to wait 72 hours" as inaction. Your framing changes their experience of it. The honest, humane version: "The brain needs time to declare itself, and we deliberately do not rush this — making a call too early is a known mistake in medicine, so the team waits and uses several independent tests before saying anything definite. Right now we are protecting her brain and gathering information." Avoid guessing at outcomes ("I've seen people wake up from worse" and "this looks really bad" are both harmful). Keep the family oriented to the plan and the timeline, escalate their questions to the team, and document the conversations. This is palliative-adjacent communication skill, and it is squarely nursing work — the same judgment the case management and goals-of-care conversations draw on.

Bottom line: neuroprognostication after cardiac arrest is deliberately slow because a fast wrong answer is fatal. The determination is the team's; the validity of that determination rests heavily on nursing — clean sedation and paralytic records, normothermia, careful test conditions, correct NSE draws, and honest, non-speculative family communication through the 72-hour wait. Know why the clock exists and you become the nurse the intensivist trusts in the room.
