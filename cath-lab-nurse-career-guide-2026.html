Cath Lab Nurse 2026: Procedures, STEMI Call, and the Pay Math Nobody Shows You

The short answer: Cath lab nursing trades the ICU's 12-hour continuous assignments for procedural medicine — PCI, STEMI activations, structural heart, and EP — built around monitoring, moderate sedation, access-site management, and crash-cart readiness when a coronary dissects on the table. Base pay is usually the hospital's standard RN scale, but call pay changes the math: STEMI call at 30–60+ minutes response time adds real income and real lifestyle cost. The unit rewards nurses who like hemodynamics, devices, and adrenaline in short bursts rather than long hauls.

The cath lab sits in a strange spot in nursing's mental map: ICU nurses think of it as "where my patient goes for an hour," and floor nurses think it's radiology. It is neither — it is a procedural critical care unit where the sickest fifteen minutes of a heart attack happen, and it offers one of the most distinctive career profiles in the hospital.

What the Work Actually Is

The room team. A typical case runs with an interventional cardiologist, a scrub tech (often RCIS-credentialed), a monitor/documenter, and a circulating RN who owns the patient: moderate sedation, hemodynamic monitoring, medications, and the emergency response if things go wrong. In many labs nurses rotate through monitoring and circulating; scrubbing is often tech territory, but cross-trained RNs exist and are prized.

Elective and urgent PCI is the bread and butter — diagnostic caths that become interventions, radial-first access, stents, and the access-site vigilance afterward (radial bands, femoral holds, and the retroperitoneal-bleed paranoia every cath lab nurse develops). STEMI activations are the identity of the job: door-to-balloon clocks, a patient who was in a grocery store forty minutes ago, and the fastest medicine in the hospital.

The escalation ladder lives here. When a PCI patient crashes, the lab places the support devices the ICU later manages: the intra-aortic balloon pump, the Impella, and in ECMO-capable centers, VA ECMO cannulation on the table. Cath lab nurses see these devices go in; it makes them fluent in the physiology in a way that transfers directly to CVICU. Structural heart (TAVR, Watchman, mitral clips) and EP (ablations, device implants) are growing service lines that many labs rotate nurses through.

The Money: Base Plus Call Is the Whole Story

ComponentHow it works
Base rateStandard hospital RN scale — cath lab rarely carries a specialty differential
Call payHourly standby rate for nights/weekends on the STEMI pager, plus time-and-a-half (or more) when activated; frequent-call labs add meaningfully to annual income
ScheduleCommonly 4 x 10s or 5 x 8s weekdays — evenings, weekends, and holidays come as call, not scheduled shifts
Lifestyle costResponse-time requirements (often 30–60 min) tether where you live and what you do on call nights

Compare this against a night-shift ICU differential position using salary by specialty: a busy STEMI center's call burden can out-earn ICU nights, while a quiet lab's won't. Ask a prospective lab two questions: how many STEMI activations per month, and how call is distributed. Those two numbers are the compensation package. Travel cath lab contracts also run persistently strong because the skill set is scarce — see how pay packages work before comparing offers.

The Skills Profile

Cath lab nursing builds deep, narrow expertise: coronary anatomy read from angiograms, beat-to-beat hemodynamics (you watch pressure waveforms for a living), moderate sedation judgment, anticoagulation management (heparin with ACT targets, bivalirudin), vasoactive response in short bursts, and radiation-safety discipline as a daily practice. What it deliberately gives up: the longitudinal assessment, multi-system juggling, and vent/CRRT management that define ICU work. Nurses coming from ICU pick up the lab quickly; nurses going ICU-to-lab-to-ICU keep both.

The CRNA answer, honestly: Most CRNA programs require current ICU experience, and most do not count cath lab as qualifying critical care on its own — the lab lacks the continuous vent/drip titration the requirement exists to test. If CRNA is the goal, the cath lab is a detour, not a path. The exception: cath lab experience layered on top of solid recent ICU years reads well, because interviewers know you've watched hearts fail in real time. Verify each program's definition of "critical care" directly — see the CRNA experience guide.

Getting In and Where It Leads

Labs strongly prefer ICU, ED, or PCU backgrounds — new grads are rare hires because the circulating nurse must run a cardiac arrest without help while the physician is scrubbed. The classic entry is 2–3 years in CVICU or a strong PCU, then an internal transfer. From the lab, careers branch to structural heart coordination, EP specialization (its own deep field), device-industry clinical roles (well-paid and heavily recruited from cath lab staff), management, and travel. CCRN gives way to CV-specific credentials here; many labs value RCIS-cross-trained RNs enough to fund the credential.

Honest fit test: If you love hemodynamics, want procedural adrenaline in 90-minute doses, and can live inside a 30-minute response radius without resenting it, the cath lab is one of nursing's best-kept secrets. If you need the 12-hour arc of owning a patient's whole story — or you're CRNA-bound — stay in the unit.
