CRNA Programs in Missouri 2026

Missouri offers four accredited nurse anesthesia programs split between its two big metros — St. Louis and Kansas City — plus a well-regarded option in Springfield. One of them, Goldfarb at Barnes-Jewish College, ranks among the very best in the nation. Here's how the Missouri programs compare and how an ICU nurse earns an interview.

Missouri CRNA market context: Missouri combines top-tier programs with a relatively affordable cost of living, which makes it attractive for out-of-state applicants too. New graduates in Missouri commonly start around $175,000–$200,000, with rural and locum assignments reaching higher. The state's program spread means most Missouri ICU nurses can train without leaving the state.

Missouri has four COA-accredited nurse anesthesia programs. Every accredited U.S. program now awards a doctorate — the COA phased out master's-level CRNA entry on January 1, 2025. Some programs award the DNP and others the DNAP (Doctor of Nurse Anesthesia Practice); both qualify graduates to sit for the National Certification Examination. Confirm current tuition, credit counts, and clinical-hour requirements directly with each school.

Goldfarb School of Nursing at Barnes-Jewish College

Goldfarb's nurse anesthesia program in St. Louis is the standout: it was ranked No. 9 nationally in the U.S. News & World Report "Best Grad Schools" 2026 nurse anesthesia rankings, and it is fully accredited by the COA. Its close tie to Barnes-Jewish Hospital — one of the highest-acuity academic medical centers in the Midwest — gives students exceptional case volume and variety. For applicants who want a top-ten program in the center of the country, Goldfarb is the anchor.

UMKC, Webster, and Missouri State

The University of Missouri–Kansas City School of Nursing and Health Studies awards a DNP and is affiliated with University Health (Truman Medical Center) for clinical training, anchoring the Kansas City side of the state. Webster University in St. Louis awards a DNAP and offers a second metro option in the eastern part of the state. The Missouri State University School of Anesthesia in Springfield runs a full-time, 36-month DNAP and has earned a maximum 10-year COA accreditation award — a strong signal of program stability and a solid option for southwestern Missouri ICU nurses.

Programs want sustained adult critical care managing ventilated patients on vasoactive infusions with arterial and central lines. A CVICU or trauma SICU role at one of these systems is far stronger on a CRNA application than a step-down or community "ICU" that does not manage that acuity.

The Application Timeline from Missouri

Working backward from a fall 2027 start: most Missouri programs accept applications roughly September–December 2026, interview January–March 2027, and decide by spring 2027. Have your CCRN completed and at least one year (ideally two) of high-acuity ICU hours documented before applications open. With programs in St. Louis, Kansas City, and Springfield, a Missouri applicant can apply to several in-state options without relocating during school.

Competition concentrates at the top: Goldfarb's national ranking draws a wide applicant pool for a small cohort. Don't apply only to the marquee name — pair a top-ranked reach (Goldfarb) with one or two strong regional options (UMKC, Webster, Missouri State) where your in-state ICU experience and connections carry more weight.
Missouri CRNA salary on graduation: New CRNAs in Missouri metros commonly earn an estimated $175,000–$200,000 in hospital employment, with rural and locum assignments pushing higher. St. Louis and Kansas City both carry steady surgical volume and anesthesia demand.
