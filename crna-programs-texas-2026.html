CRNA Programs in Texas 2026

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

Texas has more accredited nurse anesthesia programs than almost any other state, including the single largest program in the country. Here's how the Texas options compare and how an ICU nurse in Houston, Dallas, San Antonio, or Austin builds an application that gets an interview.

Texas CRNA market context: Texas is one of the strongest CRNA job markets in the nation — large population, major metro hospital systems, and a regulatory environment that supports nurse anesthetist practice. New graduates in Texas commonly start at $190,000–$220,000, with locum and rural assignments reaching $250,000–$300,000+. The state's program density means Texas ICU nurses can often study without relocating out of state.

Texas Wesleyan University — Fort Worth

Texas Wesleyan operates the largest nurse anesthesia program in the country. Students complete the front-half didactic phase at the Fort Worth campus, then move into clinical rotations at assigned sites spread across roughly nine states. That scale is a double-edged sword: a large cohort and an enormous clinical network, but also the possibility of relocating for clinicals to a site you don't choose. The 36-month DNAP is well-known to Texas hospital recruiters, and the program's size means a large statewide alumni base.

Baylor College of Medicine — Houston

Baylor's DNP nurse anesthesia program is among the most respected in the state and reports a first-time national certification pass rate in the low-to-mid 90% range. A notable feature: Baylor has historically offered a monthly stipend (around $1,000) during clinical rotations at Ben Taub Hospital to help offset the fact that CRNA students cannot work during the program. Houston's Texas Medical Center — the largest medical complex in the world — gives Baylor students extraordinary clinical volume and case variety.

Texas Christian University (TCU) — Fort Worth

TCU's DNAP is delivered in three phases including progressive clinical immersion and has been ranked in the top tier of U.S. nurse anesthesia programs by U.S. News & World Report. It is also the most expensive Texas option, reflecting its private-university tuition. Applicants who can absorb the higher cost get a strongly branded credential and a polished, well-resourced program.

UT Health Houston and the US Army Program

UT Health Science Center at Houston offers a state-university DNP with competitive tuition and direct ties to the Texas Medical Center clinical environment — an excellent value for Texas residents. The US Army Graduate Program in Anesthesia Nursing at Fort Sam Houston is fully funded for qualifying military nurses and is consistently one of the highest-performing programs nationally; it requires a military commitment and is not open to civilian applicants in the standard sense.

The Application Timeline from Texas

Working backward from a fall 2027 start: most Texas programs accept applications roughly September–December 2026, interview January–March 2027, and decide by April–May 2027. Have your CCRN completed and ICU hours documented before applications open. Because Texas has multiple programs, applying to several in-state (plus one or two out-of-state) meaningfully raises your odds without forcing a cross-country move during school.

Clinical relocation reality: Some Texas programs — Texas Wesleyan in particular — assign clinical rotation sites that may be out of state or far from your home city. Before committing, ask each program where its rotation sites are, whether you can rank preferences, and what housing support exists. A 12-month rotation in another state is a real life and financial event, not a footnote.
Texas CRNA salary on graduation: New CRNAs in Texas metros commonly earn $190,000–$220,000 in hospital employment, and independent or locum work can push total compensation above $250,000–$300,000. Texas's large surgical volume and ongoing anesthesia provider demand make it one of the best states in which to graduate and immediately find work.
