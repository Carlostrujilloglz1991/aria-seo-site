NCLEX SATA Strategy 2026

SATA questions are not harder than single-best-answer questions — they require a different cognitive approach. Most SATA errors come from answering as a group rather than as individual true/false statements.

The Core SATA Method: Independent Evaluation

Read the question stem carefully. Then look at each answer choice INDEPENDENTLY and ask: "Is this option TRUE for this patient in this clinical situation?" Do not consider which other options you've selected. Each answer is either TRUE or FALSE — not "most true" relative to the others.

Wrong approach: "I think A and C are right, so D probably isn't." Right approach: "Is D a true statement for this patient? Yes. Include it."

Why Nurses Get SATA Wrong

  • Looking for patterns: "There can't be 5 right answers" — there can. Some SATA have only 2 correct, some have all 5.
  • Second-guessing early correct choices: Once you've confirmed an answer is true, commit to it. Changing it based on what you select later leads to errors.
  • Choosing familiar options: Familiar-sounding choices that don't apply to THIS specific patient are wrong. Read the stem carefully before evaluating options.
  • Overthinking: SATA is testing safe nursing practice, not rare exceptions. If an option is a standard nursing action for this scenario, it's probably correct.

SATA Practice Example

Q: A nurse is caring for a patient receiving a packed red blood cell transfusion who develops itching, urticaria, and flushing 30 minutes into the infusion. Which actions should the nurse take? (SATA)

  1. Stop the transfusion immediately
  2. Administer diphenhydramine (Benadryl) per standing order
  3. Discard the blood bag immediately
  4. Notify the blood bank
  5. Restart the transfusion at a slower rate after symptoms resolve
  6. Document the reaction

Answer: A, B, D, F
A: Yes — stop the transfusion (febrile/allergic reaction protocol)
B: Yes — diphenhydramine for allergic reaction symptoms
C: No — do NOT discard the blood bag; return it to the blood bank for analysis
D: Yes — notify blood bank with blood bag and patient blood sample
E: No — mild allergic reactions may restart with orders, but urticaria + flushing is concerning; this is not a standard "restart" scenario without provider order specifically
F: Yes — always document

The C trap: "Discard the blood bag" sounds like a safety action but is WRONG — the bag must be returned to the blood bank for investigation. SATA frequently includes options that sound protective but are incorrect. Don't let the word "safety" override clinical reasoning.

NGN SATA Variations in 2026

Next Generation NCLEX introduces enhanced SATA formats: matrix SATA (a grid where you select multiple items across rows and columns), and SATA embedded within extended drag-and-drop cases. The independent evaluation principle still applies — each cell or item is evaluated on its own clinical merits.
