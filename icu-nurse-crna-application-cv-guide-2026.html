Your CRNA application CV is not a job resume — it's a clinical portfolio. Admissions committees want to see depth of critical care exposure, not job titles and dates.

CV vs. Resume: The Difference

A job resume highlights accomplishments in 1–2 pages. A CRNA application CV (curriculum vitae) is longer and more detailed — typically 3–5 pages for an experienced ICU nurse. It documents the breadth and depth of your clinical training in a way that allows committee members to assess your CRNA readiness.

Required CV Sections

  • Contact information and credentials (RN, BSN, CCRN, ACLS/BLS, etc.)
  • Education — nursing school, GPA if ≥3.5, honors
  • Clinical experience — most detailed section (see below)
  • Certifications — CCRN, CEN, PCCN, TNCC, etc.
  • CRNA shadowing experience — facility, hours, type of cases observed
  • Leadership and professional activities
  • Publications, presentations, research (if any)
  • Awards and recognition

How to Write the Clinical Experience Section

This is where most CRNA applicants lose points. Don't just list your job title and dates. Document specifically:

  • Bed count and level of your ICU (CVICU, MICU, SICU, CTICU, neuro ICU)
  • Patient:nurse ratio
  • Specific procedures performed or assisted (arterial line management, Swan-Ganz monitoring, IABP, CRRT, ECMO, chest tube management)
  • Common disease states managed (post-cardiac surgery, ARDS, septic shock, DKA, TBI)
  • Vasoactive drips managed by name (norepinephrine, vasopressin, epinephrine, dobutamine, milrinone)
Example weak entry: "ICU RN, XYZ Hospital, 2022–2026" → Example strong entry: "Staff RN, 28-bed CVICU, XYZ Medical Center (Level I Trauma, 750 beds), 2022–2026. Managed post-cardiac surgery patients (CABG, valve replacement, heart transplant) at 1:2 ratio. Proficient in hemodynamic monitoring (arterial lines, CVP, Swan-Ganz), IABP management, vasoactive drip titration (norepinephrine, vasopressin, milrinone, epinephrine), epicardial pacing, and CVVHD. Charge nurse 12+ months."

CRNA Shadowing Documentation

Document every shadow experience: facility name, CRNA name (if they're willing), date, hours, and the type of cases observed (pediatric, cardiac, OB, regional). Most programs want 40–80+ shadow hours. Document them all — they demonstrate intentionality about your career choice.

What Admissions Committees Actually Look For

Evidence of clinical complexity (not just number of years), autonomous nursing practice at high acuity, leadership (charge nurse, preceptor, committee work), academic readiness (GRE scores if required, science GPA), and personal statement alignment with what the CV shows.
