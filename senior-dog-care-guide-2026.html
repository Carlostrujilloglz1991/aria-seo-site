Senior Dog Care: Managing Aging, Pain, and Quality of Life

Dogs age faster than humans, and "senior" status arrives earlier than most owners expect. A Great Dane is geriatric at 7; a Chihuahua is just entering middle age. Understanding the changes that come with aging — and how to address them — is what determines whether a dog's later years are comfortable or difficult.

When is a dog "senior"? Giant breeds (over 90 lbs): 6-7 years. Large breeds (50-90 lbs): 7-8 years. Medium breeds (25-50 lbs): 8-9 years. Small breeds (under 25 lbs): 10-11 years. Earlier in giant breeds because they age faster at the cellular level.

The most common senior dog health issues

Osteoarthritis: Affects approximately 80% of dogs over age 8. Signs include reluctance to climb stairs or jump, stiffness after rest, slowing on walks, sleeping more, irritability when touched in painful areas. Many dogs hide pain exceptionally well. If your senior dog shows any of these signs, ask your vet specifically about osteoarthritis screening — it's underdiagnosed because owners attribute the signs to "just getting old."

Dental disease: Compounds with age. Untreated dental disease causes chronic pain and systemic inflammation. Senior dogs often stop showing interest in hard food or chews not because they're "picky" but because their mouth hurts.

Cognitive Dysfunction Syndrome (CDS): The canine equivalent of dementia. Signs: disorientation, changes in sleep-wake cycle (up at night, sleeping all day), getting "stuck" in corners, forgetting housetraining, reduced interaction. A 2021 UC San Diego study found 28% of dogs ages 11-12 and 68% of dogs ages 15-16 show signs of CDS. Several treatments (dietary supplements, melatonin, selegiline) have evidence supporting them.

Exercise adjustments for seniors

Senior dogs need exercise — it maintains muscle mass that supports arthritic joints, provides mental stimulation, and manages weight. But duration and intensity should decrease. Shorter, more frequent walks (three 15-minute walks instead of one 45-minute walk) put less cumulative strain on joints. Swimming is ideal if accessible — no-impact cardiovascular exercise that most dogs enjoy.

Signs you're asking for too much: lagging behind, lying down during the walk, significant stiffness or limping the following morning. Signs you need more activity: weight gain, restlessness, behavioral changes from boredom.

Pain management: the options

NSAIDs (Carprofen, Meloxicam, Galliprant): the most effective and most commonly prescribed for osteoarthritis pain. Require periodic bloodwork to monitor kidney and liver function. Not appropriate for all dogs, but well-tolerated in many senior dogs long-term.

Joint supplements (fish oil, glucosamine+chondroitin): modest evidence of benefit, extremely low risk. Fish oil (EPA/DHA) at appropriate doses has the best research support among supplements. Not a replacement for NSAIDs in dogs with significant pain.

Rehabilitation therapy: underwater treadmill, laser therapy, and acupuncture have evidence in veterinary literature for osteoarthritis pain. Increasingly available at specialty practices.

Quality of life assessment: The HHHHHMM scale (Hurt, Hunger, Hydration, Hygiene, Happiness, Mobility, More good days than bad) is a structured tool developed by Dr. Alice Villalobos for assessing quality of life in aging or terminally ill pets. Scoring it monthly provides an objective record that helps families make end-of-life decisions based on data rather than emotion alone. Ask your vet for a copy.
