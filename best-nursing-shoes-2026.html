Bottom line: There is no single best nursing shoe — the right choice depends on your specialty, floor surface, foot mechanics, and how much you move versus stand still. Clogs dominate among nurses who do sustained standing; athletic shoes dominate among nurses who cover the most distance. Safety-toe requirements narrow the field considerably. This guide breaks down the honest tradeoffs by specialty.

Nurses average 4–5 miles per shift in high-acuity units, and musculoskeletal injuries of the foot, ankle, knee, and lower back account for a significant share of occupational injury claims in healthcare. Shoe selection is one of the few ergonomic interventions entirely within a nurse's personal control. This comparison covers seven of the most commonly recommended shoes across three categories — clogs, athletic shoes, and safety-toe — evaluated against the demands of specific nursing environments.

Clogs: Dansko Professional vs. Crocs On-The-Clock

Dansko Professional (~$130–$150)

Dansko Professional clogs have been a nursing standard for decades, and the reason is structural. The rocker sole design reduces ankle plantarflexion demand during prolonged standing, which translates to lower calf fatigue over a 12-hour shift. The wide toe box accommodates nursing feet that have widened over years of shift work. The leather upper wipes clean when fluid exposure is a concern. The steel shank through the midsole provides stability on tile floors where fatigue arises from constant micro-corrections.

The Dansko is most appropriate for nurses who spend long periods standing at a single location — OR scrub nurses, procedure nurses, charge nurses who work from a station. It is not the right shoe for a nurse covering 5+ miles of corridor per shift; the rocker design that reduces standing fatigue also reduces efficiency during sustained walking.

Crocs On-The-Clock (~$45–$55)

The Crocs On-The-Clock is not the casual Croc — it is the work-specific slip-resistant version with a locked heel and a non-marking sole rated for slippery healthcare floors. At $45–$55, it is a fraction of the Dansko price and is genuinely worn by experienced nurses at major institutions. The Croslite foam is lightweight and easy to clean. The tradeoff is less structural arch support than the Dansko. Nurses with plantar fasciitis or high arches typically add an aftermarket insole (Superfeet or Powerstep) and report excellent results.

Athletic Shoes: HOKA Bondi 8, Brooks Ghost 16, and New Balance 990v6

HOKA Bondi 8 (~$165)

The HOKA Bondi 8 is the maximum-cushion option in the HOKA lineup and among the highest-cushion walking shoes available in 2026. For nurses in high-mileage environments — emergency departments, large ICUs, busy medical floors — the 37mm stack height at the heel absorbs repetitive impact in a way that lower-stack shoes cannot. The extended heel geometry promotes a smooth heel-to-toe transition that reduces fatigue during sustained corridor walking.

HOKA's rocker geometry also reduces Achilles tendon demand, making it frequently recommended by podiatrists for nurses with Achilles tendinopathy or plantar fasciitis. For ER and high-traffic ICU nurses, this is the most consistently recommended shoe in nursing communities as of 2026.

Brooks Ghost 16 (~$140)

The Brooks Ghost is a neutral-cushion running shoe that has maintained a following in nursing communities for over a decade due to its reliable fit consistency across model years and wide availability in extended sizes and widths. The DNA LOFT v2 foam provides adequate cushion for 12-hour floor shifts without the maximum-stack feel that some nurses find unstable on tile. Brooks' wide range of widths (up to 4E in men's, 2E in women's) accommodates foot shapes that HOKA's more moderate width range does not.

New Balance 990v6 (~$185)

The New Balance 990 series is among the most durable athletic shoes available, with an ENCAP midsole that maintains its cushioning properties well past 500 miles — significantly longer than most foam-based midsoles. Made in the USA, available in widths from 2A to 6E, and compatible with a wide range of aftermarket orthotics due to the removable insole. At $185 it is the most expensive athletic option here, but cost-per-mile over the life of the shoe often makes it competitive with cheaper alternatives that require replacement every 6 months.

Safety-Toe Options: When They're Required

Some nursing environments — sterile processing departments, facilities roles, OR transport, certain VA settings — require ASTM-rated safety-toe footwear. Standard nursing shoes do not qualify.

Safety toe types: Steel toe is the heaviest and least expensive; composite toe is lighter, does not conduct cold or heat, and passes metal detectors (relevant in correctional facility nursing). For most healthcare settings requiring safety footwear, composite toe is preferred unless the employer specifies steel.

The Skechers Work Sure Track (~$75–$90) offers Memory Foam cushioning and a slip-resistant outsole in a lightweight steel-toe package. ASTM F2413-18 rated and among the most comfortable safety-toe options for nursing-adjacent environments. The Timberland PRO Radius (~$120–$140) steps up to composite toe, an anti-fatigue footbed, and a more durable upper for environments with higher physical demands.

Fit note: Try nursing shoes on with the compression socks you will wear to work. A shoe that fits perfectly on bare feet may be uncomfortably tight after 6 hours with compression socks applied. Size up a half size from your casual shoe size as a starting point for athletic shoes.
