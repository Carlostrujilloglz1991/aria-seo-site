Travel Nurse Taxes in Nevada 2026

Nevada is one of the friendliest states on a travel nurse's paycheck: there is no state income tax and no local income tax, so your taxable wages arrive without a single dollar going to a state department of revenue. Las Vegas and Reno both run active hospital markets, and the combination of solid pay and zero state tax makes Nevada a favorite. The catch isn't in Nevada at all — it's in whether your home state is also tax-free, and whether your tax home holds up.

The short version: Nevada has no state income tax and no local income tax on wages, and no state tax return to file. Your taxable base rate on a Nevada assignment is free of state income tax; only federal tax and FICA apply. The trade-off is a relatively high sales tax (state plus local, often around 8%+ in the metros). The benefit is only complete if your home state is also tax-free — and your stipends still ride on maintaining a legitimate tax home.

Why Nevada is a top-tier paycheck state

Nevada is one of the handful of states with no personal income tax at all. It funds itself largely through sales tax, gaming revenue, and various business taxes rather than taxing wages. For a travel nurse, that means the taxable portion of your pay — your hourly base rate — is subject only to federal income tax and FICA, with nothing withheld for the state and no state return to file at year end. There is also no city income tax in Las Vegas, Reno, Henderson, or anywhere else in the state, so a contract in any Nevada city is taxed identically on the state side.

The home-state caveat that actually decides your savings

Here's the nuance travelers miss: earning wages in a no-tax state doesn't automatically mean you pay zero state income tax overall. What matters is your state of residence — your tax home. If your tax home is in a state that taxes its residents' worldwide income (say California or New York), that home state can still tax the wages you earned in Nevada, because you're a resident there. You'd get no benefit from Nevada's lack of a tax; you'd just pay your home state instead.

The travelers who capture the full Nevada advantage are those whose tax home is also in a no-income-tax state — Texas, Florida, Washington, Tennessee, and the like — or those who genuinely establish Nevada itself as their domicile. In that case, wages earned in Nevada face no state income tax anywhere. Many career travelers deliberately domicile in a no-tax state for exactly this reason.

No income tax doesn't cancel the tax-home rule. Nevada having no income tax says nothing about whether your stipends are tax-free. Stipends are tax-free only if you're duplicating expenses away from a legitimate tax home. If you have no real tax home, the IRS can treat you as itinerant and make your stipends taxable at the federal level — Nevada's zero state tax won't save you from that. Keep a genuine home base, keep returning to it, and keep the documentation.

The sales-tax trade-off

States without an income tax make up the revenue elsewhere, and in Nevada that's largely sales tax. Combined state-and-local sales tax in the metro areas runs on the higher side (often around 8% or more), which nibbles at day-to-day spending during your assignment. For most travelers this is a minor cost compared with the income-tax savings — you'd have to spend an enormous amount on taxable goods for the sales-tax difference to erase the benefit of paying no state income tax on your wages. Groceries and some essentials are taxed more favorably, softening the impact further.

Filing and paperwork

Because Nevada has no income tax, there's no Nevada state return to file — a real simplification at tax time. You'll still file your federal return, and you'll file a return for your home state if it has an income tax (reporting the Nevada wages there as a resident). If your tax home is a no-tax state, your state filing burden is essentially nil. As always, keep every contract, your stipend breakdown, and proof of your tax home; the absence of a state return doesn't reduce the importance of documenting that your stipends were legitimately tax-free.

Is a Nevada contract worth it?

For take-home pay, Nevada is hard to beat — no state tax on your wages, no city tax, no state return. The best value goes to travelers domiciled in another no-tax state, who pay zero state income tax on the assignment start to finish. If your tax home is a high-tax state, remember that your home state may still tax the income, so the Nevada advantage is smaller than the sticker suggests. Weigh the high sales tax and the cost of living in Las Vegas or Reno, but on the income-tax axis alone, Nevada is squarely in the top tier.

Bottom line: Nevada charges no state or local income tax and no state return — excellent for take-home pay, especially if your tax home is also tax-free. The trade-off is a high sales tax, and the tax-home rule still governs whether your stipends stay tax-free. Nail the domicile and Nevada is one of the best paycheck states on the map.
