CRNA School While Working: Can You Do Both? Real Advice

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

This is one of the most searched questions in nurse anesthesia forums, and the honest answer is: it depends on the program, the year of school, and what "working" means. Here's the reality, not the marketing version.

The Short Answer

In most accredited CRNA programs today, you cannot maintain a full-time nursing job during clinical rotations—which is the majority of the program. During didactic portions (typically the first semester to year), some students do part-time work. During clinical years, it's generally inadvisable and in some programs explicitly prohibited.

This has financial implications that catch many applicants off guard.

Why Programs Restrict Work During Clinicals

CRNA clinical rotations are not typical nursing shifts. You're in the OR at 6 AM for cases that may run into the evening. You may rotate to different hospitals, surgical centers, or even different cities. You're expected to be mentally sharp for airway management, pharmacology decisions, and hemodynamic management all day—then do didactic work and case preparation in the evening.

A CRNA student who works overnight at the hospital and then shows up to manage anesthesia for surgical patients is a patient safety concern. Programs take this seriously. Some programs have honor-code provisions prohibiting unauthorized employment during clinicals; others allow limited part-time work only with program director approval.

The Didactic Phase: More Flexibility

The first semester or year of most programs is heavy on classroom and simulation work with lighter clinical hours. Many students find they can work 12–20 hours per week during this period—usually weekends or evenings—without it compromising their academic performance.

Whether this is wise depends on you. First-semester CRNA coursework in advanced pharmacology, anatomy, and physiology is brutal for most students, especially those who've been working as nurses rather than studying. Some students who try to work during didactics discover by midterms that they're at risk of failing and have to quit the job anyway.

The smarter financial play: Save aggressively in the 12–24 months before starting the program. Living on a $100k–$120k ICU nurse salary while spending like you earn $60k creates a substantial savings cushion that can fund 28–36 months of school without requiring employment income.

Online vs. Hybrid vs. Residential Programs

Some programs offer hybrid formats where didactic coursework is completed largely online, with in-person simulation labs and immersive clinical blocks. These programs are not easier—but they can offer slightly more scheduling flexibility during non-clinical phases.

Fully residential programs with cohort-based schedules (all students move through the program together, on-site) offer the least flexibility but often the strongest peer support and clinical site coordination.

If working during school is a financial necessity for you, research program formats carefully and ask program directors directly: what percentage of your students work during the program, and what employment do they typically do?

Part-Time Per Diem: The Strategy That Sometimes Works

Some students successfully work occasional per diem nursing shifts—one or two per month—during clinical years, using vacation or personal days in the program. This requires:

  • A per diem contract that allows truly flexible scheduling (no guaranteed minimums)
  • No mandatory overtime or call requirements
  • A program that doesn't prohibit it
  • Confidence that you can show up rested and focused to your clinical assignment the next day

One or two shifts per month adds $500–$2,000 depending on your shift differential and market. It won't cover your expenses, but it maintains your nursing license active and provides a small income supplement.

The Financial Reality of CRNA School

Let's be direct about the math. A CRNA program lasting 30 months with $80,000 in tuition means:

  • Tuition: $80,000
  • Living expenses (rent, food, transportation, insurance): $2,000–$3,500/month × 30 months = $60,000–$105,000
  • Total cost of attendance: $140,000–$185,000
  • Lost nursing income (at $90,000/year × 2.5 years): $225,000 opportunity cost

Federal graduate PLUS loans cover tuition and living expenses with a single application. Most CRNA students take $100,000–$160,000 in loans. The math works because a CRNA earning $210,000 instead of $90,000 as a nurse is generating $120,000/year in additional earnings—enough to pay off $150,000 in loans in 18–24 months on an aggressive repayment plan.

If you're considering income-driven repayment or PSLF, the calculus changes—but for most CRNA graduates in private practice or non-qualifying employers, aggressive loan payoff is the right strategy.

Real Student Experiences

Students who successfully navigate CRNA school while doing occasional work have a few things in common: - They entered with substantial savings (6+ months of living expenses) - They worked per diem only, with zero required commitments - They were honest with their program director - They had a support system that handled household obligations they couldn't

Students who struggle trying to work during CRNA school typically: - Underestimated the academic load - Had financial obligations (mortgage, children's expenses) that required consistent income - Tried to maintain a regular part-time job with scheduled shifts - Didn't build savings in advance

What to Do Right Now

If you're planning to apply in the next 1–2 years: 1. Calculate your monthly living expenses, then reduce them aggressively 2. Save 12–18 months of living expenses before your start date 3. Research each target program's employment policy 4. Talk to current students (Reddit's r/BecomeACRNA is a useful resource; Facebook groups for specific programs often have current students willing to share) 5. Look into scholarships—the AANA Foundation awards grants to CRNA students, and some health systems offer tuition support in exchange for post-graduation employment commitments

CRNA school is survivable financially. It requires planning, sacrifice, and probably some loans—but graduates repay them quickly. The career math is sound.
