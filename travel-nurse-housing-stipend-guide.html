Travel Nurse Housing Stipend: How to Maximize Your Tax-Free Income

Part of the Travel Nursing Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

The housing stipend is the single most financially significant element of a travel nurse pay package—and the most misunderstood. When structured correctly, it's entirely tax-free. When handled wrong, it becomes taxable income and the IRS takes a significant cut. Here's exactly how to maximize it.

How the Housing Stipend Works

Travel nurses receive housing stipends as a reimbursement—technically a non-taxable reimbursement for expenses incurred while working away from home. The IRS allows this under the assumption that you're maintaining a permanent tax home (your primary residence) and incurring duplicate living expenses while working on assignment.

The key words: duplicate living expenses. You must actually have a tax home that you're paying for. If you've given up your permanent home, you're no longer a "traveler" in the IRS's view—you're an itinerant worker, and your stipend is taxable income.

This distinction is the foundation of travel nurse tax strategy.

What Is a Tax Home?

A tax home is your principal place of business, not necessarily where you live. For travel nurses, it's typically where you: - Have your primary nursing job or professional connections - Maintain a permanent residence (paying rent or mortgage) - Have significant personal, financial, and legal connections (bank, driver's license, voter registration, physician, family)

To legitimately claim housing stipends, you need a tax home you're genuinely maintaining. This means paying rent (even if it's below-market, even if you're renting from family at a reasonable rate) and returning there between assignments.

The Three-Part IRS Test

The IRS uses a three-factor test to determine if your travel expenses are deductible (and stipends non-taxable). You need to pass at least two of three:

  1. You have duplicate living expenses. You pay for a home at your tax home AND for housing at your assignment location.
  2. You have not abandoned your tax home. You return there between assignments, maintain connections there, and haven't given notice to vacate.
  3. You are away from your tax home temporarily. Individual assignments are 12 months or less, and you don't work in the same metro area indefinitely.

Failure on two or more factors = your stipends may be taxable.

How to Maximize Your Tax-Free Stipend

1. Establish a real tax home and protect it.

If you live in a high-cost state (California, New York, Massachusetts), consider whether your tax home can be in a lower-cost state. Many travel nurses maintain a room or small apartment with family in a low-cost-of-living state as their tax home, paying $400–$700/month. The key is that it's real, documented, and maintained.

2. Find your own housing on assignment.

Agencies offer two housing options: agency-provided housing (they arrange it, you lose the stipend) or stipend + find your own. The stipend is almost always the better financial choice. Platforms like Furnished Finder, Airbnb monthly, and Facebook housing groups for travel nurses often yield furnished apartments for $1,200–$1,800/month in most markets—well below the stipend.

Example math: - Agency housing stipend: $2,400/month - Your actual furnished housing cost on assignment: $1,500/month - Net surplus (tax-free): $900/month - Over a 13-week assignment: $2,700 in additional tax-free income

3. Know the GSA rates for your assignment location.

The General Services Administration publishes maximum per diem rates for lodging and meals and incidentals (M&IE) for every U.S. market. These rates set the ceiling for what your agency can pay tax-free per day for housing and meals. High-cost markets (San Francisco, NYC, DC) have higher GSA rates; rural markets have lower ones.

Some agencies pay up to the full GSA rate; others pay less. Knowing the GSA rate for your assignment location lets you negotiate intelligently.

4. Negotiate the stipend rate explicitly.

Agencies structure packages differently. Ask specifically: "What is the tax-free housing stipend per week?" and "What is the base hourly rate?" These two levers affect each other—agencies have a total bill rate from the facility, and they allocate it between hourly pay and stipend. Pushing for a higher stipend and lower hourly may be advantageous if you're in a lower tax bracket or want to minimize Social Security/Medicare taxes (you pay these on hourly pay but not stipends).

5. Document everything.

Keep records of your tax home expenses: rent receipts, bank statements, utility bills. Keep records of your housing expenses on assignment: lease agreements, payment receipts. If you're ever audited, documentation is the difference between a clean audit and a large tax bill.

What Not to Do

Don't accept agency housing and expect the stipend too. If the agency is providing your housing, you don't also get the housing stipend—that would be double-dipping and is prohibited.

Don't give up your permanent residence between assignments. Some travel nurses do this to save money. If you have no tax home, you have no legitimate basis for tax-free stipends. The short-term savings eliminate the long-term tax benefit.

Don't lie about your tax home. Having a "paper" tax home where you don't actually pay rent and haven't lived for years is tax fraud, not strategy. The IRS has audited travel nurses and the consequences are serious.

The Bottom Line

A well-structured travel nurse package with proper tax home documentation can result in $15,000–$30,000 of additional tax-free income per year compared with a staff nursing position at the same gross pay. The housing stipend is the engine of that advantage—protect it by protecting your tax home, finding your own housing on assignment, and keeping clean records.
