Villanova University, in the Philadelphia suburbs of Pennsylvania, offers a nurse anesthesiology program through its Fitzpatrick College of Nursing. Villanova carries a strong national brand and sits in a metro area dense with major teaching hospitals — a meaningful advantage for the clinical phase of anesthesia training. For ICU nurses in the Mid-Atlantic and beyond who are mapping a path to CRNA, Villanova is a recognizable option. Here's a practical, applicant-focused overview for 2026.

Verify the details before you apply. Program length, tuition, prerequisites, admissions criteria, and clinical partnerships change year to year and by cohort. Treat everything below as an orientation, not a source of truth — confirm current specifics directly with Villanova's nurse anesthesiology program office and official program materials before making decisions.

Program format and degree

Like every US nurse anesthesia program today, Villanova's culminates in a doctoral credential (a DNP-level nurse anesthesiology degree), in line with the national requirement that all new CRNAs graduate at the doctoral level. Programs of this type generally run about 36 months of full-time study — front-loaded didactic coursework followed by an intensive clinical residency across affiliated hospital and surgical sites. Villanova has historically developed its nurse anesthesia clinical training through partnerships with regional healthcare systems; because those affiliations shape where you rotate, confirm the current clinical-site network directly with the program.

Typical prerequisites

Nurse anesthesia admissions requirements are fairly standardized across programs. For Villanova, expect the core elements common to competitive CRNA schools:

  • An unencumbered RN license and a BSN (or equivalent) from an accredited program.
  • A minimum of one year — realistically more — of recent, full-time critical care experience in an adult ICU managing vasoactive drips, ventilators, and invasive lines.
  • A competitive GPA, with science and graduate coursework weighted heavily; many admitted applicants exceed a 3.5.
  • GRE requirements that vary by cycle — confirm whether the current year requires, recommends, or waives it.
  • CCRN certification, strong references, a personal statement, and a formal interview.
  • CRNA shadowing hours, and sometimes prerequisite graduate-level science depending on the year.

Admissions competitiveness

CRNA admission is competitive everywhere, and a well-known private university in the Philadelphia market draws a deep applicant pool. The differentiators are the standard ones: the acuity of your ICU experience (a high-acuity unit — CVICU, medical/surgical ICU, or trauma — reads stronger than a step-down), CCRN certification, an upward GPA trend, genuine understanding of the role through shadowing, and a strong interview that tests clinical reasoning. Treat every part of the application as a differentiator rather than a checkbox.

Cost and financing

Villanova is a private university, and private nurse anesthesia tuition typically reaches the six figures across the full program once fees, Philadelphia-area living costs, and the near-impossibility of working during the clinical phase are accounted for. Most students finance this with a mix of federal graduate loans and savings. The offset is the payoff — CRNAs are among the highest-earning advanced practice nurses, so the return on investment tends to be strong despite the sticker price. Model the numbers honestly with our CRNA cost breakdown before committing.

The admissions formula is the standard CRNA one — high-acuity adult ICU experience, CCRN, a strong GPA, shadowing, and a strong interview — and the private-school tuition is a real but usually worthwhile investment. Confirm the current clinical-site network and every other detail directly with the program before applying.
