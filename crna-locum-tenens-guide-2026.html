Earn $300k–$480k working assignments on your schedule — the complete guide for CRNAs

Locum tenens is the highest-paying arrangement available in the CRNA profession. While a staff CRNA averages $195,000–$235,000 annually, experienced locum CRNAs regularly clear $300,000–$480,000 — working the same number of hours, often fewer.

The math is simple: you trade the security of a W-2 position for premium per-diem rates, tax advantages unique to independent contractors, and the ability to choose your assignments. For experienced CRNAs who have paid off their student loans and built a financial foundation, locums is the wealth acceleration play most of the profession underutilizes.

What Locum Tenens Actually Is

Locum tenens (Latin: "one holding a place") means working temporary assignments at hospitals, surgery centers, and clinics that need CRNA coverage. You contract through a locum agency, work the agreed schedule, and move on. There's no PTO, no office politics, no mandatory committee work, and no 5-year career ladder.

A typical CRNA locum assignment:

  • Duration: 4–26 weeks per assignment
  • Schedule: Usually 40 hours/week, some 3×12, some 4×10
  • Housing: Typically paid by the facility or agency
  • Travel: Reimbursed or included
  • Rate: $175–$260/hour (vs. staff equivalent of $95–$130/hour)

The Tax Advantage That Multiplies Your Net Pay

1099 vs W-2: The real difference for high earners

A staff CRNA at $210,000 W-2 has no choice but to pay taxes on gross income. A locum CRNA operating through their own LLC or S-Corp can deduct:

  • Home office
  • Professional liability insurance
  • CME expenses and licensing
  • Travel to and from assignments (in some cases)
  • Health insurance premiums (self-employed deduction)
  • Retirement contributions up to $69,000/year via Solo 401k (vs. $23,500 W-2 limit)

A locum CRNA earning $380,000 who runs through an S-Corp and maximizes retirement contributions can reduce taxable income to $280,000–$300,000, while a W-2 CRNA at $210,000 has no such flexibility. The effective tax rate difference can be 8–12 percentage points on a high income.

The Solo 401k Advantage

This is the most financially significant tax structure available to locum CRNAs and it's widely underused. As a self-employed individual, you can contribute:

  • Employee side: $23,500 (2026 limit) as employee deferral
  • Employer side: Up to 25% of net self-employment income as employer contribution
  • Total possible: $69,000/year (2026 limit)

A W-2 staff CRNA maxes out at $23,500 in their 403b. A locum CRNA can shelter $69,000 — nearly 3x as much. On a $380,000 gross income, that's an additional $45,500 going into tax-advantaged accounts per year vs. a staff peer. Over 10 years at 7% growth: $650,000+ in additional investment portfolio from the tax structure alone.

How to Start as a Locum CRNA

  1. Build minimum 2 years of staff CRNA experience first. Most facilities require 2+ years of post-graduation clinical experience before placing a locum. Some require 3.
  2. Get your DEA registration and state licenses in target states. Interstate compact licensing helps but CRNA still requires individual state licenses in most states. Budget $2,000–$4,000 for multi-state licensure.
  3. Obtain tail coverage malpractice insurance. When you leave an employed position, your claims-made policy ends. Tail coverage protects past work. Budget $3,000–$6,000 for tail.
  4. Set up a business entity. Most experienced locums operate through an LLC or S-Corp to access the tax advantages above. Consult a CPA before your first assignment.
  5. Contact 2–3 agencies. Register with CompHealth, Weatherby, and one smaller boutique. Tell each your specialty (anesthesia), availability date, geographic preferences, and minimum rate.
  6. Negotiate your first contract. First offers are rarely best offers. Agencies build in margin. Asking for $10–$20/hour more with a competing offer is standard.

Before going fully locum: Build a 6-month expense reserve. Your first assignment gap, a contract cancellation, or a slower placement period shouldn't force you to take a bad assignment. Most CRNAs transition from staff to hybrid (some staff shifts + locum) before going fully locum.

Hybrid Strategy: Staff + Locum

The highest-earning CRNAs in the profession often run a hybrid model:

  • Part-time staff position at a home hospital (maintains benefits, DEA site, familiar environment)
  • Locum assignments during blocks of time off (fills income gaps, earns premium rates)

A CRNA working 36 hours/week at a staff position ($115/hr) plus 8 locum assignments/year at 40 hours each ($200/hr) can gross $280,000–$340,000 annually — significantly above a pure staff position — while maintaining the institutional connection most CRNAs want.

Common Mistakes

  • Not negotiating. Agencies expect it. If you don't ask, you don't get it.
  • Accepting assignments without verifying supervision model. Some facilities advertise CRNA-only but have physician anesthesiologists on-call. Understand the autonomy structure before accepting.
  • Failing to set up retirement before the first paycheck. You have until tax filing deadline to open a Solo 401k for the prior year, but you need it opened before December 31 to make contributions.
  • Not tracking every work-related expense. Mileage, licensure, continuing education, scrubs, stethoscopes — all deductible for 1099 CRNAs. Use a dedicated business credit card and a simple spreadsheet from day one.

The Bottom Line

CRNA locum tenens is the clearest path to $300k+ in the profession for those with experience. The combination of premium per-diem rates, housing coverage, and self-employment tax advantages creates a financial structure that's significantly superior to W-2 employment for CRNAs who can handle variable income and minimal job security.

The two requirements: 2+ years of staff experience first, and a 6-month financial runway before your first gap. Build both, then move. The earning ceiling in this profession is much higher than most CRNAs realize.
