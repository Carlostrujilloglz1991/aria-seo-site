Nursing Staffing Ratios 2026: State Laws and What They Mean for You

Only one state (California) mandates specific nurse-to-patient ratios by law. Most nurses work under hospital policies with no legal floor.

What Non-California States Do

Most states require hospitals to develop and post staffing plans, but do not set legal minimums. Staffing is determined by hospital administration based on census, acuity, and budget. This is why ICU ratios in Texas might be 1:3 on a bad census day and 1:2 on a good one — it varies shift to shift.

What nurses report: In non-ratio states, nurses frequently report 1:3 ICU assignments during staffing shortages. Document unsafe assignments using your union grievance process or the hospital's safe staffing documentation form. This creates a paper trail for regulatory complaints.

States With Active Ratio Legislation (2026)

Massachusetts, Illinois, New York, and Oregon have active staffing ratio legislation being debated as of 2026. Several have passed acuity-based staffing mandates that require documented nurse-to-patient ratios but not specific numbers.

Travel Nurses and Staffing Ratios

In California, staffing ratios are legally enforceable even for travelers — hospitals cannot use travelers to circumvent the ratios. This is one reason California travel contracts pay more: compliance with ratio law costs hospitals more per patient. If you're assigned over ratio in California, you can (and should) refuse the unsafe assignment and document it.
