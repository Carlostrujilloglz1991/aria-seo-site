1. What a 403(b) Is

A 403(b) is a tax-advantaged retirement savings account available to employees of nonprofit organizations, hospitals, public schools, and some government entities. Think of it as the hospital version of a 401(k) — the mechanics are nearly identical, but 403(b)s come with a few unique features that can work in your favor.

Like a 401(k), a 403(b) lets you contribute a portion of your paycheck before it's taxed (Traditional) or after it's taxed (Roth), the money grows tax-deferred or tax-free inside the account, and you pay taxes (or not, for Roth) when you withdraw it in retirement. Your contributions are invested in mutual funds, annuities, or target-date funds available inside your hospital's plan.

One unique feature of some 403(b) plans: nurses with at least 15 years of service with the same employer may qualify for an additional catch-up contribution called the "15-year rule," allowing up to an extra $3,000 per year above the standard limits. Ask your HR department or plan administrator if your plan supports this.

💡 403(b) vs 401(k): The Practical Difference
For most nurses, the difference between a 403(b) and a 401(k) is administrative. The contribution limits are the same. The tax treatment is the same. The main practical difference: 403(b) plans are more commonly offered with annuity options and may have older, higher-cost investment menus. Always check your expense ratios — more on this in Section 6.

2. 2026 Contribution Limits

The IRS sets annual limits on how much you can contribute to a 403(b). For 2026, the limits are:

  • Employee contribution limit (under 50): $23,500
  • Catch-up contribution (age 50 and older): Additional $7,500, for a total of $31,000
  • Ages 60–63 (SECURE 2.0 enhanced catch-up): Additional $11,250 instead of $7,500, for a total of $34,750
  • Total combined limit (employee + employer contributions): $70,000

These limits apply per person, not per account. If you have both a 403(b) and a 457(b) at the same hospital, you can max both independently — more on this in Section 5.

3. Traditional vs Roth 403(b): Which One Nurses Should Choose

This is the most common question nurses ask about their 403(b), and the answer depends on one thing: when you expect to pay a lower tax rate — now or in retirement.

Traditional 403(b)

Contributions come from pre-tax dollars. You reduce your taxable income today, the money grows tax-deferred, and you pay ordinary income tax on withdrawals in retirement. If you're in a high tax bracket now and expect to be in a lower bracket in retirement, Traditional wins.

Roth 403(b)

Contributions come from after-tax dollars. You pay tax now, the money grows tax-free, and qualified withdrawals in retirement are completely tax-free — including all the growth. If you're early in your career, expect your income to rise significantly, or want tax-free retirement income, Roth wins.

The Roth vs. Traditional Decision Flowchart for Nurses

Answer each question in order — stop at your first match.

Q1
Are you in the 22% federal bracket or lower?
Single: income under ~$100k  /  Married: under ~$190k
YES → Choose Roth. You're paying a low rate now; future growth is entirely tax-free.
NO → Continue to Q2.
Q2
Are you in the 24%–32% bracket AND actively pursuing PSLF?
Pre-tax contributions reduce your AGI, which lowers your IDR payment (see Section 7)
YES → Choose Traditional. Pre-tax 403(b) cuts your taxes AND your PSLF payments simultaneously.
NO → Continue to Q3.
Q3
Are you 50+ with a large Traditional balance already?
Large Traditional balances trigger Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) at age 73 — taxable and forced
YES → Choose Roth for new contributions to diversify your retirement tax exposure.
NO → Default to Traditional if you're in a high bracket and not on PSLF.
Pragmatic shortcut for most nurses: Roth early in career (under 35, income still climbing). Traditional when income peaks or you're on PSLF. Split 50/50 if genuinely unsure — you preserve flexibility on both sides.

4. Employer Match: The Free Money Most Nurses Leave on the Table

Many hospitals match 403(b) contributions — the most common structure is 50% of your contributions up to 6% of salary, or dollar-for-dollar up to 3–4%. This is an instant 50–100% return on that portion of your savings. Nothing in personal finance consistently outperforms it.

The Real Cost of Not Capturing Your Full Match

A nurse earning $80,000 at a hospital that matches 50% of contributions up to 6% of salary: if she contributes only 3% instead of the full 6%, she captures only half the match — leaving $1,200 per year unclaimed. Over 10 years at a 7% growth rate, that unclaimed match compounds to over $16,500. Over 20 years, it exceeds $47,000. The employer match is always Step 1 — never leave it uncaptured.

✓ Quick Action Item
Log into your benefits portal or ask HR: (1) What is your 403(b) match rate? (2) What percentage must I contribute to get the full match? Then confirm your current rate meets or exceeds that threshold.

Vesting Schedule: When Is the Match Actually Yours?

Most employer matches vest on a schedule. Common options: 3-year cliff vesting (0% until year 3, then 100%) or graded vesting (20% per year over 5 years). Your own contributions are always 100% yours immediately. Before leaving a job, check your vesting date — leaving 3 months before a cliff date can forfeit thousands.

5. 403(b) vs 457(b): Hospitals Often Offer Both — Which to Use First

Many nonprofit hospitals offer both a 403(b) and a 457(b). These are two separate plans with separate contribution limits — you can max both in the same year, doubling your tax-advantaged retirement space. But they work differently in important ways.

Feature 403(b) 457(b)
2026 contribution limit $23,500 (+ catch-up) $23,500 (+ catch-up)
Employer match common Yes Rare
Early withdrawal penalty (before 59½) 10% penalty No penalty
Can max independently of the other Yes Yes
3-year pre-retirement catch-up available No Yes (up to 2x limit)
Asset protection if employer defaults Protected (held in trust) At risk (employer assets)
Best for Long-term retirement savings Early retirement / bridge income

Priority Order

Fund your 403(b) first, up to the full employer match. After that: if you plan to retire before 59½ or want maximum flexibility, direct additional savings to the 457(b) next (no early withdrawal penalty). If you have no plans for early retirement, continue maxing the 403(b) before funding the 457(b).

💡 Combined Contribution Power
Maxing both accounts shelters $47,000 ($23,500 x 2) from income taxes in a single year — before touching an IRA. This is one of the most underused wealth-building advantages available to hospital employees, and most nurses don't know it exists.

6. Investment Options: What to Pick Inside Your 403(b)

The investments available in your 403(b) depend on your hospital's plan. Many 403(b) plans — particularly at smaller hospitals — still include insurance company annuities with high fees and surrender charges. If you see "variable annuity," "fixed annuity," or "insurance company subaccounts" anywhere in your investment menu, look closely at the expense ratios before investing.

What to Look For

Prioritize low-cost index funds with expense ratios under 0.20% per year. A fund charging 1.0% vs 0.05% might look like a minor difference — but on a $200,000 balance, the expensive fund drains $1,900 per year that could otherwise compound for you. Over 20 years, high fees can consume $100,000+ of your retirement wealth.

The Simple Portfolio for Most Nurses

If you're more than 10 years from retirement: a single target-date index fund (Vanguard Target Retirement, Fidelity Freedom Index, or Schwab Target Date equivalent for your expected retirement year) is the default-best choice. It diversifies automatically, rebalances itself, and shifts more conservative as you age. Target-date index funds should have expense ratios under 0.15%.

If your plan lacks a low-cost target-date option, build a simple allocation using whatever index funds are available: a U.S. total market index, an international stock index, and a bond index. A common age-based starting point: subtract your age from 110 to get your stock allocation percentage (the remainder in bonds).

⚠️ Watch for Annuity Products Inside Your 403(b)
Some 403(b) salespeople visit nurses directly at hospitals. Variable annuity products inside 403(b) accounts typically have expense ratios of 1.5–2.5%, plus surrender charges of 5–8% if you try to move the money. These costs compound silently for decades. If anyone pitches a 403(b) product with a "surrender schedule" or "insurance wrapper," use your plan's direct mutual fund options instead.

7. The 403(b) and PSLF Connection

This is the most valuable section in this guide for any nurse who is both pursuing PSLF and contributing to a 403(b) — yet most nurses never hear about this interaction.

Your income-driven repayment (IDR) payment for PSLF is calculated as a percentage of your discretionary income, derived from your adjusted gross income (AGI). Traditional (pre-tax) 403(b) contributions directly reduce your AGI. Lower AGI means lower discretionary income, which means a lower IDR payment, which means you pay less out of pocket over your 10 PSLF years — while the same remaining balance is forgiven at the end.

A Concrete Example

Nurse earning $85,000 per year, $90,000 in student loans, pursuing PSLF on the SAVE plan (approximately 5% of discretionary income above 225% of the federal poverty line):

  • Without 403(b) contributions: AGI = $85,000 → estimated payment ~$292/month → $35,040 paid over 10 years
  • With $15,000/year pre-tax 403(b): AGI = $70,000 → estimated payment ~$169/month → $20,280 paid over 10 years
  • Difference: $14,760 less paid toward loans — while simultaneously accumulating $150,000+ in tax-deferred retirement savings at a 7% return

Every pre-tax dollar going into the 403(b) is doing three jobs simultaneously: reducing current income tax, reducing PSLF loan payments, and building retirement wealth. This is the highest-leverage financial move available to most hospital nurses.

✓ The PSLF + 403(b) Rule
If you are pursuing PSLF, always use Traditional (pre-tax) 403(b) contributions — not Roth. The pre-tax deduction lowers your AGI, which lowers your IDR payment, which means you pay less out of pocket while the same balance is forgiven at year 10. This one decision can save $10,000–$20,000+ over the PSLF period.

8. How Much to Contribute: The Nurse Financial Waterfall

The order in which you fund accounts matters as much as the amounts. Work through these steps in sequence — complete each before moving to the next:

1

Capture Your Full Employer 403(b) Match

Contribute at least enough to receive every dollar your employer will match. This is a guaranteed 50–100% return on that portion of your savings — the highest guaranteed return available anywhere. Ask HR for your exact match rate and set your contribution to capture it fully. This is always first.

2

Build a 3-Month Emergency Fund (if not already there)

Before locking more money in retirement accounts, hold 3 months of expenses in a liquid account (high-yield savings or credit union). This prevents early 403(b) withdrawals, which trigger a 10% penalty plus income tax — one emergency fund prevents years of compounding damage.

3

Max a Roth IRA (or Traditional IRA if on PSLF)

2026 limit: $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+). A Roth IRA gives you broader investment options than your hospital's 403(b) menu. If income is under $150k (single) or $236k (married), contribute directly. Exception: if on PSLF, use Traditional IRA to reduce AGI further and lower your IDR payment.

4

Max Your 403(b) to $23,500

After the match and IRA, direct additional savings here. If pursuing PSLF: pre-tax (Traditional) contributions lower your IDR payment and current taxes. If not on PSLF: choose Traditional or Roth using the flowchart in Section 3. Increase contributions by 1% per year until you reach the max.

5

Fund the 457(b) if Available (another $23,500)

If your hospital offers a 457(b) and you have additional savings capacity, this is your next best tax shelter. Especially valuable if you plan to retire before 59½ — no early withdrawal penalty at any age. Another $23,500 of tax-advantaged space on top of your 403(b) maximum.

6

Taxable Brokerage (after all tax-advantaged space is filled)

Only after exhausting Steps 1–5. Use low-cost ETFs, prioritize tax-efficient funds, and consider tax-loss harvesting. Most nurses won't reach this step early in their career — and that's a sign the plan is working, not a failure.
