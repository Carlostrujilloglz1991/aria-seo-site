Texas Wesleyan vs Samford CRNA: The Multi-State Giant or the Birmingham Hub?

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Updated July 2026  |  More CRNA guides →

Scope note: Educational comparison for ICU nurses planning applications — not admissions advice from either school. Tuition, prerequisites, clinical-site lists, and policies change every cycle. Confirm current specifics directly with Texas Wesleyan's Graduate Programs of Nurse Anesthesia and Samford's Moffett & Sanders School of Nursing before applying.

For ICU nurses across the South, Texas Wesleyan and Samford are two of the most commonly shortlisted private CRNA programs — and they represent nearly opposite bets. Texas Wesleyan in Fort Worth runs one of the largest nurse anesthesia programs in the country, with a hallmark front-loaded design and clinical sites spread across multiple states. Samford in Birmingham runs a regionally rooted doctoral program embedded in one of the Southeast's densest medical cities. Same degree destination, very different three years.

The one-sentence difference

Texas Wesleyan trades geographic stability for scale and network reach; Samford trades national sprawl for a stay-in-one-city Southeastern home base. At Texas Wesleyan you complete front-loaded didactics in Fort Worth, then disperse to a clinical site that may be several states away. At Samford, Birmingham's hospital systems are the training ground, and most residents plant themselves in one metro for the duration.

The relocation question is the real decision

Be brutally honest about this one variable before anything else. Texas Wesleyan's distributed clinical network is a genuine strength — varied practice environments, exposure to different anesthesia care models, and sites that sometimes include high-autonomy rural placements that independent-practice-minded residents covet. But it can mean two moves in three years (to Fort Worth, then to your clinical site), which is a completely different proposition for a single nurse with a paid-off car than for a nurse with a working spouse, a mortgage, and kids in school. Samford's one-city model is the family-stability play: Birmingham is a major Southeastern medical hub with substantial surgical volume and acuity, and you can sign a three-year lease and be done with logistics.

Scale vs intimacy

Texas Wesleyan's size cuts both ways. A large cohort means a massive alumni network — useful for jobs in nearly every state — plus mature systems, deep faculty benches, and a program that has seen every problem before. It also means you're one of many, and your clinical experience depends heavily on which site you land. Samford's smaller regional footprint means more predictable training and tighter faculty relationships, with a network that is densest in Alabama and the surrounding Southeast. Match the network to where you actually want to work: national-anywhere favors Texas Wesleyan's reach; Alabama/Georgia/Tennessee/Florida panhandle favors Samford's regional gravity.

Cost and mission fit

Both are private universities, so neither competes on sticker price — pull current tuition from each program and budget the full three years, including the cost of any relocations (moving twice is a real, four-figure line item most applicants forget). Living costs in Fort Worth and Birmingham are both moderate by national standards; the wildcard is Texas Wesleyan's clinical phase, where your cost of living depends on the site you draw. One more soft factor: Samford is an explicitly faith-based institution, which some applicants actively seek and others should at least be aware of when reading the program's culture. Run the numbers with our cost guide and funding guide.

Admissions: the same Southern-competitive bar

Both expect an accredited BSN, unencumbered license, competitive overall and science GPA, at least a year of adult critical care (with high-acuity depth mattering more than raw months), CCRN, shadowing, and a selective interview. Texas Wesleyan's volume means it reviews an enormous applicant pool — polish and differentiation matter; Samford's regional identity rewards applicants who can articulate why the Southeast, and why a mission-driven program. Prep with our interview guide and personal statement examples.

Quick fit test: Mobile, network-hungry, and drawn to a big program with clinical sites (and job connections) across the country? Texas Wesleyan. Rooted in the Southeast, prioritizing family stability in one affordable medical hub, and comfortable in a faith-based institution? Samford. The front-loaded academics feel similar at both — the lifestyle logistics do not.
