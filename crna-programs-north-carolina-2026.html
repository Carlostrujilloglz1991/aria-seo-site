CRNA Programs in North Carolina 2026

North Carolina has six accredited nurse anesthesia programs, and the tuition gap between the cheapest and most expensive is enormous — roughly $46,000 to nearly $180,000. Here's how the six compare and how an ICU nurse builds a North Carolina application that earns an interview.

North Carolina CRNA market context: North Carolina pairs nationally ranked academic medical centers (Duke, Wake Forest, UNC) with large regional trauma systems in Charlotte and Greenville. New graduates in North Carolina commonly start around $185,000–$210,000, with locum and rural assignments reaching higher. Because the state's public programs are dramatically cheaper than its private ones, school choice can swing your total cost of attendance by six figures.

Duke University — Durham

Duke's nurse anesthesia DNP is the state's marquee private option and one of the most recognized programs in the Southeast. It costs an estimated $179,778 in tuition over a 36-month full-time plan, and the program received a 10-year reaccreditation in October 2023 (next COA review scheduled for April 2033). Applicants get an elite brand and the case volume of Duke University Hospital — at private-university tuition that demands a hard look at financing.

East Carolina University — Greenville

East Carolina is the value play of the state. In-state tuition runs around $46,000 — a fraction of Duke's — and the program has posted strong outcomes, including a 92% first-time national certification pass rate in 2023 with full employment for graduates. For a North Carolina resident who can relocate to Greenville and train at the high-acuity ECU Health Medical Center (a Level I trauma center), ECU is one of the best cost-to-outcome combinations in the region.

UNC Charlotte and UNC Greensboro

UNC Charlotte runs a collaborative program with Carolinas Medical Center (Atrium Health), giving students access to one of the busiest trauma and surgical systems in the Carolinas. UNC Greensboro's 36-month nurse anesthesia concentration has posted a high first-time pass rate — 96% in 2024 — and offers a public-university cost structure in the Piedmont Triad. Both are strong, more affordable alternatives to the private programs for in-state applicants.

Wake Forest and Western Carolina

Wake Forest School of Medicine has been training nurse anesthetists since 1942 — one of the oldest programs in the nation — and runs a full-time three-year DNP holding 10-year COA accreditation. Western Carolina University serves the western part of the state with a 36-month DNP, expanding access for nurses in the Asheville region who would otherwise have to relocate east. Together, the six programs make North Carolina one of the more navigable states for an in-state CRNA applicant.

The Application Timeline from North Carolina

Working backward from a fall 2027 start: most North Carolina programs accept applications roughly September–December 2026, interview January–March 2027, and decide by spring 2027. Have your CCRN completed and at least one year (ideally two) of high-acuity ICU hours documented before applications open. With six programs across the state, apply to several in-state — mixing a private reach (Duke or Wake Forest) with one or two more affordable public options (ECU, UNCG, UNC Charlotte).

The tuition gap is the decision: Duke costs roughly four times what East Carolina charges an in-state resident. Over a career, the prestige difference rarely justifies a six-figure tuition gap unless you have a specific reason to want the brand. Model total cost of attendance plus three years of lost income against expected salary before you commit — for most North Carolina residents, a strong public program is the smarter financial path.
North Carolina CRNA salary on graduation: New CRNAs in North Carolina metros commonly earn an estimated $185,000–$210,000 in hospital employment, with locum and rural assignments pushing higher. Charlotte, the Triangle, and the Triad all carry steady surgical volume and anesthesia demand.
