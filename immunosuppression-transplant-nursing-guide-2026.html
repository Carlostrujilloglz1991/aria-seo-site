HyperacuteMinutes to hours after reperfusionPreformed recipient antibodies against donor antigens (ABO incompatibility or pre-sensitized)Immediate graft failure; organ becomes mottled, cyanotic on the table; fever, hypotensionNo effective treatment — graft must be removed immediately. Acute (cellular)Days to 3 months (can occur anytime)T-cell mediated immune response against donor HLA antigensFever, graft tenderness and swelling, decreased organ function (rising Cr for kidney, rising LFTs for liver, rising BNP for heart)High-dose IV corticosteroids (pulse methylprednisolone); anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG) for steroid-refractory

4. Infection in the Immunosuppressed Patient

Immunosuppressed patients cannot mount a normal immune response — infections present ATYPICALLY:
  • Fever may be absent or blunted even with serious infection
  • WBC may be normal or only mildly elevated
  • Symptoms may be minimal until infection is severe
  • Low threshold to culture, image, and treat

Infection Timeline Post-Transplant

Time PeriodPredominant Infections
First monthDonor-derived infections, surgical site infections, hospital-acquired infections (MRSA, C. diff, Candida, gram-negatives), UTI (Foley-related)
1–6 months (peak immunosuppression)Opportunistic infections: CMV (most common), PCP (Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia), fungal (Aspergillus, Candida), Toxoplasma, EBV/PTLD, BK virus (kidney)
Beyond 6 monthsCommunity-acquired infections similar to general population; chronic rejection → bronchiectasis/recurrent pneumonias (lung); reactivation of latent infections (TB, histoplasmosis)

CMV (Cytomegalovirus) — Most Important Post-Transplant Infection

Risk: Donor CMV+ / Recipient CMV- (highest risk)
Presentation: fever, malaise, leukopenia, organ involvement (esophagitis/colitis/pneumonitis/retinitis)
Prevention: valganciclovir prophylaxis × 3–6 months
Treatment: IV ganciclovir → oral valganciclovir; reduce immunosuppression if possible
Monitoring: CMV PCR levels

5. Graft-Versus-Host Disease (GVHD)

Occurs in BONE MARROW/STEM CELL transplants (not solid organ transplants — reversed situation).
The transplanted donor immune cells attack the recipient's tissues.

Acute GVHD (first 100 days): Skin rash (maculopapular, starts on palms/soles/ears), diarrhea (can be massive, watery, bloody), elevated LFTs/jaundice
Chronic GVHD (after 100 days): Skin changes (lichen planus-like, scleroderma-like), dry eyes/mouth, lung disease (BOS), joint contractures, cytopenias

Treatment: High-dose corticosteroids first-line; ruxolitinib for steroid-refractory
Prevention: tacrolimus + methotrexate; HLA matching; T-cell depletion

6. Post-Transplant Nursing Priorities

  • Infection control: strict hand hygiene; private room; neutropenic precautions if leukopenic; avoid fresh flowers, plants, and raw foods; limit visitor exposure
  • Medication adherence: never miss doses of immunosuppressants; missing doses = rejection risk; educate on importance
  • Rejection surveillance: daily weights; UO monitoring (kidney); daily creatinine; assess for organ-specific rejection signs
  • Sun protection: skin cancer risk is 65× higher in transplant patients; sunscreen, hats, long sleeves mandatory
  • Cancer screening: increased risk of lymphoma (PTLD), skin cancer, cervical cancer, anal cancer; regular screenings
  • Drug interactions: tacrolimus and cyclosporine have extensive drug interactions through CYP3A4 — azithromycin, fluconazole, diltiazem increase levels; rifampin dramatically DECREASES levels; always check interactions
NCLEX High-Yield: Transplant & Immunosuppression
  • Hyperacute rejection: within minutes to hours; preformed antibodies; graft removed immediately
  • Acute rejection: days to months; T-cell mediated; treat with high-dose steroids
  • Tacrolimus: nephrotoxicity + neurotoxicity (tremors); monitor trough levels
  • Mycophenolate: major side effect = GI disturbances (diarrhea); teratogenic — contraindicated in pregnancy
  • Corticosteroids: hyperglycemia, infection risk, poor wound healing, avascular necrosis of hip
  • CMV: most common opportunistic infection post-transplant; prophylaxis with valganciclovir
  • GVHD: bone marrow transplant only; donor cells attack recipient; skin/GI/liver triad
  • Immunosuppressed patients: fever may be absent; low threshold to suspect infection
  • Skin cancer risk: 65× higher in transplant patients; strict sun protection required
  • Sirolimus/everolimus: hold perioperatively (impairs wound healing)
