Travel Nursing vs Staff: A 5-Year Income Comparison

Part of the Nurse Money Hub — income strategy, travel pay, and nurse financial planning in one map.

The average travel nurse earns $95,000–$115,000 per year in total compensation. The average staff RN earns $72,000–$92,000. The gross gap looks decisive — but it isn't the whole story. Benefits, retirement matching, tax treatment, and gap weeks all cut into the travel premium. Here is what the 5-year comparison actually looks like.

The real gap after adjustments: A travel nurse grossing $110,000 and a staff nurse grossing $82,000 end up about $12,000–$18,000 apart in true annual net compensation once you account for benefits, 403(b) match, and tax-free stipend validity. That's still meaningfully more for travel — but not the $28,000 the headline suggests.

The benefits gap: what travel nurses don't get

Staff nurses at most hospital systems receive a benefits package worth $18,000–$26,000 in annual value — often invisible because it never shows up as cash. Travel nurses receive limited benefits through their agency, typically worth $4,000–$8,000. The gap is real and large.

The gap weeks problem

Travel nurses assume 4 contracts per year at 13 weeks each = 52 weeks. In reality, most travel nurses have 3–6 weeks of gap between contracts. Some gaps are voluntary (vacation); others aren't (market softness, credentialing delays, failed facility audits). At $2,100/week net, a 4-week involuntary gap costs $8,400. Staff nurses receive full pay during PTO and continue accruing benefits during downtime.

The retirement contribution gap: the silent wealth killer

This is the most underestimated factor. A staff nurse at $82,000 with a 4% employer match contributes $3,280 in match annually. After 5 years at 7% average return, that's approximately $19,000 in matched + compounded money the travel nurse never received. If the travel nurse fails to self-fund retirement at equivalent levels (possible but requires discipline), the wealth gap on this factor alone rivals 6 months of the income premium.

The optimal play: Several nurses are doing this strategically — 3–5 years of travel nursing to eliminate student loans and build a 6-figure emergency fund, then transitioning to staff nursing for stability, benefits, and PSLF eligibility. The travel years are high-income, low-expense years that front-load wealth building. See our student loan forgiveness guide for how PSLF factors into this timeline.

When staff wins financially

Staff nursing beats travel nursing on a total-compensation basis when: (1) the hospital offers a strong pension (increasingly rare), (2) you're close to 10 years of PSLF payments with a large loan balance, (3) you have a working spouse with employer health coverage (eliminating the benefits gap), or (4) you work in a union hospital in California or Washington where base pay is already $110,000+.
