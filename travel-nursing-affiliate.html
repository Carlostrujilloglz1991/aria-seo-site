The Pay Gap Is Real — and Wider Than You Think

Your hospital is paying you $42 an hour. The travel nurse in your unit makes $65 an hour plus a $1,100 weekly housing stipend. That's not a rumor — it's the arithmetic of how hospitals use staffing agencies to fill holes without raising their permanent staff's wages. The result is a two-tier compensation system running openly inside the same unit, and most staff nurses tolerate it for years before asking whether they could be on the other side of it.

The total weekly difference between a staff ICU nurse at $42/hr (36 hours, after taxes) and a travel nurse on a comparable package is often $1,200–$1,800 net per week — not because travel nurses are more skilled, but because the market pays a scarcity premium. A nurse with 1–2 years of ICU, ED, or OR experience can usually qualify for travel contracts. The barrier is mostly paperwork, licensure, and knowing which agencies are worth your time.

This guide cuts through the agency marketing. The numbers below are ranges drawn from publicly posted packages and industry benchmarks as of mid-2026. Individual offers vary by specialty, facility, shift, and — most importantly — how well you negotiate.

Key number: Housing stipends are tax-free if you maintain a permanent tax home. That distinction can be worth $15,000–$25,000 in effective annual income that never shows on your W-2. Understanding the compliance rules matters more than picking the "highest paying" agency.

How to Choose the Right Agency

The 3 Questions to Ask Every Recruiter Before Signing

  1. "Can you send me the full pay breakdown in writing, line by line?" Any recruiter who hedges on this is hiding something. A legitimate agency will email you a document showing: taxable hourly rate, overtime rate, housing stipend amount, M&IE stipend, any bonuses, and the shift breakdown. If they quote you a "blended rate" or "package total" without the line items — move on. The line items determine your taxes, your compliance, and whether the stipend is legal.
  2. "What is my guaranteed minimum weekly hours, and what happens if the hospital cancels my shift?" Most contracts guarantee 36 hours per week. The fine print on cancellation varies widely. Some agencies pay you your taxable base (only) for cancelled shifts. Others pay your full hourly rate. A few offer nothing. This difference can mean $400–$700 on a single cancelled 12-hour shift — and hospitals cancel travel nurse shifts during census drops.
  3. "What's the benefits enrollment date, and is day-one coverage available?" Host Healthcare is the best-known agency for day-one medical coverage. Others have a waiting period of 30–60 days. For a 13-week contract, a 30-day waiting period means you're uninsured for the first month of your assignment. Factor that into your comparison — especially if you're leaving behind employer-sponsored insurance.

Red Flags: When to Walk Away

Red flag #1: The recruiter can't provide a written pay breakdown. Verbal promises about pay packages are unenforceable and frequently optimistic. If the offer doesn't exist in writing, it doesn't exist.
Red flag #2: The "guaranteed hours" clause has carve-outs for "low census." Some contracts allow hospitals to cancel shifts freely under a low-census clause, with the agency paying you nothing for lost shifts. Read this section of every contract before signing.
Red flag #3: The agency can't clearly explain the tax-home requirement. If your recruiter doesn't know what a tax home is, that agency's compliance infrastructure is weak. IRS scrutiny of travel nurse stipends has increased since 2023 — the wrong setup can result in back taxes on all stipend income for the duration of your contract.

The Compliance Package Explained

Travel nurse pay packages are split into two categories: taxable and non-taxable.

Taxable: Your base hourly rate, overtime, shift differentials, and any on-call pay. This shows on your W-2 and is treated like regular income. Most agencies set the taxable base at $22–$28/hr to maximize the non-taxable portion.

Non-taxable (stipends): The housing stipend and the M&IE (meals and incidental expenses) per diem are tax-free — but only if you qualify. To qualify, you must maintain a permanent tax home (a residence in another city where you have continuing financial obligations), and you cannot travel to the same metro area for more than 12 months. The IRS requires these stipends to reimburse actual duplicate expenses, not generate extra income. If the IRS audits and finds you don't have a legitimate tax home, all stipend income becomes retroactively taxable.

Practical rule: You need to be able to document your tax home with a lease, mortgage, or utility bills in your name. If you gave up your apartment before traveling and have no permanent home, talk to a travel nurse tax specialist before accepting any stipend income. The agencies do not vet this for you — compliance is your responsibility.

Compare Offers and Negotiate

The single best leverage move in travel nursing is applying to multiple agencies simultaneously. Agencies know you're doing this, and it's not bad form — it's the expected behavior of any experienced traveler. When you have two written offers in hand, you can ask Agency A to match Agency B's package. Most will, within limits. The nurses who negotiate earn $100–$300 more per week than those who take the first offer.

Don't limit yourself to one specialty listing. If you're an ICU nurse, also check ER and step-down listings — the acuity threshold varies by hospital and some step-down contracts pay comparably with less overnight intensity. California contracts typically pay $800–$1,200/week more than the national average due to mandatory nurse-to-patient ratios driving scarcity; if you have a California RN license or are eligible for endorsement, prioritize getting it.
