Nurse Freelance Writing: How to Earn $75-$200/Article

Healthcare content is one of the best-paid writing niches in freelancing — and you already have the credential that content editors most want: an active clinical license. A healthcare company or publication can hire any writer; they can only hire you if you're actually a nurse. That distinction is worth money.

Nurses who freelance write typically earn $75–$200 per article at entry level, rising to $200–$500 per piece at mid-level, and $1,000–$3,000+ per white paper or long-form report as they build a portfolio. Working 4–6 hours per week on freelance writing can realistically generate $400–$800/month in supplemental income within the first three months.

Who Hires Nurse Writers

Continuing education platforms: NursingCE, Relias, ProCE, Nurse.com, and Lippincott all produce CE content and hire clinical writers. These are the most accessible entry points for new nurse writers. Pay ranges from $150–$600 per 1-contact-hour module.

Health information websites: Healthline, Verywell Health, Medical News Today, and similar consumer health sites hire clinically credentialed writers to review and author patient-facing content. Pay: $75–$200 per article, with high-volume options for fast writers.

Hospital and healthcare system content teams: Many large hospital systems (HCA, CommonSpirit, Kaiser) have internal marketing teams that produce blog content, patient education materials, and email newsletters. They hire freelance clinical writers at $50–$150/hour.

Medical device and pharma companies: Sales training materials, clinical product guides, and educational brochures for medical device companies pay well — $100–$300/hour equivalent — because they require clinical accuracy and aren't patient-facing (lower regulatory burden than pharmaceutical copy).

Staffing and nursing publications: Nursing journals (AJN, Nursing2026, Nurse Practitioner journal), travel nursing blogs, and nursing career sites hire nurse contributors. Pay is often on the lower end ($50–$150 per article) but bylined work builds your portfolio fast.

What You Actually Need to Start

You do not need a journalism degree, a writing course, or a blog. You need:

A one-page writing resume that lists your RN license, specialty, years of experience, and any published work (including that charge nurse newsletter you wrote in 2022). Three writing samples — if you have none, write three. A 500-word patient education piece on a topic you know well, a 750-word "what to expect during X procedure" article, and a 1,000-word clinical tips article for nursing colleagues. These three samples constitute a portfolio.

A PayPal or Venmo account or a business checking account to receive payments. Many healthcare content companies prefer ACH transfers — open a simple business checking account (Relay and Bluevine are free options) so you have a professional-looking payment setup.

The fastest path to your first $100: Search "nurse writer" on Indeed and LinkedIn Jobs. Apply to any job that says "RN preferred" or "clinical background required." Write a 3-sentence cover letter: (1) You're an active RN with X years in [specialty]. (2) You can write accurate clinical content without extensive fact-checking. (3) Here are three samples. You will hear back from someone within two weeks.

How Much Can You Write in a Shift-Work Schedule

Freelance writing is asynchronous — you can write at 2 p.m. or 2 a.m. Unlike tutoring or clinical work, there's no schedule to coordinate around. A nurse working night shifts can write during the day when their brain is more alert for creative work (morning for creative writing, evening for research-heavy content).

Realistic output for a working nurse: two to four articles per week. At $100/article average, that's $800–$1,600/month. At $150/article, $1,200–$2,400/month. This is achievable without quitting your job or sacrificing all your off-days — most nurses report spending 8–12 hours per week to hit these targets once they're past the learning curve of the first month.

How to Raise Your Rates

Entry rates ($75–$100/article) reflect the fact that the editor doesn't know you yet. After three to five pieces for the same publication with zero major revisions required, you can ask for a rate increase. Most editors expect this. "I've really enjoyed working on the cardiac series — given the depth of these pieces, I'd like to move to $150/article going forward" is a normal freelance conversation, not an aggressive one.

Specialization raises rates faster than experience alone. A "nurse writer" earns $100/article. A "critical care nurse writer specializing in hemodynamic monitoring and ICU family communication" earns $150–$200/article. A "CRNA specializing in perioperative patient education content" earns $200–$350/article. The more specific your niche, the less competition and the more you can charge.

Taxes on Freelance Income

Freelance writing income is 1099 income — you'll owe both employee and employer portions of Social Security and Medicare (15.3% total on net self-employment income) plus federal and state income tax. The practical solution: set aside 25–30% of every freelance check in a separate savings account until you file taxes.

If you earn more than $1,000/quarter from freelance writing, you should make quarterly estimated tax payments to the IRS (due April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15). Underpayment penalties are small but annoying — quarterly payments eliminate them.

Legitimate deductions for nurse writers: home office (proportional to the square footage you work in), internet service (percentage used for work), writing tools and subscriptions, professional development courses, and any travel to healthcare sites you write about. Keep receipts.
