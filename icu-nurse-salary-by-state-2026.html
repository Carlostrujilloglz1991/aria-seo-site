How Much Do ICU Nurses Make by State 2026

Financial Disclaimer: Salary figures below are based on BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics, facility-reported data, and industry surveys. Actual pay varies by employer, shift, certifications, and years of experience.

ICU nurses earn meaningfully more than general floor nurses — typically 10 to 20 percent above the RN median in their state — due to patient acuity, required certifications, and mandatory overtime in understaffed units. But geography is the single largest variable in an ICU nurse's paycheck, with the highest-paying states outpacing the lowest by nearly two to one.

National median: ICU and critical care RNs earn approximately $85,000 to $95,000 annually in base pay as of 2026. With night shift differentials, charge nurse pay, CCRN certification bonuses, and overtime, total compensation frequently reaches $100,000 to $130,000+ in high-cost states.

Lowest-Paying States for ICU Nurses

ICU nurses in the South and Midwest typically earn base salaries in the $65,000 to $78,000 range. States including Mississippi, Alabama, West Virginia, Arkansas, and South Dakota consistently rank at the bottom of RN pay scales. However, cost of living in these states is substantially lower, and the effective purchasing power of an ICU nurse's salary in rural Mississippi versus urban California can be surprisingly comparable once rent, taxes, and living costs are factored in.

What Shifts the Number Most

Night shift differential

Most ICU units pay a night shift differential of $4 to $8 per hour — sometimes higher at Level I trauma centers and academic medical centers. A nurse working three 12-hour night shifts per week earns roughly $7,500 to $15,000 more per year than a pure day-shift nurse at the same base rate.

CCRN and specialty certifications

CCRN certification typically adds $2,000 to $6,000 in annual pay through certification bonuses, hourly pay bumps, or both. Specialty certifications like CCRN-K, PCCN, and CMC (cardiac medicine) may add smaller but still meaningful amounts. The ROI on CCRN is very high — the exam fee is a few hundred dollars, and the pay increase is permanent at many facilities.

Charge nurse pay

Charge RN premium typically ranges from $2 to $5 per hour above base. For an ICU nurse picking up four charge shifts per week, this adds $5,000 to $12,000 annually on top of base pay.

Overtime

Federal law requires overtime at 1.5x for hours over 40 per week. For an ICU nurse at $42 per hour base, overtime rate is $63 per hour. A nurse working two extra 12-hour shifts per month generates roughly $18,000 in gross overtime annually — before tax.

Travel ICU Nurse Pay vs. Staff Pay

Travel ICU nurses consistently earn 20 to 40 percent more in total compensation than staff nurses in the same unit. The gap is most dramatic in high-cost states like California, where crisis travel contracts in ICU units have paid $80 to $100+ per hour in recent years. The trade-off is instability, benefits gaps, and the tax home compliance burden. See our travel nurse tax home guide for the rules that make those stipends legal.

How to maximize your ICU nursing income: Earn your CCRN certification, work night shifts, pick up charge assignments, and use per diem PRN shifts at a second hospital to pad income without the administrative burden of a travel contract. If you're open to relocation, California and Washington both offer dramatically higher pay floors and meaningful union protections.

ICU Nurse Salary by State 2026

Critical care nurses earn significantly more than the general RN average -- but the gap between the lowest and highest-paying states is nearly $50,000 per year. Here is the full breakdown by state, plus what actually moves your number.

National ICU RN Salary Baseline (2026)

The national median salary for ICU RNs in 2026 sits around $85,000-$92,000 base, with total compensation (including night differential, charge pay, and overtime) typically running $95,000-$115,000 for full-time staff nurses. Travel ICU nurses earn considerably more.

How to read this data: "Base salary" is your W-2 gross before differentials or overtime. "Total comp" includes standard differentials (nights, weekends, charge) but excludes overtime and bonuses, which can add another $10,000-$30,000 at high-overtime hospitals.

What Moves Your ICU Salary Above the State Average

Unit Type

CVICU typically commands 10-18% above MICU average in the same hospital. Trauma ICU and Neuro ICU follow closely. MICU is usually the baseline rate for critical care.

Certification

CCRN certification adds $2,000-$8,000 per year in hospitals with clinical ladder programs. It also qualifies you for better travel contracts and makes you competitive for charge and flight nursing roles.

Shift Differential

Night differential at most hospitals runs 12-20% above base. Weekend differentials add another 10-15%. A $85,000 base nurse working mostly nights and weekends routinely clears $100,000 without overtime.

Charge Pay

Charge nurse differential typically runs $3-6/hour above staff rate. In smaller ICUs where every senior nurse rotates charge, this adds $7,000-$11,000/year.

How to Negotiate a Higher ICU Salary

  1. Cite your CCRN specifically as justification for the top of the posted range
  2. Use competing offers -- even a travel nursing offer as a reference point shifts the conversation
  3. Name your subspecialty experience -- CVICU, ECMO, or flight experience commands premium
  4. Ask for a higher sign-on bonus instead of base when HR is inflexible on salary bands
  5. Know the state average before you walk in -- hospitals rarely volunteer that they are below market
