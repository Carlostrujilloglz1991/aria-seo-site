Rush University in Chicago has one of the longest-running and most respected nurse anesthesia programs in the country, tied to a large downtown academic medical center with a heavy volume of complex, high-acuity cases. For an ICU nurse who wants a rigorous, urban, research-informed CRNA education, Rush is a serious target. Here's what the program is, who gets in, and how to build an application that competes.

The short version: Rush offers a Doctor of Nursing Practice (DNP) in Nurse Anesthesia, the doctoral degree now required for new CRNAs. It's a front-loaded, full-time program (roughly three years) with clinical training across Rush University Medical Center and affiliated Chicago-area sites. Expect a demand for strong adult ICU experience, a competitive GPA, current CCRN, and CRNA shadowing. Admission is competitive — apply as one of several strong applications.

What the program is

Following the profession's move to doctoral entry, Rush's pathway is a DNP with a nurse anesthesia specialty rather than a master's. The DNP trains you both as a clinician who administers anesthesia and as a practice leader who can appraise evidence and complete a scholarly project. Like most nurse anesthesia curricula, it's front-loaded: intense didactic coursework in anatomy, physiology, chemistry, and advanced pharmacology up front, followed by escalating clinical immersion. It's full-time, and students generally can't work during the program — a financial reality to plan for early.

Rush's setting is a big part of the draw. A downtown academic medical center means high surgical volume and a broad case mix — cardiac, transplant, neuro, trauma, obstetric, and complex subspecialty surgery — at levels that build deep procedural confidence. Chicago's dense hospital network also widens the range of clinical rotations available across a training cohort.

Who gets in

Competitive CRNA programs draw from a pool of already-accomplished nurses, so the bar sits high everywhere. Exact requirements change year to year and you should always confirm on Rush's official program page, but the profile of a strong applicant is consistent across top schools.

As with every top program, high-acuity experience wins. Running vasoactive drips, managing ventilators, and stabilizing unstable patients builds the judgment anesthesia training assumes. Large-center cardiac, surgical, and medical ICUs are strong backgrounds — and Chicago-area applicants often have access to exactly those units.

Cost and the hidden expense

A doctoral CRNA program is a major investment, and tuition is only part of the total. The bigger, quieter cost is the income you give up during roughly three years when the program's intensity makes working impractical. Confirm current tuition and fees directly with Rush and build a full budget that includes Chicago living costs and lost wages, not just published tuition.

Budget for the no-income years. The most common applicant miscalculation is planning only for tuition. A full-time, front-loaded program means little to no ability to work — so your real cost is tuition plus two to three years of the salary you're not earning. Line up a funding plan (savings runway, federal Grad PLUS, private gap loans) and a retirement bridge before you start.

How to build a standout application

Because most applicants clear the minimums, quality and fit are the differentiators. Get into a high-acuity ICU and stay long enough to truly master it — depth beats a bare year of experience. Earn your CCRN, and if you can, take graduate-level science courses (advanced pathophysiology, pharmacology, chemistry) to prove you can handle doctoral rigor and to raise a science GPA. Do meaningful CRNA shadowing so your statement and interview reflect a realistic understanding of the role. Line up references who can speak specifically to your clinical judgment. And prepare hard for the interview, which at top programs often tests how you reason under pressure, not just what's on your resume.

Apply broadly. Even an outstanding candidate should treat any single top program as one option among several, because acceptance rates at the most competitive schools are low and vary year to year. A strong application to Rush is also a strong application everywhere else you apply.

Bottom line: Rush University pairs a long-established DNP nurse anesthesia curriculum with the clinical volume of a downtown Chicago academic medical center — front-loaded, full-time, roughly three years, and competitive. Build depth in a high-acuity ICU, earn your CCRN, lift your science GPA, shadow real CRNAs, and budget for tuition plus the years you won't be earning. Then apply to Rush as one of several strong applications.
