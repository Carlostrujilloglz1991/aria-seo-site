1. Requirements to Become a Travel Nurse

The single most important requirement is experience. Most hospitals and agencies want at least one to two years of clinical experience in your specialty, in a similar acute care setting, before they'll place you as a traveler. This isn't gatekeeping — it's practical. Travel nurses are expected to hit the ground running with minimal orientation (sometimes just one or two days), so facilities need nurses who can function independently from day one.

Licensure requirements

You must hold a current, unencumbered Registered Nurse (RN) license. The United States uses the Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC), which allows nurses licensed in one compact state to practice in other compact states without obtaining additional licenses. As of 2026, 41 states participate. If you're licensed in a non-compact state, you'll need to apply for additional state licenses — a process that can take 6–12 weeks and cost $100–$400 per state, depending on the state.

Applying for a compact state license before you start job hunting is one of the smartest things you can do. Some agencies will help cover licensure costs; always ask before paying out of pocket.

Certifications

At minimum, you'll need BLS (Basic Life Support). Depending on your specialty, you may also need ACLS (Advanced Cardiovascular Life Support), PALS (Pediatric Advanced Life Support), NIH Stroke Scale, or specialty certifications like CCRN. Here's a quick reference:

Experience requirements by specialty

ICU, ER, and OR nurses typically need a minimum of 1–2 years in their specialty. Med-surg nurses often need 1 year. Some high-demand situations (rural critical access hospitals, crisis contracts) may take experienced nurses with slightly less, but these are exceptions rather than the rule. Start building your resume early by requesting charge experience and cross-training opportunities at your current facility.

2. How Travel Nursing Agencies Work

A travel nursing agency acts as your employer during the contract. The hospital is the client — they pay the agency a markup over your bill rate, and the agency pays you. Understanding this relationship explains almost everything about how pay packages are structured.

The bill rate and why it matters

Every agency negotiates a bill rate with each hospital — the total amount the hospital pays per hour for your services. This rate typically ranges from $80 to $200+ per hour depending on specialty, location, and demand. Out of that bill rate, the agency pays your wages, stipends, benefits, taxes, and keeps their margin. A transparent agency will show you the bill rate. Many won't.

Agency types

There are two main types: large national agencies (AMN, Aya Healthcare, FlexCare, Cross Country) and boutique agencies (often nurse-founded, with smaller caseloads and more attentive recruiters). Large agencies have more contracts and leverage; boutique agencies often have better communication. Many experienced travelers work with 2–3 agencies simultaneously to access more jobs and create competitive pressure on pay packages.

3. How to Find and Choose the Right Agency

The agency you sign with will determine a large portion of your experience — your pay, how fast your credentials are processed, how responsive your recruiter is, and how quickly they find you your next contract. Don't just Google "travel nurse agencies" and sign with the first one that calls you.

What to look for in an agency

  • Transparency about bill rate: The best agencies share the bill rate upfront or negotiate with you openly. This lets you verify the pay package is fair.
  • Responsive recruiters: Your recruiter is your lifeline. Test them before you commit — do they answer texts at 9pm when you have a contract question? Do they remember details about your situation?
  • Benefits quality: Day-one health insurance matters. So does whether they offer 401(k), dental/vision, and professional liability coverage.
  • Contract volume in your specialty: Ask how many open ICU (or whatever your specialty) contracts they have right now. Agencies with few contracts in your area will pressure you into suboptimal placements.
  • Crisis contract capability: Some agencies specialize in rapid-response crisis placements that pay significantly more. If you want that option, make sure they have it.

4. The Application and Credentialing Process

Once you've chosen an agency (or two), the application and credentialing process begins. This is more involved than a typical job application and can take 2–8 weeks, so plan accordingly.

Step 1: Application and recruiter intake

You'll complete an application, have an initial call with your recruiter, and discuss your specialties, preferred locations, start date flexibility, and compensation goals. Be specific about what you want — don't let the recruiter decide your priorities for you.

Step 2: Skills checklist and testing

Most agencies require specialty-specific skills checklists (typically 100–200 items, self-rated 0–4) and some require computer-based assessments. Be honest — inflating your scores can result in unsafe placements and contract termination.

Step 3: Credentialing

This is the most time-consuming part. You'll need to provide: employment verification for the past 2–5 years, license verifications, immunization records (Hep B, MMR, Varicella, Tdap, flu), TB testing results, background check consent, drug screen, and reference contacts. Having these organized before you start will shave weeks off the process.

Step 4: Contract and job offer

When a position is confirmed, you'll receive a contract detailing hours, shift type, unit, start/end dates, guaranteed hours, and pay package breakdown. Read it carefully. Ask your recruiter to clarify anything that's vague. Never assume verbal promises will appear in a contract without verifying.

5. What to Expect on Your First Contract

Your first assignment will be the hardest — not because the clinical work is beyond you, but because everything around the clinical work is new. New EHR system, new documentation policies, new faces, new city, new housing. Give yourself grace and prepare proactively.

Orientation and floating

Most facilities give travel nurses 1–3 days of orientation. You may be required to float to other units — check whether float requirements are specified in your contract and to which units. Some contracts explicitly limit floating; others don't. If floating is a concern, get it in writing upfront.

Housing

You have two options: agency-provided housing (the agency books and pays for housing, and deducts or accounts for it in your pay package) or a housing stipend (you find and pay for housing yourself, keeping any money you don't spend). Most experienced travelers take the stipend and find their own housing via Furnished Finder, corporate apartments, or Airbnb for stays longer than 30 days. Your first contract: agency housing removes one variable while you learn the ropes. Future contracts: DIY housing often nets $300–$800 extra per month.

The 50-mile rule and tax home

Travel nurse stipends (housing, meals and incidentals) are tax-free under IRS rules — but only if you maintain a tax home and are working away from it. Your tax home is generally where your permanent residence is located, where you pay rent or own property, and where you return between contracts. The common "50-mile rule" is a shorthand — what actually matters is that you are duplicating living expenses. Keep documentation: utility bills, mail receipts, lease agreements at home. This is the single most important piece of financial understanding for travel nurses. Consult a travel nurse tax professional (several specialize in this); the cost is worth it.

What if the contract isn't working?

Contract cancellations happen. A facility can cancel with typically 14 days' notice for non-clinical reasons. You can request early termination, though there are usually financial and reputational consequences. If your contract is cancelled by the facility due to census issues, a good agency will work hard to find you another immediate placement. Ask your recruiter how they handle cancellations before you sign.

6. Pay Package Basics

The travel nurse pay package looks more complicated than a staff job offer, but once you understand the components you'll be able to evaluate any offer quickly.

Taxable base pay

This is the hourly rate that appears on your W-2. Travel nurses often have a lower taxable base pay than staff nurses — this is by design. The rest of the compensation is made up through tax-free stipends. Taxable base pay is typically $20–$35/hour.

Tax-free stipends

Housing stipend (e.g., $1,200–$2,500/month), meals and incidentals (e.g., $250–$500/week), and sometimes travel reimbursement (usually a one-time amount at the start and end of contract). These are non-taxable for eligible travelers, which is why the total take-home for travel nurses is often significantly higher than it appears on paper.

Benefits

Day-one health, dental, and vision coverage is standard at quality agencies. Some offer 401(k) with matching. Professional liability (malpractice) insurance should always be included — confirm it. Some agencies also offer completion bonuses paid at the end of the 13-week contract.

Example pay breakdown — ICU Nurse, California assignment (13 weeks):
  • Taxable base: $28/hr × 36 hrs/wk = ~$1,008/wk
  • Housing stipend: $1,500/wk (tax-free)
  • M&IE stipend: $350/wk (tax-free)
  • Total gross weekly: ~$2,858
  • Take-home (after tax on base only): ~$2,600–$2,700/wk
  • 13-week total take-home: ~$33,800–$35,100

Actual figures vary by location, specialty, demand, and agency. Always get a written breakdown.

7. Frequently Asked Questions

How much do travel nurses actually make?

Median all-in compensation runs $2,200–$3,500 per week for most RNs in 2026, depending on specialty and location. ICU, ER, OR, and L&D nurses in high-demand states (California, New York, Florida) often reach $3,500–$5,000+/week during high-demand periods. Crisis contracts in underserved areas can go higher.

Can I travel with a family?

Yes, and many nurses do. It takes more planning — school-age children complicate 13-week relocations — but some families treat it as an extended road trip lifestyle. Others keep a home base and the traveling parent goes solo. Housing decisions become more complex (you'll need a 2-bedroom at minimum), but the pay increase often more than covers it.

What happens between contracts?

The gap between assignments ("on call" or "on the beach") is typically 1–3 weeks, though it can be longer if you're transitioning between states that require additional licensing. Some nurses use this time for vacations; others feel financial pressure to minimize it. Having 2–3 months of living expenses in savings before you start traveling dramatically reduces this stress.

Do I need to pay for my own malpractice insurance?

No — reputable agencies include professional liability insurance. Confirm it's in your contract. The coverage should be at least $1 million per occurrence and $3 million aggregate.

Is travel nursing worth it financially?

For most nurses with 1–2+ years of experience who can tolerate location change, travel nursing is one of the highest-ROI financial moves available. The combination of elevated hourly rates, tax-free stipends, and zero student loan payments (if using travel income for aggressive payoff) can set a nurse up significantly ahead of peers in just 2–3 years of travel contracts.

What if I have student loans?

Travel nursing income can accelerate loan payoff significantly if you redirect stipends toward principal. However, if you're pursuing Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF), travel nursing for a private agency disqualifies your payments — you'd need to work for a qualifying nonprofit or government facility.
