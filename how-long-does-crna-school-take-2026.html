How Long Does CRNA School Take 2026

Part of the CRNA Career Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information. Program requirements and durations vary; confirm specifics with each program directly.

The short answer: CRNA school itself takes 28 to 36 months. But the full path from "I'm an RN thinking about CRNA school" to "I'm a practicing CRNA" typically spans 7 to 9 years from the point of entering nursing. Understanding each stage helps you plan accurately — and avoid the false start of applying before you're ready.

Full CRNA timeline: BSN (4 years) → ICU experience requirement (1–2 years minimum; most applicants have 2–4 years) → application and admission process (6–12 months) → CRNA program (28–36 months) → NCE board exam (1–3 months after graduation). Total: approximately 7 to 9 years from starting nursing school.

Stage 1 — BSN and Initial Nursing Licensure (4 Years)

All CRNA programs require a Bachelor of Science in Nursing (BSN) and an active, unencumbered RN license. Accelerated BSN programs for students with a prior bachelor's degree take 12 to 18 months. Traditional 4-year BSN programs form the base. An ADN nurse who later earned a BSN via an RN-to-BSN bridge program also qualifies; the RN-to-BSN typically adds 1 to 2 years beyond the ADN.

Stage 2 — ICU Experience (1–3+ Years)

Every accredited CRNA program requires a minimum of one year of full-time critical care experience in an adult ICU setting. In practice, applicants who gain admission typically have 2 to 4 years of ICU experience. The most competitive applicants have experience in high-acuity settings: cardiac ICUs, surgical ICUs, neurological ICUs, or multi-specialty trauma ICUs with exposure to hemodynamic monitoring, vasoactive drips, ventilator management, and invasive procedures.

This stage is not just a checkbox — it is the clinical foundation that CRNA school builds upon. Programs can tell immediately whether an applicant truly understands what they're seeing in the ICU or is just logging hours. Strong ICU nurses with CCRN certification, experience as a charge nurse, and exposure to ECMO, IABP, or advanced hemodynamics are significantly more competitive applicants.

Stage 3 — Application and Admission (6–12 Months)

CRNA programs typically have application cycles with one annual cohort per program. Most applications are due 6 to 12 months before the program start date. The application process includes: official transcripts, GRE scores (some programs have dropped this requirement — check each program), CCRN certification (strongly preferred, required at some programs), three letters of recommendation, a personal statement, and a clinical interview.

The CASPA (Centralized Application Service for Physician Assistants) is not used for CRNA — most programs have their own application portals. A small number use a centralized CRNA application service. Plan your application timeline carefully: if program start dates are in January or May, your application may be due 9 to 12 months earlier.

Stage 4 — The CRNA Program (28–36 Months)

As of 2025, all CRNA programs are doctoral-level (DNP or DNAP). Program length ranges from 28 months (the shortest fully-accredited programs) to 36 to 40 months for programs with additional research or DNP project requirements.

Programs are typically full-time and incompatible with full-time clinical employment. Some programs allow limited per diem or PRN shifts during early didactic phases but most clinical-phase students have no realistic time for outside work. Stage 5 — NCE Board Exam and Licensure (1–3 Months Post-Graduation)

Upon graduation, candidates are eligible to sit for the National Certification Examination (NCE) administered by the NBCRNA. The NCE is a computer-adaptive exam covering anesthesia principles, pharmacology, and clinical management. First-time pass rates for most accredited programs exceed 90%. After passing, the candidate is eligible for CRNA state licensure as an APRN in their state of practice.

From test application to results typically takes 4 to 8 weeks. Many new CRNAs receive a temporary practice permit allowing them to begin working while awaiting official licensure — check your state board for specifics.

Can You Accelerate the Timeline?

Several strategies genuinely shorten the path: entering an accelerated BSN (saves 2 years vs. a traditional BSN if you have a prior degree), choosing a 28-month CRNA program over a 36-month one (saves 8 months of lost income and living expenses), and applying during your second year of ICU experience rather than waiting for year three or four. Applying once and getting in on the first try saves 1 to 2 years compared to applicants who apply multiple cycles. CCRN certification, a strong GPA, and pre-application shadowing in ORs all improve first-application acceptance odds.

The efficient path: Accelerated BSN (18 months) → 2 years MICU/SICU + CCRN certification → apply during year 2, start program year 3 → 30-month DNP program → NCE. Total from starting nursing school: approximately 7.5 years. Total from passing NCLEX-RN: approximately 5.5 years.
