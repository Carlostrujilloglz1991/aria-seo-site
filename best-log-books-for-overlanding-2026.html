Best Log Books for Overlanding in 2026

Updated June 2026 | 7-minute read

Short version: Serious overlanders track two things: the rig and the route. The best overlanding log books have vehicle maintenance logs (fluids, tires, winch cable, lift kit service intervals), trip journals with waypoints and campsite notes, pre-departure checklists, and recovery gear inventory. We reviewed the top options for 2026.

Overlanding is gear-intensive, mechanical, and expensive to get wrong in a remote location. A blown CV axle 40 miles from pavement because you forgot the last service interval isn't adventure — it's a recovery bill. A missed campsite GPS coordinate means you can't return to the spot you spent four hours finding.

Paper logs persist when your phone dies. They don't require signal. They don't need a charging cable. And they can survive conditions that kill electronics.

What an Overlanding Log Book Should Track

Two categories: the rig, and the routes.

For the rig: oil changes with mileage and oil type, differential fluid intervals, transfer case service, winch cable inspection dates, tire rotation and pressure logs, lift kit component inspection, skid plate torque checks, battery condition, and any modifications with install dates and part numbers. The modification log alone is invaluable when you're troubleshooting an electrical issue six months after installation.

For the routes: GPS coordinates of campsites and waypoints, trail conditions observed, obstacles encountered and how they were handled, recovery gear used and any issues found, fuel consumption data (useful for planning remote-route fuel carries), and photos cross-referenced by entry date.

The Recovery Gear Inventory You Need Separate

One log most overlanders skip: a recovery gear inventory with condition ratings. Every piece of recovery gear degrades. Kinetic ropes weaken at attachment points. Shackle pins corrode. Hi-lift jack teeth wear. A winch cable develops kinks that compromise strength.

A dedicated gear inspection log — reviewed before each major trip — documents condition, last inspection date, and replacement schedule. The $30 you spend on a replacement shackle before a trip beats the $400 recovery and the embarrassment of a failed shackle mid-pull.

Digital GPS vs. Paper Routes

gaia GPS, OnX Offroad, and similar apps are excellent for navigation. They're bad for long-term route documentation. Apps change, subscriptions end, data migrates poorly, and a phone dropped in a creek takes your track log with it.

Paper route logs are slow and require deliberate effort. That's also why they persist. The notes you took in 2019 on a route you want to repeat in 2027 are still readable. The gaia tracks from 2019 may or may not be exportable to whatever platform you're using in 2027.

Use both. Trust paper for anything you want to remember long-term.
