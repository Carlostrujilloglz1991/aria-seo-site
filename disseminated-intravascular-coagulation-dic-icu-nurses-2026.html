Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation: The ICU Nurse's Guide

Part of the ICU Emergencies Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Disseminated intravascular coagulation is the disorder that seems to break the rules: the patient is clotting and bleeding at the same time. Tiny clots form throughout the microcirculation while the blood oozes from every line site and puncture. It is never a primary diagnosis — it is always the downstream signature of something else that is very wrong. The nurse who recognizes the pattern early buys time to fix the cause.

The short version: DIC is a consumptive coagulopathy — widespread activation of clotting uses up platelets and clotting factors faster than the body can replace them, so the patient forms microthrombi and bleeds. The labs show falling platelets, a rising D-dimer, a falling fibrinogen, and lengthening PT/PTT. The only definitive treatment is treating the underlying trigger; blood products are given to the patient who is actively bleeding or about to have a procedure, not to correct a number.

What is actually happening

Some massive stimulus — sepsis, trauma, an obstetric catastrophe, a cancer, a severe transfusion reaction — dumps tissue factor and inflammatory signals into the bloodstream and switches on the clotting cascade everywhere at once. The result is uncontrolled thrombin generation and fibrin deposited throughout the small vessels. Those microclots consume platelets and clotting factors and shred red cells as they squeeze past (producing schistocytes on the smear). Then, having burned through the clotting machinery, the blood can no longer clot where it should — so the same patient who is throwing microthrombi starts to bleed from IV sites, the ET tube, the GI tract, and surgical wounds. Clotting and bleeding are not two problems; they are two faces of one runaway process.

The lab picture that names it

No single test diagnoses DIC. It is a pattern of trends moving in a characteristic direction, read against a plausible trigger:

TestDirection in DICWhy
Platelet countFallingConsumed by widespread clotting
D-dimer / FDPsHigh and risingFibrin is being formed and broken down everywhere
FibrinogenFalling (may start high, then drop)Consumed faster than the liver replaces it
PT / INR and aPTTProlongedClotting factors used up
Blood smearSchistocytesRed cells sheared by microclots

The most specific single move is watching the trend: a platelet count and fibrinogen that are dropping hour over hour while the D-dimer climbs, in a patient sick enough to have a trigger, is DIC until proven otherwise. Scoring systems (such as the ISTH DIC score) formalize this by assigning points to the platelet count, the D-dimer, the fibrinogen, and the PT.

Find the trigger — that is the treatment

DIC does not have its own cure. It stops when the thing driving it stops. The nurse's most valuable contribution, beyond supporting the patient, is helping the team keep the trigger in focus:

  • Sepsis — the most common ICU cause. Source control and antibiotics are the real DIC treatment. See our ICU sepsis protocol guide.
  • Trauma / massive hemorrhage — the dilution and consumption of massive bleeding; managed alongside the massive transfusion protocol.
  • Obstetric emergencies — placental abruption, amniotic fluid embolism, retained products.
  • Malignancy — especially acute promyelocytic leukemia and metastatic adenocarcinoma.
  • Severe transfusion or immune reactions and extensive burns.

When do you give blood products?

This is the point that trips people up. In DIC, you do not transfuse to fix a lab value in a patient who is stable and not bleeding. Product is given to the patient who is actively bleeding, or who needs an invasive procedure, or whose numbers are dangerously low:

Rough targets when the patient is bleeding: platelets for significant thrombocytopenia; fresh frozen plasma to replace clotting factors when PT/PTT are prolonged; and cryoprecipitate when fibrinogen is low (cryo is the concentrated fibrinogen source). Follow your facility thresholds and the ordering team — this is directional, not a protocol.

The worry that transfusing "adds fuel to the fire" of clotting is largely theoretical in the bleeding patient; the bleeding is the immediate threat and gets treated. Heparin is used only in selected DIC phenotypes where thrombosis dominates (for example, certain cancers), and that decision belongs to the team — it is never a reflex.

Do not chase numbers in a stable patient. A low platelet count or fibrinogen in DIC is treated when there is bleeding or a planned procedure, not because a value looks bad on the flowsheet. Transfusing a non-bleeding DIC patient exposes them to product risk without benefit.

What the nurse is watching and doing

DIC is a surveillance diagnosis, and the bedside nurse often sees it before the labs are back:

  • Oozing from everywhere. Bleeding from IV and arterial line sites, around the ET tube, from the Foley, gums, and old puncture sites — bleeding that does not respect a single source is the classic clue.
  • New microthrombotic signs. Cool, mottled, or dusky fingers and toes; rising creatinine (renal microthrombi); confusion; and worsening oxygenation all suggest the clotting side.
  • Serial labs. Trending platelets, fibrinogen, PT/PTT, and D-dimer on the schedule the team sets — the trend is the diagnosis.
  • Bleeding precautions. Hold pressure longer, minimize unnecessary sticks and invasive lines, use soft oral care, and avoid IM injections.
  • Hemodynamic support. Volume, pressors, and product per orders; DIC patients are often in shock from the underlying process.

DIC vs. its mimics

A falling platelet count with organ injury is not automatically DIC. The thrombotic microangiopathies — TTP and HUS — also drop platelets and shear red cells, but in those disorders the PT, PTT, and fibrinogen are usually normal because the clotting cascade is not being consumed. That single distinction (normal coags in TTP vs. deranged coags in DIC) is one of the most useful at the bedside. See our companion guide on TTP. Likewise, heparin-induced thrombocytopenia drops the platelet count but drives clotting, not bleeding, and the coags are typically normal.

Bottom line

DIC is the body's clotting system running out of control and then running out of supplies — clotting and bleeding braided together. Recognize the pattern (oozing from everywhere plus falling platelets and fibrinogen, rising D-dimer, and prolonging coags), remember that the only cure is fixing the trigger, and give product to the patient who is bleeding or facing a procedure — not to a number on the screen. The nurse who notices the diffuse ooze and the drifting trend early is the one who gets the team looking for the sepsis, the abruption, or the malignancy that is actually driving it.
