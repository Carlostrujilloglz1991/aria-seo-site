📄 Your First 3 Financial Moves as a Nurse

Do these in order. Each one takes less than 30 minutes to set up.

Move 1

Open a High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)

Your money is probably sitting in a regular savings account earning 0.01% interest — basically nothing. High-yield savings accounts from online banks pay 4%–5% APY right now. That's 400–500x more on the same dollars, with zero extra risk (FDIC insured).

Which one to open: SoFi, Ally, or Marcus by Goldman Sachs are the consistently top-rated options. As of 2025, SoFi and Ally offer the best combination of rates + no fees + no minimums.

  • Go to sofi.com/savings or ally.com
  • Open a savings account (takes ~10 minutes, needs your SSN and routing number)
  • Set up a recurring transfer: even $50/paycheck builds fast
  • Label the account "Emergency Fund" — this is your first goal
🔔 First target: $1,000. Then build to 3 months of expenses. Most nurses reach this in 3–4 months by auto-transferring $200–300 per paycheck.
Move 2

Calculate Your Debt Payoff Timeline

You can't pay off debt faster if you don't know how long it actually takes at your current rate. Most people have no idea — they just make minimum payments and feel vaguely hopeless. Here's the actual math:

Months to payoff = -log(1 - (balance × monthly_rate) / payment) ÷ log(1 + monthly_rate) Example: Balance: $18,000 (student loans) Interest rate: 6.5% APY → monthly rate = 0.065 / 12 = 0.00542 Monthly payment: $350 Months = -log(1 - (18000 × 0.00542) / 350) ÷ log(1 + 0.00542) = ~72 months (6 years) Add $100/month → $450/month → drops to ~53 months (4.4 years) Add $200/month → $550/month → drops to ~43 months (3.6 years)

You don't need to calculate this by hand. Use unbury.us — free, no sign-up, shows you the exact payoff date and interest cost for any extra payment.

  • List every debt: balance, interest rate, minimum payment
  • Use the avalanche method (pay highest rate first — saves the most money)
  • Pick ONE debt to attack with extra payments; pay minimums on everything else
  • Every $50 extra per month matters more than it feels like it does
💡 Key insight: On a $18k student loan at 6.5%, paying an extra $200/month saves you $2,800+ in interest and 2.5 years of payments. That's real money for 5 minutes of math.
Move 3

Set Up Your First Retirement Contribution

The right order matters here. Most nurses work for employers who offer a 403(b) — the hospital version of a 401(k). You also have the option of an IRA (Individual Retirement Account) you open yourself.

Which first?

  • Step 1 — 403(b) up to your employer match. If your hospital matches contributions (e.g., 3% of salary), contribute AT LEAST that amount first. This is a 100% instant return on your money. If you skip it, you are leaving free salary on the table. No exception.
  • Step 2 — Open a Roth IRA. After capturing the match, open a Roth IRA at Fidelity or Vanguard (both are free, no minimum). Contribute up to $7,000/year (2024 limit). Roth = you pay taxes now, withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. For most nurses at mid-career income, this beats a traditional IRA.
  • Step 3 — Back to 403(b). If you have more to invest after maxing the Roth IRA, go back to your 403(b) up to the $23,000 annual limit.
📈 Starting at 30 vs. 40 difference: $300/month at 7% average return starting at age 30 = $340,000 at retirement. Starting at 40 = $162,000. The decade costs you $178,000. Start this month, not next year.

How to set it up: Contact your HR department or benefits portal and ask to start or increase your 403(b) contribution. For a Roth IRA, go to fidelity.com or vanguard.com and open an account (takes 15 minutes, need SSN + bank info).
