Travel Nurse Compact License Guide: NLC Member States, How to Get One & Endorsement Explained

The Nursing Licensure Compact (NLC) allows RNs and LPNs to practice in multiple states on a single license. For travel nurses, it eliminates the most significant administrative bottleneck in the industry. Here is how it works and how to get one.

As of 2026: 41 states and territories are NLC members. If you live in a compact state and hold a compact license, you can practice in all other compact states without obtaining individual state licenses. This is worth hundreds of dollars and weeks of administrative time per state.

How the NLC Works

The NLC is administered by the National Council of State Boards of Nursing (NCSBN). Under the compact, a nurse's primary state of residence (their "home state") issues the multistate license. The nurse can then practice in other compact states (called "remote states") under that single license without endorsing into each state individually.

Important: the compact license follows your primary state of residence, not your birthplace or your first license. If you move from Texas (compact) to California (non-compact), you lose your compact privileges because California is not a member. If you move from California to Texas, you can apply for a compact Texas license.

Current NLC Member States (as of 2026)

The majority of states are now NLC members. Major compact states include Texas, Florida, North Carolina, Virginia, Arizona, Colorado, Utah, Tennessee, Georgia, Ohio, Pennsylvania, and many others. Major non-compact states as of this writing include California, New York, Illinois, Massachusetts, Oregon, and Washington — though legislative activity in several of these continues.

Always verify current membership at ncsbn.org/compact before planning a contract, as states can be added or have temporary inactive status during legislative transitions.

How to Obtain a Compact License

If you currently live in a compact state and do not yet have a compact license, the process is straightforward. Contact your state board of nursing and request that your existing license be upgraded to a multistate (compact) license. Most states handle this through an online application with a fee of $25 to $75. You will need to verify your primary state of residence (typically with a driver's license, utility bill, or state ID).

If you are relocating to a compact state in order to obtain compact privileges, you must update your driver's license and other residency documentation to the new state first. The state board requires proof of primary residence in the compact state.

Endorsement vs. Multistate Privileges

Endorsement refers to obtaining an individual single-state license in a non-compact state (or in a compact state before the compact took effect). Each endorsed license is independent and must be renewed independently per that state's renewal cycle. Endorsement typically costs $100 to $300 per state and takes 4 to 16 weeks for processing.

Multistate privileges (from your compact license) are automatic and immediate for all compact member states and cost nothing beyond your home state license fee. For travel nurses, the math is clear: a compact license saves you $300 to $1,200 per year in endorsement fees and eliminates weeks of waiting for licenses to process between contracts.

Non-compact state assignments: If a contract is in California, New York, or another non-compact state, you will need to apply for endorsement in that state. Begin the application immediately when you know you want to work there — California in particular has an unusually long processing time (6 to 12+ weeks). Some travel nurses apply for California, New York, and Oregon licenses proactively before they have specific contracts, since these states consistently have high-paying assignments.

Maintaining Your Compact License

Renew your compact license in your home state according to that state's renewal schedule (typically every 2 years). If you receive a disciplinary action in any compact state, it applies to your home state license and effectively suspends your privileges everywhere. The compact does not provide a workaround for disciplinary matters.

Travel nurse strategy: Before your first contract, verify your compact status, order your license verification documents, and begin any non-compact state endorsements for states you want to work in during the next 12 months. Do this before you have a contract lined up — processing time is the only variable you cannot control.
