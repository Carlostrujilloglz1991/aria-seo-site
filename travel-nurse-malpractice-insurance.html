Travel Nurse Malpractice Insurance: What You Need and What It Costs

Part of the Travel Nursing Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

Malpractice claims against nurses are more common than most nurses expect, and travel nurses carry unique risks that staff nurses don't face. Here's what coverage you actually need and how to get it.

Why Travel Nurses Have Elevated Risk

Travel nurses practice in unfamiliar facilities, often without the institutional knowledge that reduces error risk. You don't know this unit's workarounds, equipment quirks, or informal protocols. You're covering for shortages, which means higher patient ratios and less backup. And you're working in states where you may not have practiced before.

None of this means you'll be sued—but it does mean your malpractice risk profile is different from a staff nurse who has worked the same unit for three years.

What Your Agency Provides

Most travel nursing agencies carry liability coverage for their employed nurses under a commercial general liability policy or a professional liability policy that covers nurses working on agency contracts. This is typically:

  • Occurrence-based or claims-made: The type matters. Occurrence coverage protects you for any incident that occurred during the policy period, regardless of when the claim is filed. Claims-made coverage only protects you if the policy is active when the claim is filed.
  • Limits: Agency policies typically carry $1 million per occurrence / $3 million aggregate.

Important: always ask your agency for a certificate of insurance before your first assignment. Get the answer to these questions in writing: 1. Does the policy cover you specifically as an employee? 2. Is it occurrence or claims-made? 3. What are the per-occurrence and aggregate limits? 4. Are you covered if a claim is filed after your contract ends?

The Coverage Gap Problem

Agency coverage has a critical limitation: it protects you as an employee of the agency. If you're alleged to have acted outside your nursing scope, if you're sued personally (not just the agency), or if the claim arises in a way the agency's insurer disputes, you may be without protection.

This is why most liability attorneys and professional nursing associations recommend that nurses—especially travel nurses—carry their own individual professional liability policy in addition to any agency coverage.

Individual Nursing Malpractice Insurance

Individual nursing malpractice policies are inexpensive relative to the protection they provide. Cost: $100–$350/year for most bedside nurses. ICU and procedural nurses at the higher end of that range.

Providers that specialize in nursing coverage:

NSO (Nurses Service Organization): The most commonly used. Coverage options from $1M/$6M (per occurrence/aggregate). Annual premiums in the $100–$175 range for most RNs. Claims-made policies; occurrence available at higher premium.

CPH & Associates: Competitive pricing, occurrence-based coverage available. Often recommended by nursing associations.

Proliability (formerly Marsh): Backed by a large insurance company; flexible options including occurrence coverage.

HPSO: Similar to NSO; endorsed by some nursing associations.

When comparing policies, the key variables are: occurrence vs. claims-made, per-occurrence limit, aggregate limit, and whether the policy covers license protection (defense costs in Board of Nursing investigations).

Occurrence vs. Claims-Made: Why It Matters for Travel Nurses

This distinction is especially important for travel nurses who change assignments frequently:

Occurrence-based policy: A claim can be filed years after you cancel the policy, and as long as the incident occurred during the policy period, you're covered. For a nurse who practices in many states across many years, this is the cleanest protection.

Claims-made policy: The policy must be active when the claim is filed. If you cancel coverage and a claim comes in six months later, you're not covered—unless you purchased a "tail" policy (extended reporting period). Tail policies can cost 100–200% of the annual premium.

For most travel nurses, occurrence-based coverage is worth the modest additional premium.

Do You Need Coverage in Every State You Work In?

Individual nursing malpractice policies typically cover you nationally—all 50 states. You don't need separate policies per state. Confirm this with your insurer when enrolling.

License Protection Coverage

This is often overlooked and extremely valuable. A Board of Nursing investigation or complaint—even a frivolous one—can cost $5,000–$25,000 in attorney's fees to defend. Most nursing malpractice policies include license protection coverage up to a set limit ($25,000–$35,000 is common). Without coverage, you pay out of pocket.

Travel nurses are particularly exposed here because they work in multiple states, have multiple licenses to protect (or Compact License privileges to defend), and practice in unfamiliar environments where complaints are more likely.

What a Policy Actually Covers

Standard nursing malpractice coverage includes:

  • Civil monetary damages in malpractice claims
  • Defense attorney fees (often the most significant cost even when you win)
  • Settlement costs
  • License protection (investigation defense)
  • Coverage for Good Samaritan acts
  • Personal injury coverage (defamation, invasion of privacy claims)

What it typically excludes: intentional criminal acts, sexual misconduct, willful misconduct.

The Real Cost of Not Having Coverage

A travel nurse sued after a patient incident in an unfamiliar facility, without individual coverage, relying entirely on agency coverage that disputes the claim: potential out-of-pocket exposure of $100,000–$1,000,000 in defense costs and settlement payments.

The NSO policy covering an ICU travel nurse: approximately $145/year.

The math is not close. This is the cheapest important insurance any nurse can buy.

Action Steps

  1. Request a certificate of insurance from your agency showing your coverage, policy type, and limits
  2. Purchase an individual occurrence-based policy (NSO and CPH & Associates are good starting points)
  3. Confirm national coverage and license protection are included
  4. Keep the policy active even between assignments—claims can emerge months or years after an incident
  5. If you've had prior claims or investigations, disclose them accurately on the application (misrepresentation voids coverage)

Get this in place before your next assignment starts. The cost is trivial; the protection is significant.
