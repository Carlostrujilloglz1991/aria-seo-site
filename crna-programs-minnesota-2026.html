CRNA Programs in Minnesota 2026

Minnesota punches well above its weight in nurse anesthesia. It's home to the University of Minnesota — the first program in the country accredited to offer the entry-level doctorate — and to the world-renowned Mayo Clinic. For an ICU nurse, that's an unusually deep bench of accredited programs in one state. Here's the 2026 landscape, what it costs, and how to get in.

The short answer: Minnesota has roughly 4 accredited CRNA schools offering nurse anesthesia programs at the doctoral level. The University of Minnesota was the first U.S. program accredited for the entry-level DNP, and Mayo Clinic offers a DNAP with elite clinical training. All require ICU experience and a competitive GPA.

Accredited CRNA programs in Minnesota

Every program below is accredited by the Council on Accreditation of Nurse Anesthesia Educational Programs (COA). Confirm current accreditation status on the COA list before applying, since review dates change.

University of Minnesota holds a special place in the field as the first nurse anesthesia program in the country accredited to offer the entry-level Doctor of Nursing Practice, and it earned the maximum 10-year accreditation approval at its last review. As a public flagship, it's often the most cost-effective option for Minnesota residents.

Mayo Clinic School of Health Sciences in Rochester offers a Doctor of Nurse Anesthesia Practice (DNAP) with the kind of high-acuity, high-volume clinical exposure that the Mayo name implies — a powerful draw for applicants who want to train at one of the most respected medical centers in the world.

Saint Mary's University of Minnesota runs a COA-accredited DNP in nurse anesthesiology, and the Minneapolis School of Anesthesia — in partnership with Metropolitan State University — delivers an accredited program within a doctoral framework. Together they give Minnesota applicants four distinct routes, an unusually rich set of options for a single state.

Resident status is the cost lever. Minnesota's public flagship (University of Minnesota) typically offers lower in-state tuition than the private programs. If you're a Minnesota resident, the public route can meaningfully reduce the debt you carry out of a three-year program you generally can't work through.

Admission requirements

Minnesota programs use the same competitive benchmarks as the rest of the country. Minimums make you eligible; the admitted profile is higher.

Competition is fierce at the marquee names. Mayo and the University of Minnesota draw applicants from across the country, so don't assume in-state status is enough. Apply to several of Minnesota's programs, and consider strong neighbors in Wisconsin, Iowa, and the Dakotas to widen your odds.

How to strengthen your application

Get into the highest-acuity ICU you can and stay long enough to lead — a busy CVICU, SICU, or trauma unit carries more weight than years on a quieter floor. Earn your CCRN early; it's close to non-negotiable. If your GPA is below ~3.4, add or retake upper-level science courses and earn As to show momentum. Document CRNA shadowing before applying, and treat the interview as the deciding factor, because once you clear the screen, it usually is.

Build against a checklist, not a feeling. Benchmark your GPA, ICU type, hours, CCRN, and shadowing against the typical admitted profile, then close your widest gap first. See the CRNA school requirements guide, the acceptance-rate breakdown, and the ICU-to-CRNA timeline.

Bottom line

For a state its size, Minnesota offers an exceptional set of CRNA programs — a historic public flagship at the University of Minnesota, world-class clinical training at Mayo, and solid options at Saint Mary's and the Minneapolis School of Anesthesia. In-state status can cut your cost, but the best-known programs are nationally competitive, so apply broadly. Land a high-acuity ICU, a CCRN, and a ~3.5 GPA, and a Minnesota CRNA seat is well within reach.
