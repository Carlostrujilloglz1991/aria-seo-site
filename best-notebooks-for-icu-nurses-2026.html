Why ICU Nurses Need a Specific Notebook

A notebook that works for a graduate student is completely wrong for bedside nursing. In the ICU you need something that fits in a scrub pocket (or clipped to a lanyard), survives contact with saline, blood, and alcohol wipes, lies flat so you can write on it one-handed, and has enough structure to track drip rates, I&O totals, and family contact notes across a 12-hour shift.

I've gone through probably 40 different notebooks over the years working ICU. Here are the ones that made the cut.

The ICU Brain Sheet vs. a Notebook

Most ICU nurses use a printed brain sheet — a one-page template with sections for vitals, drips, labs, I&O, and family info — folded into quarters and kept in their pocket. A notebook complements this, not replaces it. The brain sheet is your shift-at-a-glance; the notebook is for everything that doesn't fit: physician conversations, drug calculations you ran twice to be sure, your own clinical observations.

If your unit doesn't have a standard brain sheet, make one. One hour in Word or Google Docs, print 50 copies, done. Your charge nurse will thank you.

HIPAA and Your Notebook

Patient names, room numbers, MRNs, diagnoses — these are PHI. A notebook with patient identifiers cannot leave the hospital. Either keep a separate non-patient notebook (dates, drug references, clinical concepts) or use the disposable approach: one cheap notebook per shift, shred it in the hospital shredder before you badge out.

The Rite in the Rain is popular partly because nurses use initials and room numbers only, no full names, so the notebook can stay with them across shifts without HIPAA concerns.

Best Pens for Bedside Notebooks

For waterproof paper (Rite in the Rain): Fisher Space Pen or any standard ballpoint. Gel pens smear. For regular paper: Pilot G2 or Zebra F-301 — smooth, reliable, clip onto the notebook spiral. Keep two in your pocket. One will run dry mid-shift and you will not have time to find another.
