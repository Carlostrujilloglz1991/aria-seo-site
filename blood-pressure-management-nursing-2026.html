Accurate BP Measurement Technique

Common Errors That Falsely Elevate BP

  • Cuff too small: Most common error; falsely high reading; cuff bladder should encircle 80% of arm circumference
  • Arm not at heart level: Arm below heart = falsely high; arm above heart = falsely low (each inch ≈ 2 mmHg)
  • Patient not rested: Should sit quietly for 5 minutes before measurement
  • Back not supported / feet dangling: Both increase BP reading; patient must be seated with back supported, feet flat on floor
  • Recent activity, caffeine, smoking: Wait 30 minutes after smoking or caffeine, 5 minutes after exercise
  • White coat hypertension: Anxiety-related spike in clinical setting; use home BP log for comparison
  • Talking during measurement: Raises BP; patient should not speak during measurement
  • Crossed legs: Crossing legs at the knee raises SBP by 2–8 mmHg

Proper Technique

  • Patient seated, back supported, feet flat, arm at heart level, uncrossed legs
  • Rest 5 minutes before measurement
  • Apply cuff to bare arm (not over clothing)
  • Use appropriately sized cuff
  • Two readings 1–2 minutes apart; average them
  • Take bilateral arm readings on initial assessment; use higher-reading arm going forward

Orthostatic Hypotension

Orthostatic hypotension = drop in SBP ≥20 mmHg or DBP ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing. Major fall risk. Always assess when titrating antihypertensives.

Measuring for Orthostatic Hypotension

  • Take BP supine after resting 5 minutes
  • Have patient stand (or sit up if unable to stand)
  • Recheck BP and HR at 1 minute and 3 minutes after position change
  • Positive result: SBP drops ≥20 mmHg or DBP drops ≥10 mmHg
  • Symptoms: dizziness, lightheadedness, syncope, blurred vision

Interventions

  • Rise slowly from supine → sitting → standing (30 seconds each position)
  • Dangle legs before standing; hold rails
  • Adequate hydration; avoid alcohol and prolonged standing
  • Review medications (offenders: alpha blockers, diuretics, antidepressants)
  • Compression stockings for venous pooling
  • Bedside commode for patients at high fall risk

Patient Education for Hypertension

  • DASH diet: Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension; rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy; limit sodium to <2,300 mg/day (ideal <1,500 mg/day)
  • Sodium: Teach label reading; 1 tsp salt = ~2,300 mg sodium; processed foods are the main culprit
  • Exercise: 150 minutes/week moderate aerobic activity lowers SBP by 5–8 mmHg
  • Weight: Each 1 kg weight loss lowers SBP ~1 mmHg
  • Alcohol: Limit to ≤1 drink/day women, ≤2 drinks/day men
  • Smoking cessation: Does not lower resting BP but dramatically reduces cardiovascular risk
  • Medication adherence: HTN is "silent" — patients feel fine and stop meds; teach that HTN is a chronic condition requiring lifelong management
  • Home BP monitoring: Monitor in the morning before medications; log readings; bring log to appointments
  • Do NOT stop medications abruptly (especially beta-blockers and clonidine — rebound effect)

NCLEX High-Yield Points

  • ACE inhibitors: dry cough is the most common side effect; switch to ARB if intolerable
  • ACEi and ARBs: CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy (teratogenic — renal agenesis, oligohydramnios)
  • Beta-blockers: never stop abruptly; mask hypoglycemia symptoms; avoid in asthma
  • Thiazide diuretics: monitor K+ (hypokalemia), Na+ (hyponatremia), glucose, uric acid
  • Orthostatic hypotension: SBP drop ≥20 or DBP drop ≥10 within 3 min of standing
  • Cuff too small = falsely HIGH reading (most common measurement error)
  • Ischemic stroke acute: do NOT lower BP unless >220/120 (permissive hypertension preserves penumbra)
  • Clonidine and beta-blockers: rebound HTN if stopped suddenly — taper always
  • Methyldopa = drug of choice for hypertension in pregnancy
