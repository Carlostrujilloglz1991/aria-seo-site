Home Inspection Red Flags That Should Make You Walk Away

Updated July 2026  |  11-minute read  |  Buying a Home

A home inspection is not just a formality before closing — it's your best opportunity to discover what a home is really going to cost you to own. Most inspection findings are manageable maintenance items. Some are negotiating chips. And a handful are serious enough that the right move is walking away from the deal entirely.

This guide covers the red flags that fall into each category, with real cost estimates so you know what you're looking at when the report lands in your inbox.

Why Inspection Findings Are Not Created Equal

Every home inspection report will have a list of findings. A 20-year-old home might have 30–50 items flagged. This is normal. What separates experienced buyers from first-timers is the ability to sort those findings into three buckets:

  • Walk-away issues: Structural, safety, or environmental problems with large, uncertain repair costs
  • Negotiating items: Real issues where you can ask for a credit or repair
  • Normal wear items: Expected maintenance for the home's age; don't use these to renegotiate unless extremely severe

Red Flags: Consider Walking Away

Foundation and Structural Issues

Horizontal cracks in a concrete block foundation. Vertical cracks are usually settling. Horizontal cracks indicate lateral soil pressure — the wall is being pushed inward. This is a serious structural failure mode. Repair: $8,000–$30,000+, depending on extent of movement and repair method (wall anchors, carbon fiber straps, piering). A structural engineer assessment ($600–$1,200) is mandatory before proceeding. Even after repair, this is a permanent disclosure item that reduces resale value and may affect financing.
Signs of foundation settlement or movement. Doors and windows that won't open or close, floors that slope noticeably, gaps between walls and ceiling/floor, stairstep cracks in brick exterior — all suggest the foundation is moving. Causes range from drainage issues ($3,000–$8,000 to fix) to failed soil conditions requiring piering ($15,000–$60,000+). Without a structural engineer assessment, you cannot know which end of that range you're facing.

Water Intrusion and Moisture

Active water in the basement or crawl space. Standing water or staining at the base of walls indicates ongoing intrusion. Repair ranges from $4,000 for basic exterior grading and gutter extensions to $15,000–$35,000 for full interior waterproofing systems and sump pump installation — depending on the root cause. If a seller has concealed moisture evidence (fresh paint over stained concrete, new carpet over a wet slab), consider the ethics of the transaction alongside the repair cost.
Mold in living spaces or HVAC system. Surface mold in a bathroom is common maintenance. Mold in wall cavities, the attic, the crawl space, or spreading across HVAC ductwork is a different matter entirely. Professional mold remediation: $2,500–$15,000 depending on extent. If mold is in the HVAC system, the entire system may need decontamination or replacement. Always get an independent mold test before buying any home with suspicious staining or musty odors — inspectors note visible mold but don't test for it.

Electrical

Federal Pacific Electric (FPE) Stab-Lok panel or Zinsco panel. These panels, common in homes built between 1950–1990, have documented failure rates and are considered fire hazards by many electricians and insurance underwriters. Some insurers will not write policies on homes with these panels. Replacement: $2,500–$6,500 for a full panel upgrade. This is not a negotiating chip — it's a safety issue and an insurance obstacle. Walk away or require full replacement before closing.
Aluminum branch circuit wiring (not service entrance wiring). Aluminum wiring was used in branch circuits in many homes built 1965–1973 as copper prices spiked. Aluminum expands and contracts differently than copper, loosening connections over time and creating fire risk. Solutions include: pigtailing all outlets and switches with copper using CO/ALR connectors ($1,500–$4,500 for a full house) or complete rewiring ($8,000–$20,000). Any home with aluminum branch wiring needs this disclosure — check the listing and ask directly before the inspection.
Double-tapped breakers, missing GFCI outlets near water, amateur electrical work. These are significant safety items but far more manageable. A licensed electrician can address these for $500–$2,500 in most cases. Negotiate a credit rather than walk away.

Roof

Multiple layers of shingles at end of life. Two layers of worn shingles means the roof needs full tear-off and replacement. Combined with any signs of deck damage (soft spots, visible rot in the attic), you're looking at $12,000–$22,000 for a full replacement on a typical home. This is a legitimate walk-away trigger if the seller won't negotiate meaningfully on price or provide a credit.
Sagging ridge line or rafters. The roof should be straight when viewed from outside. Sagging indicates structural failure of the rafters, ridge board, or collar ties. This is not a roofing problem — it's a structural problem that requires a structural engineer assessment. Repairs can range from $5,000–$25,000 depending on the scope.

Pest and Environmental

Active termite infestation or significant termite damage. Termite treatment costs $500–$3,000 for the pest control itself. The real cost is structural wood damage — termites consume load-bearing beams and floor joists from the inside. Replacing structural members in a crawl space: $4,000–$15,000. In severe cases, floor systems need complete reconstruction. Always get a separate termite/WDO (wood-destroying organism) inspection in addition to the general inspection — many general inspectors don't certify for WDO.
High radon levels. Radon above 4 pCi/L (the EPA action level) is very common in certain geological zones and is the second-leading cause of lung cancer in the US. Radon is not a walk-away issue — mitigation systems cost $800–$2,500 installed and are highly effective. Require the seller to install one or provide a credit.

Items That Are Normal — Don't Over-Negotiate

Every home has deferred maintenance. These findings are normal for a home of any age and are not appropriate leverage for price renegotiation:

  • Dirty air filters, missing dryer duct covers
  • Minor caulk gaps around tubs, sinks, or windows
  • Exterior paint peeling in isolated areas
  • Gutters needing cleaning
  • Standard cosmetic issues: dated fixtures, scratched floors, worn carpet
  • Light switches or outlets slightly loose (not unsafe, just maintenance)

First-time buyers who try to renegotiate on every minor item often lose goodwill with sellers and sometimes lose the deal — over issues that total $400 in maintenance. Save your negotiating capital for real problems.

The inspector you choose matters: Look for inspectors certified by ASHI (American Society of Home Inspectors) or InterNACHI. Ask how long the inspection takes — less than 2 hours on a full home is a red flag. A thorough inspection of a 2,000 sq ft home takes 2.5–3.5 hours. Attend in person and follow the inspector through every room, every attic access point, and every crawl space entry.

After the Report: What to Do

Sort findings into walk-away, negotiate, and normal-wear categories. Get independent repair estimates from licensed contractors for anything in the first two categories — don't rely on the inspector's estimates (they're not contractors and their ranges are often wide). Present specific, documented repair costs to the seller, not vague objections. Ask for credits, not repairs where possible — sellers rushing to close often make poor repair choices, and a credit lets you control the quality of the fix.

If the seller refuses to negotiate on legitimate findings that total more than 1–2% of the purchase price, walk away and invoke your inspection contingency. That's what contingencies exist for. An earnest money deposit of $7,500 on a $375,000 home is fully protected by a properly written inspection contingency — you do not lose it when you walk away from a bad inspection.

Bottom Line

Home inspection findings range from noise to genuine deal-breakers. Foundation movement, active moisture intrusion, hazardous electrical panels, structural roof failure, and active pest damage are the issues that warrant serious reconsideration. Everything else is a negotiation. Know the cost of what you're looking at, get independent estimates, and let the numbers drive the decision — not the emotional exhaustion of being deep in a purchase process.
