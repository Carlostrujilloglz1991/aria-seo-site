The agency you choose determines your pay package, your housing stipend, your recruiter responsiveness at 2 AM when something goes wrong, and whether you get extensions at hospitals you love. The differences between a good agency and a bad one are worth $10,000–$30,000 per year. Here's a direct comparison for ICU specialty nurses in 2026.

The one strategy that beats everything: Sign with 2–3 agencies simultaneously. Agencies rarely share exclusive assignment access, so being on multiple rosters gives you more assignment options and creates natural competition on pay packages. Tell each recruiter you're working with others — they know anyway, and transparency builds a better working relationship.

Agency Deep Dives

Aya Healthcare

Top ICU volume Transparent pay Recruiter quality varies

Why ICU nurses use Aya: The largest independent travel nursing agency in the US. More assignment options than almost any competitor. Their app shows gross weekly pay clearly — no "mystery packages." Hospital relationships are strongest in California, Texas, and Florida.

Pay packages: Generally competitive to above-market in most specialties. ICU packages typically include strong base rates in high-cost markets. Housing stipends track GSA rates closely.

The catch: Recruiter quality varies enormously. Ask specifically for a recruiter who has ICU specialty experience. If your recruiter doesn't know what a PA catheter is, request a transfer.

Best use: First agency for new travelers, or primary agency in markets where they have strong hospital contracts (CA, FL, TX specifically).

Stability Healthcare

Above-market pay ICU specialist focus Strong stipends

Why ICU nurses use Stability: Smaller agency with a reputation for competitive pay packages, particularly for critical care nurses. They're known in the ICU community for taking the time to structure packages that maximize take-home on stipend optimization.

Pay packages: Routinely above-market on base rate + housing stipend combination. Worth getting a quote here to use as a benchmark when negotiating with larger agencies.

Assignment volume: Smaller hospital network than Aya or AMN. Best in specific markets — check assignment availability in your target area before committing.

Best use: Second or third agency for experienced travelers who know their market and want to optimize pay. Excellent benchmark for negotiating with larger agencies.

TNAA (Travel Nurse Across America)

Recruiter consistency Good mid-market pay Not always highest packages

Why ICU nurses use TNAA: The agency with the strongest reputation for recruiter retention and consistency. If you value having the same recruiter for years rather than someone who leaves every 6 months, TNAA is consistently mentioned. Their benefits package (health insurance options, 401k) is above average for the industry.

Pay packages: Competitive but not typically the highest in the market. Their value proposition is reliability and service consistency over maximum pay.

Best use: Primary agency for nurses who've been burned by recruiter turnover, or who want a long-term relationship with one agency rather than constantly shopping packages.

AMN Healthcare

Largest hospital network VMS contracts = lower pay Negotiate harder here

Why nurses use AMN (and why they complain about it): AMN has the largest hospital network of any agency — many major health systems have exclusive or preferred vendor contracts with AMN. If you want to travel to a specific prestigious hospital, AMN may be your only direct option.

The VMS problem: AMN operates many Vendor Management System (VMS) contracts where they're the manager of record for multiple agencies. This middle-man position sometimes results in lower pay packages than going direct. Always check if you can submit to the same hospital through a different agency before accepting an AMN offer.

Negotiation tip: AMN has more negotiating room than they typically show on first offer. Push on housing stipend specifically — this is where the VMS margin often lives.

Best use: When you specifically want a hospital that has an AMN exclusive contract. Otherwise, compare against Aya or Stability first.

Red Flags When Evaluating Any Agency

  • Pay package won't be broken down in writing. Any agency that gives you a total weekly number but won't show the breakdown (taxable rate + housing stipend + M&I) is hiding something. The breakdown matters because it affects your tax liability and your stipend documentation.
  • Recruiter can't explain your benefits clearly. If your recruiter stumbles on "what's the health insurance start date" or "how does the 401k match work," they're inexperienced. This matters when something goes wrong mid-contract.
  • Completion bonus terms are vague. Get completion bonus details in writing. "You'll get a bonus if you complete the contract" is not sufficient. You need: exact amount, payment date, conditions that could forfeit it.
  • No 24/7 clinical support contact. If something happens at 2 AM on your assignment — patient escalation, facility conflict, safety issue — you need a number to call. Agencies without this are not equipped for ICU nurse placements.
  • Housing is mandatory agency-provided with no stipend option. You should have the choice. If an agency refuses to allow self-managed housing with stipend, find one that does.

How to Get the Best Package From Any Agency

  1. Get offers from at least 2 agencies for the same assignment. Agencies sometimes have the same hospital contract and can bid against each other. Even if they don't, having a competing offer is leverage.
  2. Ask what the gross weekly is before taxes and what the stipend breakdown is. Compare apples to apples — total gross pay including stipends, not just hourly rate.
  3. Ask about the housing stipend relative to the GSA rate for that zip code. If they're offering significantly below GSA rate, there's room to push.
  4. Ask about extension bonuses. Many facilities pay higher rates on extension — agencies don't always volunteer this information, but most facilities pay a premium to retain good travelers through extension.
  5. Ask about referral bonuses. Most agencies pay $500–$2,000 for referred nurses who complete a contract. If you're traveling with a colleague, this is free money.

The Multi-Agency Strategy in Practice

The nurses who consistently earn the most from travel nursing maintain relationships with 2–3 agencies simultaneously. The practical playbook:

  • Primary agency: Your go-to for most assignments. Strong relationship with your recruiter. They know your preferences, specialty, and target markets.
  • Secondary agency: For markets where your primary doesn't have strong contracts, or to benchmark pay packages on every assignment your primary quotes.
  • Spot agency: Sometimes a specific hospital is only accessible through one agency. Keep credentials current with 1–2 specialty agencies that focus on your target facilities.

Keeping credentials current across multiple agencies takes 2–4 hours per year (mostly updating health records, skills checklists, and license documentation). The return: $5,000–$20,000 in additional annual pay from competitive package negotiation.
