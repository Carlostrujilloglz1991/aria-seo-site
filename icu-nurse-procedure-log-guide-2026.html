How to Build an ICU Nurse Procedure Log for CRNA School

Medical Disclaimer: This article is general educational information for licensed clinicians and students, not medical advice or a substitute for your institution's protocols or a provider's orders. Always follow your facility's policies and a provider's orders.

Updated 2026  |  More nurse finance guides →

Most ICU nurses don't start logging their procedures until they're ready to apply to CRNA school — and then they're scrambling to reconstruct numbers from memory. Don't make this mistake. Start logging now, even if CRNA school is two years away.

Why Procedure Logs Matter

CRNA programs don't just want ICU experience — they want to understand the complexity of your practice. A nurse who has managed 200+ arterial lines, run CRRT on 30+ patients, and assisted with 50+ intubations tells a very different story than one who "worked in an ICU for two years."

Procedure counts also feed your personal statement and interview. When you say "I've managed vasopressors for hemodynamically unstable patients," the committee wants to believe you mean it — and numbers make it credible.

What to Log

Log every procedure where you were the primary nurse, and every procedure you assisted with at a meaningful level.

Tier 1 — Log Every Single Occurrence

  • Arterial line management (each patient day, not just insertion)
  • Central line management
  • Mechanical ventilation management
  • Vasopressor initiation and titration (note which drugs)
  • CRRT management
  • Cardioversion / defibrillation
  • Temporary pacemaker management

Tier 2 — Log Monthly Totals

  • ABG interpretation and ventilator adjustments made
  • Hemodynamic monitoring (PAC, IABP if applicable)
  • Chest tube management
  • Code responses (note your role)

Tier 3 — Log Total Career Numbers

  • Total intubation assists
  • Total arterial line insertions (vs. management)
  • Total hours as charge nurse or resource RN

The Logging System

Paper beats digital for daily logging — a small pocket notebook lives in your scrubs. At the end of each shift, take 2 minutes to note: date, procedures, key clinical details. Then transfer to a spreadsheet weekly.

Your spreadsheet columns: Date | Patient type | Procedure | Your role (primary/assist) | Notes (anything notable about the case)

Keep this spreadsheet in a personal location — not on hospital systems. It's your professional record, not the hospital's.

The Clinical Story Bank

Beyond counts, log patient cases that demonstrate your best clinical thinking. De-identify completely (no name, DOB, MRN, dates specific enough to identify) and write a paragraph capturing:

  • Clinical presentation and acuity
  • Your assessment and the key decisions you made
  • What you managed, adjusted, or escalated
  • Outcome

These become your interview answers. "Tell me about your most critical patient" is infinitely easier to answer when you have 10 documented clinical stories to choose from.

When to Start

Now. The ICU nurse who applies to CRNA school with 2 years of experience and a detailed procedure log is a stronger candidate than the one with 3 years and nothing logged. The log signals intention, organization, and professional self-awareness — qualities programs want in anesthesia trainees.
