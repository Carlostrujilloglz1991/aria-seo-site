Travel Nurse Tax Home Requirements: How to Qualify and Protect It

The tax home is the most important — and most misunderstood — concept in travel nursing finance. Your entire housing and meal stipend structure depends on it. Lose your tax home, and every dollar of tax-free stipend you've received becomes retroactively taxable. Here's how tax home actually works, and how to protect yours.

What's at stake: Travel nurse stipends (housing, meals, incidentals) are only non-taxable if you are "temporarily away from home" on assignment. If you don't have a legitimate tax home, you're not temporarily away from anywhere — and the IRS can reclassify your stipends as ordinary income, plus back taxes, interest, and potentially penalties.

What Is a Tax Home?

A tax home, per IRS guidelines, is your principal place of business or employment — not your personal residence, though ideally they overlap. For travel nurses, the tax home is typically the area where you have your primary nursing job, maintain significant professional ties, and where you return between contracts. The IRS Publication 463 governs this; the key test is whether you are "away from home" on a temporary basis.

The critical word is temporary. A work assignment is temporary if it is expected to last (and actually lasts) less than one year. If you accept a position that is expected to be indefinite, or if you extend a contract at one location past the one-year threshold, that location can become your new tax home — eliminating the "away from home" status and making stipends taxable.

The Three-Factor IRS Test

The IRS uses three factors to evaluate whether you have a legitimate tax home. You don't need all three, but the more you have, the stronger your position:

FactorWhat It MeansHow to Satisfy It
1. Business connectionYou have real work expenses or income in the areaPer diem staff position, PRN work, moonlighting
2. Duplicate living expensesYou maintain a home and pay to live elsewhere on assignmentRent, mortgage, or housing costs at your home base
3. Main home (family ties)Your family, home, and life are anchored thereSpouse/family lives there, mail address, voter registration

Factor 2 — maintaining genuine duplicate living expenses — is the most important practical protection. If you have truly given up your housing at home (moved out, subletting fully, sold the home) and are living entirely on your travel assignments, you likely do not have a tax home.

What Counts as Maintaining a Tax Home

To maintain your tax home, you need to demonstrate real financial ties to a location between contracts. Strong evidence includes:

  • A lease or mortgage in your name that you are actively paying
  • Returning to the home between contracts (not just on paper)
  • A per diem or PRN shift at a local hospital during your time home
  • Voter registration, driver's license, and vehicle registration at the home address
  • State income tax filing as a resident of that state
  • Banking and financial accounts at that address

Simply having a parent's or friend's address on file is not sufficient — especially if you're never paying anything to stay there. The IRS looks at the economic reality, not just the paperwork.

Common Mistakes That Destroy Tax Home Status

Extending a single contract past 12 months at one facility. Even if you're technically on a "travel" contract, staying at one location for more than a year makes that location your new tax home. If you want to stay in an area long-term, rotate between facilities in that geographic area (different hospital systems, not just different units).

Other common mistakes: giving up your apartment at home and living exclusively on assignment locations; failing to document any local income or work connection to your home base; and assuming that having a state driver's license is enough. Documentation and economic substance are what matter in an audit.

The 50-Mile Rule (Sort Of)

Many travel agencies and nurses cite a "50-mile rule" — the idea that your assignment must be 50 miles from your tax home to qualify for stipends. The IRS does not have a strict 50-mile rule. What the IRS requires is that the assignment be far enough that staying overnight is a genuine necessity, not convenience. In practice, most agencies use 50 miles as their internal guideline, and placements closer than 50 miles to your tax home are generally not offered or accepted for stipend-eligible contracts.

If You're Concerned About Your Tax Home Status

If you're reading this and worried your current setup doesn't qualify, the right move is to consult a CPA who specializes in travel nurse taxes — not a general-practice accountant who files your return but may not know the nuances of IRC Section 162 and temporary work location rules. Resources like Traveltax.com and specialized travel nurse CPA services exist specifically for this situation.

The travel nurse tax reference books available on Amazon can give you a solid foundation, but for anything involving back-tax exposure, professional advice is worth every dollar.

The practical summary: Your tax home is valid as long as you maintain real housing costs at a fixed location, return there between assignments, and keep your work assignments temporary (under one year at any single facility). The stipends are only tax-free if you're genuinely incurring duplicate living expenses. Document everything — lease agreements, receipts, pay stubs from local PRN work — and never extend a single contract past 12 months at one site.
