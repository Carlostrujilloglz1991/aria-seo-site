Georgetown CRNA Program 2026

Georgetown University's Nurse Anesthesia program is a well-known name on the East Coast, based in Washington, D.C. Here's how the DNP is structured, what admissions actually weighs, and how an ICU nurse builds a competitive application to one of the region's established programs.

Quick profile: Georgetown's Nurse Anesthesia program sits within its School of Nursing and awards a Doctor of Nursing Practice (DNP). It's a full-time doctoral program with a strong clinical network across the Washington, D.C. metro and mid-Atlantic. Georgetown's Jesuit tradition emphasizes cura personalis — care of the whole person — which the program threads through its approach to clinical education.

Why Georgetown

Georgetown offers a nationally recognized university brand paired with a dense, high-volume clinical region. The Washington, D.C. metro packs together major academic medical centers, federal and military healthcare, and large community hospital systems, giving anesthesia residents broad case exposure across diverse patient populations. For nurses who want a respected program in a major metro — and who value the mission-driven, whole-person framing Georgetown brings — it's a compelling target.

Prerequisite courses, standardized-test policy, and required documentation shift between cycles, so confirm the current requirements directly with Georgetown's School of Nursing. The underlying expectation is stable: strong academics plus genuine high-acuity critical care experience.

The DNP Structure

Like all accredited nurse anesthesia programs since the Council on Accreditation's January 1, 2025 doctoral-entry requirement, Georgetown awards a DNP. Expect a front-loaded curriculum — advanced physiology, pharmacology, and anesthesia principles up front — transitioning into a progressive clinical residency across affiliated sites, plus a DNP scholarly project. The program is full-time and demanding; working during it is generally not feasible, so plan your finances for a period with little to no income.

Plan a balanced list: Georgetown is competitive. Apply alongside two or three regional target programs and at least one safer option so your cycle isn't riding on a single selective decision.

Building a Competitive Application

The strongest Georgetown applicants pair solid academics with real critical-care depth: one to two-plus years in a high-acuity ICU independently managing ventilated patients on vasoactive drips with arterial and central lines, the CCRN, and CRNA shadowing that lets them speak specifically about the role. Your personal statement and interview should show that you understand what anesthesia practice involves and why you want it — not simply that you want to leave the bedside. Leadership and precepting experience help you stand out in a strong pool.

Cost and Financial Planning

Georgetown is a private university, so tuition sits at the higher end, and the full-time doctoral schedule means little income during the program. Most residents rely on federal graduate loans and savings. The payoff comes after: new CRNAs commonly start around $185,000–$215,000, with locum and high-demand assignments reaching $240,000–$290,000+. The D.C. metro is a high-cost area, so budget realistically for living expenses on top of tuition. Model your total cost of attendance before committing.

Bottom line: Georgetown is an established, respected nurse anesthesia program in a clinically rich metro. Target it with a year or more of high-acuity ICU, a CCRN, real shadowing, and a specific, mission-aware application — and keep a balanced program list so your CRNA path isn't dependent on one selective outcome.
