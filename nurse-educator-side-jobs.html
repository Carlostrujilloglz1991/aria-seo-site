Nurse Educator Side Jobs: Teach CNA and EMT Courses for Extra Income

Nurses are uniquely positioned to earn extra income as instructors and educators — because the healthcare training industry has a constant, structural need for clinically credible instructors, and your RN license and bedside experience satisfy that need. The barrier to entry is lower than most nurses expect. Here's how to find teaching opportunities and what they pay.

Why this works: CNA programs, EMT courses, BLS/ACLS/PALS instructor programs, and community college nursing adjunct positions all need nurses who can teach clinical skills credibly. As a bedside ICU nurse, you have the clinical knowledge these programs need. The teaching skills themselves are trainable; the clinical credibility takes years and you already have it.

CNA Program Instructor

Certified Nursing Assistant programs are run by community colleges, vocational schools, long-term care facilities, and private training companies. State regulations typically require CNA program instructors to hold an active RN license with at least 2 years of experience (1 year of which must be in long-term care in some states — check your state's specific requirement). Some states allow RNs with acute care experience to qualify with a brief long-term care orientation.

CNA courses typically run 4 to 12 weeks and combine classroom instruction with clinical skills labs. As an instructor, you teach basic nursing procedures, infection control, patient rights, and supervised clinical practice. The content is below your ICU expertise level — which makes it manageable on top of a full-time nursing schedule.

Pay: CNA instructors at community colleges earn $30 to $60/hour as adjunct faculty, or a flat course stipend of $2,000 to $5,000 per course depending on the institution. Private training companies may pay $25 to $45/hour. Not high compared to your nursing rate, but the work is significantly less demanding than bedside care.

EMT and First Responder Courses

EMT programs at community colleges and fire departments regularly seek nurse instructors for clinical components. The National Registry of Emergency Medical Technicians doesn't have a formal nurse instructor credential — requirements are set by state EMS regulatory agencies. Many states allow RNs with relevant clinical experience to serve as clinical instructors for EMT courses, particularly for modules on patient assessment, IV skills, and medical emergencies.

Contact your local community college's EMS program coordinator directly. These positions are rarely publicly listed — the coordinators tend to maintain a roster of local clinicians willing to teach on a per-course basis. Fire department training academies and municipal EMS systems are another avenue.

BLS, ACLS, and PALS Instructor

This is the most accessible and immediately monetizable educator role for most ICU nurses. The American Heart Association (AHA) BLS/ACLS/PALS Instructor pathway requires:

  • Current provider certification in the course you want to teach (BLS, ACLS, PALS)
  • Completion of a Heartsaver Instructor course (available online)
  • Monitored teaching session with an approved Training Center
  • Affiliation with a Training Center (hospital, EMS agency, community college, or commercial training company)

Once credentialed, you can teach BLS/ACLS/PALS through your employer's training center (many hospitals run their own), through a regional training center that pays instructors per class, or independently by starting your own training business (more on that below).

Pay for institutional instructor positions: $25 to $50/hour for teaching inside a hospital or academic training center. Independent ACLS/BLS training businesses charge $50 to $250 per student per course and can be highly profitable with sufficient volume. AHA instructor resources are available through the AHA directly and on Amazon.

Community College Nursing Adjunct Faculty

Community college and vocational nursing programs hire adjunct (part-time) clinical faculty to supervise nursing students in clinical rotations. This is the most time-intensive educator side role — you're responsible for supervising 8 to 10 students during clinical shifts — but it's also the most professionally influential and often the best paid among educator side roles.

Requirements vary by state and institution: most require a BSN minimum, many prefer MSN, and all require relevant clinical experience. ICU nurses are well-positioned to supervise medical-surgical and critical care clinical groups. Community college adjunct clinical faculty typically earn $40 to $75/hour, with some institutions offering a flat clinical day rate of $250 to $450.

Starting an Independent Training Business

The most scalable educator side income for nurses is running an independent BLS/ACLS/PALS training company. After completing AHA instructor certification and affiliating with a training center, you can market directly to businesses (gyms, schools, daycares, dental offices, corporate offices) that need employee BLS certification. These clients don't need hospital-grade training — they need convenient, competitively priced basic life support renewal, and you can provide it.

A nurse running weekend BLS classes with 8 students at $75 per student earns $600 before expenses per 4-hour class. Two classes per weekend generates $1,200 — $62,400 per year. Add ACLS re-certifications for nursing and EMS markets at $150 to $200 per student and the revenue compounds. This is a real business that many nurses run successfully alongside full-time bedside positions.

Tax Treatment of Side Teaching Income

Teaching income from external employers (not your primary hospital) is typically W2. Independent training business income is self-employment income. Self-employment income is subject to SE tax but opens SEP-IRA contribution space — a meaningful retirement benefit for high-earner nurses looking to shelter additional income. The CRNA school application benefit: Teaching experience demonstrates exactly the communication and leadership skills that CRNA programs want. An ICU nurse who teaches ACLS certification courses can credibly say in a personal statement and interview that they've been explaining advanced cardiac life support to other healthcare providers — which is a preview of the educator role CRNAs play in practice. It's one more dimension that separates a one-dimensional application from a multifaceted one.
