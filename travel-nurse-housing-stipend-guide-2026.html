The housing stipend is the biggest single lever on travel nurse compensation — often $1,500–$3,000 per month, tax-free, on top of your base taxable wages. Understanding how stipends are set, when they're tax-free, and how to maximize them is worth more than negotiating your hourly rate.

How Travel Nurse Housing Stipends Work

Housing stipends are designed to reimburse you for the cost of maintaining temporary housing near your assignment. They're classified as "expense reimbursements" rather than income — which is why they're tax-free (when properly structured).

Two Ways Agencies Handle Housing

ModelHow It WorksProsCons

The stipend arbitrage: If your housing stipend is $1,800/month and you find housing for $1,200/month, you keep the $600 difference — tax-free. Over a 13-week contract, that's $1,800 saved. Over a full year of travel nursing, $7,200 extra in your pocket just from being a smart housing shopper.

How Stipend Amounts Are Set

Housing stipend amounts are tied to the GSA (General Services Administration) per diem rates for each location. The IRS requires that stipends don't exceed the applicable government per diem rates to maintain their tax-free status. This means:

  • High-cost cities (NYC, San Francisco, LA) have higher GSA rates → agencies can offer higher housing stipends
  • Low-cost rural areas have lower GSA rates → stipends are lower
  • Stipends can't legally exceed the GSA rate for that area without becoming taxable

The Roommate Strategy

Some experienced travel nurses travel in pairs with a friend or partner (both nurses on separate contracts at the same hospital or nearby hospitals). Splitting a two-bedroom apartment cuts housing costs roughly in half — turning a $2,000 stipend with $1,600/mo housing cost into a $2,000 stipend with $800/mo housing cost. $1,200/month profit per nurse, both still earning full stipends.

Tax-Free Status: The Requirements

Your housing stipend is only tax-free if you meet the IRS requirements for travel away from home. The key requirements:

  1. You have a legitimate tax home — a primary place of business/residence that you maintain even while on assignment
  2. You are temporarily away from your tax home — working in a different metropolitan area
  3. You have duplicate expenses — you're paying for your home base AND your assignment housing
If you don't have a tax home, your entire housing stipend is taxable. A $2,000/month housing stipend without a valid tax home = $24,000 in taxable income you didn't report. The IRS can audit and claw back taxes for up to 3 years (6 years in some cases). This is not a gray area.

Maintaining a Tax Home on a Nurse's Budget

The cheapest legitimate tax home options:

  • Rent a room from a family member or friend — $200–$400/month, with a signed lease/rental agreement and proof of payment (bank records). Must be fair market value for the local area.
  • Maintain your existing apartment — don't give it up. If your lease is $1,200/month and you're earning $2,000/month housing stipend, you're still netting $800/month tax-free by keeping the apartment.
  • Own property — a mortgage or inherited property gives you the strongest possible tax home. The mortgage payments during assignments are your dual-expense documentation.

Maximizing Your Housing Stipend: The Playbook

1. Always Take the Stipend Over Agency Housing

Agency-provided housing is convenient but leaves money on the table. Unless you're in a location where you genuinely can't find cheaper housing, the stipend-and-find-your-own approach produces $300–$800/month in pure profit per contract.

2. Book Monthly, Not Weekly

Most Airbnbs offer 15–40% discounts for monthly bookings. The same property that costs $2,800/month as individual weeks costs $1,800–$2,200 with a 28-day booking. Always book the maximum duration upfront.

3. Know Your Assignment City's Neighborhoods

Housing 20–30 minutes from the hospital is almost always dramatically cheaper than housing 5–10 minutes away. If you have a car (and most travel nurses do), the extra commute pays for itself with $300–$600/month in housing savings.

4. Lock In Housing Before Signing the Contract

Don't accept a contract in an expensive city without confirming you can find housing below your stipend amount. Some markets (NYC in particular) are so expensive that even with a $3,000/month stipend, you may net nothing after housing costs. Know the number before you sign.

5. Negotiate the Housing Stipend Separately

When a recruiter quotes you a package, push back specifically on the housing stipend: "What's the GSA rate for that zip code?" If the stipend is significantly below the local GSA rate, you have room to negotiate upward. Agencies set stipends with margin — there's often $100–$300/week of negotiating room.
