Lithium Toxicity for ICU Nurses 2026 — The Narrow-Window Drug That Poisons Slowly

Part of the ICU Emergencies Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Lithium is a lifesaving mood stabilizer with almost no margin for error. The dose that treats and the level that poisons sit right next to each other, and the things that push a stable patient into toxicity — a stomach bug, a new blood-pressure pill, a hot week — are ordinary life events. The nurse who understands how lithium leaves the body, and why the level and the patient don't always agree, protects a fragile therapeutic balance.

The short version: Lithium has a narrow therapeutic index (roughly 0.6–1.2 mEq/L therapeutic) and is cleared entirely by the kidneys, handled like sodium. Anything that lowers renal clearance or causes volume depletion — dehydration, AKI, thiazides, ACE inhibitors/ARBs, NSAIDs, low salt — raises the level. Toxicity is dominated by neurologic signs: tremor, ataxia, confusion, myoclonus, seizures, and in severe cases coma. Chronic toxicity is more dangerous at a given level than acute because tissue is already loaded. Treatment is aggressive isotonic fluids (restore renal clearance) and hemodialysis for severe cases. The level and symptoms can lag each other.

Why the kidney is the whole game

Lithium is handled by the kidney almost exactly like sodium: it is filtered and largely reabsorbed in the proximal tubule, and reabsorption climbs whenever the body is trying to hold onto sodium and water. That single fact explains nearly every toxicity trigger. When a patient is volume-depleted (vomiting, diarrhea, poor intake, heat), the kidney avidly reabsorbs sodium — and lithium along with it — and the level rises. Drugs that shift renal handling do the same: thiazide diuretics, ACE inhibitors and ARBs, and NSAIDs all reduce lithium clearance and can tip a stable patient into toxicity. A declining GFR from any cause does it too. So a huge part of nursing vigilance is spotting the setup before the level ever climbs.

The medication reconciliation is a lithium safety check. A new thiazide, ACE/ARB, or a run of NSAIDs, layered on a gastro illness or a heat wave, is a classic recipe for creeping lithium toxicity in a patient who "always took it fine." Flag these combinations, and watch renal function and hydration closely.

Acute vs chronic — the level can fool you

Lithium toxicity comes in patterns that behave differently. In acute overdose (a stable patient who suddenly takes a large amount), the early picture is often GI — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea — and the serum level can be high while the patient is still relatively well, because lithium hasn't yet distributed into tissues (especially the brain). In chronic toxicity (levels creeping up on a maintenance patient), tissue and brain are already saturated, so severe neurologic symptoms can appear at a lower serum level. This is why you treat the patient, not just the number: a "not that high" chronic level with confusion and ataxia can be more dangerous than a higher acute level in someone who looks okay. Levels also need correct timing (a true trough, not a post-dose peak) to interpret.

The neurologic picture and the other organs

The nervous system dominates serious lithium toxicity: a coarse tremor, ataxia, dysarthria, hyperreflexia, myoclonus, agitation or confusion, and at the severe end seizures and coma. A feared complication is SILENT (Syndrome of Irreversible Lithium-Effectuated NeuroToxicity), where neurologic deficits persist after levels normalize — a reason not to be casual about "the level came down." Lithium also causes nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (dilute urine, high output, worsening dehydration — a vicious cycle that raises the level further), thyroid dysfunction, and cardiac conduction/rhythm effects. Because dehydration both causes and results from lithium toxicity, restoring volume is doing double duty.

Treatment: fluids, then dialysis

The cornerstone is isotonic IV fluid (normal saline) to restore intravascular volume and renal perfusion, which increases lithium excretion — this alone corrects many cases. Stop the lithium and any contributing drugs (thiazide, ACE/ARB, NSAID), correct dehydration, and monitor levels serially, remembering that they can rebound as tissue lithium re-equilibrates. Hemodialysis is the definitive treatment for severe toxicity: significantly elevated levels with severe symptoms, renal failure (the patient can't clear it on their own), altered mental status/seizures, or hemodynamic instability. A practical dialysis caveat — lithium redistributes out of cells after a run, so levels often rebound and a second session may be needed. Activated charcoal does not bind lithium, so it isn't used for the lithium itself (though whole-bowel irrigation may be considered for large sustained-release ingestions).

Fluids are the first and best move. For most lithium toxicity, generous isotonic saline to reverse volume depletion and restore renal clearance is the highest-value early intervention a nurse can advocate for — and stopping the offending co-medications. Reserve dialysis for the severe/renal-failure cases, and expect a post-dialysis level rebound.

The nursing bottom line

Lithium is a narrow-window drug cleared entirely by kidneys that handle it like sodium, so dehydration, kidney injury, and thiazide/ACE-ARB/NSAID combinations quietly push stable patients into toxicity. The illness is mostly neurologic — tremor, ataxia, confusion, myoclonus, seizures — and the serum level can mislead: high but well early in acute overdose, and dangerously symptomatic at a lower level in chronic toxicity because the brain is already loaded. Treat with aggressive isotonic fluids to restore renal clearance, stop the drug and its accomplices, and use hemodialysis for severe or renal-failure cases while anticipating a rebound level. Charcoal doesn't help. The nurse's leverage is catching the setup on the med list and the hydration status before the level climbs, treating the patient rather than the number, and respecting that neurologic injury can outlast a corrected level.
