Travel nurses have a financial profile most credit card issuers don't design for — high income, significant housing and meal expenses, frequent city changes, and tax-free stipends that don't show up as "income" on paper. The wrong card leaves $800–$2,000 per year in rewards on the table. Here's what actually works.

Travel nurse edge: You spend heavily in categories that premium cards reward — gas, dining, lodging, and occasionally flights. A strategic card stack (2 cards maximum) captures 3–5% back on nearly everything without the complexity.

What NOT to Do (Common Travel Nurse Card Mistakes)

1. Using a debit card for anything rewardable

Every dollar spent on a debit card is a missed reward. A nurse spending $3,000/month and using a debit card leaves $720–$1,800/year in rewards uncaptured. Use credit cards for everything, pay in full every month.

2. Carrying a balance

The math inverts immediately. A 2% cash back card with a 24% APR costs you 22 net percent per dollar carried. This guide assumes you pay in full — if you can't commit to that, don't optimize for rewards, focus on eliminating the debt first.

3. Opening too many cards at once

Each hard inquiry drops your score 3–5 points temporarily. Opening 5 cards in a year to chase sign-on bonuses is counterproductive — it signals risk to lenders and can affect your ability to get a mortgage or auto loan. Stick to the 2-card stack and add one more only after 12+ months.

4. Picking the airline card of your home city

Travel nurses fly to assignments, not vacation destinations. The United/Delta/Southwest co-branded cards are optimized for loyalty flyers who want elite status. You don't need status on a carrier — you need transferable points. Chase Sapphire and Amex points transfer to multiple airlines, giving you flexibility across every assignment city.

5. Ignoring the sign-on bonus timing

Sign-on bonuses are some of the highest-value financial moves available. A $750 travel credit from a 60,000-point bonus on a $95/year card pays for 7+ years of annual fees in one move. The trick: open the card the week before you have a large planned expense (new assignment startup costs, medical equipment, flights). Don't manufacture spending — just align the timing.

Credit Card Strategy for CRNA School

If you're planning CRNA school, your income will drop significantly during the program. Plan your credit card strategy now:

  • Get your high-limit cards while you have travel nurse income — credit limits are set based on income at application time
  • The Chase Sapphire Preferred should be opened no later than your last year of travel nursing — the points accumulate while you're earning, and you redeem during school when cash flow is tight
  • Don't close cards when income drops — closed accounts hurt your credit utilization ratio
  • The Amex Blue Cash Preferred can be downgraded to the no-fee Blue Cash Everyday if you need to eliminate the annual fee during school
Bottom line: A travel nurse on the average 13-week contract earning $3,500–$5,000/week has unusual financial leverage — high income, high spending, and a career structure that generates sign-on bonuses. A strategic 2-card setup converts that spending into $1,000–$2,000 per year in additional value with 30 minutes of setup. The nurses who optimize this stack consistently end up with free flights and several thousand dollars in annual cash back that directly accelerates the CRNA school fund or passive income investments.
