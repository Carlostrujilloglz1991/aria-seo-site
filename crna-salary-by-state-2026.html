Why Opt-Out States Pay More

States that have opted out of the federal Medicare anesthesiologist supervision requirement — allowing CRNAs to practice independently without physician oversight — typically have higher CRNA salaries for two reasons. First, CRNAs in these states carry the full anesthesia responsibility and are billed as the sole provider, capturing the full anesthesia fee rather than splitting it with a supervising anesthesiologist. Second, opt-out states are disproportionately rural, where physician (and anesthesiologist) shortages create strong demand and scarcity-driven premiums. As of 2026, approximately 20 states have exercised the opt-out option. See our CRNA opt-out states guide for the complete list.

Independent Practice vs. Anesthesia Care Team

In Anesthesia Care Team (ACT) models — where an anesthesiologist medically directs 2 to 4 CRNAs — CRNA compensation is typically lower because billing is shared and the anesthesiologist captures a portion of the professional fee. In independent practice settings (common in rural critical access hospitals and ambulatory surgery centers in opt-out states), the CRNA bills as the sole anesthesia provider and retains the full fee. For self-employed or group-practice CRNAs, gross billings can reach $500,000 to $700,000 per year; after overhead and malpractice insurance, net income exceeds $300,000 to $400,000.

Locum / Travel CRNA Pay

Locum tenens and travel CRNAs consistently earn premium rates — often $200 to $350 per hour plus housing and travel stipends. For a CRNA willing to take 13-week travel contracts, total annual compensation can reach $350,000 to $500,000 or more. The trade-off: no benefits, self-employment tax on 1099 income, licensing fees in multiple states, and the administrative overhead of managing your own taxes and retirement accounts as an independent contractor.

Why Rural States Pay More

The single biggest predictor of CRNA salary is the degree to which a market depends on CRNAs to deliver anesthesia independently. In states where CRNAs practice without physician supervision — which includes most rural states and many that have opted out of the Medicare supervision requirement — CRNAs function as the sole anesthesia provider. Hospitals in these markets must pay competitively to attract and retain them because there is no cheap backup.

Urban markets with large academic medical centers often pay less because the supply of anesthesiologists is higher, creating more competition and allowing hospitals to use CRNAs in supervised roles with lower autonomy and lower pay.

CRNA vs. Anesthesiologist: The Pay Comparison

Anesthesiologists earn $350,000-$450,000+ on average, compared to $215,000-$240,000 for CRNAs nationally. The gap is real but narrowing in independent practice states. When you factor in the 4-year medical school debt ($250,000-$350,000 average), 4-year residency with resident pay, and delayed career start, the lifetime earnings comparison is much closer than the annual salary gap suggests.

A CRNA practicing independently in a rural market at $300,000+, starting work at 28-30 versus a physician starting at 32-33 with significant debt, accumulates comparable or greater net wealth over a 30-year career in many scenarios.

CRNA Locum Tenens: The Highest-Earning Path

Locum tenens CRNAs work short-term contracts at hospitals, surgery centers, and pain management practices that need temporary coverage. Rates in 2026 typically range from $220-$350/hour, with some rural and urgent placements reaching $400+/hour. At 40 hours/week, a locum CRNA earning $280/hour grosses $583,000 annually — before benefits considerations. The trade-off: no paid time off, no health insurance (must purchase independently), and the logistical demands of constant relocation.

The geographic strategy: Many CRNAs work the first 5-10 years in urban markets for training variety and career building, then relocate to rural markets where pay is highest and autonomy is greatest. The combination of urban training depth and rural market premium represents the peak earnings path for most CRNAs willing to relocate.
