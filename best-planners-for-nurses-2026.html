Best Planners for Nurses in 2026

For 12-Hour Shifts, ICU, Travel Nurses & Nursing School | Updated June 2026 | Written by a nurse

Quick answer: Most planners are designed for 9-5 weekday workers. Nurses need something different — undated (so missed days on days off don't create guilt), shift-aware scheduling, and space for CEU tracking and license renewal dates. Our top overall pick is the Nurse's Weekly Shift Planner. For travel nurses specifically, see card #4.

Standard planners fail nurses for one simple reason: they're built around Monday–Friday, 8–5 schedules. When you work three 12s this week, have four days off, then work a weekend, a dated planner becomes a record of blank pages.

After reviewing 11 nurse-focused planners available in 2026, here's what actually works for shift workers in healthcare.

What to Look for in a Nurse's Planner

  • Undated format — non-negotiable for shift workers. A dated planner you miss for four days feels like failure.
  • Shift-aware layout — not "morning / afternoon," but actual shift blocks (7a-7p, 7p-7a)
  • License and CEU tracker — renewal dates, completed contact hours, certification expiration
  • Self-care and recovery sections — post-night-shift recovery is real work
  • Goal planning beyond clinical — for nurses pursuing CRNA, NP, or financial independence

What About Digital Planners for Nurses?

Digital planners (Notion, GoodNotes templates, etc.) work well for some nurses, especially those already heavy tablet users. The tradeoff: phones and tablets aren't allowed in many clinical areas, so a paper planner stays accessible during breaks and handoffs. For anything involving clinical notes or patient information, paper also avoids HIPAA compliance concerns that can arise from personal device use.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use a regular planner as a nurse?

You can, but you'll fight the format constantly. Dated daily planners create guilt on days off. Weekly spreads with M-F layouts don't match shift schedules. The nurse-specific options above are priced the same as generic planners and built to actually fit your schedule.

What should a nurse track in a planner?

At minimum: shift schedule, license renewal dates, CEU hours completed, certification expiration (BLS, ACLS, PALS, CCRN, etc.), and professional development goals. If you're pursuing advanced practice (CRNA, NP, DNP), add study hours and program milestones.

I'm a new grad nurse — which planner should I start with?

The Nurse Self-Care Planner (#1 above) is the best starting point. It's general enough to adapt to any unit, includes self-care tracking (which new nurses routinely neglect), and has enough goal-setting structure to grow with you.
