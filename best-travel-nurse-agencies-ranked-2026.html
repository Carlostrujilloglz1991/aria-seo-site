Choosing a travel nursing agency is one of the most consequential decisions a traveler makes — and most nurses make it by responding to the first recruiter who calls them. That's not a strategy. Agency choice affects your pay package (sometimes by $300–$600/week on the same contract), how fast you get credentialed, how responsive your recruiter is at 2am when you have a question, and what happens when a contract gets cancelled.

This comparison covers five agencies extensively referenced in travel nursing communities, based on publicly available nurse reviews, industry analysis, and reported pay transparency practices. We evaluated each on pay transparency, recruiter responsiveness, benefits quality, contract volume, and crisis capability.

Host Healthcare — Best Starting Point for New and Experienced Travelers

#1 Overall Recommended Starting Point

Host Healthcare

Host Healthcare consistently earns top marks in travel nursing community reviews for pay transparency, recruiter responsiveness, and benefits quality. Unlike some larger agencies that hide bill rates and obscure pay packages, Host Healthcare is known for open communication about total compensation — including sharing bill rates when nurses ask directly, which is still uncommon in the industry.

Day-one health insurance is offered with multiple plan tiers, including dental and vision. Professional liability coverage is included. Recruiters are frequently mentioned by name in positive reviews — a signal that relationships are sustained rather than handed off. For nurses doing their first contract, this combination of transparency and responsiveness significantly reduces the stress of an already-complex process.

Contract volume is strong but not the deepest on the market — if you have a very niche specialty in a very specific geography, Aya or AMN may have more options. For the majority of specialties in most markets, Host has competitive listings.

TNAA — Travel Nurse Across America

#2 Overall

Travel Nurse Across America (TNAA)

TNAA is a mid-size agency with a strong reputation in nurse communities for recruiter longevity — the same recruiter often stays with a nurse across multiple contracts, which creates better context and continuity than agencies that frequently reassign accounts. They're consistently mentioned in positive light on r/TravelNursing for their recruiter relationships and benefits quality.

Pay transparency is good but not quite at Host Healthcare's level — bill rate sharing is inconsistent depending on the recruiter. Benefits include day-one health coverage, competitive health plan quality, and 401(k) with matching at 1%, which is above average for the industry. Housing stipend amounts are competitive in most markets.

Contract volume is solid in the South and Midwest but somewhat thinner in high-cost coastal markets compared to Aya or AMN. For nurses who prioritize long-term recruiter relationship over maximum contract options, TNAA is a strong choice.

Strengths

  • Recruiter longevity — same recruiter across contracts
  • Day-one benefits with strong plan quality
  • 401(k) with matching (rare in this industry)
  • Good reputation in Midwest and Southeast markets

Limitations

  • Thinner contract volume in coastal markets
  • Bill rate transparency inconsistent by recruiter
  • Limited crisis contract specialization

Best for: Nurses who want a long-term agency relationship, nurses working primarily in the South or Midwest.

FlexCare Medical Staffing

#3 Overall

FlexCare Medical Staffing

FlexCare stands out for a specific approach to pay transparency: their "S3 (Single Salary Schedule)" model shows nurses a unified pay rate rather than breaking out stipends separately. This makes it easier to compare FlexCare's offer against another agency's offer on an apples-to-apples basis, which is a genuine advantage for nurses who struggle to decode complex pay packages.

Recruiter quality is generally well-reviewed but more variable than Host or TNAA — a common note is that experience depends heavily on which recruiter you land with. Contract volume is solid but smaller than the largest national agencies. Benefits include day-one health coverage and competitive stipends.

FlexCare's sweet spot is nurses who want pay simplicity and are in markets where FlexCare has strong hospital relationships (Pacific Northwest, California, and some Northeast markets).

Strengths

  • S3 model makes pay comparison easier
  • Strong in Pacific Northwest and California
  • Transparent total compensation approach
  • Day-one benefits

Limitations

  • Recruiter quality variable by individual
  • Smaller contract volume than large nationals
  • Less strong outside core markets

Best for: Nurses in West Coast markets, nurses who want pay transparency in a simplified format.

Aya Healthcare

#4 Overall

Aya Healthcare

Aya Healthcare is one of the largest travel nursing agencies in the US by contract volume. If your specialty is uncommon or you want to work in a location with limited contract availability, Aya's scale is a real advantage — they simply have more listings than most competitors in most markets. They're also strong in crisis and rapid-response contracts, which offer premium pay rates for nurses willing to work in high-need situations.

The tradeoffs that appear frequently in nurse community discussions: recruiter responsiveness is more inconsistent than at boutique agencies, and pay transparency is lower than Host Healthcare or FlexCare. Nurses report that Aya's pay packages are often opaque, and getting bill rate information requires more persistence. Benefits are solid but not exceptional.

Aya is often best used as one of several agencies a traveler works with simultaneously — for access to their large contract volume — rather than as a sole agency.

Strengths

  • Largest contract volume in most markets
  • Strong crisis contract capability
  • Good for niche specialties and hard-to-fill locations
  • Solid benefits quality

Limitations

  • Pay transparency below industry best
  • Recruiter responsiveness inconsistent
  • Better as one of multiple agencies than sole agency
  • Higher nurse-to-recruiter ratio than boutique agencies

Best for: Experienced travelers who understand pay packages and want maximum contract volume, niche specialties, crisis nurses.

AMN Healthcare

#5 Overall

AMN Healthcare

AMN Healthcare is the largest healthcare staffing company in the US. Their scale gives them hospital relationships and contract volume that no boutique agency can match. Their technology platform and credentialing infrastructure are among the most sophisticated in the industry — nurses who appreciate efficient digital onboarding often point to AMN as a standout.

The consistent feedback from travel nursing communities, however, is that AMN's size creates a transactional feel. Pay packages are frequently reported as below market rate for the bill rate; the large scale means less individualized recruiter attention; and pay transparency is often described as the weakest among the agencies reviewed here. AMN is frequently used by hospitals as a last-resort agency, which can mean better access to contracts others can't fill — but it also means you may be negotiating against their scale rather than with it.

Strengths

  • Largest hospital contract network in the US
  • Strong technology and digital credentialing
  • Access to contracts unavailable at smaller agencies
  • Good for nurses who want institutional stability

Limitations

  • Pay packages frequently reported as below market
  • Lowest pay transparency of agencies reviewed
  • Less individualized recruiter relationship
  • Reported difficulty negotiating on pay

Best for: Nurses who need very specific hospital system access, nurses who prioritize platform stability and efficiency over recruiter relationship.

Working With Multiple Agencies: The Smart Traveler's Approach

Experienced travel nurses almost universally recommend working with 2–3 agencies simultaneously rather than committing exclusively to one. Here's why this matters:

The same contract, different pay

Multiple agencies can have contracts at the same hospital. The total bill rate is set by the hospital, but each agency's internal margin differs. Submitting to the same position through two agencies and comparing the pay packages directly is one of the most effective ways to leave nothing on the table.

Leverage and negotiation

When you can tell your recruiter "Agency B is offering $400/week more for the same contract," you have real leverage. Some agencies have flexibility in their margin; others don't. You won't know which until you test it.

Protection against cancellations

If your contract is cancelled mid-run, having an existing relationship with a second agency means faster placement options. Starting a new agency relationship after a cancellation, when you need income immediately, is a much weaker position.

How to manage multiple agencies without conflict

The key rule: never allow two agencies to submit your profile to the same facility for the same position simultaneously. This creates a conflict that can disqualify you from both. When submitting to a position, ask each recruiter which facility it's at and confirm you haven't already been submitted there by another agency.

Recommended approach for first-time travelers: Start your process with Host Healthcare for their recruiter support and transparency. Once you understand how pay packages work and feel confident evaluating offers, add a second agency (TNAA or FlexCare depending on your geography) for comparison. After your first contract, add a third agency with high volume (Aya or AMN) for access to more listings. By contract three or four, you'll have the context to evaluate every offer critically.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many agencies should I work with at once?

Two to three is the sweet spot for most travelers. More than three creates too much coordination overhead (tracking which agency submitted you where) without proportional benefit. Start with one or two on your first contract; build to three as you gain experience.

Can agencies match each other's offers?

Sometimes. If you have a competing offer in writing, some agencies will match or come close to it. Others have rigid pay scales and can't. Sharing a competing offer is not a betrayal — it's a negotiation. Most recruiters expect it from experienced travelers.

What's a red flag in a recruiter relationship?

Pressure to sign quickly, unwillingness to put compensation promises in writing, refusal to provide a written pay package breakdown before asking for a commitment, and inability to tell you which facility a contract is at (a common tactic to prevent you from submitting through another agency).

Does agency size predict pay quality?

Not directly. Some of the lowest reported pay packages in nurse communities come from the largest agencies. Smaller boutique agencies often operate with lower margins and pass more compensation to nurses because they compete on nurse satisfaction rather than hospital-side scale. This is why bill rate transparency matters — size alone doesn't tell you where the margin is going.
