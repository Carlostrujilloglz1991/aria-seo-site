How Nurses Can Earn $500-$2,000/Month Teaching Online

Nurses are some of the most credible educators in healthcare — and nursing education has a permanent, structural shortage of qualified instructors. The Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that nursing schools turn away qualified applicants every year due to insufficient clinical faculty, not insufficient classroom space. That shortage is your opportunity.

Online nursing education isn't just Udemy courses. It spans adjunct faculty positions at accredited schools, one-on-one tutoring, continuing education content creation, and corporate training for healthcare companies. The income range varies significantly by channel — here's a breakdown of each.

Online Adjunct Faculty: $1,000–$3,500 Per Course

Community colleges and online nursing programs hire adjunct clinical faculty — part-time instructors who typically need an MSN or at minimum a BSN with clinical experience. If you have your BSN and 3–5 years of specialty experience, many programs will hire you for online theory courses even without a master's degree, particularly in rural or high-demand areas.

Adjunct pay is calculated per credit hour or per course. A 3-credit online nursing theory course typically pays $1,500–$3,500 per 8-week or 16-week semester. Teaching two courses per semester equals $3,000–$7,000 per semester — roughly $6,000–$14,000 per year in supplement income for 10–15 hours of work per week per course.

Where to apply: WGU (Western Governors University), Chamberlain University, Grand Canyon University, and Rasmussen College all hire online adjuncts. Local community colleges with online LPN-to-RN bridge programs are often easier to break into than large national programs.

The MSN question: An MSN opens adjunct positions at more schools and at higher pay tiers. If you don't have one, several employers (WGU, Chamberlain) offer tuition assistance that can cover 70–100% of MSN costs. Getting an MSN while teaching one adjunct course per semester is a common ladder.

NCLEX Tutoring: $50–$120/Hour

The NCLEX pass rate has fluctuated significantly since the 2023 NextGen NCLEX launch. Many nursing students, particularly those who failed their first attempt, seek one-on-one tutoring from an experienced RN who can explain clinical reasoning in a way that clicks.

Private NCLEX tutoring charges $50–$120/hour depending on your specialty knowledge and platform. Wyzant, Superprof, and direct client referrals from nursing schools are the main channels. Setting a minimum package (three sessions, not one-off) filters out uncommitted clients and creates consistent income: 6 clients × 3 sessions × $65/hour = $1,170 for 18 hours of work.

If you've worked med-surg, you're strong on most NCLEX content. If you're a specialty nurse (ICU, OR, OB), pair with a med-surg coach for client referrals, or focus your niche on the clinical reasoning questions where specialty nurses excel.

Continuing Education Content: $500–$2,000 Per Module

Nurses are required to complete continuing education hours to maintain licensure. Someone has to create that content. Healthcare staffing agencies, hospitals, professional nursing organizations, and CE platforms (NursingCE, Relias, ProCE, NurseCE4Less) all commission CE modules from clinically credible writers and instructors.

A typical 1-contact-hour CE module requires 1,000–1,500 words of learning content, 5–10 test questions, and references. Payment varies: $300–$600 for a 1-hour module from a mid-tier platform, $800–$2,000 from hospital systems commissioning proprietary content, and $150–$400 for contributing to multi-author course collections.

To break in: pitch your specialty directly. An ICU nurse with 5+ years of experience pitching a vasopressor titration module to NursingCE is far more likely to get a yes than a generalist submission. Lead with your credentials and clinical specifics.

Corporate Healthcare Training: $75–$150/Hour

Home health agencies, assisted living companies, insurance companies, and medical device manufacturers all train staff on clinical topics. They hire RNs with specialty experience to develop or deliver that training. This is often project-based — a 10-hour training curriculum project at $100/hour = $1,000 — rather than ongoing employment.

Start by pitching local home health agencies or DME (durable medical equipment) companies. They often run on thin margins and can't afford full-time clinical educators, making them ideal buyers for part-time contract training work. A 2-hour annual competency training session for 20 staff at $100/hour = $200 per session, but you can package and sell the same material to multiple agencies.

Create and Sell Your Own Course: $100–$800/Month Passive

Udemy and Teachable allow nurses to sell recorded video courses. The passive income here is real but takes significant upfront effort — a good 3-hour course requires 40–100 hours of production time (recording, editing, captions, thumbnails, description writing).

Realistic first-year income from a Udemy nursing course without an existing audience: $100–$400/month. With an audience (a few thousand TikTok followers, a Facebook group, or an email list): $400–$1,200/month. Evergreen topics with low competition perform best: specialty certification prep (CCRN, CEN, CNOR), clinical reasoning for new grads, and niche clinical skills tutorials.

Which Path Fits Your Life Right Now

Adjunct faculty work requires the most ongoing time commitment (course prep, grading, student communication) but offers the most reliable monthly income and the potential to grow into full-time faculty with benefits. Tutoring offers the highest hourly rate but requires active selling and scheduling. CE content creation offers good pay with flexible timelines. Courses offer passive income but require the most upfront investment.

The sweet spot for most working nurses: one or two steady tutoring clients ($300–$500/month) plus a CE module or two per year ($600–$1,200 annually), with a longer-term goal of qualifying for adjunct work once the MSN is complete. You can be earning $500–$800/month from teaching within 60 days of starting, without quitting your day job.
