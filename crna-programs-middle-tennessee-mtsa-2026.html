The Middle Tennessee School of Anesthesia (MTSA), located just outside Nashville in Madison, Tennessee, is unusual among CRNA programs: it's one of the country's oldest freestanding, anesthesia-focused schools rather than a nurse anesthesia track buried inside a larger university nursing college. That specialization is its identity — the entire institution exists around nurse anesthesia education. For ICU nurses mapping a path to CRNA, MTSA is a distinctive option worth understanding. Here's a practical, applicant-focused overview for 2026.

Verify the details before you apply. Program length, tuition, prerequisites, and admissions criteria change year to year and by cohort. Treat everything below as an orientation, not a source of truth — confirm current specifics directly with MTSA's admissions office and official program materials before making decisions.

Program format and degree

Like every US nurse anesthesia program today, MTSA's entry-level program culminates in a doctoral credential (a DNP/DNAP-level nurse anesthesia degree), in line with the national requirement that all new CRNAs graduate at the doctoral level. Programs of this type generally run about 36 months of full-time study — front-loaded didactic coursework followed by an intensive clinical residency across affiliated hospital and surgical sites. Because MTSA is a dedicated anesthesia institution, its curriculum, faculty, and culture are built entirely around this single track, which some applicants find appealing. MTSA has also historically offered post-master's completion pathways for practicing CRNAs; confirm which programs are currently open.

Typical prerequisites

Nurse anesthesia admissions requirements are fairly standardized across programs. For MTSA, expect the core elements common to competitive CRNA schools:

  • An unencumbered RN license and a BSN (or equivalent) from an accredited program.
  • A minimum of one year — realistically more — of recent, full-time critical care experience in an adult ICU managing vasoactive drips, ventilators, and invasive lines.
  • A competitive GPA, with science and graduate coursework weighted heavily; many admitted applicants exceed a 3.5.
  • GRE requirements that vary by cycle — confirm whether the current year requires, recommends, or waives it.
  • CCRN certification, strong references, a personal statement, and a formal interview.
  • CRNA shadowing hours, and sometimes prerequisite graduate-level science depending on the year.

Admissions competitiveness

CRNA admission is competitive everywhere, and MTSA's national reputation as a dedicated anesthesia school draws a strong applicant pool. The differentiators are the standard ones: the acuity of your ICU experience (a high-acuity unit — CVICU, medical/surgical ICU, or trauma — reads stronger than a step-down), CCRN certification, an upward GPA trend, genuine understanding of the role through shadowing, and a sharp interview. Applicants often note that MTSA looks for a clear, demonstrated commitment to the anesthesia profession specifically — this is a school where fit and focus matter.

Cost and financing

As a private, specialized institution, MTSA's tuition typically lands in the six figures across the full program once fees, Nashville-area living costs, and the near-impossibility of working during the clinical phase are accounted for. Most students finance this with a mix of federal graduate loans and savings. The offset is the payoff — CRNAs are among the highest-earning advanced practice nurses, so the return on investment tends to be strong despite the sticker price. The admissions formula is the standard CRNA one — high-acuity adult ICU experience, CCRN, a strong GPA, shadowing, and a strong interview — with an added emphasis on demonstrated commitment to the profession. Confirm every current detail, including which program tracks are open, directly with MTSA before applying.
