The 2026 Travel Nurse Tax Hub — Tax Home, Stipends, and Every State in One Place

The one-sentence version: Your stipends are only tax-free if you maintain a legitimate tax home, you generally owe income tax both where you live and where you work (with a credit so you're not fully double-taxed), and the burden of proving all of it sits on you — not your agency. This hub organizes the whole system: the three core rules, the multi-state filing mechanics, and the individual state guide for every state we cover.

Travel nurse taxes are not hard because the math is hard. They're hard because the system was designed for people who live and work in one place, and you don't. The recruiters quoting you "$2,400/week, mostly tax-free" are describing the best case of a set of rules they are not responsible for helping you meet. This page is the map; each linked guide is the territory.

Start Here: The Three Rules That Decide Everything

1. The tax home rule

Tax-free housing and meal stipends exist for one reason: the IRS lets employers reimburse you for duplicating living expenses while traveling away from your tax home for work. No tax home, no duplication — and every stipend dollar becomes taxable wages. What makes a tax home real (regular work area or maintained residence, duplicated expenses, ongoing ties) is the single most important concept in travel nursing finance, and it's covered in depth in the travel nurse tax home guide.

2. The duplicated-expenses test

Keeping a tax home means actually paying for it — rent or mortgage at home while also paying for housing at your assignment. Renting your home out full-time, crashing with parents for free, or letting the lease lapse between contracts are the classic ways travelers quietly lose their tax home without noticing until an audit. The complete travel nurse tax guide walks the whole test with examples.

3. The one-year rule

Work in the same metro area long enough — the IRS uses one year as the line where an assignment stops being "temporary" — and that area can become your new tax home, converting your stipends to taxable income going forward. Extensions feel harmless one at a time; the calendar doesn't care. Timelines, the 30-day-break myth, and how to sequence contracts are in the step-by-step filing guide.

Multi-State Filing: How the Pieces Fit

The general shape for most travelers: you file a resident return in your home state (which taxes all your income, wherever earned) and a nonresident return in each state you worked (which taxes what you earned there). Your home state then usually gives you a credit for taxes paid to other states, so the same dollar isn't fully taxed twice — you effectively pay the higher of the two rates. Reciprocity agreements, states with no income tax on either end, and cities with their own income taxes (looking at you, Ohio and Pennsylvania locals) all bend that general shape, which is exactly what the state guides below are for.

The move that saves the most money isn't a deduction: it's choosing where your tax home is and which states you work in with the filing consequences priced in ahead of time. A $2,200/week package in a no-income-tax state can out-net a $2,400/week package in a high-tax state — run it before you sign, not in April. The travel nurse budgeting guide has the net-pay comparison worksheet.

Paying As You Go and Staying Audit-Proof

Two housekeeping systems keep the whole thing from blowing up. First, quarterly estimates: if you have 1099 income (per diem apps, local agency gigs, side income) or your multi-state withholding comes up short, the quarterly tax guide covers safe-harbor rules and how to actually send the payments. Second, the paper trail: keep every lease, mortgage statement, utility bill, contract, and mileage log that proves your tax home and duplicated expenses — scanned, dated, and boring. If you pick up extra shifts at home between contracts, the overtime tax guide explains why the big check gets over-withheld (not over-taxed). And before you count stipend money as savings, reconcile it against the GSA per diem rates guide — packages that pay stipends above the federal rate for the area are a known audit flag.

Honest bottom line (and a disclaimer): Most travel nurses do not need exotic strategies — they need a real tax home, clean records, correct multi-state returns, and packages evaluated on net pay. This hub and its guides are education, not tax advice: rules change, states differ, and your facts matter. For anything beyond a routine year, use a CPA who works with travel nurses specifically — the fee is usually recovered in the first mistake they prevent.
