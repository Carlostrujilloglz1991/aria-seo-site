Nurse Per Diem Shifts: How to Find and Maximize Extra Income

Per diem nursing — picking up shifts as needed at a flat hourly rate without a guaranteed schedule — is the most flexible way for nurses to earn extra income using their existing license and skills. Done strategically, it can add $15,000 to $30,000 per year on top of your primary income. Here's how to find per diem positions and maximize what they pay.

Per diem vs. PRN: These terms are often used interchangeably, but there's a subtle difference in some health systems. "Per diem" typically means as-needed with no committed hours or benefits. "PRN" (pro re nata, Latin for "as needed") can mean the same thing or may imply a minimal shift commitment per pay period. Confirm the actual requirements before accepting either designation — some "PRN" positions require 4 to 6 shifts per month and a certain number of weekend or holiday shifts.

Where to Find Per Diem Nursing Positions

Your own hospital first: The easiest per diem position to land is at your own employer. Most health systems maintain a float pool or per diem pool that allows staff nurses to pick up extra shifts. Ask HR or your manager about the internal per diem pool — at many facilities, staff nurses can add per diem status at a higher hourly rate for shifts picked up beyond their scheduled hours.

Other hospitals in your area: Many hospitals near your primary employer will hire per diem nurses — you just need a separate application and orientation (usually 1 to 3 days). Having per diem credentials at 2 to 3 facilities in your metro area dramatically expands your shift availability, especially useful if one hospital has low census while another is slammed.

Staffing agency per diem: Agencies like Aya Healthcare, AMN, Cross Country, and regional agencies also place per diem (local) nurses in addition to travel nurses. You pick your shifts, decline what you don't want, and can often earn more per hour than a direct hospital per diem rate — though you'll typically be self-responsible for benefits.

Gig-style nursing apps: Platforms like Clipboard Health, ShiftMed, and CareRev connect nurses directly with facilities for per diem shifts. These platforms often pay same-day or next-day and require minimal onboarding. ICU nurses are in particularly high demand on these platforms — critical care shifts are harder to fill than med-surg.

An ICU nurse earning $40/hour base who picks up agency per diem at $55/hour is earning 37.5% more for the same clinical work. Over 6 extra shifts per month, that's roughly $1,620/month in additional income — $19,440 per year. The math compounds quickly when you add in holiday and high-demand shift bonuses.

Timing Your Shifts for Maximum Pay

Not all per diem shifts pay the same. Facilities pay premiums for the hardest-to-fill times. The highest-paying windows are typically:

  • Weekend nights: Weekend differential plus night differential stack — often the highest base rate of any shift
  • Major holidays: Christmas, Thanksgiving, New Year's, and Fourth of July typically pay time-and-a-half to double-time plus differential
  • Last-minute urgent fills: Call-out shifts posted within 24 hours often include an additional incentive bonus of $50 to $200 flat on top of the hourly rate
  • Short-notice crisis rates: During high census or surge periods, some facilities offer "crisis" or "incentive" rates that can double the normal per diem rate for that specific period

Set up alerts on any per diem apps you use so you see last-minute postings immediately. The nurses who earn the most from per diem are the ones who are responsive and available for urgent fill requests — not the ones who browse casually a week in advance.

Protecting Your Primary Job While Per Diem

Many nurses assume their primary employer has no say over outside per diem work. That's not always true. Some health systems include non-compete-adjacent clauses in employment contracts or have conflict-of-interest policies. Review your employment agreement and understand whether you need to disclose outside employment. At most systems there's no issue, but being surprised mid-tax-season is avoidable.

Watch your hours and fatigue. Per diem income is only valuable if you can perform safely on both your primary shifts and your extra ones. Working 60+ hours per week consistently is a patient safety risk and a burnout risk. Most nurses doing sustainable per diem work pick up 2 to 4 extra shifts per month — not 2 per week indefinitely.

Tax Implications of Per Diem Income

If you work per diem through an agency that pays W2, taxes are withheld normally. If you work through a gig platform that pays you as a 1099 contractor (increasingly common), you're responsible for self-employment tax (15.3% on top of income tax) plus making quarterly estimated tax payments. Budget approximately 25 to 30% of 1099 per diem income for taxes. The advantage of 1099 income is that it opens SEP-IRA contribution space — see our travel nurse retirement guide for how to leverage this.

Per diem as a tax home tool: For travel nurses, maintaining a PRN or per diem position at a hospital near your tax home base is one of the strongest ways to demonstrate a business connection to your home area — which is a key factor in preserving your tax home status. The per diem position doesn't need to be extensive: 2 to 4 shifts per month may be sufficient to demonstrate active employment ties. This is a strategy worth discussing with a travel nurse CPA.
