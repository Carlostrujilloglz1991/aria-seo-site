Michigan offers several established nurse anesthesia programs clustered around Detroit and Flint, plus strong clinical access through the state's large academic and trauma systems. Here's how the Michigan programs compare and how an ICU nurse in Detroit, Ann Arbor, Flint, or Grand Rapids builds a competitive application.

Michigan CRNA market context: Michigan combines major academic medical centers with a strong manufacturing-region hospital network, producing steady anesthesia demand statewide. New graduates commonly start around $185,000–$215,000, with locum and rural assignments reaching $240,000–$290,000+. Lower cost of living than the coasts means that salary stretches further, an advantage for nurses paying down school debt.

All Michigan programs are doctoral-entry. The Council on Accreditation phased out master's-level CRNA entry on January 1, 2025, so every accredited program now awards a DNP. Tuition ranges shown are estimates of total program cost and vary with residency status and cohort year — confirm current figures directly with each school. The public-university options (UM-Flint, Wayne State, Oakland) generally offer the best value for Michigan residents.

University of Michigan-Flint

UM-Flint's DNP nurse anesthesia program carries the University of Michigan name and draws on clinical sites across mid- and southeast Michigan. The program is well regarded regionally and gives students access to high-acuity rotations within strong partner health systems. For Michigan residents, the public-university tuition is competitive relative to the private alternatives.

Wayne State University — Detroit

Wayne State's nurse anesthesia program is based in the heart of the Detroit Medical Center complex, one of the busiest trauma and critical-care environments in the Midwest. Students train amid extremely high case volume and acuity, including Level I trauma. The combination of an established program, public-university tuition, and a dense urban clinical environment makes Wayne State a strong choice for many Michigan applicants.

Oakland University and University of Detroit Mercy

Oakland University's DNP, developed in partnership with the Beaumont (now Corewell Health) system, places students within one of the largest hospital networks in southeast Michigan. University of Detroit Mercy offers a private-university DNP with a long nursing tradition and clinical ties across the Detroit metro. Together with Wayne State and UM-Flint, these programs give the southeast-Michigan corridor real depth of options.

Programs want sustained adult critical care managing ventilated patients on vasoactive infusions with arterial and central lines. A CVICU or trauma SICU role at one of these systems is far stronger on a CRNA application than a step-down or community-hospital "ICU" that does not manage that acuity.

The Application Timeline from Michigan

Working backward from a fall 2027 start: most Michigan programs accept applications in the fall 2026 window, interview over the winter, and decide by spring 2027. Have your CCRN completed and ICU hours documented before applications open. With several in-state programs clustered in the southeast, applying to multiple Michigan schools (plus one out-of-state) raises your odds without forcing a move out of the region.

Cohort and clinical-site reality: Michigan's programs lean heavily on the Detroit and Flint metros for didactic coursework, even though clinical rotations may send you to partner hospitals elsewhere in the state. If you live in Grand Rapids, the Upper Peninsula, or northern Michigan, confirm where the classroom phase is held and where rotations occur before committing — relocation or a long commute is common.
Michigan CRNA salary on graduation: New CRNAs in Michigan commonly earn $185,000–$215,000 in hospital employment, and independent or locum work can push total compensation above $240,000–$290,000. Combined with a lower cost of living than the coasts, Michigan is a strong place to graduate, find work quickly, and pay down school debt fast.
