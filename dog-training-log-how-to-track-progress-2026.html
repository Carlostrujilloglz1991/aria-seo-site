Dog Training Log: How to Track Progress and Make Training Stick

Dog training without records is the same workout session done repeatedly until it stops feeling productive. You remember the good days and forget the plateaus, then wonder why your dog still bolts for the door three months in. A training log makes the pattern visible and keeps you progressing.

Why it matters: Dogs don't learn from single sessions — they learn from patterns across sessions. A log lets you see that your dog does better on leash recall after morning training than afternoon training, or consistently struggles with "stay" when there are children nearby. That's trainable information you can't extract from memory alone.

What to log after every session

Date, time, duration. Location (indoor vs outdoor, quiet vs distracting). Which commands or behaviors you worked on. Success rate (rough estimate: 8/10 times, 3/10 times). What reward worked best. Your dog's energy level. Anything unusual: distraction, a scare, a win, a setback.

You don't need to write paragraphs. Three to five lines per session is enough. A notes app, a Google Doc, or a simple lined notebook all work. The act of writing triggers you to evaluate what happened rather than just moving on.

A sample log entry

FieldExample
Date / TimeJuly 12, 7:15 AM
LocationBackyard (low distraction)
Duration12 minutes
Commands worked"Sit," "Stay" (3 sec delay), "Come"
Success rateSit 9/10, Stay 5/10, Come 8/10
Reward usedFreeze-dried chicken — high drive today
NotesNeighbor's lawnmower = major distraction; stay fell apart completely; rebuild without distractors

How to read the patterns

After two weeks, look back at your entries. You'll likely notice things you couldn't see in the moment: which commands have consistent success rates above 80% (ready to add difficulty), which ones spike and dip based on context (need more proofing in new environments), and how your dog's performance correlates with factors like exercise before the session, time of day, or treat type.

A well-trained "sit" in a quiet kitchen should get a 9/10. The same "sit" in a park with other dogs should start at 3-4/10 and climb with repetition in that environment. If park performance never improves over three weeks, the log tells you that — and tells you to go back to lower-distraction environments and rebuild.

Using the 3D rule in your log

Professional trainers use the "3 Ds" framework: Duration, Distance, and Distraction. Add only one D at a time. Your log should note which D you were challenging that day. A stay that falls apart in a new environment doesn't mean the dog forgot the behavior — it means you jumped to distraction without enough proofing. The log makes that mistake visible.

Celebrate small wins in writing. Note when your dog offers a behavior without being asked, performs well in a new environment for the first time, or holds a "stay" two seconds longer than ever before. Progress in dog training is non-linear and easy to miss without documentation. Written proof of improvement is what keeps you motivated through the plateaus.

When to escalate or reassess

If a specific behavior hasn't improved over 3-4 weeks of consistent training, the log reveals it objectively. At that point, three options: simplify the criteria (make the task easier), change the reward, or consult a certified professional trainer (CPDT-KA or KPA-CTP credentialed). Persistent plateaus in specific behaviors sometimes indicate pain, fear, or a training approach that doesn't match the dog's learning style — not stubbornness.
