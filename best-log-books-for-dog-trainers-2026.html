Why Dog Trainers Need Structured Log Books

Memory fails in complex behavioral cases. A reactive dog with multiple triggers, competing reinforcement histories, and multiple family members involved in training requires documented baseline behavior, intervention history, and progress tracking. Without a log, you're re-assessing from scratch every session. With one, you see trajectory clearly and can adjust.

What Should a Dog Training Log Include?

A professional dog training log should track: dog name, breed, age, and handler name; session date and duration; commands practiced with success rate percentage; current behavior baseline for target behaviors; threshold distance for reactive dogs; reward type and value; compliance rate on previously learned behaviors; and homework assignment for the handler. If you're doing group classes, add group dynamics notes and individual dog progress per session.

For Service Dog Trainers Specifically

Public access training requires even more detailed documentation. ADI-compliant programs document specific public access behaviors, task training by disability category, exposure history, and test readiness assessments. A specialized service dog training log keeps you audit-ready and builds the documentation trail your clients may need for housing and travel accommodations.
