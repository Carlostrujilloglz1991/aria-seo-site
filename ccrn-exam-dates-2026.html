CCRN Exam Dates 2026 — How AACN Scheduling Works and When to Test

By The ICU Notebook — Updated 2026 · 7-minute read

This article was created with AI assistance and reviewed for clinical accuracy.
The CCRN does not have fixed exam dates: Unlike many licensing exams with scheduled windows, the CCRN is offered continuously year-round through Pearson VUE testing centers and via online remote proctoring. Once your application is approved by AACN, you choose your own date. This is good news for scheduling flexibility and challenging news for people who need an external deadline to stay on track.

Test Center vs. Online Remote Proctoring: Which Should You Choose?

AACN and Pearson VUE offer the CCRN via two formats: in-person at a Pearson VUE testing center, or online at home via OnVUE remote proctoring software. Each has genuine advantages and specific failure modes worth understanding before you choose.

FactorPearson VUE Test CenterOnVUE Remote (at-home)

Online remote proctoring failure modes are real. Multiple CCRN candidates report exam interruptions or terminations due to OnVUE detecting a noise violation (air conditioning, a pet, a notification sound from another device), a webcam angle issue, or internet interruption. If you live with other people or in a noisy environment, a testing center is strongly recommended. The inconvenience of traveling to a center is trivial compared to having your exam voided mid-question.

How Far in Advance Should You Schedule?

The practical answer: schedule the exam on the same day you receive your ATT letter, even if the test date you choose is 6–8 weeks out. Pearson VUE scheduling fills in popular time slots (Saturday mornings, early weekday slots before hospital shifts) at major testing centers. If you wait two weeks after receiving your ATT to schedule, you may find that the convenient slots near your home are gone and you're commuting further or testing at inconvenient times.

The strategic scheduling principle: Book your exam before you feel ready. The act of booking a specific date converts abstract studying into purposeful preparation. Most nurses who delay scheduling are not waiting because they need more time — they're waiting because the exam feels less frightening when it's theoretical. Book the date first; the preparation intensifies to meet it. This is the most consistent piece of advice from nurses who pass on the first attempt.

Your 90-Day ATT Window: Don't Lose It

Your Authorization to Test has an expiration date — typically 90 days from issuance. If you don't schedule and sit for the exam within that window, your ATT expires and you must pay a reactivation fee ($75–$100) to receive a new one. Your application itself doesn't expire (your eligibility window is typically 3 years from application approval), but failing to use your ATT wastes the scheduling fee. If life circumstances mean you genuinely cannot test in the 90-day window, contact Pearson VUE before the ATT expires — extensions are sometimes available with documentation.

Rescheduling and Cancellation Policies

Pearson VUE allows free rescheduling up to 24 hours before the exam appointment. Within 24 hours, a rescheduling fee applies (typically $35–$50). No-shows without cancellation forfeit the exam fee entirely. If you need to reschedule, do it early — the last-minute psychology of "I don't feel ready" is rarely a reliable signal that you need more time. Most candidates who reschedule based on nerves perform similarly on their rescheduled date as they would have on the original date.

Day-of Logistics for Test Center Exams

Arrive at the Pearson VUE test center 30 minutes before your scheduled exam time. Bring two forms of ID, one of which must be government-issued with your photo and signature (driver's license or passport). Your name on both IDs must match your AACN account exactly — nickname mismatches ("Bob" vs. "Robert") cause check-in delays and sometimes exam denials. You cannot bring any personal items into the testing room: no phone, no watch, no food or drink. The center provides a locker. You will receive a whiteboard or scratch paper for calculations. The exam is 150 questions with a 3-hour time limit; most candidates finish in 2–2.5 hours.

The morning of your exam: Eat a real breakfast. Your brain runs on glucose and the exam is cognitively demanding. Arrive calm rather than rushed. The 30-minute early arrival window is protective — use it to settle, not to cram. You won't learn anything useful in 30 minutes that changes your outcome, but arriving stressed absolutely will. The content is in your head. The only variable left is execution.
