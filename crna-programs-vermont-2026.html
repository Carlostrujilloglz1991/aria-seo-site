Vermont has no in-state accredited CRNA program. That doesn't close the door — it means Vermont nurses build their plan around nearby New England schools, several of which place students at Vermont clinical sites. With the right strategy, a Vermont base is very workable.

The landscape: There is no COA-accredited nurse anesthesia program located in Vermont. Vermont does, however, host clinical training for out-of-state programs — for example, the University of New England (Maine) uses sites such as Northeastern Vermont Regional Hospital in St. Johnsbury. So while you will enroll out of state, some of your clinical hours can be close to home.

Why Vermont Has No Program (and Why That's Manageable)

Vermont's small population and limited number of large surgical centers make it hard to sustain the case volume a nurse anesthesia program needs. That is the same dynamic seen in other small, rural states. The practical takeaway is simple: plan to attend school in a neighboring state, and treat Vermont as a strong clinical-site and eventual-practice location rather than an enrollment location.

Admission Requirements

Wherever you apply, the national CRNA template holds. You will need a BSN (or equivalent qualifying nursing degree) with a competitive GPA, a minimum of one year of full-time adult critical-care experience, an unencumbered RN license, and current BLS and ACLS certification. The ICU requirement is non-negotiable, and competitive applicants typically bring more than the minimum — the CCRN credential strengthens a file; see the CCRN guide. For the complete checklist, review the general CRNA school requirements.

Use Vermont's clinical-site role to your advantage. Programs that already place students at Vermont hospitals are natural targets — you may be able to complete part of your training near home even while enrolled out of state. Ask each program directly which clinical sites it uses in and around Vermont.
Confirm the current details. Program availability, residency rules, clinical-site arrangements, and admission requirements change. Treat this page as a planning baseline and verify each program's current information on its official site and the COA accredited-programs list before applying.

Application Strategy for Vermont

Build a regional plan from day one. Anchor your list with the University of New England (which uses Vermont clinical sites), then add Massachusetts and Connecticut programs. Strengthen your file with the required ICU year (ideally more), the CCRN, and a sharp personal statement, and plan financially for relocation or commuting. For realistic expectations, see CRNA acceptance rates.

Bottom Line

Vermont has no in-state CRNA program, but it sits inside a strong New England cluster — and some of those programs already train students at Vermont hospitals. Apply regionally, lean on the schools that use Vermont clinical sites, and you can pursue nurse anesthesia without leaving the area for good. For the bigger picture, see how to become a CRNA and the CRNA job outlook.
