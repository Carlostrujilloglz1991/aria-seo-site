7 Nurse Side Hustles That Pay Over $50/Hour in 2026

Part of the Nurse Money Hub — side income, passive income, and nurse financial independence in one map.

Your nursing license is not just an employment credential — it's a qualification that opens doors to income streams most professionals can't access. Here are seven that realistically pay above $50/hour in 2026, along with honest setup time and earning ceilings.

One rule before you start: Check your state's Nurse Practice Act and your employer's conflict-of-interest policy before starting any side work. Most do not prohibit outside clinical work, but some employers restrict you from working at competing facilities within a specific radius. Knowing this upfront prevents problems later.

1. Legal nurse consulting: $75–$150/hour

Law firms handling medical malpractice, personal injury, and workers' compensation cases need RNs who can review medical records, identify standard-of-care deviations, and explain clinical concepts to attorneys and juries. Legal nurse consultants (LNCs) typically charge $75–$150/hour for record review and up to $200/hour for expert witness testimony. Setup: CLNC certification from the American Association of Legal Nurse Consultants ($1,500–$2,500), then outreach to plaintiff and defense law firms. Most LNCs start getting consistent work within 6 months. ICU, ER, and OR experience are particularly valued by malpractice attorneys.

2. Per diem / PRN shifts at competing facilities: $55–$90/hour

Most major metro areas have per diem RN rates that run $10–$25/hour above regular staff pay. Critical care-certified nurses (CCRN) command the top of the range. Through agencies like Nomad Health, Aya Healthcare, and ShiftKey, experienced ICU nurses can pick up $65–$90/hour for per diem shifts with zero contract commitment. This is the lowest-barrier entry on this list — if you're already experienced, you can start within 2–3 weeks of applying.

3. PICC and vascular access: $60–$100/hour

Nurses with PICC insertion certification can work as vascular access specialists, either employed by infusion companies or on a contract basis for hospitals without dedicated IV teams. Independent vascular access contractors earn $60–$100/hour, often in 4–6 hour blocks. Certification: CRNI or VA-BC credential through the Infusion Nurses Society. Demand is strong because PICC insertion is a specialized skill many hospitals outsource entirely.

4. Telehealth triage: $50–$75/hour

Telehealth platforms — including MDLive, Teladoc partners, and nurse triage services — routinely hire experienced RNs for telephone and video triage roles at $50–$75/hour. Most are 1099 positions with flexible scheduling that works around shift schedules. ICU and ER backgrounds are preferred because of comfort with acute symptom assessment. You can often start with as little as 5 hours per week.

5. Nursing CEU course creator: $50–$200/hour (effective rate)

Nurses with deep specialty knowledge can create continuing education courses for platforms like Relias, Nurse.com, or independently through their own site. The upfront time investment is significant — a solid 1.5-credit CEU course takes 30–60 hours to build. But once approved and published, each sale generates passive income. A well-made ICU course priced at $29 that sells 500 units annually generates $14,500 from one-time work. At a realistic 60-hour build time, that's $242/hour effective rate in year one, and pure profit in year two.

6. Nurse coaching and health consulting: $75–$200/hour

Nurse coaches help patients, caregivers, and even other nurses navigate health decisions, care transitions, and chronic disease management. The International Nurse Coach Association (INCA) offers a certification pathway. Rates range from $75–$200/hour for private clients. This hustle requires building a client base (typically 6–18 months of marketing) but has no overhead costs and is fully flexible.

7. Expert witness testimony: $200–$500/hour

Experienced nurses with 10+ years in a specialty can serve as expert witnesses in medical litigation. This pays the highest hourly rate on this list — $200/hour for deposition preparation and $300–$500/hour for actual testimony. Cases are episodic (you might work 40 hours on one case over 6 months) but the effective hourly rate is unmatched. Getting established requires either a legal nurse consulting background or a direct referral from an attorney who already knows your credentials.
