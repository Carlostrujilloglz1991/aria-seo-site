AI Medical Scribes Compared: Abridge vs Suki vs DeepScribe vs Nabla

Ambient AI scribes have moved from novelty to mainstream in clinical documentation. They listen to a patient encounter and draft the note — cutting the after-hours "pajama time" that drives so much burnout. If you're a nurse practitioner, CRNA, or clinician evaluating one, four names come up constantly: Abridge, Suki, DeepScribe, and Nabla. Here's how they compare and what to weigh before adopting one.

The short version: All four are ambient AI scribes — they capture the visit conversation and generate a structured clinical note. Abridge is known for deep enterprise/health-system EHR integration. Suki pairs ambient notes with a voice assistant for hands-free commands. DeepScribe emphasizes specialty customization and coding support. Nabla is popular with independent clinicians and smaller practices for its accessibility. The right pick depends on your EHR, your setting (health system vs solo), and your budget.

What an ambient AI scribe actually does

The workflow is similar across products: with patient consent, the tool listens to the visit (in person or telehealth), transcribes it, and uses AI to produce a draft note — typically a structured SOAP or specialty-formatted note — that the clinician reviews, edits, and signs. The promise is time: less typing during and after the visit, more eye contact with the patient, and shorter charting backlogs. The critical caveat is that the clinician remains responsible for the final note — these are drafting aids, not autopilot.

Abridge — the enterprise integrator

Abridge has become a leading choice for large health systems, in part because of strong electronic-health-record integration (notably within Epic workflows) and enterprise-grade deployment. If your organization is standardizing on an ambient scribe across hundreds or thousands of clinicians, Abridge is frequently on the shortlist. For an individual clinician, availability usually depends on whether your employer has adopted it.

Suki — ambient plus voice assistant

Suki combines ambient documentation with a voice assistant you can command hands-free ("Suki, add to the assessment..."). That dual capability appeals to clinicians who want to both auto-draft notes and dictate or navigate by voice. It integrates with major EHRs and targets both health systems and practices.

DeepScribe — specialty and coding focus

DeepScribe leans into specialty-specific customization and support for accurate coding and documentation completeness — helpful where billing capture and specialty note structure matter. Practices that want the note tuned to their specialty's conventions, and that care about coding support, often evaluate DeepScribe closely.

Nabla — accessible for independents

Nabla has gained traction with independent clinicians and smaller practices for being relatively easy to adopt without a massive IT project. For a solo NP, a small group, or a clinician who wants to try ambient scribing without an enterprise contract, Nabla is a common entry point.

What to actually evaluate before adopting

The checklist that matters: Does it integrate with your EHR? How accurate are notes for your specialty and patient population (including accents and multi-speaker visits)? What's the pricing model (per-clinician subscription vs enterprise)? How much editing do notes actually need? What's the learning curve? And — non-negotiable — how does it handle privacy, consent, and HIPAA?

The privacy and responsibility caveats

Read the compliance fine print. These tools record and process protected health information, so you need a proper Business Associate Agreement, clear patient consent practices, and confidence in the vendor's data handling. Just as important: the AI can mishear, hallucinate details, or miss nuance. You must review and correct every note before signing — the legal and clinical responsibility stays with you, not the software. Never adopt one outside your organization's approved, sanctioned tools.

Why this matters for nurses and the CRNA path

Ambient AI documentation is increasingly part of advanced-practice and provider workflows — and it's a genuine AI tool nurses are encountering at the bedside and in clinics. Understanding how these systems work, and their limits, is becoming a practical clinical-literacy skill. For nurses moving toward NP or CRNA roles where you own the documentation, knowing how to use an ambient scribe well — and where it can fail you — is worth learning early.

Bottom line

Abridge, Suki, DeepScribe, and Nabla all do the same core job — turn a conversation into a draft note — but they aim at different users. Pick Abridge for enterprise/EHR-deep deployments, Suki for ambient plus voice control, DeepScribe for specialty and coding depth, and Nabla for accessible independent use. Whatever you choose, judge it on EHR fit, accuracy in your specialty, cost, and airtight privacy — and always review the note before you sign it.
