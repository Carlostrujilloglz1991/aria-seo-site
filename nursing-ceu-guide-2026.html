Nursing CEU Guide 2026: Requirements, Free Sources, and How to Make CE Count for Your Career

The bottom line on CE: Most states require 20–30 contact hours of continuing education for RN license renewal every 2 years, with some states requiring zero formal CE (relying on competency attestation instead). Free, legitimate CE is widely available — you should not be paying $100+ per renewal cycle unless you're specifically targeting a certification. The best approach: meet your state's requirements with free or low-cost CE that also advances your professional goals.

Continuing education is a license requirement that most nurses approach reactively — completing the minimum hours at renewal time, usually by binging whatever's cheapest. A small but growing segment of nurses approach CE strategically: choosing content that advances CRNA applications, builds clinical credibility, or documents professional development for clinical ladder portfolios. Both approaches meet the requirement; only one makes the hours count twice.

The Best Free CE Sources for Nurses in 2026

NursingCE.com (free tier): Offers free CE courses with certificate download upon completion of a post-test. Covers a wide range of clinical and professional topics. Popular with nurses who want to complete CE quickly without cost.

Medscape Nursing: Free CE in partnership with ANCC. Wide range of clinical content, particularly strong in pharmacology, disease management, and emerging clinical topics. Free account required for certificate download. Frequently updated with content relevant to ICU and acute care nurses.

ANCC (American Nurses Credentialing Center) CE: ANCC offers some free CE modules and has a robust CE catalog. Important because ANCC approval is one of the most widely recognized CE provider designations — if your state specifies "must be ANCC-approved," these count.

American Association of Critical-Care Nurses (AACN): AACN members get access to substantial CE, including CE specifically designed for ICU nurses. AACN membership costs approximately $50–100/year but provides CE value that far exceeds the cost for critical care nurses. Also provides CCRN exam prep resources.

AHA (American Heart Association) training: ACLS, BLS, and PALS renewal courses count as CE in many states. Most nurses must renew these annually or biannually anyway — if your state accepts AHA training as CE, count those hours.

Professional conferences: Nursing conferences (AACN's NTI, ENA annual, specialty conferences) offer CE certificates for attendance. If your employer pays for conference attendance, you're earning CE at no personal cost. Conference CE tends to be high-quality and clinically relevant.

Your hospital's education department: Most hospitals offer internal CE through skills labs, mandatory annual training, and educational events. Annual fire safety training, BLS renewal, mandatory compliance modules, and unit-based education may all carry CE credit depending on your hospital's approval status. Check with your education department — many nurses are already completing CE through required training and don't realize it counts.

Paid CE Platforms Worth the Cost

For nurses who prefer structured, high-quality CE and are willing to pay modestly:

CEUfast ($30–50/year unlimited): One of the most cost-effective unlimited CE subscriptions. Wide topic range, ANCC-approved, certificate download with post-test completion.

Relias ($40–80/year): Strong in specialty nursing areas, well-organized by specialty. Commonly used by hospitals for mandatory training.

Nurse.com (powered by AMN Healthcare): Subscription-based CE with broad content. Often has promotional offers and first-year discounts. Good for nurses who want organized course progression.

Making CE Strategic: Aligning CE with Career Goals

The simplest insight most nurses miss: CE content can simultaneously satisfy renewal requirements AND advance specific career goals. You don't have to choose between checking the compliance box and building your professional portfolio.

If you're pursuing CRNA: Choose CE in pharmacology, critical care, hemodynamics, pain management, and anesthetic-adjacent topics. Completing AACN's CE catalog for critical care nurses, advanced cardiac life support update modules, and clinical pharmacology CE gives you content that strengthens your CRNA application knowledge base while satisfying renewal requirements. Some CRNA programs specifically ask about CE completed in the 2 years before application.

If you're pursuing clinical ladder advancement: CE in the education, leadership, or evidence-based practice domains can be documented in your clinical ladder portfolio. Check which domains your facility's ladder requires and prioritize CE that maps to those areas. See the clinical ladder guide.

If you're preceptoring: Preceptor-specific CE — on adult learning principles, clinical teaching, competency evaluation — often counts toward CE requirements and directly strengthens your preceptor documentation. Some hospitals offer preceptor training programs that provide both CE credit and formal preceptor designation.

If you're interested in nursing informatics or healthcare AI: CE in healthcare technology, electronic health records, nursing informatics, and clinical decision support is available through AMIA and technology-focused CE providers. This is a growing area and CE documentation positions you for informatics roles or certifications (ANCC Nursing Informatics certification).

CCRN and Specialty Certification CE

Maintaining a specialty certification like CCRN requires CE as part of recertification. CCRN recertification (every 3 years) requires 100 CERPs (CCRN Educational Renewal Points) or a re-exam. Most of these can be earned through CE that also satisfies your state's renewal requirement — making CCRN holders essentially "double-dipping" on CE to meet both requirements simultaneously.

If you hold CCRN and are in a CE-required state, build your CE plan around AACN-approved content that satisfies both CCRN recertification AND state renewal at once. This is the most efficient approach — 30 hours of state-required CE and 100 CERPs are not necessarily different courses.

CE documentation discipline: Keep your CE certificates in a dedicated folder (digital or physical) for the entire renewal period. State boards of nursing conduct random audits of CE compliance. If audited, you must produce certificates proving completion within the renewal period. Most nurses who fail audits didn't fail to complete CE — they failed to keep the certificates. Cloud-stored certificate folders (Google Drive, etc.) protect against hard drive failures.
