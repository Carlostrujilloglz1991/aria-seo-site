CRNA Programs in South Carolina 2026

South Carolina concentrates its nurse-anesthesia training in two strong, long-accredited public programs — one in Charleston, one in Columbia. Both carry the maximum 10-year accreditation, a sign of program stability. Here is the 2026 landscape, what it costs, and how to get in.

The short answer: South Carolina has 2 accredited CRNA programs — the Medical University of South Carolina (MUSC) in Charleston and the University of South Carolina (USC) in Columbia — both doctoral DNAP programs holding full 10-year COA accreditation.

Accredited CRNA programs

Every program below is accredited by the Council on Accreditation of Nurse Anesthesia Educational Programs (COA). Confirm current accreditation status on the COA list before applying, since review dates change.

The Medical University of South Carolina anchors the state's nurse-anesthesia training with a Doctor of Nurse Anesthesia Practice that builds on more than 50 years of program history. MUSC's post-baccalaureate DNAP earned a full 10-year accreditation in June 2020, and as an academic medical center it brings high-acuity clinical exposure directly in-house.

The University of South Carolina in Columbia offers a 36-month DNAP that begins each May and graduates three years later. It received the maximum 10-year accreditation status in fall 2019, with its next COA review scheduled for fall 2029 — a long runway that signals program stability.

Both are public-university programs, which generally keeps in-state tuition more reasonable than the private options found in neighboring states, and both feed directly into the National Certification Examination pathway to CRNA practice.

Public programs, stable accreditation. Both South Carolina options are public and both hold the maximum 10-year accreditation cycle — a strong signal of program health. For in-state residents, this combination of stability and public tuition is hard to beat.

Two seats' worth of competition. With only two programs in the state, South Carolina applicants face tight odds. Apply to both MUSC and USC, and keep strong programs in North Carolina, Georgia, and Tennessee on your list so one cycle does not cost you a year.

How to strengthen your application

Get into the highest-acuity ICU you can and stay long enough to lead — a busy CVICU, SICU, or trauma unit carries more weight than years on a quieter floor. Earn your CCRN early; it is close to non-negotiable. If your GPA is below ~3.4, add or retake upper-level science courses and earn As to show momentum. Document CRNA shadowing before applying, and treat the interview as the deciding factor, because once you clear the screen, it usually is.

Build against a checklist, not a feeling. Benchmark your GPA, ICU type, hours, CCRN, and shadowing against the typical admitted profile, then close your widest gap first. See the CRNA school requirements guide, the acceptance-rate breakdown, and the ICU-to-CRNA timeline.

Bottom line

South Carolina offers two strong, long-accredited public CRNA programs — MUSC in Charleston and the University of South Carolina in Columbia — both doctoral DNAP tracks with the maximum 10-year accreditation. Public tuition and proven stability make them attractive in-state options. Land a high-acuity ICU year, a CCRN, and a ~3.5 GPA, apply to both, and widen your net regionally to protect your timeline.
