TNAA Review 2026: Travel Nurses Across America

Part of the Travel Nursing Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

TNAA — Travel Nurses Across America, based in North Little Rock, Arkansas — is the agency travelers mention when the conversation turns to housing. In an industry that has largely pushed housing risk onto the nurse via stipends, TNAA kept a real housing department. That single structural choice shapes most of what's distinctive about the agency.

The one-line version: TNAA is a traditional full-service agency whose standout assets are a genuine housing operation, 24/7 clinical support, and an unusually open "ask us anything" posture on pay breakdowns. The price of full service is the usual one: the margin comes from somewhere, so the gross can trail leaner competitors.

The housing department: why it matters

Most agencies today hand you a housing stipend and a goodbye wave — you find the apartment, you carry the lease risk, you eat the loss if a contract cancels in week three. TNAA still operates agency-placed housing as a first-class option: their housing staff source and arrange furnished housing near your facility, and if you take the stipend instead, they'll still actually help you search. For first-time travelers, nurses taking assignments in brutal rental markets, and anyone who has been burned by a mid-contract cancellation while holding a personal lease, this is not a soft perk — it's risk transfer, and it has real dollar value that never shows up in a gross-pay comparison.

Agency housing is not free money. If you take placed housing, the cost comes out of the same bill rate that would otherwise fund your stipend — and the stipend a disciplined traveler pockets by finding cheaper housing disappears. Take placed housing when the risk transfer is worth it (short-notice starts, tight markets, first contracts); take the stipend when you can beat the market. Do the math per contract with our housing stipend guide.

Transparency without a marketplace

TNAA is a recruiter-model agency, but it has leaned publicly into pay-package openness — the pitch is that a recruiter will break down the entire package (taxable rate, stipends, travel, bonuses) line by line on request rather than quoting one blurry weekly gross. That's meaningfully better than the industry's worst habits, while still being weaker than a marketplace where the number is printed before anyone can size you up. Treat it accordingly: use the openness, ask for the full breakdown in writing, and still benchmark against posted-pay platforms like Trusted Health or Nomad Health before you sign.

Clinical support and the safety net

TNAA staffs round-the-clock clinical support — experienced clinicians a traveler can call when an assignment turns unsafe: impossible ratios, floating outside competency, documentation pressure. Most travelers never use this; the ones who need it, need it badly. An agency's willingness to back a nurse against a facility is the single hardest thing to evaluate from the outside, and a permanent clinical team is at least structural evidence that the capacity exists. Where it shows its limits

The full-service model has a full-service cost structure: housing departments and 24/7 clinical teams are paid out of the same bill rate as your package, so TNAA's gross numbers often trail lean marketplaces and hard-nosed mid-sizers on identical contracts. If you always take the stipend, always find your own housing, and never call support, you are subsidizing services you don't use — the leaner models exist precisely for you. Job volume is solid across core specialties but, as with every mid-size agency, the mega-agencies' exclusive facility relationships still control some markets, particularly rural and niche-specialty postings.

Who it fits — and who it doesn't

TNAA fits first-time travelers who want the sharp edges filed off (housing handled, someone to call at 3 a.m.), nurses taking short-notice contracts in expensive or unfamiliar markets, and anyone who values a clinical escalation path more than the last $100/week of gross. It fits less well for maximum-rate hunters, stipend arbitrageurs who consistently beat market rents, and self-directed veterans who'd rather bank the service premium — those travelers belong on a marketplace, with TNAA as the comparison point that tells them what the service premium costs.

Bottom line: TNAA is the strongest full-service argument left in travel nursing: real housing infrastructure, a genuine clinical safety net, and a culture of explaining the package instead of hiding it. Pay for that bundle only if you'll use it — and if you will, TNAA belongs on your shortlist next to Host Healthcare.
