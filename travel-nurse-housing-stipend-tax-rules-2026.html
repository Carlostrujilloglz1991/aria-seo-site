Travel Nurse Housing Stipend Tax Rules 2026

Your housing stipend can be 100% tax-free — or fully taxable — depending on a few IRS rules most travel nurses don't know until audit time. Here's exactly what determines which applies to you.

The Short Answer

Travel nurse housing stipends are tax-free when you have a legitimate "tax home" that you maintain and return to. If you don't have a real tax home, every dollar of your stipend is ordinary income — and you'll owe back taxes plus penalties if the IRS catches it.

What Is a Tax Home?

The IRS defines your tax home as your principal place of business — not where you sleep, but where you regularly work. For travel nurses, this usually means your permanent residence in your home state, where you maintain a financial footprint.

To qualify, you generally need at least two of these three things:

  • You pay rent or mortgage at a home address in your home state
  • You return to that address between assignments (or at holidays/vacations)
  • You have not abandoned that address as your main home
Key IRS Test: If you truly live on the road with no permanent address — staying in agency housing, crashing with family, or moving from city to city — you do not have a tax home and your stipends are taxable. This is more common than agencies admit.

The "Duplicate Expense" Requirement

This is where most travel nurses get tripped up. To receive tax-free stipends, the IRS expects you to be incurring housing costs in two places simultaneously: your tax home and your assignment location. If your parents let you store stuff at their house for free, that doesn't count as maintaining a tax home.

Acceptable proof of tax home expenses:

  • Lease agreement or mortgage statements in your name at your home address
  • Utility bills, renter's insurance, or storage unit in your name
  • Car registration, voter registration, or driver's license at that address
  • Bank statements showing you pay housing costs from home

The 12-Month Rule — Extensions and Resets

Watch this rule carefully: Once you've worked at the same facility for 12 consecutive months, the IRS considers that your tax home. Your stipends become taxable from month 13 onward — even if your agency still pays them the same way.

How to handle extensions:

  • Break in service: A gap of more than 30 days resets the 12-month clock at most facilities
  • Same system, different campus: Working at Hospital A and Hospital B in the same health system may still count as the same assignment — get this in writing from a travel tax specialist
  • Contract extensions: Every extension adds to your running total at that location

Common Mistakes That Make Your Stipend Taxable

1. Giving Up Your Apartment Between Assignments

Many nurses cancel their lease to save money between assignments. The moment you stop paying for housing at your tax home, you may lose the tax-free status of your stipend. Subletting is often a better option — you maintain the lease while reducing costs.

2. Staying on Assignment More Than 12 Months

Hospitals love extending great travelers. Know your running total at every facility and have a clear plan before month 12.

3. Not Keeping Records

The IRS doesn't audit most travel nurses — but when it does, it goes back three years and wants documentation. Keep lease agreements, bank statements, and proof of visits home.

4. Trusting Your Agency's Tax Advice

Agencies are not your tax advisors. Some push higher stipend / lower base pay packages that look good on paper but require a valid tax home to be legitimate. Always verify with a travel nurse tax specialist.

Rule of thumb: If you have a solid, documentable tax home, maximize the stipend package. If you're unsure about your tax home status, take the higher-base option. The penalties for improperly claiming tax-free stipends are not worth the risk.

What to Do Right Now

If you're currently on assignment or about to sign a contract:

  1. Confirm you have a valid tax home with documented expenses (lease, mortgage, or storage plus utilities)
  2. Track your days at each assignment — know where you stand relative to the 12-month clock
  3. Consult a travel nurse tax specialist (not a general CPA) at least once — it's a few hundred dollars that can save thousands
  4. Keep a folder (physical or digital) with lease agreements, bank statements, and proof of visits home

Travel nursing can be one of the most lucrative career paths in healthcare — the tax advantages are a big part of why. But they only work when your paperwork is airtight.
