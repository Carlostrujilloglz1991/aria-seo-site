Best Log Books for Beekeepers in 2026

Updated June 2026 | 6-minute read

Short version: Hive inspection records are the difference between reacting to problems and preventing them. The best beekeeper log books track queen status, brood pattern, honey stores, varroa mite counts, and treatments in one place — organized by hive and date so you can actually compare across inspections.

Experienced beekeepers keep records. New beekeepers think they'll remember. Three inspections in, with multiple hives, you won't.

What you need to track per inspection: date and weather conditions, queen status (seen/eggs/brood pattern), population estimate, honey and pollen stores, signs of disease or pests, varroa mite wash count if performed, any treatments applied, and notes on temperament. That's 9 data points per visit, per hive, across every inspection of the season.

A structured log book makes this fast. A blank notebook makes it inconsistent — you'll record different things each time and won't be able to compare across visits.

What to Look For

The best hive inspection log books have pre-structured forms (not blank pages), enough space per entry for real notes, a seasonal summary section for end-of-year review, and a hive ID system so records from multiple hives stay separated. Bonus points for varroa tracking pages and treatment logs built in.

Digital vs. Paper for Hive Records

Apps like Hive Tracks and ApiaryBook exist. They're fine, especially if you're managing 20+ hives commercially. For hobbyist beekeepers with 1–10 hives, paper wins for speed: you're in the hive, wearing gloves, holding a frame. Tapping a phone screen with sticky gloves is slower than filling a checkbox with a pencil.

The hybrid approach works well: paper log in the field during the inspection, photo of the log pages at the end, photo stored in a phone folder organized by hive. You get the speed of paper and the backup of digital.

The Record You'll Actually Be Glad You Kept

Three years into beekeeping, the record you'll be most grateful for isn't the honey harvest log. It's the queen history: when each queen was introduced, whether she was marked, her egg-laying pattern over her life, and when you replaced her.

Queen quality degrades over time. A 3-year-old queen is statistically more likely to fail than a 1-year-old. Beekeepers who track queen age and pattern can replace queens proactively, before the colony declines. Those who don't often lose the hive trying to figure out why production dropped.

The log book is the queen record. Everything else is a bonus.
