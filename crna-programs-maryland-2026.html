CRNA Programs in Maryland 2026

Maryland punches above its size in nurse anesthesia: two of the country's most respected academic programs sit in Baltimore, with a federal military program just outside Washington. For an ICU nurse in the Baltimore–DC corridor, the case volume and program prestige are hard to beat. Here's how the Maryland programs compare.

Maryland CRNA market context: Maryland's programs are anchored to elite academic medical centers — Johns Hopkins and the University of Maryland Medical Center (home of the R Adams Cowley Shock Trauma Center). That translates to extraordinary clinical exposure. New graduates in the Baltimore–DC region commonly start around $185,000–$215,000.

Maryland has three COA-accredited nurse anesthesia programs, all awarding the Doctor of Nursing Practice. Every accredited U.S. program now awards a doctorate — the COA phased out master's-level CRNA entry on January 1, 2025. The Uniformed Services University program serves active-duty military nurses. Confirm current tuition, credit counts, and clinical-hour requirements directly with each school.

University of Maryland

The University of Maryland School of Nursing (UMSON) offers a full-time, 36-month DNP nurse anesthesia specialty in Baltimore, COA-accredited with its next program review scheduled for October 2027. UMSON pairs with the University of Maryland Medical Center and the R Adams Cowley Shock Trauma Center, giving students some of the highest-acuity trauma and surgical exposure in the country. Recent cohorts have reported a 100% first-time NCE pass rate and 100% employment. As a public program, in-state tuition (roughly $89,000 all-in) runs well below the out-of-state total — a meaningful advantage for Maryland residents.

Johns Hopkins University

The Johns Hopkins School of Nursing DNP in nurse anesthesia carries the weight of the Hopkins name and clinical system — one of the most prestigious academic medical centers in the world. Students train across the Johns Hopkins Hospital's high-acuity services. As a private program, tuition is higher than UMSON, but the brand recognition and clinical depth are exceptional. Both Baltimore programs admit small, competitive cohorts.

Programs want sustained adult critical care managing ventilated patients on vasoactive infusions with arterial and central lines. A CVICU, trauma SICU, or Shock Trauma role is far stronger on a CRNA application than a step-down or community "ICU" that does not manage that acuity.

The Application Timeline from Maryland

Working backward from a fall 2027 start: most Maryland programs accept applications roughly September–December 2026, interview January–March 2027, and decide by spring 2027. Have your CCRN completed and at least one year (ideally two) of high-acuity ICU hours documented before applications open. With two strong Baltimore programs plus easy access to programs in Virginia, Pennsylvania, and DC, Maryland applicants can build a robust list without relocating during school.

Prestige means small, selective cohorts: Hopkins and University of Maryland both draw national applicant pools and admit limited seats. Don't apply only to the two marquee Baltimore names — widen your list to nearby Virginia and Pennsylvania programs so a single competitive cycle doesn't become a wasted year.
Maryland CRNA salary on graduation: New CRNAs in the Baltimore–DC region commonly earn an estimated $185,000–$215,000 in hospital employment, with locum and federal assignments pushing higher. The corridor's dense academic and surgical volume keeps anesthesia demand steady.
