Pet Emergency Kit: What to Have Ready Before You Need It

Pet emergencies happen without warning and often at night or on weekends when your regular vet is closed. Having supplies, information, and a plan prepared in advance is the difference between reacting effectively and panicking.

The first thing to prepare: The phone number of your nearest 24-hour emergency veterinary clinic, saved in your phone right now. Know the route. Know the hours. This single piece of preparation costs nothing and has saved lives.

The home pet first aid kit

Keep all supplies in a labeled container (a fishing tackle box or small plastic bin works well) stored in an accessible location:

Digital rectal thermometer + lubricantNormal temp: dogs 101-102.5°F, cats 100.5-102.5°F
Sterile gauze pads and roll gauzeWound dressing
Self-adhesive bandage (Vetrap)Bandage that sticks to itself, not fur
Saline eye washEye irrigation, wound rinsing
Hydrogen peroxide 3% (fresh, unopened)Inducing vomiting in dogs ONLY, per vet instruction

Diphenhydramine (Benadryl, plain)Mild allergic reactions — dose per vet guidance

Information to prepare in advance

Write this on a card kept with the kit and saved in your phone: your pet's species, breed, weight, age, current medications and doses, known allergies, vaccination status, and microchip number. Your regular vet's name, number, and emergency protocol. Nearest 24-hour emergency clinic address and phone. ASPCA Animal Poison Control: (888) 426-4435 (note: charge per call).

What to do in specific emergencies before reaching the vet

Toxin ingestion: Call ASPCA Poison Control or your vet immediately before inducing vomiting. Some toxins cause more damage coming back up. The substance ingested, amount, and time since ingestion are the critical details to have ready.

Wound bleeding: Apply firm, direct pressure with a clean cloth for 5 minutes before checking. Do not remove and replace — lifting the cloth disrupts clot formation. If blood soaks through, add more material on top and press harder.

Suspected fracture: Minimize movement. Slide the pet onto a rigid surface (a cutting board for small pets, a piece of plywood for larger ones) without bending the affected limb. Muzzle if there's any risk of biting.

Heatstroke: Move to a cool environment. Apply cool (not cold or ice) water to the paws, armpits, and groin. Do not put ice directly on skin. Transport to vet immediately even if the animal seems to recover — internal organ damage is not visible.

CPR for pets: Pet CPR and first aid certification is available through PetTech and Red Cross (dogs and cats course). A 2-hour online course costs $25-$40 and covers the procedures that buy time before reaching emergency care. Worth the investment for anyone with a senior pet or a breed prone to cardiac issues.
