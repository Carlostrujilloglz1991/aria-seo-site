Remote Nursing Jobs That Pay $70,000+ (No More Night Shifts)

If you're a nurse reading this at 3 AM during your fourth consecutive night shift, know that there is a version of your career where you work from home, set regular hours, and earn a comparable salary. Remote nursing is no longer a niche category -- it's a legitimate career track that has expanded significantly since telehealth infrastructure matured, and it pays well enough to be a real alternative to bedside work.

Here are the remote nursing roles paying $70,000-$130,000+, what qualifications they require, and how to make the transition from the floor.

1. Telehealth Nurse / Virtual Triage RN

$65,000-$95,000/year

Telehealth triage nurses assess patients via phone or video, make clinical recommendations, and triage to appropriate care levels. This is the most direct transition from bedside nursing -- you're still making clinical decisions, just remotely. Platforms include Teladoc, MDLive, Fonemed, TriageLogic, and hospital-operated virtual care centers.

Requirements

  • Active RN license (compact NLC license preferred -- covers 40+ states)
  • Minimum 3-5 years clinical experience, typically ER or med-surg
  • Strong communication skills and comfort with protocol-based triage
  • HIPAA-compliant home office setup

Where to find jobs

Search "telehealth triage RN remote" on Indeed, LinkedIn, and company career pages for Teladoc, MDLive, Anthem, and Cigna. Hospital systems with virtual care centers (Mayo Clinic, Intermountain, UPMC) also hire telehealth nurses directly -- often with better benefits than standalone telehealth platforms.

2. Remote Case Manager / Utilization Review Nurse

$75,000-$105,000/year

Case managers work for insurance companies (Cigna, Aetna, United Healthcare, Humana), managed care organizations, and third-party administrators to review medical claims, determine medical necessity, and coordinate care for complex patients. The vast majority of these positions are fully remote with standard Monday-Friday business hours. No nights. No weekends. No holidays.

Requirements

  • RN license + 3-5 years clinical experience (any specialty, but med-surg, ortho, and medical case backgrounds are highly sought)
  • CCM (Certified Case Manager) credential adds $5,000-$12,000 to salary and prioritizes your application -- worth pursuing if you're serious about this track
  • InterQual or Milliman criteria experience is often listed in job postings but can be learned on the job
Pro tip: Many insurance companies have dedicated nurse career pages separate from their main job boards. Search "[Company name] nurse careers" directly rather than relying on aggregator listings -- internal postings often appear days earlier and sometimes include referral programs that jump your application to the top.

3. Clinical Informatics / EHR Implementation Nurse

$85,000-$130,000/year

Healthcare organizations that implement, upgrade, or optimize electronic health record systems (Epic, Cerner, Meditech) need nurses who understand clinical workflows. These roles involve training clinical staff, building order sets, configuring documentation tools, and troubleshooting workflow problems. Implementation consultants working for Epic, Cerner, or healthcare consulting firms are often fully remote with periodic on-site travel (typically 25-50% travel at implementation firms; less at health systems maintaining their own systems).

Requirements

  • RN license + EHR experience from bedside (even basic Epic charting experience is a starting point)
  • Epic certifications (available through Epic's own training programs after employer enrollment) are the gold standard. Epic-certified nurses are actively recruited.
  • ANCC Informatics Nursing Certification (RN-BC) is a credential that signals formal expertise in the field

Entry path: Many nurses break into informatics by becoming a "super user" on their current unit, then applying for an internal informatics or education coordinator role. This is a no-resume-gap transition that keeps income continuous while you build the credential.

4. Remote Nurse Educator / Staff Development (Hospital Remote)

$72,000-$100,000/year

Large hospital systems increasingly allow nurse educators and staff development specialists to work remotely, especially since eLearning, virtual simulation, and video training have become normalized. These roles develop training programs, update competency assessments, create onboarding materials, and coordinate continuing education.

Requirements

  • MSN preferred (BSN may be accepted with significant experience)
  • NPD-BC certification (Nursing Professional Development) is the relevant credential
  • Experience as a charge nurse, preceptor, or unit educator is a common pathway

5. Legal Nurse Consultant (Home-Based)

$80,000-$150,000+/year

Legal nurse consultants work for law firms reviewing medical records, writing case summaries, assessing standards of care, and preparing materials for litigation. This is 100% desk work you can do entirely from home. LNCs typically work as independent contractors managing multiple simultaneous cases, with rates of $100-$200/hr for their billable time.

Requirements

  • RN license + 5+ years clinical experience
  • CLNC certification (Certified Legal Nurse Consultant) through Vickie Milazzo Institute is the industry standard
  • Strong written communication -- case reports are the primary deliverable

6. Pharmaceutical Medical Science Liaison (MSL)

$120,000-$175,000/year + bonus

Medical Science Liaisons serve as the scientific and clinical bridge between pharmaceutical companies and healthcare providers. Nurses with specialty experience (oncology, neurology, cardiology) who also have advanced degrees (MSN, DNP) are competitive candidates. Most MSL roles are field-based but 80-90% remote/home-office in terms of office setup -- you travel to accounts but you don't commute to a corporate office.

Requirements

  • Advanced degree (MSN, DNP, or PharmD) strongly preferred
  • Clinical specialty expertise in a pharmaceutical focus area
  • Willingness to travel 30-60% of the time (regional territory, not national)

How to Get Your First Remote Nursing Job When You Have No Remote Experience

The most common barrier nurses face is the "chicken and egg" problem: remote jobs want remote experience, but you can't get remote experience without a remote job. Here's how to break through:

Leverage your current employer first

The path of least resistance is transitioning to a remote or hybrid role within the health system where you already work. Informatics, staff development, and quality improvement departments within your own hospital are the most accessible. You have an internal track record they can evaluate and an understanding of their specific systems.

Build a specific credential bridge

Each remote nursing category has a credential that signals seriousness: CCM for case management, RN-BC Informatics for health IT, CLNC for legal consulting. Getting the credential while still working bedside shows hiring managers you're committed to the transition -- not just shopping for an easier schedule.

Network in LinkedIn remote nursing groups

Search "remote RN," "case management nurse," and "telehealth nurse" on LinkedIn and follow the accounts actively posting in those spaces. Many remote nursing positions are filled through referrals before they're ever posted on job boards. A warm introduction from someone already in the role is the fastest path to an interview.

Apply to insurance company nursing roles directly

Cigna, Aetna, United Healthcare, Humana, and Anthem collectively employ thousands of remote nurses. They post roles constantly and are accustomed to onboarding experienced bedside nurses into remote case management and utilization review positions. These companies are often the easiest entry point into remote nursing because they have structured training programs that don't assume prior remote experience.

The Salary Reality Check

Remote nursing jobs at the $70,000-$90,000 level are real and accessible for nurses with 3-5 years of bedside experience and the right credential. Remote nursing jobs at $100,000-$130,000 generally require advanced degrees or specialty certifications. The top of the range ($150,000+) requires either years of remote career building (LNC practice, informatics leadership) or an advanced practice credential.

The trade-off compared to bedside nursing is usually this: you give up differential pay and overtime potential, but gain predictable hours, no physical toll, and in many cases the same base salary with better work-life balance. For nurses with 5+ years of experience, the net financial impact of going remote is often neutral to slightly positive -- with an enormous quality of life upside.

The Bottom Line

The nursing shortage means employers need you. The telehealth expansion means they need you virtually. The credential gap in nursing informatics and case management means qualified applicants are in short supply. If you're a bedside nurse who wants off the floor -- not out of nursing -- there has never been a better time to make that transition. The remote nursing career path is real, it's growing, and it starts with one credential and one application.
