The Nursing Licensure Compact (NLC) is the single most valuable administrative tool for travel nurses. One license, issued in your home state, lets you practice in 40+ states without applying for additional licenses. For a travel nurse targeting high-paying markets across multiple states, the compact can save $2,000–$8,000 in licensing fees and months of processing time.

The compact advantage in numbers: Without compact — applying for individual licenses in 5 states costs $500–$2,000+ and 4–12 weeks per state. With a compact license — walk into any compact state assignment immediately. If your home state is compact, you already have it.

How to Get Your Compact License

Step 1: Establish Primary State of Residence (PSR)

Your compact license is issued through your primary state of residence — where you physically live and claim as your legal domicile. This must be a compact state. Documentation typically required:

  • State-issued driver's license or ID showing the address
  • Vehicle registration in the state (if applicable)
  • Voter registration in the state
  • Bank statements or utility bills showing the address
  • Federal income tax return listing the state address

You don't need to own property — renting a room in a compact state and maintaining documentation there is sufficient for most boards.

Step 2: Apply Through Your Home State Board

Apply for your RN license through your PSR's State Board of Nursing. On the application, indicate you're applying for the compact license. Most compact states issue the compact designation automatically when residency is established — there's no separate compact application in most states.

Step 3: Verify Your Compact Status

Check Nursys.com (the National Licensure Verification System) to confirm your license shows as "multi-state" rather than "single-state." This is the verification agencies and hospitals use.

The Travel Nurse Compact Strategy

If Your Home State Is Already Compact

You likely already have compact privileges. Verify on Nursys.com. If your license shows single-state, contact your state board — it may be a designation error that's correctable without re-applying.

If Your Home State Is NOT Compact

You have two options:

  1. Apply for individual state licenses as needed for each assignment. This is the slower, more expensive path but doesn't require changing your legal domicile.
  2. Change your primary state of residence to a compact state. This requires genuinely establishing domicile (not just a mailbox) in a compact state. Travel nurses who do this — often establishing legal residency in a compact state while traveling — gain immediate multi-state access. The logistics: maintain an actual address in the compact state, update your driver's license, voter registration, and bank accounts.

APRN Compact: Different from RN Compact

The NLC covers RNs and LPNs only. APRNs (NPs, CRNAs, CNMs, CNSs) have a separate compact — the APRN Compact — which has slower adoption. As of 2026, fewer states have enacted the APRN Compact. CRNAs and NPs planning to travel need to verify their specific state's APRN compact status independently, as it differs from the RN compact map.

What Happens to Your Compact License When You Move?

If you move to a new state, you must apply for a license in your new home state within 90 days (most states). Your compact privileges transfer to your new state if it's also compact. If you move to a non-compact state, you lose compact privileges and must operate on single-state licenses until you re-establish residency in a compact state.

Travel nurses who maintain a permanent compact-state address while traveling keep their compact privileges indefinitely — as long as the compact state remains their PSR.
