Travel Nurse Taxes in New Hampshire 2026

Part of the Travel Nurse Tax Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

New Hampshire is one of the most tax-friendly states a travel nurse can work: it has never taxed earned wages, and its old tax on interest and dividends has now been repealed. That doesn't make taxes disappear — the federal stipend rules still govern your pay. Here's how it works.

Not tax advice. General educational information only. Your situation is fact-specific — consult a tax professional who understands travel healthcare before filing.

No State Income Tax on Wages

New Hampshire does not tax earned income. There is no state income tax on wages, so the taxable wage portion of a New Hampshire assignment isn't subject to any state income tax — only federal income tax and FICA (Social Security and Medicare). For years New Hampshire did tax interest and dividends through a separate Interest & Dividends (I&D) tax, but that tax was phased down and repealed effective 2025. As of 2026 there is effectively no state personal income tax at all, which makes New Hampshire comparable to Texas, Florida, and Nevada for travelers.

The Federal Rules Still Apply: Wages vs. Stipends

No state income tax doesn't change the federal split of your pay. Every travel contract divides pay into a taxable hourly wage and non-taxable reimbursements (housing, meals, incidentals). Those stipends are only tax-free at the federal level if you maintain a genuine tax home and are temporarily working away from it.

Tax Home: Still the Central Rule

Your tax home is generally the place where you keep a permanent residence and to which you regularly return. To keep stipends tax-free, you must be duplicating living expenses — maintaining a home base you pay for and paying for lodging on assignment. If you have no legitimate tax home (an "itinerant" worker in IRS terms), your stipends become taxable wages — and even in no-tax New Hampshire that means a bigger federal bill.

New England border note: Many New Hampshire assignments are near the Massachusetts line, and some travelers live in one state and work in the other. Massachusetts does tax income earned there, so where you work — not just where you sleep — drives your state liability. If you split time across the border, track which wages were earned in which state.

The 12-Month Rule

The IRS generally treats a work location as "temporary" only if the assignment is realistically expected to last one year or less. Work the same New Hampshire area — Manchester, Nashua, the Seacoast, the Lakes Region — continuously beyond roughly 12 months and the IRS can deem it your new tax home, making stipends there taxable. Travelers commonly rotate locations and take breaks before returning to keep assignments clearly temporary.

Practical New Hampshire Takeaways

New Hampshire is a genuinely tax-friendly state for travelers now that both the wage income tax (never existed) and the interest-and-dividends tax (repealed) are off the table. Your take-home advantage over a high-tax state is real. Just remember the federal tax-home rules make your stipends tax-free everywhere, so protect your tax home, watch the Massachusetts border if you cross it, and don't overstay one area past the 12-month zone. Frequently Asked Questions

Does New Hampshire tax travel nurse wages? No — New Hampshire has no state income tax on earned wages. You still owe federal income tax and FICA on the taxable wage portion.

What about the interest and dividends tax? New Hampshire's former I&D tax was phased out and repealed effective 2025, so for 2026 there is effectively no state personal income tax.

Are my stipends tax-free in New Hampshire? They're tax-free federally only if you maintain a valid tax home and work temporarily away from it. No state income tax doesn't change that federal requirement.
