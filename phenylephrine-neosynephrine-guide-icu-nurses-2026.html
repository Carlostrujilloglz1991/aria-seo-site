Phenylephrine (Neo-Synephrine): The ICU Nurse's Guide

Part of the ICU Pharmacology Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Phenylephrine is the simplest pressor to understand and one of the most useful to have in your pocket — literally, as a push-dose syringe. It does one thing: squeeze the vessels. No inotropy, no rate boost. That purity makes it the right tool in very specific situations and the wrong tool in others, and knowing the difference is bread-and-butter anesthesia thinking.

The short version: Phenylephrine is a pure alpha-1 vasoconstrictor. It raises blood pressure by clamping down systemic vascular resistance, with no direct effect on contractility. Its signature: reflex bradycardia (the heart slows as pressure rises). It's a mainstay push-dose pressor and the pick when you want pressure without tachycardia.

What phenylephrine is and how it works

Phenylephrine stimulates only alpha-1 receptors, causing arterial and venous vasoconstriction. That raises systemic vascular resistance and blood pressure. Because it doesn't touch beta receptors, it adds nothing to heart rate or contractility directly. In fact, as the pressure climbs, the baroreceptor reflex often slows the heart — the classic reflex bradycardia that defines this drug.

Phenylephrine is the classic push-dose pressor: a small IV bolus to bridge a patient through brief, sharp hypotension — after intubation meds, during a spinal, or a transient drop — while you address the cause or spin up an infusion. Every ICU and anesthesia nurse should know their institution's push-dose setup and never mix it from memory.

When phenylephrine is the right pick

It shines when you want to raise pressure without adding heart rate: a tachycardic patient who's hypotensive, aortic stenosis or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy where you want tone but not tachycardia, or hypotension from vasodilation (e.g., after induction or neuraxial anesthesia). It's clean, predictable, and easy to titrate.

The wrong pick for a failing pump. Because it only constricts, phenylephrine raises the afterload the heart pumps against without helping contractility. In cardiogenic shock or a poor-output ventricle, clamping down harder can drop cardiac output. Match the drug to the problem: low tone gets phenylephrine, a weak pump needs an inotrope like dobutamine.

The side effects that matter at the bedside

Reflex bradycardia. Expected, not always a problem — but in a patient dependent on heart rate for cardiac output it can backfire.

Reduced cardiac output. Increased afterload can lower output in a compromised ventricle.

Extravasation. Like other vasoconstrictors, it can injure tissue if it infiltrates; central access is preferred for prolonged infusions, though its peripheral and push-dose use is well established.

Bedside rule of thumb: Reach for phenylephrine when the patient is hypotensive and tachycardic, or when you specifically don't want more rate. Reach for norepinephrine when you want tone plus a little inotropic support, as in septic shock.

Bottom line

Phenylephrine is the pure squeeze: alpha-only vasoconstriction, reflex bradycardia, and no inotropy. It's the go-to push-dose pressor and the smart choice when you need pressure without tachycardia — but the wrong choice for a failing pump. Master when to grab it versus norepinephrine at the bedside, and you'll make that decision instinctively the first time a blood pressure drops in the OR.
