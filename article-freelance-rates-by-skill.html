What to Charge as a Freelancer in 2026: Market Rates by Skill

Underpricing is the most common mistake new freelancers make, and it creates a self-reinforcing problem: low rates attract price-sensitive clients, which produces bad testimonials, which makes raising rates harder. Getting your pricing right from the start — or correcting it as soon as possible — is the single most impactful business decision you'll make. Here are 2026 market rates by skill category, with the math to figure out what you actually need to charge.

Your freelance rate must cover more than your equivalent salary. As a freelancer, you pay both sides of self-employment tax (15.3%), your own health insurance, retirement savings, business expenses, and have no paid time off. A $50/hour freelance rate is not equivalent to a $50/hour employee salary. It's closer to $35–$38/hour in take-home terms after these costs.

The freelance rate formula

Start with your target annual take-home income. Add back taxes (roughly 25–30% of gross), health insurance ($4,000–$12,000/year depending on plan), retirement contributions (aim for 15% of gross), and business expenses (software, equipment, professional development: $2,000–$6,000/year). Then divide by your realistic billable hours per year — which for most full-time freelancers is 1,000–1,200 hours, not 2,080, because time goes to admin, marketing, and client acquisition.

Example: Target take-home $65,000 + taxes ($22,000) + health insurance ($7,000) + retirement ($13,000) + expenses ($4,000) = $111,000 gross needed. Divide by 1,100 billable hours = $101/hour minimum rate. Many freelancers are shocked by this number — and then realize they've been dramatically underpricing.

Project rates vs hourly rates

Experienced freelancers generally prefer project-based pricing over hourly. Project pricing rewards efficiency: if you write faster because you've written 500 articles, you earn more per hour without raising your quoted rate. For clients, project pricing is also preferable because they know the cost upfront. A 1,500-word SEO blog post might be priced at $300–$600 as a project, which translates to $100–$150/hour for an experienced writer who completes it in 2–4 hours.

Retainer arrangements: Monthly retainers are the most financially stable freelance structure. A client committing to 10 hours/month at $100/hour = $1,000/month of predictable revenue. Three to four retainer clients at that level can form a stable full-time freelance income. Retainers typically command a slight rate premium (10–15%) because you're reserving capacity for the client.

When to raise your rates

Raise rates when you're booked more than 80% of your available time for two consecutive months (demand is outpacing supply), when you get a new certification or significant portfolio addition, annually at the start of a new year, and every time you take on a new client (existing clients often get "loyalty" rates, while new clients pay current market). Never apologize for rate increases. Send a 30–60 day notice and state the new rate matter-of-factly: "Beginning [date], my rate for [service] will be $X."

The platform trap: Fiverr, Upwork, and similar marketplaces train clients to expect extremely low prices and train freelancers to race to the bottom. These platforms are useful for building a portfolio from zero, but most established freelancers find better clients at better rates through direct outreach, referrals, and LinkedIn. Treat platforms as a launchpad, not a long-term business model.
