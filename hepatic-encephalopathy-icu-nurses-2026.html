Hepatic Encephalopathy for ICU Nurses 2026 — When the Liver Lets the Brain Fog Over

Part of the ICU Emergencies Hub — browse every related guide in one place.

Hepatic encephalopathy (HE) is the reversible decline in brain function that happens when a failing liver can no longer clear the toxins — chiefly ammonia — that a healthy liver normally detoxifies. It ranges from subtle personality change and day-night reversal all the way to deep coma, and it is one of the most common reasons a patient with liver disease lands in the ICU. The encouraging part is that HE is usually reversible: treat the trigger, lower the ammonia burden, and the brain often clears. The ICU nurse sits at the center of that recovery — assessing mental status, protecting the airway, delivering lactulose, and hunting down the precipitant.

The short version: A failing liver lets ammonia and other toxins reach the brain, causing confusion that grades from subtle (Grade 1) to coma (Grade 4). Treatment is two-pronged: lower the ammonia (lactulose first-line, rifaximin added) and find and fix the precipitant (infection, GI bleed, constipation, dehydration, electrolyte shifts, sedatives). The nurse's job: serial neuro assessment, airway protection as grades climb, titrating lactulose to stool output, and searching for the trigger.

The ammonia story

Gut bacteria produce ammonia as they break down protein. Normally the liver extracts that ammonia from portal blood and converts it to urea for excretion. In cirrhosis or acute liver failure, two things happen: the liver cells can't do the conversion, and blood shunts around the liver through collateral vessels, so ammonia bypasses detoxification and reaches the systemic circulation. It crosses into the brain, where it disturbs neurotransmission and causes astrocytes to swell — producing the confusion, and in severe cases the cerebral edema seen in acute liver failure. Ammonia is the useful mental model, but a single ammonia level correlates poorly with severity; HE is a clinical diagnosis, graded at the bedside, not by a lab number.

Grading what you see

The West Haven criteria give a shared language for how deep the encephalopathy runs. Learning to grade it — and to notice a change of even one grade — is core nursing work, because a rising grade signals both a worsening liver and a rising airway risk.

GradeWhat the patient looks likeKey nursing concern
1Mild confusion, altered sleep, short attention, subtle tremorEasy to miss; establish a baseline
2Lethargy, disorientation, asterixis (flapping tremor), inappropriate behaviorFall and safety risk rising
3Marked confusion, very drowsy but rousable, incoherentAirway protection becoming a concern
4Coma, unresponsive to stimulationAirway usually needs to be secured

Asterixis — the coarse flap when the patient holds the wrists extended — is a classic, quick bedside sign of grade 2 to 3 HE and worth checking on rounds.

Hunt the precipitant — that is the real fix

HE rarely worsens for no reason. Something tipped the patient over, and finding it is often more important than any drug. The classic precipitants are infection (including spontaneous bacterial peritonitis), a GI bleed that dumps a protein load of blood into the gut, constipation, dehydration and over-diuresis, electrolyte disturbances (especially low potassium and metabolic alkalosis), kidney injury, and sedating medications. A nurse who notices a patient hasn't stooled in days, is running a fever, has a dropping hemoglobin, or was just given a benzodiazepine has likely found the trigger — and flagging it changes the whole course.

Sedatives are a trap. Benzodiazepines and opioids are cleared by the liver and dramatically worsen HE — and a confused, agitated HE patient can look like they "need something to settle down." Avoid sedatives when possible; agitation in HE is usually treated by fixing the ammonia and the trigger, not by sedating the brain further. If sedation is unavoidable for safety or intubation, it is done deliberately and cautiously, knowing it will cloud the neuro exam.

Lactulose, rifaximin, and titrating to stool

The workhorse treatment is lactulose, a non-absorbable sugar that does two things: it draws ammonia into the gut and traps it for excretion, and it acidifies the colon and speeds transit so less ammonia is absorbed. It is dosed to effect — titrated to roughly two to three soft stools per day, given orally, by tube, or by retention enema in a comatose patient who can't swallow safely. Too little and the HE doesn't clear; too much and the patient becomes dehydrated and hypokalemic, which itself worsens HE. Rifaximin, a poorly absorbed antibiotic that reduces ammonia-producing gut bacteria, is added to lactulose and is especially useful for preventing recurrence. Tracking stool output is not a menial task here — it is how the primary therapy is dosed.

The nursing watch: airway and safety

As the grade climbs, the two dominant nursing concerns are the airway and safety. A patient sliding from grade 2 toward grade 3 to 4 is losing the ability to protect their airway and is at real risk of aspiration; escalate early rather than waiting for a coma. Meanwhile the confused, disinhibited patient is a serious fall and safety risk — reorientation, a safe environment, and close observation matter more than restraints, which can increase agitation. Serial, documented neuro checks are the backbone: a patient who is one grade worse than your last assessment is telling you the ammonia burden or the trigger is winning, and that is the moment to act.

A falling mental status is a call, not a note. The single most important nursing signal in HE is a downward trend in the level of consciousness. Combined with a check for the precipitant — fever, a GI bleed, no recent stool, a new sedative, worsening kidney function — a documented grade change gives the team exactly what they need to intervene before the airway is lost. Don't wait for the next scheduled assessment when the patient is clearly deeper than before.

The nursing bottom line

Hepatic encephalopathy is reversible brain dysfunction from a liver that can no longer clear ammonia, graded at the bedside from subtle confusion to coma. Treatment is lowering the ammonia with lactulose titrated to two to three soft stools a day, adding rifaximin, and — just as importantly — finding and fixing the precipitant, whether that's infection, a bleed, constipation, dehydration, electrolytes, or a sedative. Avoid worsening it with benzodiazepines, protect the airway as the grade climbs, keep the confused patient safe, and treat any drop in level of consciousness as an event that needs an immediate response.
