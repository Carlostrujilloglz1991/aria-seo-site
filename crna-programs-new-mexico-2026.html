CRNA Programs in New Mexico

New Mexico has exactly one accredited nurse anesthesia program — New Mexico State University's DNP in Nurse Anesthesiology. It is brand new, built deliberately around the state's rural anesthesia shortage, which makes it both a real opportunity and a single, competitive target.

The landscape: New Mexico has a single COA-accredited nurse anesthesia program — the New Mexico State University School of Nursing in Las Cruces, which enrolled its first cohort in January 2024. With one young program in the state, in-state applicants should plan to apply across the Southwest as well.

New Mexico State University's School of Nursing runs the only nurse anesthesia program in the state. It awards a Doctor of Nursing Practice with a Nurse Anesthesiology specialty, takes three years to complete full-time, and runs roughly 102 credit hours with a minimum of 2,000 clinical hours — the level of training required to sit for the national certification exam. The program is accredited by the Council on Accreditation of Nurse Anesthesia Educational Programs (COA) and opened its first cohort in January 2024.

Built for Rural New Mexico

NMSU launched this program for a specific reason: New Mexico and the surrounding region face a severe shortage of anesthesia providers, especially in rural health centers and critical-access hospitals. The curriculum is deliberately oriented toward rural health and the health disparities common across the state. For applicants, that focus is a feature worth speaking to directly — a candidate who understands and wants to serve rural anesthesia needs fits the program's mission and should say so in their application.

Admission Requirements

NMSU follows the national CRNA template. You will need a BSN (or an equivalent qualifying nursing degree) with a competitive GPA, a minimum of one year of full-time adult critical-care experience, and current BLS and ACLS certification. As everywhere, the ICU requirement is non-negotiable, and competitive applicants typically bring more than the minimum year, high-acuity experience, and the CCRN credential — see the CCRN guide. For the full checklist, review the general CRNA school requirements.

One young program means you should apply widely. With a single, recently opened cohort in the state, treating NMSU as your only target is risky. Build a regional list that includes nearby states with established programs — Texas, Arizona, and Colorado are all within reasonable reach and expand your odds considerably.
Confirm the current numbers. Because this program is new, cohort size, tuition, credit count, and admission rules are still settling and can change year to year. Treat the figures here as a planning baseline and verify NMSU's current details on its official site and the COA accredited-programs list before applying.

Application Strategy for New Mexico

Because New Mexico has a single, new program, your plan should be regional from day one. Apply to NMSU — and lean into its rural-health mission in your essay — but build a list that also includes Texas, Arizona, and Colorado to give yourself realistic odds across a competitive applicant pool. Strengthen your file with the required ICU year (ideally more), the CCRN, and a sharp personal statement. For realistic expectations, see CRNA acceptance rates.

Bottom Line

New Mexico State University in Las Cruces is the state's only accredited CRNA program: a three-year, COA-accredited DNP in Nurse Anesthesiology built specifically to address New Mexico's rural anesthesia shortage. It's a genuine opportunity, but with one young cohort, New Mexico applicants should apply regionally to give themselves a fair shot.
