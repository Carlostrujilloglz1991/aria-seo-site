Ask a group of travel nurses where they found their last contract and Vivian Health comes up constantly — usually alongside a misunderstanding about what it actually is. Vivian is not a staffing agency. It doesn't employ you, pay you, or hold your contract. It's a job marketplace: an aggregator that pulls travel, permanent, per-diem, and local-contract listings from many staffing agencies into one searchable feed, with estimated pay displayed up front. That one design decision — showing the money before you talk to a recruiter — is why it matters, and also the source of its biggest limitation.

How the model works

Agencies pay to source candidates through the platform; nurses use it free. You build one profile, browse aggregated listings with pay estimates, and when you apply, you're routed to a recruiter at whichever agency holds that contract. From that point forward, the relationship is a normal agency relationship — the agency employs you, negotiates your package, and handles payroll, housing stipends, and benefits. Vivian is the storefront, not the store. That means everything we cover in how pay packages actually work, W-2 vs 1099, and housing stipends still applies in full once you click apply.

Where Vivian genuinely helps

  • Pay transparency as negotiating leverage. The travel industry historically ran on information asymmetry: the recruiter knew the bill rate, you knew nothing. Seeing estimated weekly pay across many agencies for the same market compresses that asymmetry. Even if you never take a Vivian-sourced contract, twenty minutes of browsing tells you whether your recruiter's "great package" is actually mid — ammunition for the conversation we script in the negotiation guide.
  • One profile, many agencies. Instead of re-entering your license, certifications, and work history into a dozen agency portals, you maintain one profile and let agencies come to you. For nurses comparing multiple agencies — which you should always do (see AMN vs Aya vs Cross Country) — this is a real time saver.
  • Market discovery. Filtering by specialty, state, and shift across the whole aggregated market surfaces contracts and locations you'd never see inside a single agency's inventory — useful when you're optimizing for a specific goal, like a tax-friendly state or proximity to a CRNA program you're targeting.

Where it can't help you

  • The estimate is not the offer. Displayed pay is typically the agency's advertised package, often built on maximum stipends and specific shift assumptions. The real number emerges in negotiation with the recruiter, and it can land lower — or higher, if you push. Treat listings as market data, not promises.
  • It doesn't vet the agency experience. Aggregation means the same feed contains excellent agencies and chaotic ones. Recruiter quality, cancellation behavior, and payroll reliability — the things that actually ruin contracts — still require your own diligence on the specific agency behind the listing.
  • Duplicate and stale listings. The same hospital need often appears through multiple agencies at different advertised rates, and hot listings can be filled before the feed updates. High-demand contracts still go to nurses with fast, complete profiles and responsive recruiters.

The marketplace and the gig app are frequently confused, and they're structurally opposite: Vivian routes you into traditional stipend-based agency contracts, while gig platforms pay a flat taxable hourly rate per shift with no contract at all — a completely different tax and income profile, which we break down in the Clipboard Health review and gig vs agency.

Bottom line: Vivian Health is worth using for what it is — the best free window into travel nurse market pricing, and a low-effort way to be visible to many agencies at once. It is not a substitute for vetting the agency, negotiating the package, or understanding your own tax situation. Use the marketplace to gather leverage; use your own judgment to sign.

A smart workflow

Browse Vivian to map the market rate for your specialty and target states. Shortlist two or three agencies whose listings consistently look strong, then vet those agencies independently. Negotiate using the market data you gathered. Before signing, run the package through the full checklist — taxable base vs stipends, cancellation terms, guaranteed hours — from the pay package guide, and sanity-check the career math in travel nurse salary 2026 and travel vs staff over 10 years.
